## Source of Clean Energy
### Biomass
#biomass #biofuels
> Biomass energy is energy from organic matter—both plant materials and animal waste. Plant-based biomass energy is considered a "sustainable" source of energy because plants absorb carbon dioxide from the atmosphere, and when burned, emit the same amount of carbon dioxide back into the air. So in theory, biomass energy is carbon "neutral." But the reality is quite different

About 10% of global primary energy comes from biomass. Many rural people around the developing world rely on wood, charcoal, animal dung, and other biomass to provide energy for household cooking and heating, and small-scale rural industrial processes, like brick-making and food processing.
- You can read more about biofuels [here](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-981-15-3761-5_8).
- Seems to be a pretty controversial one. 
https://textbook.terra.do/class/m-energy-3?token=energy3&utm_source=drip&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=Free+energy+class+-+email+with+modules&utm_content=Links+to+access+Energy+Class

_Modern_ bioenergy sources include:

- Solid fuels like torrefied wood pellets or briquettes
- Biofuels, which include: 
    - First-generation fuels from food crops (ethanol and biodiesel)
    - Second-generation fuels from lignocellulosic biomass
    - Third-generation fuels from algae
    - Fourth-generation fuels like electrofuels and photobiological solar fuels—these are still experimental systems

[[FACTS about Biomass]]

in 2022, a [five-year study](https://civileats.com/2022/02/14/how-corn-ethanol-for-biofuel-fueled-climate-change/) of the impact of corn ethanol—a widely used fuel additive in the U.S.— found that it has led to increased fertilizer use, water pollution, and likely _at least 24 percent more emissions than gasoline._

The [World Resources Insitute reports](http://www.wri.org/research/avoiding-bioenergy-competition-food-crops-and-land) that increasing biofuels cultivation takes precious land away from food production and that ambitious biofuel targets could significantly increase the “food gap” between crop calories available vs. those needed in 2050.

While large-scale use of biofuels has fallen out of favor, biomass energy based on crop residues and/or animal waste can be sustainably used on a _small scale_, and can often be an excellent solution for rural energy needs. 

> A good exemple is a story featured in Ecologie360 (p21) of a farmer running all his vehicles on Cows poos in Dordogne, using all animal wasts  to ferment where it produce Biomethane. Transforming it into fuel is much better for the environment. Other farmer choose to stock the effluents to aliment their reservoir, resulting in emissions of methane

### Nuclear 
Nuclear energy can, in theory, be generated via two processes: [fission or fusion](https://www.eia.gov/energyexplained/nuclear/). All commercial nuclear power is produced through fission, which involves the splitting of atoms to release energy. Fusion is the process of combining or fusing atoms—it's how the energy in the sun is generated. No fusion reactor exists, though it remains the holy grail of nuclear research. Recently, some [research breakthroughs](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2022/feb/09/nuclear-fusion-heat-record-a-huge-step-in-quest-for-new-energy-source) have excited the community, but we are still far from building a commercial fusion plant that produces power cheaply.

# SUMMARY
- The central challenge facing the global energy system in the context of climate change is meeting development needs by enhancing access to modern energy services while rapidly decarbonizing the whole energy system to solve climate change. Energy transitions are taking place around the world, and these stories look quite different in different places.    
     
- Cleaning up electricity is the most important lever for climate action in the energy sector. There is no solution to climate change without this. We need to get to 90-100% clean electricity systems by 2050, and ideally sooner.   
     
- Cleaning up the grid is especially important as "electrify everything" emerges as the broader climate solution for much of energy needs in transport, buildings and industry. As we deploy technologies like electric vehicles, it's imperative they run on clean electricity. Electrifying everything also helps to alleviate some of the challenges of building a 100% clean electricity system—we cover this topic in the next class.   
     
- Clean electricity solutions already exist and are highly cost-effective now. On the generation side, these solutions include solar, wind, geothermal, small hydro and small biomass energy systems. More controversial solutions also include large-scale biomass, nuclear, and large hydropower technologies.  
     
- There are _technical challenges_ to running a grid system on variable sources of electricity, like solar and wind power. Solutions combine storage, hydrogen, demand response, new transmission and a smart grid.   
     
- There are also _market, regulatory and policy challenges_ to achieving 100% clean electricity systems. New utility planning processes, and new market and incentive structures, like performance-based rate-making and real-time pricing are in development. These can be highly specific to local contexts and the legacy of existing systems.   
     
- There are very important _political and justice issues_ involved in building a clean grid. Struggles around inequitable access to energy, subsidies,  jobs and minerals supply chains illustrate some of these challenges.   
     
- Finally, technical, market and regulatory, and political and justice issues are _deeply interrelated and shape each other._ The best solutions are those that take an integrated, systems view of the problem. Even if you're working for a company designing, say, smart meters, you're likely to design a much better product if you understand the _whole_ context into which your product will be deployed.

### The Global Power sector has nearly reached Peak GHG Emissions
==Source: [https://www.newscientist.com/article/2395956-global-power-sector-has-almost-hit-peak-greenhouse-gas-emissions/?utm_campaign=RSS%7CNSNS&utm_source=NSNS&utm_medium=RSS&utm_content=currents](https://www.newscientist.com/article/2395956-global-power-sector-has-almost-hit-peak-greenhouse-gas-emissions/?utm_campaign=RSS%7CNSNS&utm_source=NSNS&utm_medium=RSS&utm_content=currents)==

> Global greenhouse gas emissions from generating electricity would already be on the decline this year, were it not for drought – itself potentially caused by climate change – leading to a shift away from hydropower to fossil fuel generation in China, the US and India.

See more about 2022 Power Emissions on [[CO2Emissionsin2022.pdf]]

Generating electricity by burning fossil fuels is the still the largest single source of carbon emissions, responsible for [around 40 percent](https://www.nature.com/articles/s43017-023-00406-z) of the global total. The [rapidly rising share of renewable energy](https://www.newscientist.com/article/2387712-the-renewable-energy-revolution-is-happening-faster-than-you-think/) in the mix has led to [forecasts](https://ember-climate.org/insights/research/global-electricity-review-2023/) that power sector emissions would peak as soon as this year, an important milestone towards decarbonisation. But the peak hasn’t yet been reached.

According to research [report](http://ember-climate.org/insights/research/global-electricity-mid-year-insights-2023) from Ember, a substantial jump in wind and solar generation, as well as lower demand for energy, led to a decrease in emissions in several countries. This included a 
- 17 per cent decline in the European Union, 
- a 12 per cent decline in Japan and an 8.6 per cent decline in the US. 
- Emissions in India rose by 3.7 per cent,

However, the reductions were offsetted by a 7.9% increase in China, due to record-low hydropowers due to drought. 
Drought in US, INdia and everal other coutnries alos saw a decline of 8.5% in hydropower. 

> Despite adding more power capacity, Hydropower production is not going up
> 

Also bear in mind that we saw a decrease in demand in many countries last winter due to midl temperatures. 
But bottom line is, if it weren't for the droughts, emissions from the Power sector would have **fallen nearly 3%**
