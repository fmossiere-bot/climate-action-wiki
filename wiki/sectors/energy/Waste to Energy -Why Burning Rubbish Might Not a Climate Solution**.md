Waste to energy plants burn municipal, agricultural, and industrial waste at high temperatures to generate electricity and heat. The idea has a certain logic: deal with two problems at once, reduce landfill and produce power. In practice, the reality is more complicated.

> Project Drawdown, one of the most rigorous climate solution databases available, rates waste to energy as "[not recommended](https://drawdown.org/explorer/deploy-waste-to-energy) " as a climate solution. The core problem is that incineration produces greenhouse gas emissions during combustion, much like fossil fuels, and is significantly less energy efficient than most alternatives. In regions where large amounts of plastic waste are burned, emissions can actually be higher than simply sending the waste to landfill.

There is also a structural tension built into the model. Waste to energy plants need a steady, reliable supply of waste to operate efficiently. This creates a perverse incentive: **rather than reducing waste at source**, the system depends on maintaining it. Some EU countries have taken this to its logical extreme, importing waste from other countries to keep their incinerators running. That directly undermines the circular economy principles the EU is trying to embed.

> The air pollution dimension is also significant. Even with advanced filtration systems, incineration releases pollutants including particulate matter, heavy metals, and toxic compounds. In many countries, these plants tend to be located in lower-income or more vulnerable communities, raising serious questions about environmental justice.

It is worth distinguishing waste to energy incineration from other thermal technologies that are sometimes grouped with it. Pyrolysis and gasification, which use heat to convert organic materials without direct combustion, are assessed separately by Project Drawdown and perform considerably better in terms of emissions and energy efficiency. They are not the same thing, and the distinction matters when evaluating policy or investment decisions.

Where incineration may still have a limited role is in handling post-recycling residues, hazardous waste, or medical waste, particularly in dense urban areas where land for landfill is genuinely scarce. But even then, it requires strict regulation, continuous monitoring, and should be treated as a last resort rather than a mainstream solution.

The clearest takeaway is this: **waste to energy is a waste management tool, not a climate solution**. Recycling, composting, source reduction, and emerging technologies like pyrolysis consistently deliver better emissions outcomes.
