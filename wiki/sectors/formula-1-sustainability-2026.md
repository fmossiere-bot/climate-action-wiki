# Formula 1: On Track for Net Zero by 2030

## What is it

Formula 1 has committed to becoming net zero carbon by 2030. Its annual sustainability review for the 2025 season shows the sport is making measurable progress. **Total CO₂ emissions have fallen 35% from the 2018 baseline**, dropping from 228,793 tonnes to 148,805 tonnes, with a 12% reduction in a single year between 2024 and 2025. The figures cover F1's own operations as well as the factories, operations and travel of all ten teams.

## Why it matters

Global sport has a significant carbon footprint, and Formula 1, with its global circus of races across five continents, is one of the most logistically complex. What F1 is demonstrating is that even a sport with this level of travel and infrastructure can cut its emissions substantially through operational redesign, without giving up the spectacle. That is potentially useful evidence for other sports and large travelling businesses.

## Biggest emissions levers

The sport's emissions reductions have come primarily from three areas: logistics, facilities, and fuel.

**Logistics** are the biggest variable. Shifting freight from air to sea, and clustering the race calendar geographically so that equipment and people move more efficiently between nearby venues, have cut logistics emissions 21% year on year and 29% since 2018. For example, grouping the Japanese, Australian and Chinese rounds in spring (instead of splitting them across the calendar) reduces unnecessary long-haul freight movements. One calendar reorganisation cut logistics emissions on a sequence of races by almost 3%.

**Factories and facilities** have seen the sharpest drop: a 64% reduction vs 2018 and 14% year on year, driven by renewable energy procurement and building efficiency improvements across team headquarters.

**Sustainable fuels** entered the race calendar itself from 2026. F1 now mandates advanced sustainable fuels in competition, which produce 80% fewer greenhouse gas emissions than conventional fossil-fuel comparators.

## What "net zero by 2030" actually means

F1's commitment requires a minimum 50% absolute reduction in emissions compared to the 2018 baseline. Any remaining unavoidable emissions must be offset using "credible programmes aligned with best practice guidance." Emissions data is calculated through a third-party reporting platform to ensure accuracy and transparency.

The sport is on track: a 35% reduction in seven years, with four years remaining, means the remaining 15-percentage-point gap is achievable if the current trajectory continues.

## Key facts

- Total CO₂ emissions: 228,793 tonnes (2018) down to 148,805 tonnes (2025) — a 35% reduction ([BBC Sport, 2026](https://www.bbc.com/sport/formula1/articles/cvgjp4rj2p5o))
- 12% reduction in a single year (2024 to 2025)
- Factory and facilities emissions down 64% since 2018
- Logistics emissions down 21% year on year, 29% since 2018
- From 2026: sustainable fuels mandatory in races, cutting per-race fuel GHG emissions by 80%
- Net zero target = minimum 50% absolute reduction vs 2018; remaining unavoidable emissions offset
