### Countries actions - EU example
Source: https://climateactiontracker.org/sectors/aviation/country-action/ 

##### Target do not explicitly cover emissions from International aviation

The EU and its 27 member states include international aviation in their NDC for 2030 (Germany and the European Commission, 2020). The [Union’s climate neutrality target](https://climateactiontracker.org/countries/eu/net-zero-targets/), however, does not explicitly cover emissions from international aviation.

Despite efforts from the European Commission and the European Parliament to reform the EU ETS and introduce a tax on kerosene and quota for SAF, the EU still has very limited measures in place to reduce emissions from international aviation: 

- The EU includes international aviation in its 2030 NDC, but its climate neutrality target doesn't explicitly cover these emissions.
- Despite efforts to reform the EU ETS and introduce a kerosene tax and SAF quotas, EU measures to reduce international aviation emissions remain limited.
- The EU ETS currently only covers flights within the European Economic Area (EEA) and some flights to/from Switzerland and the UK.
- A 2013 "stop the clock" decision limited the EU ETS to intra-EEA flights to support a global approach via ICAO.
- Recent EU ETS revisions expanded coverage to flights between the EEA and outermost EU regions (with exceptions).
- The inclusion of all international flights departing the EEA in the EU ETS is suspended until at least 2026, pending an assessment of CORSIA.
- Expanding the EU ETS to all departing international flights is considered a missed opportunity for greater emission reductions compared to CORSIA.
- The EU ETS is gradually phasing out free allowances for airlines but will allocate free SAF allowances until 2030 to incentivize SAF use.
- The revised EU ETS Directive mandates reporting of non-CO2 aviation impacts starting in 2025, initially for flights within the EEA and to the UK/Switzerland.
- The European Commission may propose expanding the EU ETS to include non-CO2 forcers based on a review of the MRV results by the end of 2027.
- A proposed kerosene tax for intra-EU flights faces potential exemptions by member states.
- The ReFuelEU Directive mandates minimum SAF shares at EU airports, starting at 2% in 2025 and reaching 70% in 2050, including specific targets for synthetic fuels.
- While the SAF quota is important, it may fall short of what's needed for a 1.5ºC trajectory, with concerns about the sustainability of some allowed biofuels.

### Recommendations for Government actions
Governments should pursue a range of measures to decarbonise the aviation sector, including:

> - Scaling up the use of SAF to 10-15% in 2030 and 100% in 2050;
> - Investing in research and development of other decarbonisation options, such as electric batteries for small aircrafts;
> - Implementing operational measures, such as optimising routing, air traffic flow management, and minimising flight distances;
> - Improving energy efficiency of aircrafts; and
> - Reducing demand for international aviation.

While governments, ICAO, and industry focus on the first four measures, albeit to varying degrees, reducing demand receives very little attention. However, it is unlikely that the aviation sector can move towards a 1.5º-compatible trajectory without reducing demand.

### What about Carbon Credits, what is CORSIA? 
Source: https://carbonherald.com/opinion-aviations-biggest-climate-opportunity-is-in-clean-cooking/

> [!NOTE] What is CORSIA
> **CORSIA** stands for **Carbon Offsetting and Reduction Scheme for International Aviation**.
> It’s a **global climate scheme for the aviation sector**, created by the **International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO)** (a UN agency), to address the growth in **CO₂ emissions from international flights**.

> CORSIA aims to ensure that **any growth in international aviation emissions above a baseline is offset**, mainly by:
- Improving efficiency
- Using sustainable aviation fuels (SAF)
- **Buying carbon offsets** for remaining emissions

#### **Why CORSIA exists**

International aviation emissions are:
- Not covered by national climate targets under the Paris Agreement
- Growing rapidly compared to other sectors

CORSIA was designed as a **global, harmonised approach**, so airlines aren’t regulated differently country by country.

#### **How CORSIA works**

1. **Baseline emissions**
- Originally based on 2019–2020 emissions
- Adjusted due to COVID to use **2019 only**
2. **Monitoring & reporting**
- Airlines must **monitor, report, and verify (MRV)** their CO₂ emissions from international flights
3. **Offsetting**
- If an airline’s emissions exceed the baseline, it must:
- Purchase **eligible carbon credits**
- Cancel them to compensate for excess emissions

#### **Phases of CORSIA**

- **Pilot phase (2021–2023)** – voluntary
- **First phase (2024–2026)** – voluntary
- **Second phase (2027–2035)** – mandatory for most countries

> Some countries are exempt (e.g. least developed countries, small island states).

#### **What emissions are covered**

- **Only international flights** (not domestic aviation)
- Only **CO₂** (for now)
- Focuses on **emissions growth**, not total emissions

#### **What counts as an eligible offset**

Offsets must meet strict ICAO criteria:
- Additionality
- Permanence
- No double counting
- Robust verification

ICAO approves **specific programmes and vintages** (not all carbon credits qualify).

#### **Common criticisms**

- Relies heavily on **offsets**, not direct reductions
- Does not address **non-CO₂ impacts** (contrails, NOx)
- Baseline approach allows absolute emissions to remain high
- Quality concerns about some offset credits

#### **Why this matters (especially in Europe & Ireland)**

- CORSIA operates **alongside** the EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS)
- For many flights, airlines must comply with **both**, though rules are being aligned to avoid double regulation
- Relevant for:
- Airlines
- Corporate travel emissions
- Sustainability and climate reporting

#### Example of Offsets - Gold Standard & Clean Cooking
As CORSIA develops from policy into real-world delivery, Gold Standard’s position as an operational leader for CORSIA provides a unique insights into the process. How are regulatory clarity, market-first developments, and on-the-ground experience are unlocking CORSIA-aligned supply? 

> By investing in clean cooking carbon credits through CORSIA, airlines could solve one of the world’s most intractable problems. clean cooking is an under-recognised opportunity to deliver real climate impact while improving lives.

Although clean cooking is sometimes viewed as complex or hard-to-measure area,  high-quality programmes designed with robust, conservative and evidence-based  methodologies can provide verified emission reductions and wide-ranging social  benefits. With ICAO confirming that Gold Standard is approved to supply eligible  units for CORSIA’s Second Phase from 2027-2029, there is a real opportunity to  direct climate finance to a sector that can deliver real-world impact for people and  nature, provided integrity stays at the centre.

### The Role of Corporate Travel
Source: WDHT Article - Decarbonizing Corporate Air Travel: The Business Imperative 

> This article examines the urgent need for **decarbonization within corporate air travel**, highlighting that although aviation accounts for a relatively small percentage of global emissions, business flying drives a disproportionately large share of this environmental impact. While **business travel spending is projected to rebound** significantly, the text emphasizes that most corporations are **failing to measure, report, or set credible targets** for reducing their flight-related emissions, which include crucial non-CO₂ effects like contrails. 


Corporate action is highly effective, as a small group of frequent flyers and large companies hold the power to make **significant and rapid cuts** necessary to align with global climate goals. 

> [!NOTE] Some FACTS about Corporate travel
> - Business travel accounts for about **15–20%** of air travel emissions globally and **25–30%** at the European level.
> - Business travelers make up only **12%** of passengers in Europe, yet account for around **30%** of all aviation demand.
> - A 2023 study found that **85%** of global corporations have no credible plans to reduce emissions from corporate flying.
> - In the updated 2025 travel smart ranking, **44%** of the 326 companies still lack specific targets for business travel.
> - Companies that establish specific targets for air travel have achieved an average emissions reduction of **48%**, compared to just **28%** for those without targets.
