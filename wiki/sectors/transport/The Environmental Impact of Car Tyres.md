#### Key Numbers

- **1.2 billion** tyres are produced globally every year, more than 38 per second
- **92.6%** of a tyre's environmental impact happens during its use phase, not during manufacturing
- Manufacturing and raw material production account for **10.8%** of the lifecycle impact
- **8 million tonnes** of tyre waste (rubber, carbon, silica, steel, textile, chemicals) go uncollected each year
- More than **17 million tonnes** of end-of-life tyres are collected annually worldwide
- Tyres are responsible for **10 to 15%** of a car's total carbon footprint, and for around **20%** of fuel consumption

---

#### What a Tyre Is Made Of

A tyre is a complex product. It combines more than 200 different materials, and changing one can affect safety, performance, or durability. According to Michelin, the average car tyre is composed of:

- 25% natural rubber (from hevea trees)
- 25% synthetic rubber (polymer chains, carbon-based molecules)
- 20% reinforcing materials such as carbon black and silica
- 15% textile or metallic reinforcements
- 15% chemical additives and processing oils

This complexity makes sustainable substitution very difficult. Each material serves a specific function, and reliability cannot be compromised.

---

#### The Environmental Challenge

The tyre industry has a significant environmental footprint across its full lifecycle, from raw material extraction through to end-of-life disposal.

**During production**, the process is energy-intensive and relies heavily on fossil fuels. Natural rubber cultivation can contribute to deforestation, and manufacturing releases volatile organic compounds and other pollutants.

**During use**, the impact is much larger than most people realise. Tyre wear generates microplastic particles that spread into soils and waterways. Road runoff carries tyre-related chemicals into aquatic environments. And because tyres directly influence fuel efficiency, heavier or less efficient tyres increase a vehicle's emissions over its entire lifetime.

**At end of life**, tyres are extremely difficult to process. They are not biodegradable, and they cannot simply be melted down and reused. Landfill disposal is increasingly restricted. In France, tyre burning is prohibited, but it still occurs in other parts of the world, releasing toxic substances into the air.

---

#### What Is Happening on Recycling

The recycling picture remains limited but is slowly improving. In France, around 530,000 tonnes of tyres are collected each year, at a collection rate of 93.5%. Of those:

- 38% go to material recovery (steel, rubber crumb, moulded objects)
- 48.6% go to energy recovery (cement kilns, urban heating)
- 13.4% go to reuse or retreading

Globally, 8 million tonnes still go uncollected each year. New techniques such as pyrolysis and vapothermolysis offer a potential route to a "zero waste" future for tyres, but these are not yet at industrial scale.

#### Michelin and the Road to Sustainability

With 180 million tyres sold every year, Michelin is the global market leader and has set itself two public targets: **40% sustainable materials by 2030**, and **100% by 2050**.

Today, the company reports that **31% of the materials it uses are renewable or recycled**. Natural rubber accounts for the largest share (around 25%), while recycled materials remain at only 2 to 3%. Reaching the 2030 target will require significant progress in sourcing and material science.

The challenge is not only technical. There is currently no agreed European definition of what counts as a "renewable" tyre material, which makes it hard to compare claims across manufacturers or to set meaningful regulation.

There is also a structural problem with the use phase. As electric vehicles become heavier and car sizes increase, tyres must carry more load while still achieving a minimum range of around 50,000 kilometres. Meeting those performance and safety requirements while also switching to bio-based or recycled materials is a genuine engineering constraint, not just a commercial one.

Michelin is exploring future technologies including airless tyre designs, 3D-printed seasonal tread components, and tyres that could last significantly longer than current models. These remain in development.

**A European regulation on tyre sustainability is expected in 2026**, though the specific obligations for manufacturers and consumers are not yet defined.

---

#### What This Means for Drivers

Most people choose tyres based on price, brand, and performance ratings. Very few factor in environmental impact. This is partly because there is little visible information at the point of sale, and partly because tyre labelling currently focuses on fuel efficiency and wet grip rather than lifecycle impact.

The coming EU norm may begin to change this, but the pace of change in this sector is slow, and the technical barriers are real. In the meantime, the most practical action for drivers is to maintain correct tyre pressure (which directly affects both fuel consumption and tyre wear) and to extend tyre life through regular rotation and alignment checks.
