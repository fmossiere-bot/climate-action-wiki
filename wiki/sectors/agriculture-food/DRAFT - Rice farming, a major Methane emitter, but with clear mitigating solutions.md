> [!NOTE] Rice Farming has a major impact
> **Rice farming** is another major methane source, producing 18-20% of agricultural methane emissions. Flooded rice fields deplete oxygen and fuel methane production. The gas then escapes mainly through rice plants or as bubbles that rise from the soil — though emissions vary depending on irrigation methods, soil types and fertilizer use.

### GHG Footprint impact
###### A kilo of rice can cause more emissions than burning a litre of diesel

> The 2 main source of GHG from Rice farming are: 
> - **Methane (CH₄):** Flooded rice paddies create anaerobic conditions that foster methane-producing microbes. Rice cultivation contributes approximately 10% of global agricultural methane emissions.
> - **Nitrous Oxide (N₂O):** Intermittent flooding, a method used to reduce methane emissions, can inadvertently increase nitrous oxide emissions, a greenhouse gas with a global warming potential nearly 300 times that of CO₂ .​

Rice deserves a place in our consciousness not only as a food on your table but as an important piece of the puzzle. 
- Europeans and Americans get just **1-2% of their food energy from this crop**, but this is much higher in Asia
- **89% of the world's rice is harvested is consumed in Asia**. 
- Around 600 Millions Co2e of methane is thought to be emitted from rice paddles, accounting for 1.2% of the global carbon footprint
- Even more significant at the 1**67 millions tones of fertiliser used on those fields, mainly nitrogen based**. 
- The majority is eaten in its region which reduce the transport impact, which would be through cargos anyway. 


### Other Impact

> Rice is 20% of the world's food energy for just 3.5% of its Carbon Footprint. 

**Water Consumption**

Rice farming is water-intensive, accounting for nearly 30% of the world's freshwater usage. This high demand exacerbates water scarcity issues, especially in regions already facing water stress.

**Soil and Air Quality**

- **Soil Degradation:** Continuous flooding can lead to soil nutrient depletion and reduced fertility over time.​
- **Air Pollution:** Post-harvest burning of rice straw releases pollutants, contributing to air quality deterioration and health issues.

### There are ways to reduce Methane Production in Rice farming
See [[DRAFT - 6 Ways to Reduce Methane from Agriculture#Curbing Methane from Rice]] 

One of the biggest problem of rice production is the excess water, and the excess use of fertiliser. 

> It is possible to grow rice without flooding the field and therefore cutting the methane emissions. However, it's harder work, and may require more fertiliser. 

To address these environmental concerns, several sustainable practices are being adopted:

- **Alternate Wetting and Drying (AWD):** This irrigation technique reduces methane emissions by up to 90% and conserves water without compromising yields.
- **Integrated Rice-Fish Farming:** Introducing fish into rice paddies can naturally control pests and reduce methane emissions by altering microbial communities. 
- **Improved Fertiliser Management:** Precision application of fertilisers minimises nitrous oxide emissions and prevents nutrient runoff.​
- **Straw Management:** Instead of burning, rice straw can be repurposed for livestock feed or mushroom cultivation, reducing air pollution and adding economic value.
