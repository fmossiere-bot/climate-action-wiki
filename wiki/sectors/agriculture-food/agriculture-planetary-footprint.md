# Agriculture's Planetary Footprint

## What it is

Agriculture is the single largest human transformation of the planet — **bigger in scale and environmental impact than fossil fuels**. Beginning roughly 12,000 years ago, farming expanded slowly across the Earth. In the 20th century it accelerated sharply: first through geographic expansion (clearing forests and grasslands), then through the 1960s Green Revolution, which added industrial fertilisers, irrigation, and pesticides to squeeze more output from each hectare.

Today, croplands and pastures together cover **37% of Earth's ice-free land surface** — more than Asia and Europe combined, and far more than all cities and settlements (1–3%). Most of this land is not growing food for people: **75% of all agricultural land is used to raise animals or grow animal feed**, including most of the world's corn and soy production.

## Why it matters

The scale of agriculture means it is the primary driver of almost every major environmental crisis on the planet — biodiversity loss, freshwater depletion, nitrogen pollution, and climate change. Understanding this is essential because most climate conversations focus on energy and transport while agriculture's outsized role goes underappreciated. Any serious path to a sustainable planet runs through how we farm and what we eat.

## Land Use and Biodiversity Loss

Farming has consumed **more land and driven more species to extinction** than any other activity in human history — more than timber, mining, urban sprawl, or climate change. Entire biomes have been effectively erased: the prairies of North America and the Atlantic rainforest of Brazil are now almost entirely farmland. The cerrado of South America, forests in West Africa, and peatlands in Indonesia continue to be cleared at alarming rates, primarily for pasture and soy for animal feed.

## Freshwater Depletion

Agriculture accounts for **70% of global freshwater use** — roughly 2,800 km³ per year, enough to fill the Grand Canyon two-thirds full. This level of extraction is unsustainable in many regions. The **Colorado River** now rarely reaches the ocean; the **Aral Sea** — once one of the world's largest inland bodies of water — has almost entirely disappeared after rivers feeding it were diverted to grow cotton. Water tables are falling across major breadbasket regions from India to the US High Plains.

## Nitrogen and Chemical Pollution

The industrial use of synthetic fertilisers has **more than doubled** the natural background flows of nitrogen and phosphorus across the Earth's surface. Excess nitrogen and phosphorus wash off fields into waterways, triggering explosive algae growth that strips oxygen from the water and kills marine life. The **"Dead Zone" in the Gulf of Mexico** — fed by fertiliser runoff travelling down the Mississippi River — is among the largest in the world. Similar dead zones exist near almost every coastline downstream of intensive agriculture.

## Agriculture as a Climate Driver

Direct agricultural emissions (crops, livestock, and associated land use) account for **~22% of global greenhouse gas emissions**. When the full food system is included — processing, transport, packaging, refrigeration, and food waste — the figure rises to **approximately one-third of all human emissions**.

The biggest single driver is **deforestation**, primarily to create pasture and grow soy for animal feed in the Amazon, Central Africa, and Indonesia. This alone accounts for ~10–11% of global emissions — comparable to the entire US economy. Cattle also produce large quantities of **methane**, a greenhouse gas far more potent than CO₂ over the short term. Overuse of fertilisers releases **nitrous oxide**, another powerful greenhouse gas.

## Summary findings

- Cropland + pasture = **37% of Earth's ice-free land** — the planet's dominant land cover by far.
- **75% of agricultural land** exists to support livestock, directly or via feed crops.
- Agriculture uses **70% of global freshwater** — the single biggest human use of fresh water.
- Fertiliser runoff has **more than doubled** natural nitrogen flows, creating dead zones in coastal waters worldwide.
- Direct agriculture and land use = **~22% of global GHG emissions**; the full food system = **~33%**.
- Deforestation for farming = **~10–11% of global emissions** — equivalent to the entire US economy.
- Saving tropical forests is among the **highest-leverage climate solutions** available.

## ✅ Update: Global Meat Supply Trends (June 2026)

A new FAO report (The Guardian, June 2026) confirms the upward trajectory. The average person now eats **six times more chicken** than in 1961 (3 kg → 17 kg) and twice as much pork (15 kg). Overall meat supply rose from 25 kg per person in 1961 to 47 kg in 2022. Agricultural emissions are forecast to rise 7.6% over the next decade, with livestock responsible for 80% of that increase. The report was criticised by scientists for failing to recommend reducing consumption in wealthy nations. Source: [fao-global-meat-supply-2026](../../sources/fao-global-meat-supply-2026.md)
