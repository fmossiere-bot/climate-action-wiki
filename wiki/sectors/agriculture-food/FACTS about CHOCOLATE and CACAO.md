### 3. Ethically Challenging
It is possible to pay farmers a premium while selling single-origin chocolate at a cheaper price – but it means companies have to transform the way it’s made

> Modern reports of rampant human rights and environmental abuses within the chocolate industry emerged more than 20 years ago. Millions of farming families in the west African countries of Ivory Coast and Ghana – where the majority of the world’s commodity cocoa crop is grown – subsisted on less than $1 a day. Children were performing forced labor, wielding machetes and spraying toxic agrichemicals instead of attending school. And vast swaths of tropical forest were being clearcut to make room for more cocoa.

In 2000, chocolate processors and manufacturers formed the World Cocoa Foundation, tasked with leading the charge toward a fair, sustainable cocoa sector. Many companies vowed to enact their own internal reforms, too, funding a wide array of initiatives aimed at enhancing supply chain traceability, empowering women, preventing deforestation, and stamping out the worst forms of child labor.


Browsing the pages Nestlé devotes to its [Cocoa Plan](https://www.nestlecocoaplan.com/), Mars to [Cocoa for Generations](https://www.mars.com/sustainability-plan/cocoa-for-generations), Mondelēz to [Cocoa Life](https://www.cocoalife.org/), or Hershey’s to [Cocoa For Good](https://www.thehersheycompany.com/en_us/home/sustainability/sustainability-focus-areas/cocoa.html), it seems as if progress is well under way. In reality, though, industry watchdogs agree that little headway has been made on the path to a fair and sustainable cocoa sector.

###### In mainstream chocolate, you have a whole system set up that doesn’t want to change

> According to the [recent Cocoa Barometer report](https://voicenetwork.cc/cocoa-barometer/) issued by the Voice Network, a leading consortium of NGOs and trade unions working on sustainability in cocoa, deforestation continues at an alarming rate in Ghana and Ivory Coast. Child labor is [still widespread on cocoa farms](https://www.theguardian.com/global-development/2020/oct/20/chocolate-industry-slammed-for-failure-to-crack-down-on-child-labour), perhaps even more so than when it was first uncovered two decades ago. The driving factor behind both is that the vast majority of west African farmers earn well below a living income.


> [!NOTE] Farmers don't really get their fair share
> **The price of cocoa today on the New York Mercantile Exchange is $2,400 a metric ton; it fluctuates a great deal, but the average price of cocoa has been $2,400 a ton for five decades. While chocolate bars have gotten more expensive, cocoa farmers have continued to be paid the same. In the 1970s, [according to Fairtrade](https://www.ucl.ac.uk/news/2021/jun/opinion-why-efforts-cote-divoire-and-ghana-help-cocoa-farmers-havent-worked), the price of cocoa accounted for up to 50% of the value of a chocolate bar, but fell to 16% in the 1980s. Today, farmers receive about 6% of the value of every chocolate bar sold.**

## At the same time, Chocolate can be good for you 

Chocolate does present some benefit, both physiologically and Emotionally. But that doesn't mean you need to abuse it: 
### **Physiological Benefits of Chocolate / Cacao**

#### 1. **Rich in Antioxidants**
- **Cacao** is loaded with flavonoids, especially epicatechin, which help neutralize free radicals and reduce oxidative stress.
- These antioxidants support **cellular health**, slow down aging, and may help prevent chronic diseases.
#### 2. **Cardiovascular Health**
- Flavonoids in cacao **improve blood flow**, **lower blood pressure**, and **reduce LDL (bad) cholesterol** while raising HDL (good) cholesterol.
- Regular intake of high-quality dark chocolate (70% cacao or more) is linked with **reduced risk of heart disease**.
#### 3. **Brain Function & Cognitive Support**
- Cacao boosts **cerebral blood flow**, potentially enhancing focus, memory, and cognitive performance.
- Contains **theobromine** and **small amounts of caffeine**, mild stimulants that can increase mental alertness without the jitteriness of coffee.
#### 4. *Nutrient-Dense Superfood**
- A good source of **magnesium**, **iron**, **zinc**, **copper**, and **potassium**.
- Magnesium is crucial for **muscle relaxation**, **nervous system balance**, and **energy production**.
#### 5. **Anti-Inflammatory Effects**
- Caao’s polyphenols may help reduce chronic inflammation, supporting **immune health** and **joint function**.

### 💚 **Emotional & Psychological Benefits**
#### 1. **Mood Elevation**
- Cacao stimulates the brain to release **endorphins**, **dopamine**, and **serotonin**, neurotransmitters associated with **pleasure**, **motivation**, and **well-being**.
- Contains **phenylethylamine (PEA)**—sometimes called the "love chemical"—which can create feelings of excitement and joy.
#### 2. **Stress Relief & Calm**
- The magnesium in cacao helps relax the nervous system and muscles, offering **natural stress and anxiety relief**.
- Dark chocolate has been shown to **lower cortisol levels** (the stress hormone) in some studies.
#### 3. **Heart-Opening & Connection (Ceremonial Use)**
- In its raw, ceremonial form, cacao has been used traditionally in spiritual and community settings for **heart-opening**, **emotional clarity**, and **deep connection**.
- Many people report feeling **grounded**, **present**, and more emotionally connected after cacao ceremonies.
#### 4. **Sensory & Comfort Pleasure**
- Chocolate engages multiple senses—smell, taste, texture—which can create a deeply **soothing and satisfying experience**.
- Can serve as a comforting ritual, supporting emotional resilience (though overreliance may be a red flag).
### ✅ Best Ways to Enjoy Chocolate for Benefits
- Choose **dark chocolate (70%+)** or **raw ceremonial-grade cacao**.
- Avoid heavily processed chocolate with added sugars, dairy, or artificial additives.
- Use cacao mindfully—either as a **nutritious treat** or a **ritual for emotional and spiritual nourishment**.

> [!NOTE] Prices are high
> The price of cacao, the essential ingredient in chocolate, has been climbing steadily since August 2022, reaching unprecedented highs in late 2024 and sending shockwaves through the global confectionery market1. This surge has significant implications for chocolate makers, consumers, and the economies of cacao-producing countries. Factors driving this price surge include supply and demand, weather patterns, production trends in major cacao-producing countries, market speculation, and the potential of alternative uses for cacao.
> [https://www.voanews.com/a/cocoa-prices-triple-in-one-year-as-climate-change-hits-crops/7538765.html](https://www.voanews.com/a/cocoa-prices-triple-in-one-year-as-climate-change-hits-crops/7538765.html)

### **Historical and Current Cacao Prices**

Cacao prices have historically fluctuated between $2,000 to $4,000 per metric ton. However, recent years have witnessed a dramatic departure from this norm. In 2023, the average annual price for cacao was $3.28 per kilogram. By December 2024, prices had soared to almost $12,000 per metric ton, an 88% increase in just three months and nearly triple the price a year ago2. This price surge is reflected in various market data sources:

While there are several factors contributing to the increase in cacao prices, the most significant one appears to be the impact of **climate change on cacao production in West Africa**. This region accounts for a large portion of the world's cacao supply, and recent changes in weather patterns have severely affected harvests . Excessive rainfall in Ghana and Côte d'Ivoire during late 2023 led to outbreaks of diseases like swollen shoot virus and black pod disease, which damage cacao pods and reduce yields . This, combined with the effects of El Niño, has resulted in lower-than-average harvests for the past few years .  


> [!NOTE] In addition to climate change, other factors contributing to the price increase include:
> - **Increased demand:** Global demand for cocoa is steadily increasing, putting further pressure on supply .  
> - **Limited geographical production:** Cacao can only be grown in specific regions near the equator, making it vulnerable to disruptions in those areas .  
> - **Market speculation:** As news of crop failures spread, speculators drove prices up further by betting on continued increases .  
> - **Underinvestment in farmland:** Decades of low prices have discouraged investment in cacao farms, leading to lower yields and soil quality .  
> - **EUDR limits:** Recently imposed restrictions on the sale of cocoa produced on deforested land in the EU are also contributing to the price increase .  

###### The combination of these factors has created a perfect storm in the cacao market, driving prices to unprecedented levels.

##### Evaluation of the impact

To evaluate Tony's Chocolonely's environmental impact, here are the factors we can consider based on standard environmental, social, and governance (ESG) criteria and guidance from sources like the CDSB and TCFD frameworks:

- **Biodiversity Impact**:
- Evaluate the sourcing of raw materials, especially cocoa. Biodiversity-related risks could stem from deforestation and habitat loss in cocoa-producing regions, which is a significant global concern.
- Assess whether the company monitors and mitigates biodiversity impacts through sustainable farming practices or support for agroforestry systems.

- **Climate Impact**:
- Look at greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions across their value chain (Scopes 1, 2, and 3). The TCFD encourages full disclosure of emissions and alignment with climate goals.
- Consider if the company has implemented a decarbonization strategy, such as renewable energy usage, reducing transportation emissions, or transitioning to low-carbon packaging materials.

-  **Sustainable Land Use**:
- Cocoa production is linked to land-use changes. Review how Tony’s Chocolonely ensures that its cocoa is grown sustainably, avoiding deforestation and promoting soil health.
- Deforestation: [https://online.flippingbook.com/view/371809889/55/](https://online.flippingbook.com/view/371809889/55/)


 - **Water Usage**:
- Assess the company's impact on water resources in their production and supply chain. Sustainable water management is particularly important in agriculture-heavy supply chains.

-  **Waste Management and Circular Economy**:
- Examine whether the company minimizes waste and embraces circular economy principles, such as recyclable or compostable packaging.

-  **Certifications and Standards**:
- Investigate the certifications they adhere to (e.g., Fairtrade, Rainforest Alliance) and how these align with their environmental claims.

-  **Reporting and Transparency**:
- Check if they publicly disclose their environmental performance in line with recognized frameworks like GRI (Global Reporting Initiative) or the TCFD recommendations.
