### Summary

Coffee is one of the most consumed drinks in the world, and its environmental footprint is larger than most people realise. Global coffee consumption **has been increasing steadily for almost 30 years**. With a daily average consumption of 2.7 cups of coffee per person, coffee is now Canada’s most popular drink. It is estimated that **around two billion cups of coffee are consumed daily worldwide**.The problems run from the farm to the cup: water use, greenhouse gas emissions, deforestation, and now a growing vulnerability to climate change that threatens production in key regions. The good news is that small changes in how you brew and consume coffee can make a real difference.

---

### Key Numbers

- Around **2.25 billion cups** of coffee are consumed globally every day
- **130 to 140 litres of water** are needed to produce a single cup of coffee
- **18,900 litres of water** are required to produce 1 kg of coffee beans
- Coffee production accounts for **40 to 80%** of the total greenhouse gas emissions across the full coffee supply chain
- A single cup of coffee can produce up to **50 grams of CO2**, excluding packaging
- Adding milk can double this amount, with large lattes potentially reaching **340 grams of CO2**
- Production in parts of Colombia's Sierra Nevada has shrunk by **35% in the last five years** due to climate change
- At least **100 million people** around the world depend on coffee farming for their livelihood

---

### Why Coffee Has a Large Footprint

Coffee goes through many stages before it reaches your cup: cultivation, harvesting, processing, transport, roasting, brewing, and disposal. Each step has an environmental cost, but they are not equal.

> The coffee cultivation phase accounts for the largest share of emissions, ranging from 32 to 78% of total carbon emissions depending on the production method. **Fertiliser application is the greatest single source of carbon emission at the farm level.** The use of nitrogen-based fertilisers, which require large amounts of natural gas to produce, is a significant contributor.

Water use is another major concern. The Water Footprint Network estimates the global average water footprint of a single 125ml cup of coffee at 140 litres, more than the water used in a 16-minute shower. **Most coffee is grown in regions that already face water stress, and increasing global demand is putting further pressure on local water sources.** Processing coffee also generates wastewater that, if not properly managed, can contaminate rivers and affect aquatic ecosystems.

International transportation is responsible for around 15% of the greenhouse gases associated with coffee, with exporting adding a further 6 to 11% to total CO2 emissions.

---

### Climate Change Is Now Threatening Supply

Coffee farming is becoming harder as temperatures rise. Arabica, the most popular variety, can only tolerate mean annual temperatures of up to around 24 degrees Celsius. Farmers in Colombia's Sierra Nevada report that temperatures now regularly reach 28 degrees in the afternoon, conditions that would have been unusual just a few decades ago.

Warmer temperatures are also bringing new plant diseases to regions that were previously free of them. Rust fungus, brown eye spot, and borer insects are spreading into higher altitudes as the climate shifts. Farmers are being pushed to plant on higher ground, which is both more difficult and more expensive.

Global coffee production in 2023 was around 11 million tonnes, with Brazil, Vietnam, Indonesia, Colombia, and Ethiopia as the main producers. Around 51% of the world's coffee comes from South and Central America. These are the regions most exposed to changing climate conditions.

>Some farming cooperatives are already adapting. In Colombia, the Red Ecolsierra cooperative, which brings together more than 400 families, is using agroforestry methods, planting trees alongside coffee crops to provide shade, protect soil, and support biodiversity. They are also investing in bioproducts and conservation projects, partly funded through Fairtrade premiums and carbon credits.

### How You Brew Matters

Not all coffee has the same footprint, and the way you prepare it makes a bigger difference than many people expect. The key variable is how much coffee you use.

Research consistently shows that **the amount of ground coffee used per cup is the main driver of emissions**, more so than the brewing method or the packaging. This is why capsule coffee is not as bad as its reputation suggests: a capsule typically uses 6 to 14 grams of coffee, compared to 25 grams for a standard filter coffee. The savings in coffee grounds offset the environmental cost of the capsule itself.

A comparison from Italian research found that a Moka pot, commonly used across Europe, produces significantly fewer emissions per cup than a capsule machine, largely because of the extra packaging and waste associated with capsules. Instant coffee also performs relatively well on emissions, again because of the smaller amount of coffee involved.

That said, packaging can account for nearly one-third of average coffee companies' CO2 emissions. Most coffee capsules are not made from recycled materials and are neither genuinely recyclable nor compostable. The recyclability of aluminium capsules depends entirely on whether they are actually returned and processed, which varies greatly by country and supplier.

One factor that rarely gets discussed is milk. The carbon emissions from milk account for over 75% of the total emissions of a latte. If you drink milk-based coffees regularly, switching to an oat milk alternative has a significantly larger impact than changing your brewing method.

### What You Can Do

The most effective actions are straightforward:

**Reduce consumption.** Drinking one fewer cup per day has a greater impact than any change in brewing method or packaging.

**Avoid overusing ground coffee.** If you use a filter machine or French press, use the recommended amount rather than adding extra. Wasting coffee is one of the least efficient things you can do in terms of emissions.

**Be careful with milk.** Dairy milk adds substantially to the footprint of any coffee drink. Plant-based alternatives, particularly oat milk, are considerably lower in emissions.

**Choose a reusable cup.** Disposable cups, including paper ones, end up in landfill and produce methane as they break down. A reusable cup is one of the simplest changes to make.

**Look for certified coffee.** Fairtrade, Rainforest Alliance, and Bird-Friendly certifications do not eliminate the footprint of coffee, but they indicate better farming practices and more sustainable land management.

---

### The Bigger Picture

Coffee is not the largest contributor to individual carbon footprints, but it is a product most people use daily, which makes it worth paying attention to. The farming communities that produce it are already bearing the costs of a changing climate, through lower yields, higher costs, and growing uncertainty about the future of their crops.

The relationship between coffee and climate runs in both directions. Coffee farming, when done poorly, contributes to deforestation and emissions. But when done well, using shade trees and agroforestry methods, it can support biodiversity and help sequester carbon. Supporting those better practices, through purchasing choices and certification, matters.
