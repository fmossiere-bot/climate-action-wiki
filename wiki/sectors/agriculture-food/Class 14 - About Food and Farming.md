### The centre of everything is SOIL. 

> Soil is the largest land-based reservoir of carbon, holding 3-4 times more carbon than the atmosphere

However, human activities like clearing forests and converting native ecosystem to industrial residences, agriculture land combined with poor soil management over the yeasrs have created a carbon deficit in soils... and a carbon Source to the atmosphere ... 

##### Put another way, these activities create an opportunity to re-store “lost” carbon back into the soil, drawing carbon down from the atmosphere via photosynthesis

### Principle 1 - Keep the soil covered
Soil should be kept covered , it is not exposed to air, it reduces winter and water erosion, reduce soil evaporation, moderate the soil tempreature, reduce compations. ![[Screenshot 2023-10-31 at 11.08.34.png]]

### Principle 2 - Minimize Disturbance

![[Screenshot 2023-10-31 at 11.09.27.png]]

Soil disturbances can be biological, physical, or chemical. This reduces and removes pore spaces from the soil, restricting infiltration and destroying the interlinkages in the soil, and kills soil life, ultimately causing:  
 
- Water erosion
- Wind erosion and air pollution
- Ponding/pooling water due to compaction beneath the surface 
- Crusting soil that restricts plant emergence
- Soil organic matter depletion

### Principle 3 - Maximise diversity
...above and below the ground

As this image shows, diversity above ground is mirrored below ground. Diversified cropping systems reduce soil-borne disease and increase water and nutrient use efficiency and soil-functional microorganisms.

The best way to keep the soil cover is through continual living roots in teh ground. Cover crops in between harvest ensure a continuous supply of root carbon .

### Principle 5 - Integrate animals
Animals, plants, and soils have played a synergistic role together over geological time. In recent years, animals have played a reduced role due to being placed in confinement, and fewer farms now include livestock as part of their overall operation. Note that “integrating animals” doesn’t just mean grazing cows; it can also mean integrating services from bees and other pollinators, birds, and non-domesticated animals that can help control pests and disease on annual vegetable fields or perennial farmscapes. For example, putting in owl boxes or perches for predatory birds can allow them to take care of your rodent or small bird predation concerns!

> [!The following benefits arise from various forms of grazing livestock:]
>   
> - Fall or winter grazing to convert high carbon annual crop residue to low carbon organic material balances the carbon/nitrogen ratio and prepares crop rotation residue for no-till seeding.
> - Spring or summer grazing annual and/or perennial plants with short exposure periods followed by long recovery periods spurs the plants to regrow and harvest additional sunlight and CO2.
> - Grazing livestock reduces nutrient export from our cropland and hayland fields.  In lieu of transporting feed to a feed lot, we can reverse the roles and have the livestock graze the material in place, recycling the majority of nutrients, minerals, and carbon.
> - Grazing can manage weed pressure in lieu of an herbicide.
> - Grazing cover crops and/or crop residues allow us to take the livestock off the perennial grasslands earlier in the fall, extending the grass recovery period and providing a higher livestock nutritional diet.
> - Grazing reduces livestock waste associated with confinement, helping manage our water quality and nutrient management concerns.

### Alternatives to direct payments for soil carbon storage

It’s important to keep the focus on enabling (or paying) farmers to enact better practices to manage their soils for carbon sequestration AND other important goals (crop production, biodiversity, watershed health).

> Along those lines, alternatives to offsets include proposals to compensate farmers more broadly for delivering ecosystem services (whole farm revenue support) or incentivizing diversification of crops and cover cropping without tying the compensation to specific amounts of carbon—and without claiming those sequestered tons of carbon as one’s own reductions. Different countries have different policies or NGO activities to better align incentives for farmers regenerating their agroecosystem, and we encourage you to look these up in your country and share what you find with your cohort.

Regardless of issues with measuring soil carbon, it is abundantly clear that we need to devote much more time and effort to conserving and improving soil health on agricultural lands worldwide.ncentivize other things (like crop diversity, ecological proacte in the foarm) rather than purely Carbon credits. 
Additional optional resources on soil carbon data collection efforts, measurement tools, and the rapidly evolving world of soil science:  

Beyond carbon sequestration impacts, there is great value in implementing practices such as cover cropping, composting, rotational grazing, and agroforestry.
These practices often also lead to:

- improved soil water holding capacity
- improved crop quality
- increased resilience to weather events like drought, extreme rainfall, and heatwaves
- improved pest management, and
- improved income for farmers who are able to successfully diversify crops and thus mitigate marketplace risks associated with monocrops—which adds up to a lot of climate resilience value!

In short, the benefits are not just in terms of carbon, but other social and ecological outcomes as well. This is a key—but often overlooked—feature of carbon sequestration opportunities.
