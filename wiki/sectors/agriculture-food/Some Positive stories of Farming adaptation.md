## Solar Power Doubled Mango Production — and Farmers’ Incomes — in Chamwino, Tanzania
*==Source: https://www.wri.org/insights/solar-pumps-mango-production-chamwino-tanzania?apcid=0065aea19f4a6d4198ca0d00&utm_campaign=wridigest&utm_medium=email&utm_source=wridigest-2025-03-12==*

> In this very dry region where water supply are hard to come by reliably, Solar-powered water pump have brought stability, reliability, decreased the cost and the environmental impact of using a Diesel powered water pump before. 

In 2019, World Resources Institute partnered with Tanzania's Traditional Energy Development Organization (Ta-TEDO) to identify areas where decentralized renewable energy could help support agriculture. Through this initiative, AMCOS received funding and assistance to install new solar-powered water pumps with the capacity to irrigate 80 hectares (200 acres) and a drip irrigation system capable of covering 10 hectares (25 acres).

> [!NOTE] Sustainability doesn't have to mean lowering revenue, quite the opposite
> 
> With a reliable, sustainable and affordable water supply, mango yields skyrocketed. Between 2021 and 2022, production more than doubled from an average of 40 fruits per tree to 97. Farmers who adopted drip irrigation counted over 120 mangoes per tree the same year. And average incomes from mango sales nearly doubled, increasing from around TZS 150,00 (US$56) to TZS 297,000 (US$112) per month.

###### In addition, compared to running the diesel pump, the solar pumps saved the cooperative over TZS 2.3 million (US$900) per year on fuel and maintenance. 

> Cooperatives like that are a model of success and sustainability

Chamwino's water shortages are not unique. Growing populations, competing demands and poor water management have strained Tanzania's water supplies, [pushing it past](https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/673961509974154698/pdf/120954-NWP-PUBLIC-p156957-p164469-86p-WorldBankNovFR.pdf) the "water stressed" threshold. Across Africa, around [1 in 3 people](https://www.afro.who.int/health-topics/water) face water scarcity. And shifting weather patterns due to climate change only exacerbate the problem. In the past 15 years, Tanzania has seen seven significant droughts.

In addition, support resources are still scarce. To expand the cooperative's initial success, **farmers need more training on agricultural best practices,** such as **irrigation and intercropping**, as well as how to use modern farming technologies beyond the solar pumps, like pest management systems or specialized pruning tools. But plans to develop a capacity-building network between farmers and supportive stakeholders (via a special purpose vehicle) were delayed.

## Chemical Free farming brings life back to rural France
==*Source: [https://youtube.com/watch?v=9e_5NYVmm4I&si=ObKrBW56xOl6xTUd](https://youtube.com/watch?v=9e_5NYVmm4I&si=ObKrBW56xOl6xTUd)*== 

> Faced with widespread chemical pollution and a dramatic collapse in biodiversity, farmers and scientists in western France have been testing a revolutionary farming model for over 30 years. No pesticides, no synthetic fertilisers – just nature. And it works: steady yields, stable incomes, and a thriving ecosystem. A one-of-a-kind collaboration in Europe.

Facing a drop in Biodiversity. 

This report highlights the critical decline in biodiversity, particularly among birds and insects, citing **heavy pesticide use** and **massive monocultures** as the primary causes. It showcases a significant European project where hundreds of farmers and researchers are collaborating to implement **agroecology**, a method of farming that works with nature to ditch toxic chemicals without sacrificing harvests or income. Key practices discussed include **soil conservation** and relying on **pollinators**—whose increased presence has boosted yields—along with monitoring indicator species like earthworms to track soil health. The source also touches upon the ongoing debate in France regarding the reintroduction of a controversial pesticide, acetamiprid, which underscores the conflict between maximizing **high yield and profit** versus protecting **public health and nature**, while noting that transitioning to sustainable agroecology is a complex process requiring time and financial support.
