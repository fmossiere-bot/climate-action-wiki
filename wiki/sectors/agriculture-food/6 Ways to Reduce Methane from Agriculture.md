## From Better Breeding to Cow-Burp Vaccines, Emerging Solutions Could Curb Agricultural Emission
Source: https://www.wri.org/insights/reducing-agricultural-methane-new-solutions?apcid=0065aea19f4a6d4198ca0d00&utm_campaign=wridigest&utm_medium=email&utm_source=wridigest-2025-03-12 

######  [Nearly half](https://www.ccacoalition.org/sites/default/files/resources/2021_Global-Methane_Assessment_full_0.pdf) of global methane emissions come from agricultural activities such as livestock production, rice cultivation and burning crop waste. 


#### Where do Agriculture Methane emissions come from? 
![[sources-methane-emissions.png]]

> [!NOTE] Ruminants account for 55-60% of that
> > Ruminants like cattle, sheep and goats naturally produce methane as a byproduct of digestion, known as **“enteric fermentation.”** Microorganisms in their stomachs break down food, generating methane that is mainly released through burping. Ruminant livestock account for over 55-60% of agriculture's methane emissions

> **Manure** produces 6-8% of agricultural methane emissions (though some research suggests these emissions could be significantly higher). In large dairy and pork production facilities, or “factory farms,” manure is often flushed outside using a water jet and put into tanks or lagoons, where it is stored as liquid or slurry.

> **Rice farming** is another major methane source, producing 18-20% of agricultural methane emissions. Flooded rice fields deplete oxygen and fuel methane production. The gas then escapes mainly through rice plants or as bubbles that rise from the soil — though emissions vary depending on irrigation methods, soil types and fertilizer use.

> **Burning crop “residues”**— waste leftover once crops are harvested, such as rice and wheat stalks and other biomass — is a common practice in some countries, contributing 3-4% of agricultural methane emissions while also spewing air pollution and endangering human health.


6 solutions are highlighted here 

### Making Livestock More Efficient

> In many developing countries, livestock produce far less milk and meat per animal than farms in places like the U.S. There’s a big opportunity to make animal production more efficient by improving the quality of animal diets, breeding higher-quality livestock, and ensuring better animal health.

> [!NOTE] Better Feed
> One proven way to do this is by feeding animals more digestible feed, which helps them both burp less and absorb more nutrients for growth and milk production. In the U.S., for example, [milk production grew by 53%](https://www.ers.usda.gov/amber-waves/2022/march/u-s-dairy-productivity-increased-faster-in-large-farms-and-across-southwestern-states#:~:text=The%20study%20found%20the%20primary,also%20weighed%20on%20productivity%20growth.) between 1990 and 2021, while enteric methane emissions per unit of milk decreased by about 25%

While many farms in developed countries already use highly digestible feeds, farmers in Africa and South Asia often feed their animals low-quality crop residues such as rice straw and wheat straw, which can make up to 70% of a cow's diet.

### Stopping Methane Before it Escapes: Methane Inhibitors

> One way farms can further cut emissions is by using **methane-inhibiting feed additives,** which can help reduce the amount of methane cows produce during digestion. These inhibitors are particularly suitable for commercial and large-scale farms, as cost can be a barrier for small-scale farms and methane inhibitors do not always result in productivity gains.

> [!NOTE] 30% reduction? 
> For example, a synthetic molecule called **3-nitroxypropanol (3-NOP)** has gathered interest in the last few years, undergoing extensive R&D trials and securing regulatory approval for use in beef or dairy cows in over 60 countries. Given in small doses through cow feed, it can reduce methane production [by around 30%](https://doi.org/10.3168/jds.2022-22211)**.**

Another methane-inhibiting feed additive that has secured considerable market interest in recent years is red seaweed, or _Asparagopsis._ Red seaweed is native to Australia and approved for use in beef feedlots in the country. While research shows that its methane mitigation potential can reach up to 90%, long-term trials have shown [about 30%](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0022030224014309) effectiveness in reducing methane.

### Breeding Low-Methane Cows

> Methane production from cows is linked to their genetics, with some cows inherently producing more methane than others. Selecting superior-quality breeds that are healthier and feed-efficient is a long-standing farm practice. Similarly, breeding and selecting **low-methane cows** that naturally emit less methane is possible

> [!NOTE] 22% reduction 
> On average, low-methane cows can produce [22% less methane emissions](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0022030223004022?via%3Dihub) than high-emitting ones. As opposed to methane inhibitors, methane mitigation lasts the entire lifespan of a cow and may even be passed down to offspring.

### Anti-Methane Vaccines

> Another emerging solution in its early research phase focuses on vaccines that prevent methane formation in cows’ stomachs. These **methane vaccines** can be administered just like any other vaccine to calves, and the effect lasts through a cow’s life

Some promising R&D efforts on vaccines are [currently targeting a 30% methane reduction](https://www.bezosearthfund.org/news-and-insights/pioneering-cattle-methane-vaccine-project-launched-by-bezos-earth-fund). 


### Alternatives to Manure Digesters

>  Biogas digesters use manure to produce biogas, which is a mixture of methane and carbon dioxide. Digester projects capture this methane to produce electricity or compress it to make “renewable natural gas.” While digesters are capable of reducing methane emissions, our report notes that reductions have often been overestimated by failure to account for methane leaks from the digester and emissions from the “digestate,” the wet material that emerges from the digester at the end and produces methane.


### Curbing Methane from Rice

> Many approaches can help reduce methane emissions from rice farming, beginning with adopting high-yielding rice varieties that can significantly lower methane emissions per kilogram of rice produced. Also, among thousands of rice breeds worldwide, some naturally emit less methane than others. Identifying and cultivating these low-methane varieties [could reduce emissions by 22-51%](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33387767/).

> [!NOTE] A promising variety
> One such promising variety is SUSIBA 2, which has been adopted in some parts of the world. A [2025 study](https://www.cell.com/molecular-plant/abstract/S1674-2052\(25\)00029-2?_returnURL=https%3A%2F%2Flinkinghub.elsevier.com%2Fretrieve%2Fpii%2FS1674205225000292%3Fshowall%3Dtrue) appears to have uncovered the biochemical mechanisms behind SUSIBA 2’s ability to release less methane: methane-suppressing chemicals in their root systems. Such innovation could pave the way for breeding superior rice varieties that emit up to 70% less methane.

> A simple and inexpensive way to reduce methane from rice production is [Alternative Wetting and Drying](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0168192324001904) (AWD). Rice grows in “flooded” fields; AWD involves letting the water completely dry before flooding again. Several variations of this practice exist and can reduce methane emissions by 40-45%. This approach also saves water, benefitting water-scarce regions. However, the ability to use AWD depends on the reliability of drainage and water supplies

##  The breakthrough technology reducing greenhouse gas emissions

Source: https://www.rte.ie/news/environment/2025/0311/1501355-farms-environment/ 

> GasAbate, a product developed by a Galway-based company called Glasport Bio. has developed a non-invasive new breakthrough technology capable of slashing greenhouse gas emissions from manure by **more than 80%**.


The GasAbate system was installed on Mr Goggin's farm last autumn when his animals were still out in the fields. 
Easy to install, The liquid then raises the oxygen level in the slurry pits. This prevents the anaerobic microbes that produce methane gas from working.

> [!NOTE] How does it work? 
> In a slurry tank, you've got an anaerobic environment. That means no oxygen. These are the conditions for anaerobic microbes to create greenhouse gases. That’s methane, nitrous oxide, hydrogen sulfide and ammonia. What we're doing with our additive is temporarily altering that environment by adding an oxygen-reactive species of chemical to inhibit the creation of those noxious gases

#### It is a cheap solution with better return than other avenues

## The impact of Digester for manure - a real solution to livestock emissions?
Source: [Nscientist article](https://www.newscientist.com/article/2522081-are-manure-digesters-a-real-solution-to-dairy-farm-emissions/)


#ireland #agriculture 

> in the US, Anaerobic digesters converting manure to biogas reduce methane emissions from livestock, but incentives for them have encouraged factory farms get bigger. 

**What is Manure?** 
**Manure** is animal waste: mainly dung and urine, often mixed with bedding, wash water, or stored as **slurry** (liquid manure). In farming systems, manure is used as fertiliser because it contains nutrients like nitrogen, phosphorus, and organic matter.

 **Why manure creates methane?**
Methane forms when manure decomposes **without oxygen** (anaerobic conditions), especially when stored in tanks, lagoons, pits, or slurry stores. Solid manure in drier, more aerated heaps usually produces less methane than liquid slurry systems.

> [!NOTE] **In Ireland: manure vs cows themselves**
For Ireland, the **much bigger methane source is the cow’s digestion**, not manure. The Irish EPA’s 2023 agricultural emissions breakdown shows:
**Enteric fermentation** (methane from digestion in cattle/sheep, mostly belching): **63.1%** of agricultural emissions
**Manure management** (methane + nitrous oxide from storage/spreading): **11.9%** of agricultural emissions 

  So, very roughly:
- Methane from the animals themselves is **about 5 times larger** than manure-related emissions
- Cattle dominate this because Ireland has a large dairy and beef herd.

Teagasc also notes that around **90–95% of livestock methane linked to ruminants is enteric methane**, with the rest from manure syste

> That said, manure that has gone through a digester [emits 91 per cent less methane](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40572-025-00512-8)during storage. This solution can work for large scales farms, with the right mix of livestock. 

A digester captures methane from slurry/manure that would otherwise escape from tanks or lagoons, then uses it as energy (electricity, heat, or upgraded biomethane). That can:

- **Cut manure methane emissions**
- Replace fossil gas/diesel
- Produce **digestate** that can be used as fertiliser
- Create an extra farm income stream 


**The catch in Ireland**
Ireland’s cattle slurry is **wet and relatively low-energy**, so slurry alone often gives poor economics. Teagasc notes that **co-digestion** (mixing slurry with grass silage, food waste, or residues) improves gas yield and viability.
