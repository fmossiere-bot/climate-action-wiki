### Satellite detection of Methane leaks
#Methane 

==Source: https://www.newscientist.com/article/2419765-satellite-launched-to-track-down-leaks-of-potent-greenhouse-gas/?utm_campaign=RSS%7CNSNS&utm_source=NSNS&utm_medium=RSS&utm_content=currents ==

Satellite launched to track down leaks of potent greenhouse gas

> MethaneSAT will orbit the planet 15 times a day to capture unprecedented amounts of data on methane emissions from human and natural sources

“We designed MethaneSAT explicitly to serve one goal,” says [Steven Hamburg](https://www.edf.org/people/steven-hamburg) at the Environmental Defense Fund (EDF), the non-profit advocacy group that developed the satellite along with a consortium of universities and aerospace firms. “To produce policy-relevant data to track methane emissions from the oil and gas industry, globally.”

Assessing progress towards those pledges is difficult. Current methane emissions remain poorly quantified, leaks are challenging to track and aerial surveys and on-the-ground monitoring are expensive – and some countries don’t allow them. MethaneSAT joins a growing constellation of methane-sensing instruments in orbit aiming to provide a better view. Existing satellites, like the European Space Agency’s TROPOMI, sense methane emissions across large regions. Others, like the 11 methane-sensing instruments run by Canadian company GHGSat, focus on identifying specific point sources of methane.

> In contrast, MethaneSAT will regularly monitor methane at high resolution in between these scales, enabling researchers to quantify emissions across the areas relevant to oil and gas production as well as map their probable sources. “We needed to be able to see all the emissions and resolve them in space,” says Hamburg.

#### Contrails
Contrails have a complex effect on climate, one that is being studied and without a clear answer. You can see more details about Contrails on [[Flight Carbon Footprint and Offset Guide#What about Contrails?]]
- Google is one of the only Flight carbon calculator which integrate contrails in its emissions. But as we saw, this could be tricky. 

> [!NOTE] Re-routing
> That said, AI Could help in re-routing airplanes on different routes by predicting the impact on the formation of those contrails. They need specific conditions to appear which could be avoided by changing altitude. 
> This could result in an increase of fuel consumption of around 5%, but in a reduction of contrails of around 50%. 

See more on https://sites.research.google/contrails/ 

> [!NOTE] GOOGLE Project Contrails
> Project Contrails is a Google AI initiative that uses machine learning to help airlines reduce contrail formation, thereby mitigating the climate impact of aviation.
> 
> Contrails, or condensation trails, are line-shaped clouds formed by water vapor condensing around airplane emissions. They can trap heat, contributing to global warming, especially at night when they don't reflect sunlight.
> 
> Project Contrails uses AI to predict where contrails are likely to form based on weather data, satellite data, and flight data. This allows pilots to adjust flight paths to avoid contrail formation with minimal fuel usage increase (0.3% more fuel across an airline's fleet).
> 
> Key findings from the project include:
> 
> - AI predictions reduced contrails by 54% in test flights with American Airlines.
> - The contrail avoidance cost is estimated to be in the range of $5-25/ton CO2e, making it a cost-effective climate solution.
> 
> The project is still under development, with future plans to expand the models to cover more geographical areas
> Other Source: https://sustainabilitymag.com/articles/google-ai-can-reduce-aviations-contrails-climate-impact

## Impact on energy consumption in the US due to new data centers
#US 
Let's talk about the macro. There was recently a report just last month from the Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory about the power that is currently needed to run the data centers in this country, and the power that will soon be needed as the AI boom sparks demand for more and more of these data centers. This report said that between 2018 and 2023, the power to run data centers around the US went from 1.9% of total annual electricity consumption to 4.4%, more than double.

And this report estimated that the energy demands of data centers, of which AI is a major part, will continue to increase over the next few years and could by 2028 make up between 6.7% and 12% of total US electricity consumption. 

> [!NOTE] Research Gate paper on US Data Centers
> A new study analyzing 2,132 US data centers (78% of the nation's total) reveals a significant surge in their energy consumption and carbon emissions, largely driven by the AI boom. Since 2018, data center emissions have tripled, reaching 105 million metric tons of CO2 for the 12 months ending August 2024, representing 2.18% of national emissions (compared to approximately 131 million metric tons for domestic commercial airlines). Energy consumption by these centers has also doubled since 2018, now accounting for 4.59% of all US energy use. While pinpointing the precise contribution of AI is challenging, researchers emphasize its rapidly growing impact, especially with the shift towards more complex "multimodal" AI involving images, video, and audio, which require exponentially larger data sizes. Notably, 95% of US data centers are located in areas with energy sources dirtier than the national average, resulting in a 48% higher carbon intensity. The researchers have developed a data portal to track these emissions, aiming to inform future regulations, though they anticipate little regulatory action in the next four years.
> 
> Source: [Research Gate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/351803384_The_environmental_footprint_of_data_centers_in_the_United_States)
