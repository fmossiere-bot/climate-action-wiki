## Digital Transformation for Pollution Prevention and Elimination

Addressing pollution presents a paradox: it's nearly impossible to eliminate without severely impacting our ability to survive. **Our societal infrastructure—encompassing production, distribution, and consumption—necessitates some level of pollution.** The challenge is not to halt the cycle of production, distribution and consumption entirely, which is impractical, but to find a viable balance. 

> We must manage our systems in a way that maintains the supplies of goods and services while keeping pollution levels below thresholds that pose serious risks to our health, exacerbate climate change, and accelerate biodiversity loss.

> [!NOTE] What is Pollution Prevention (P2) - Watch [VIDEO](https://youtu.be/pM-jOssklAM)
> Systemic shifts, including the transition towards circular economies, the promotion of sustainable lifestyles, and green energy transitions at both individual and institutional levels, along with targeted government policies, can significantly prevent pollution at the source. These approaches are more impactful than post-facto clean-up, recycling, treatment, or disposal of pollutants. Digital transformation equips us with the tools to implement these systemic changes, thereby enhancing the effectiveness of our pollution prevention efforts



### Transition towards a Circular Economy

Today, our economy is largely based on a linear “take-make-use-dispose” model
###### To put this in perspective, according to the [Circularity Gap Report, 2024(opens in a new tab)](https://www.circularity-gap.world/2024#download), between 2016 and 2021, the global economy has consumed 582 billion tonnes of materials—nearly as many materials as the 740 billion consumed in the entire 20th century

> There is an urgent need to move away from the linear “take-make-use-dispose” model and transition to a regenerative growth model, in which the value of products, materials and resources is maintained in the economy for as long as possible

What are the systemic shifts necessary to reduce pollution? As in reducing its level and cleaning up pollutants from the environment. 

Digital Transformation can help doing that by
### Monitoring and Reducing Air Pollution

> Technologies like IoT sensors and AI-powered data analytics enable the identification of pollution sources and the tracking of changes over time through real-time monitoring and analysis. This information enhances our decision-making capabilities and empowers proactive interventions to mitigate pollution and protect public health and the environment.

### Preserving Freshwater
**ecosystems critical to supplying humanity with fresh water are degrading due to climate change, overexploitation, and pollution at an unprecedented rate**

> Utilizing satellite imaging, drones, and advanced sensor networks, we can monitor water bodies with precision, detecting contaminants and pollutants. These critical insights into the health of freshwater ecosystems aid in the implementation of targeted conservation efforts. 
> Example [VIDEO](https://youtu.be/Im72J__8ezE)

### Safeguarding Oceans and Marine Health
Oceans and coastal waters receive much of their waste and pollutants - including plastic debris, nutrients, oil, heavy metals, and radioactive waste - from land-based sources. Each year, it is estimated that more than 8 million tonnes of plastic end up in the oceans - the equivalent of emptying a garbage truck of plastic every minute.

> Tools such as satellite monitoring, underwater drones, and sensor networks allow for precise tracking of ocean conditions, identifying areas affected by pollution and habitat degradation. These technologies provide real-time data on water quality, marine biodiversity, and pollutant levels, including the distribution and concentration of plastic debris, helping us protect and restore marine ecosystems.

### Tackling Chemical Pollution
Chemicals are integral to modern life and are used across sectors ranging from agriculture to manufacturing. More than 95% of all manufactured goods rely on chemical technologies.

###### **The evidence for chemical risks is constantly growing, with an estimated 1.6 million deaths in 2016 from exposure to selected chemicals.** 

> Internet of Things (IoT) devices, remote sensors, and real-time data analytics allow us to gain precise insights into chemical levels in water, soil, and air. This data helps identify pollution sources and concentrations, facilitating efforts to address contamination effectively.

### Addressing Land and Soil Pollution
Thousands of years of human activities, the improper disposal of hazardous and urban waste, alongside urban sprawl, industrial operations, transportation, mining, military activities, and conflicts, have contributed significantly to land and soil pollution

[VIDEO - Soil Pollution, a hidden reality](https://youtu.be/wHcY-iFSYZM)
###### **Over 75% of the Earth’s land area is now considered degraded, with projections suggesting this could rise to 95% by 2050 if current trends persist.**

> Tools such as remote sensing, Geographic Information Systems (GIS), and drones significantly improve the accurate mapping and monitoring of contaminated sites and enable effective strategies for detecting, preventing, and remediating land and soil pollution, thereby protecting ecosystems and human health.

### Optimising Waste Management
**By 2025, the world’s cities alone are projected to produce 2.2 billion tonnes of waste annually, more than three times the amount generated in 2009.**
Mining contributes significantly to this problem, too

###### **Between 400,000 and 1 million people die in the Global South each year as a result of diseases related to mismanaged waste**

> AI-powered inventory management systems can enable innovative solutions for reducing food waste through improved tracking and smart logistics. Real-time data analytics can optimize waste collection routes, reducing fuel consumption and emissions. Smart sensors can monitor waste levels, preventing overflows and enabling timely pickups
