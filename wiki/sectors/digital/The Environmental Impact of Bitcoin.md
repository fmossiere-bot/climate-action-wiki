2022 was marked by the rush—and crash—of cryptocurrency. But it is now on the rebound, and the [recent approval by the US securities regulator for bitcoin to be the first US-listed exchange traded funds (ETF)](https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2024/jan/11/bitcoin-etf-approved-sec-explained-meaning-securities-regulator-tweet) has been a real boost to the currency value. What will be the implication for the environment? probably not good.

> **"Every Bitcoin transaction drains 16,000 litres of water - enough to fill a swimming pool. It's time to ponder the unseen ripple effects of digital currency."**

This startling revelation serves as a vivid illustration of the extensive resources and environmental toll associated with Bitcoin transactions. It highlights the broader environmental footprint of digital currencies that often goes unnoticed. Just as every swipe of a card or click to transfer funds seems inconsequential, the energy and resource demands behind each Bitcoin transaction are immense.

### Carbon Footprint of Bitcoin

The environmental impact of Bitcoin extends beyond water. Bitcoin mining, the process by which transactions are verified and new bitcoins are created, use staggering amounts of energy ([https://digiconomist.net/bitcoin-energy-consumption](https://digiconomist.net/bitcoin-energy-consumption)).

> The carbon footprint of a single Bitcoin transaction is equivalent to over 700,000 Visa transactions or watching YouTube for 50,000 hours.

It is estimated that Bitcoin mining has generated 200 Million tonnes of CO2-e since creation, but bear in mind that 92% were in the last 4 years.

![](https://media.licdn.com/dms/image/D4E12AQErbkc_5iG0wg/article-inline_image-shrink_1500_2232/0/1709903678520?e=1717632000&v=beta&t=25XhF6gb09huaG98COore3AJHX64B17A_X6_u3lmXuY)

### Why is this a potential issue?

While that number might seem small today, and a friend reminded me that CryptoCurrencies is a fraction of the financial transaction impact (rightly so), it is growing.

- Globally, bitcoin alone [consumes 0.7% of the total electrictity worldwide,](https://ccaf.io/cbnsi/cbeci/comparisons) but in the US for instance, this is already [estimated](https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=61364#) at 2.3% of the Nation's electricity, pushing prices up in some states like Texas and [pushing some nation to tax](https://www.newscientist.com/article/2421745-us-government-wants-to-tax-bitcoin-to-reduce-its-environmental-impact/?utm_campaign=RSS%7CNSNS&utm_source=NSNS&utm_medium=RSS&utm_content=currents) or completely ban bitcoin mining. It is growing and accelerated (27% YoY consumption increase).
- Banning or Taxing will only solve one particular nation's problem , but not our environmental problem, as we saw with China's ban which effectively pushed those mining operation to countries like Kazakhstans which rely much more on Fossil Fuel, abundant and cheap, with favourable regulatory environment.

> Today, Kazakhstans hosts 18% of the mining industry

  

### What is the solution?

Make it more efficient.

Cryptocurrency is here to stay but like everything digital, it needs to be built or transformed to be efficient. Other cryptocurrencies like [**Etherum**](https://www.newscientist.com/article/2369304-cryptocurrency-ethereum-has-slashed-its-energy-use-by-99-99-per-cent/?utm_campaign=RSS%7CNSNS&utm_source=NSNS&utm_medium=RSS&utm_content=currents) have ditched their system and overnight slashed energy consumption by 99.9%.

Other emerging Cryptos, like [eco coin](https://www.ecocoin.com/), are built with sustainability at its core and with no water consumption.

So it is possible, but it seems that bitcoin has no intention to make a change, with the argument that it would compromise its security. and that its de-centralise system make it too difficult. Now I don't know enough about blockchain to validate this or not, but new [emerging report that its "creator" knew early on about the negative impact Bitcoin](https://www.newscientist.com/article/2418762-bitcoin-creator-satoshi-nakamoto-dismissed-early-climate-concerns/?utm_campaign=RSS%7CNSNS&utm_source=NSNS&utm_medium=RSS&utm_content=currents) could have and decided to ignore it (back in 2009) is not really making the case.

  

### What about renewable energy?

Yes, there are opportunities to mitigate these effects. Renewable energy sources, such as solar and wind, are increasingly being used to power Bitcoin mining operations, offering a more sustainable path forward. But let's not be blind.

The argument that a lot of those mining data centre are using renewable are only partly true. As a matter of fact, more than **50% of bitcoin mining happens in the US and Kazakstan**. Both countries heavily use Fossil Fuel. If it was in Iceland which has abundant and cheap clean energy, I would be less concerned.

> 50% of bitcoin mining happens in the US and Kazakhstan

Another issue at the moment is that, when they use renewable, it might be at the expense of slowing down access to renewable for other businesses or household. In ireland for instance, New permit for Data Centres have been put on hold as **[IEA expects the sector to consume 32 per cent of total electricity by 2026](https://www.irishtimes.com/business/2024/01/24/data-centres-to-consume-one-third-of-states-electricity-by-2026/)** **compared to 17 per cent in 2022**. A lot of those new applications don't have a solid "plan" in terms of bringing their own power which is what is necessary, and what Google for instance is doing.

  

The journey towards reducing the environmental impact of Bitcoin and other digital currencies is complex, but it's a necessary one. Innovations in technology, use of renewable and changes in the protocols can decrease their environmental toll. Like any other financial or consumer product, customer pressure can have a significant impact.
