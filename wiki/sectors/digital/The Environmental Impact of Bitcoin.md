Bitcoin mining uses vast amounts of energy and water. A single transaction consumes enough water to fill a swimming pool and has a carbon footprint equivalent to 700,000 Visa transactions. Global electricity consumption from Bitcoin is growing at 27% per year.

Banning mining in one country does not help, it just moves the problem somewhere with dirtier energy, as China's ban demonstrated. The renewable energy argument is only partly valid, as most mining still happens in fossil fuel-dependent countries.

Other cryptocurrencies have shown that 99% energy reductions are technically possible. Bitcoin has chosen not to follow that path. Without stronger regulation, carbon pricing, or consumer pressure, its environmental footprint will keep growing.

### Key Numbers

- **16,000 litres of water** are consumed per Bitcoin transaction, roughly enough to fill a swimming pool
- One Bitcoin transaction has a carbon footprint equivalent to **700,000 Visa transactions** or **50,000 hours** of YouTube
- Bitcoin mining has generated an estimated **200 million tonnes of CO2** since its creation, with 92% of that occurring in the last four years
- Bitcoin consumes **0.7% of total global electricity**, and already **2.3% in the United States**
- Electricity consumption is growing at **27% per year**
- In Ireland, data centres are expected to consume **32% of total national electricity by 2026**, up from 17% in 2022

---

### What Is the Problem?

Bitcoin relies on a process called mining, where powerful computers verify transactions and create new coins. This process requires enormous amounts of energy and water, most of it invisible to the person making a transaction.

The scale is difficult to grasp. Since Bitcoin was launched, its cumulative carbon emissions are estimated at 200 million tonnes of CO2. The vast majority of this happened recently, as adoption accelerated. The water consumption figures are equally striking: each individual transaction uses roughly as much water as a swimming pool holds.

What makes this particularly concerning is the growth rate. This is not a stable, manageable footprint. It is expanding rapidly, and the recent approval of Bitcoin exchange traded funds in the United States is likely to drive further demand.

---

### Why Banning or Taxing Does Not Solve It

Several countries have responded by restricting or taxing Bitcoin mining. **China introduced a broad ban, and the US government has explored taxation as a way to limit environmental harm.**

The problem is that banning mining in one country does not reduce global emissions. It simply moves the activity elsewhere, often to countries with weaker environmental regulation and a heavier reliance on fossil fuels.

China's ban is a good example. A significant share of the mining operations that left China relocated to Kazakhstan, which depends heavily on coal and other fossil fuels for its electricity. Today, Kazakhstan hosts around 18% of the global Bitcoin mining industry. The emissions did not disappear; they shifted somewhere with a higher carbon intensity.

---

### The Renewable Energy Argument Has Limits

Some argue that Bitcoin mining can be made clean by powering it with renewable energy. This is partly true, but it needs to be looked at carefully.

> More than 50% of Bitcoin mining currently takes place in the United States and Kazakhstan. Both countries generate a large share of their electricity from fossil fuels. The claim that mining runs on renewables applies to a limited portion of the industry.

There is also a wider issue of competition for clean energy. When data centres and mining operations consume large amounts of renewable electricity, that power is no longer available to households or other businesses. In Ireland, this tension is already visible. New permits for data centres have been paused as the sector's electricity demand has grown so fast that it risks crowding out other users.

The situation is different in places like Iceland, where electricity comes almost entirely from geothermal and hydropower sources, and where capacity is less constrained. But Iceland is an exception, not a model that scales globally.

---

### Is There a Better Way?

Other cryptocurrencies have shown that the current approach is not the only one. **Ethereum, one of the largest digital currencies after Bitcoin, changed its underlying system in 2022 and reduced its energy consumption by approximately 99.9% overnight.** Newer currencies, such as Ecocoin, are being designed from the start with low energy use and no water consumption.

These examples show that it is technically possible to build cryptocurrency systems with a much smaller environmental footprint.

Bitcoin, however, has shown little appetite for change. The argument from its community is that switching systems would compromise the security and decentralisation that define it. This may be a genuine technical constraint, though it is worth noting that early communications suggest Bitcoin's original developers were aware of the environmental concerns as far back as 2009 and chose not to address them.

---

### What Would Actually Help?

There is no simple fix, but several things could make a meaningful difference:

- Stronger carbon pricing that reflects the true cost of energy-intensive mining
- Requirements for mining operations to source and bring their own renewable energy, rather than draw from existing grids
- Consumer and investor pressure on exchanges and funds to account for environmental impact
- Continued development of more efficient protocols and alternative currencies

Cryptocurrency is unlikely to disappear. But like any other energy-intensive sector, it will need to become more efficient if it is to have a credible place in a lower-carbon economy.
