**Digital Transformation for Sustainable Development**

> This is a pivotal moment for our world. We can either choose to **harness digital technologies to build a sustainable future** **OR** allow unchecked digitalization to **accelerate the drivers of the Triple Planetary Crisis: climate change, nature loss and pollution.** 

- Digitilisation can both be a driver for the 2030 agenda, but left unchecked, it can also accelerate our demise. 

## Contextualising Digital Transformation

> [!NOTE] Digitization vs Digitalization vs Digital Transformation
> Digitization entails converting information from analog to digital. Digitalization is the use of digital technologies to turn products and services into a digital format to drive efficiency and innovation
> 
 > Digital transformation can be understood as a systems-level economic, societal and environmental transformation triggered by widespread digitalization.
 
> **Digital transformation** unfolds as new possibilities emerge from replacing non-digital with digital processes (**Digitization**) or replacing older technology with next-generation digital technology that enables new innovation and creativity. It goes beyond simply enhancing and supporting traditional methods (**Digitalization**)

## Aligning Digital Transformation and Sustainable Development

> [!NOTE] We need to do it right
> The evidence keeps mounting that while a global economic system based on maximizing shareholder value has led to impressive growth rates, and brought high living standards to many of us, it has come at an enormous cost to people and the planet.
> 

- Any project needs to involve many stakeholders, but more importanyl, needs to involved the local communities. See this video as an example of a project in Tuvalu: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4laF_zBtur8 
- We need to build competencies on both Digital and Sustainability

> On the one hand, the public sector and policymakers need to build up their own digital capacities and understand both the environmental opportunities and risks of digital transformation. On the other hand, entrepreneurs, computer scientists and engineers need to understand the environmental impact of their products and services. Moreover, digital and sustainability literacy must be integrated into academic curricula and vocational training.


### What is CODES
Source: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=igJKUQXe3EU 

CODES stand for Coalition for Digital Environmental Sustainability - CODES aims to steer the digital age towards a just and sustainable transition. It proposes to do this with 3 systemic-shifts:
1. Align Digital and Sustainability  with a commong purpose with standard, training and joint action
2. Mitigate the negative impact of the digital age - such as emissions, e-waste and digital divide
3. Accelerate Digital Innovation for sustainability - innovaiton labs, circular economy and Just transition

### 1. Energy use and Emissions

Computers, mobile phones, internet servers and crypto farms require enormous amounts of energy. Data center in particular use vast amount of energy and use large quantities of water, which can impact local water supply- See [[DRAFT - IRELAND Data Centre Dilemna]] for more on Data Center in Ireland for instance 
###### According to an estimate by the ITU, ICT’s share of global electricity consumption was projected to reach 3.2% in 2020

It is also forecasted to increase. This is specially important for Data centers and the recent AI boom, which is straining the US with access to power, and slowing down reneable energy transition for others like Ireland. 

![[Screenshot 2024-10-07 at 16.08.08.png]]
The graph above shows that the total ICT electricity demand is rising. Data centres will likely constitute a bulk of this demand in the coming years.

### 2. Waste Generation

Wehn we think about the interent of things, we think about the energy to run our devices and the data centers, but also manufacturing those and the increasing demand requires a lot of metal extraction. 

> To meet this growing demand, the extraction of minerals and metals like graphite, lithium, and cobalt could increase by 500% by 2050 severely impacting the land, water, air, and biodiversity.  

When poorly governed, mining can threaten our environment: destroy natural landscapes, pollute groundwater, and displace indigenous people. Ultimately these minerals and metals - fused together as an electronic device - end up in large e-waste dumping sites

### 4. Tacking Misinformation

###### It takes true stories about 6 times longer to reach 1,500 people than it does for false stories to reach the same number of people 

As seen during COVID-19, misinformation travels fast ... and trying to debunk it with fact check proves to be uneffective. See this video on Climate misinformation: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QMJHi4f_uas 

> [!NOTE] Did You Know? 
> It has been shown that **debunking** and fact-checking fake news can lack effectiveness because once people are exposed to a falsehood, it is difficult to correct it.  
> Analogous to the process of medical immunization, “**prebunking**,” or preemptively warning and exposing people to weakened doses of misinformation, can help cultivate “mental antibodies” against fake news.

### 6. Preventing Rights Violations
**Three important domains** in the digital space, linked to environmental and social sustainability, need specific attention in this regard.

1. Many regions in the world are witnessing conflicts between indigenous or local communities who want to preserve natural resources that maintain their livelihoods and the mining operations extracting metals and minerals such as cobalt, graphite, copper, and rare earth metals vital to the production of electronics fuelling the digital transformation.  _Watch [this video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XtlcjGDEljQ) to gain insight into a land use conflict in Indonesia._
2. Digital Surveillance - Several nations are witnessing crackdowns to curb dissent on their activists and journalists in the form of surveillance of their personal and professional lives.
3. Violation of Digital Privacy - As more and more websites, applications, and software have a global footprint, they are being exploited by many actors to collate information and data about people - _Watch [this video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fRACqv1gFmA) on how technologies like AI can be exploited to suppress individual privacy and other human rights._

## Accelerating Innovation

Some good example of Digital Innovation which helps our planetary fight
1. [Climate TRACE](https://www.climatetrace.org/) harnesses satellite imagery and other forms of remote sensing, artificial intelligence, and collective data science expertise to track human-caused GHG emissions as they happen. [VIDEO](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QDT2C6Eo9PQ)
2. As part of the European Commission’s Green Deal and Digital Strategy, [Destination Earth](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/destination-earth) aims to develop – on a global scale - a highly accurate digital model of the Earth to monitor and predict the interaction between natural phenomena and human activities. [VIDEO](https://youtu.be/FKVHZlGqEyw)
3. NVIDIA plans to build the world’s most powerful AI supercomputer dedicated to predicting climate change. Named [Earth-2, or E-2](https://blogs.nvidia.com/blog/2021/11/12/earth-2-supercomputer/), the system would create a digital twin of Earth in the Omniverse [VIDEO](https://youtu.be/gTiqV3akBIE)


### Catalyzing a Sustainable Circular Economy
Digital Transformation can be the lever to drive this much-needed transition.
Intelligent assets, connectivity, and monitoring play a crucial role in the proliferation of circularity. A [**Digital Product Passport**](https://wupperinst.org/en/a/wi/a/s/ad/7784) that tracks a product’s climate, environmental, and social impacts throughout the value chain can be a game changer in rapidly scaling this transition


### Empowering Sustainable Consumption
Unsustainable consumption results from the increasing demand for fossil fuels, growing carbon emissions, and deepening inequalities, which have profound negative implications for people and the planet
1. As global greenhouse gases increase due to pollution and industrial processes, so do global temperatures. As a result, extreme weather events, such as heat waves, droughts and unequal rain patterns, become more frequent.
2. These events add an extra burden on agriculture and food production, which increases food prices when coupled with overconsumption and economic shocks. Consequently, food accessibility is compromised, and food security is undermined.
3. In 2019, the amount of materials used to meet demands for goods and services - Domestic Material Consumption - amounted to 95.1 billion metric tons. Most of these are not adequately managed, generating increasing waste and pollution
