### Global Biodiversity Framework

> On December 19, 2022, 196 countries reached an agreement on the Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework (GBF). This program sets an ambitious path to achieve a global vision of **a world living in harmony with nature by 2050** and **provides a framework for global action on biodiversity and paving the way for a nature-positive and sustainable world by 2050**


###### The framework calls for the effective conservation and management of at least 30 per cent of the world’s lands, inland waters, coastal areas and oceans, as well as the restoration of 30 per cent of degraded ecosystems by 2030.

In adopting the GBF, all parties committed to setting national targets to implement it based on the 23 global targets. The success of the GBF will depend on our ability to effectively plan, monitor, report, and review its implementation at the national level.

## Conservation of Biodiversity 

> Conservation is the proactive effort to protect and maintain the existing biodiversity within ecosystems. It entails the establishment and management of protected areas, the implementation of legal measures to prevent over-exploitation, and educational initiatives to raise awareness about the significance of biodiversity

###### Besides the direct benefits of biodiversity in the form of food, fibre, firewood, and pharmaceuticals, there are many indirect benefits we receive through ecosystem services such as pollination, pest control, climate moderation and flood control.


> [!NOTE] About the Biodiversity Intactness Index
> The Biodiversity Intactness Index (BII) measures biodiversity change using abundance data on plants, fungi and animals worldwide. The Index shows how local terrestrial biodiversity responds to human pressures such as land use change and intensification. 
> **Only the darkest areas have retained enough natural biodiversity to be within the proposed planetary boundary where the Biodiversity Intactness Index is above 90%.**
> ![[Screenshot 2024-10-10 at 18.17.12.png]]
> Source: https://www.nhm.ac.uk/our-science/services/data/biodiversity-intactness-index.html

### FOOD SYSTEMS

![[Screenshot 2024-10-10 at 18.40.49.png]]

Global food systems are a direct driver of accelerating biodiversity loss. Food production is degrading or destroying natural habitats and contributing to species extinction. Our current food system is structured to drive demand, leading to biodiversity loss through:  
1. the continued conversion of natural or semi-natural ecosystems to managed ones; and 
2. the use of unsustainable agricultural practices at the farm level, landscape level and global level

### CITIES AND INFRASTRUCTURE
> Urban ecosystems, characterized by intensified land use, green space fragmentation, and the introduction of non-native species, often exhibit lower biodiversity than natural environments. 

###### Urbanization is the third most prevalent threat to species on the IUCN Red List. 

This process displaces native flora and fauna, diminishing species abundance and diversity, while physical and chemical barriers created by urbanization hinder species movement, limiting access to food, shelter, and breeding partners.

## Ecosystem Restoration

> Restoration is a targeted approach aimed at repairing and rejuvenating ecosystems that have been degraded or altered by human activities. Through active interventions such as reforestation, habitat rehabilitation, and the reintroduction of native species, restoration seeks to reverse the negative impacts of environmental damage

## Sustainable use of Biodiversity

Sustainable use involves the responsible and balanced use of natural resources to meet current needs without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.

 > Sustainable use goes beyond conservation and restoration by addressing the broader context of human development. It encompasses practices that ensure ecosystems remain healthy and productive over the long term, recognizing the interconnectedness of ecological processes, societal needs, and economic activities.
 

> [!NOTE] Earth Overshoot Day
> **Earth Overshoot Day marks the date when humanity’s demand for ecological resources and services in a given year exceeds what the Earth can regenerate in that year.**  
> In 2023, it fell on 2 August. Source: https://overshoot.footprintnetwork.org/newsroom/visuals/
> ![[Screenshot 2024-10-11 at 17.05.16.png]]


### LAND AND FRESHWATER

> The rapid pace of urbanization, industrial expansion, and agricultural intensification has set in motion a cascade of environmental repercussions, triggering land degradation and intensifying water scarcity. This overexploitation poses severe threats, including soil erosion, deforestation, and the depletion of aquifers, impacting not only the environment but also food security and the well-being of communities worldwide.

###### Around 1.6 billion people’s livelihoods depend on forests, where the highest levels of terrestrial biodiversity are found, making sustainable land use critical to human well-being

> [!NOTE] Digital and Water resources
> Leveraging technologies such as artificial intelligence, remote sensing, and data analytics, we can monitor and manage land and water resources with unprecedented precision. These digital tools enable real-time tracking of environmental changes, allowing for proactive and informed decision-making. Smart water management systems leverage IoT devices to monitor water quality, consumption patterns, and leaks, promoting efficient use and conservation.
