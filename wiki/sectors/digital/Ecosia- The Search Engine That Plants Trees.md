**What is Ecosia?**

Ecosia is a Berlin-based search engine founded in 2009 by Christian Kroll. Its mission is simple: use the money it makes from online searches to plant trees around the world. Instead of keeping its profits, Ecosia reinvests all of them into reforestation projects, powers its searches with renewable energy, and does not track or sell user data.

It works just like Google or Bing — you type a search, you get results. The difference is what happens with the money your search generates.

---

**How does it actually work?**

When you search on Ecosia, ads appear in the results (similar to Google). Those ads generate a small amount of revenue. Ecosia spends at least 80% of that advertising revenue on planting trees. You don't pay anything — it's the advertisers who are funding the trees, through your clicks.

Ecosia typically plants a tree roughly every 0.8 seconds.

---

**Why is it greener than Google?**

There are two main reasons.

First, the energy side. Ecosia runs its search servers on renewable energy sources like wind and solar power, which gives it a lower environmental footprint than search engines that still rely on more carbon-heavy energy sources.

Second, the impact side. Unlike Google, Ecosia is carbon negative — meaning it compensates for more CO₂ than it emits, thanks to its tree-planting model.

There is one thing worth knowing though: when you search on Ecosia, the actual results come from Microsoft Bing, not Ecosia's own index — so Ecosia doesn't generate its own web results independently. That said, the money still flows back to the trees.

On the privacy side, unlike Google, which builds user profiles to serve ads, Ecosia does not track individual user data, sell information to third parties, or keep permanent search histories.

---

**Where do they plant trees?**

Ecosia has funded tree planting in over 35 countries. They focus on places where deforestation is most damaging, and where trees can have the biggest impact on local ecosystems and communities.

Some of the key regions include Nicaragua and Peru, Burkina Faso and Malawi, Indonesia and Australia, and Madagascar — where they built a tree nursery for 200,000 trees back in 2018.

More recently they have expanded into Togo, Vietnam, and the Netherlands. In October 2025, the top five countries receiving funding were Madagascar, Brazil, Togo, Uganda, and Mali.

Ecosia works with over 200,000 tree planters and 125 organisations worldwide, focusing on planting native species — including many that are endangered or vulnerable.

---

**Progress so far**

Ecosia recently celebrated planting its 250 millionth tree, becoming the world's largest planter of native trees.

In 2024 alone, Ecosia planted and protected 18.4 million trees across around 1,600 different species.

To keep things transparent, Ecosia publishes a detailed monthly financial report showing its income, expenses, and how much money went toward tree planting.

---

**Can I use it?**

Yes. Ecosia is available as a browser extension for Chrome, Firefox, and Safari, and also has mobile apps for iOS and Android. In April 2024, they also launched their own Chromium-based desktop browser.

Switching takes about two minutes, and the search experience is very close to what you're used to. It's probably the easiest climate action you'll ever take.

## Going Further: The Campaign for a Climate Nobel Prize

In December 2025, Ecosia CEO Christian Kroll took a step beyond tree-planting. He went to a Berlin notary and committed **€1 million** as the seed endowment for a new Nobel Prize category: Climate and Planetary Health. He then wrote to the Nobel Foundation asking them to adopt it.

The Nobel Prize currently covers six categories: Physics, Chemistry, Physiology or Medicine, Literature, Peace, and Economics. The last new category was Economics, added in 1969. Kroll's argument is simple: "When Alfred Nobel created the prizes, he wrote that they must serve the greatest benefit to humankind. Today, this means protecting the planet we all depend on."

The campaign website is at climatenobelprize.org. Ecosia proposes three areas of recognition:

- **Pragmatic Governance** — implementing smart, effective policies that accelerate proven solutions
- **Scaling Prosperity and Markets** — innovations and business models that create sustainable prosperity at scale
- **Building Common Ground** — uniting diverse perspectives to create shared, practical solutions

Ecosia would have no influence over nominations or laureates. The money is committed; the Nobel Foundation decides. A symbolic blue chair was handcrafted and placed among the laureate seats at the Nobel ceremony, waiting to be filled.

The campaign is backed by climate activist Luisa Neubauer and energy economist Dr Claudia Kemfert, who called a Climate Nobel "a global signal that climate protection secures the future and deserves the highest recognition."
