## What is cryptocurrency mining, and why does it matter?

Most of the environmental discussion around cryptocurrency focuses on Bitcoin. But it is worth starting broader. There are now **over 10,000 active cryptocurrencies**. The ones that carry the biggest environmental footprint share one thing: they use a system called proof-of-work to validate transactions.

Proof-of-work requires computers to race against each other to solve complex mathematical puzzles. The first to solve the puzzle gets to add the next block of transactions to the chain — and earns a coin reward for doing so. The catch is that this process runs continuously, globally, consuming vast amounts of electricity 24 hours a day. The more the coins are worth, the more miners join, and the more energy is consumed.

The scale of that energy use — and what it means for the climate, water supplies, and waste — is what the rest of this article is about.

## The global carbon footprint of crypto


Cryptocurrency mining now accounts for roughly 0.6% of global electricity consumption and around 0.2% of global CO2-equivalent emissions, according to the Centre for International Governance Innovation. Those percentages may sound modest, but the absolute numbers are not, and the trend is upward.

Bitcoin alone consumed approximately 121 terawatt-hours (TWh) of electricity in 2023, and projections point to over 130 TWh by 2025. The broader mining industry, when you include all proof-of-work cryptocurrencies, contributes an estimated 139 million tonnes of CO2-equivalent per year once all greenhouse gas sources are counted.

The IMF has warned that by 2027, crypto mining could account for 0.7% of total global CO2 emissions — a seemingly small share that would still exceed the entire annual emissions of many countries.

> 139 million tonnes CO₂-equivalent — the estimated annual GHG footprint of the global cryptocurrency mining industry, according to recent research


The growth is not linear. A 400% rise in Bitcoin's price between 2020 and 2021 was followed by a 140% surge in the network's electricity use. The pattern repeats: higher prices attract more miners, which drives up energy demand, which drives up emissions. The environmental impact of crypto is tightly tied to its market value and the market has been climbing.

## Where the mining happens and why that matters



Not all electricity is created equal. A mining operation running on Norwegian hydropower has a very different carbon footprint from one running on coal in **Kazakhstan**. So the geography of mining matters enormously.

The **US is now the largest single mining location, accounting for roughly 50% of global Bitcoin** mining activity. Kazakhstan, Russia, Canada, and Malaysia round out the major hubs. **China, which once dominated the sector, banned mining in 2021**,  a decision that had unintended consequences.

When China shut its doors, mining operations relocated fast. **Kazakhstan became a primary destination overnight, at one point hosting around 18% of global Bitcoin mining.** The reason was simple: abundant coal and gas power, low electricity prices, cold winters that cut cooling costs, and a regulatory environment that initially welcomed the industry. For miners, it checked every box.

The environmental consequence was a rise in the carbon intensity of the global network, since Kazakhstan's grid runs heavily on fossil fuels. The influx also strained the country's electricity infrastructure, leading to power shortages and prompting authorities to restrict mining activity. Kazakhstan's story illustrates a pattern: mining gravitates toward cheap energy, and cheap energy is usually fossil fuel energy.

> _"Because countries use different sources of energy to generate electricity, their electricity production impacts on climate, water, and land are not the same." — United Nations University research, 2023

## Venezuela: a different kind of crypto story


Venezuela's relationship with cryptocurrency is a useful case study in what can go wrong when a government uses digital currency as a workaround for political and financial problems, rather than as a genuine innovation.

Facing economic collapse and international sanctions, President Maduro launched the Petro in 2018 — a state-backed cryptocurrency nominally tied to oil reserves. It was presented as a way to bypass US sanctions and stabilise the economy. Even Venezuela's own central bank refused to accept it, and after six years of failed implementation, the Petro was quietly shut down in 2024.

What followed was arguably more concerning. Between 2023 and 2024, Venezuelan authorities seized approximately $500 million worth of Bitcoin from mining operations, reportedly redirecting it into state control. In May 2024, the government banned private Bitcoin mining entirely, citing pressure on the electricity grid — while simultaneously consolidating crypto assets under state management.

Venezuela is less a major mining hub in the way Kazakhstan is, and more a cautionary tale about how cryptocurrency can be used to circumvent financial oversight, fund authoritarian governments, and evade sanctions. The line between innovation and exploitation is thin when regulatory controls are absent.

## Deregulation and financial risk — a growing concern

The environmental problems with cryptocurrency are real. But there is a second set of risks that deserves equal attention: the financial and governance risks that come with weak oversight.

Cryptocurrency has long been used for money laundering, fraud, and sanctions evasion — not by all users, but at a scale that is impossible to ignore. In 2025, crypto-linked illicit financial flows reached an estimated $158 billion globally, an all-time high and more than triple the 2024 figure, according to Kroll's financial compliance research.

The timing is significant. In early 2025, the US Department of Justice disbanded its dedicated cryptocurrency enforcement unit. The IRS team overseeing anti-money laundering compliance at crypto firms dropped to its lowest staffing level since 2017. A number of enforcement actions against crypto companies were quietly dropped.

The result, in the words of financial compliance analysts, has been "a more permissive environment for illicit actors."

> $158 billion — estimated global illicit crypto-linked financial flows in 2025, an all-time high (Kroll, 2026)


This matters for businesses and sustainability professionals for a reason that is not always obvious. Supply chain due diligence, Scope 3 reporting, and anti-corruption compliance are all areas where exposure to crypto assets — whether through payments, investments, or treasury holdings — may create undisclosed risks. If your pension fund holds Bitcoin ETFs, or if a supplier accepts crypto payments, the financial and reputational exposure exists whether or not it shows up in a standard risk register.

## Bitcoin versus the rest: why the design choice matters


It is worth being precise here, because not all cryptocurrencies carry the same footprint.

Bitcoin remains committed to proof-of-work. Its community has consistently resisted switching to a lower-energy alternative, with the argument that the current system is fundamental to its security and decentralisation. A single Bitcoin transaction in 2025 is estimated to use around 707 kWh of electricity — roughly what an average European household uses in two months.

**Ethereum, the second-largest cryptocurrency by market value, made a different choice**. In September 2022, it switched from proof-of-work to proof-of-stake — a system where validators stake existing coins rather than compete through computing power. The result was immediate: Ethereum's energy consumption dropped by approximately 99.84% overnight. It is now possible to validate Ethereum transactions on a standard laptop.

>  **99.84% — reduction in Ethereum's energy consumption after switching to proof-of-stake in 2022 (Ethereum Foundation)**

> Other cryptocurrencies, including Solana, Cardano, and Avalanche, were built on proof-of-stake from the start. The technology to make this transition exists and has been proven at scale. Bitcoin's refusal to change is not a technical limitation — it is a deliberate choice.

Early correspondence between Bitcoin's pseudonymous creator Satoshi Nakamoto, now in the public domain, suggests that concerns about Bitcoin's environmental impact were raised as early as 2009 and dismissed. Whether that makes Bitcoin's footprint more or less acceptable is a question each person will answer differently. But it does make the "we didn't know" argument harder to sustain.

## The water and waste nobody talks about

Carbon gets most of the headlines. But cryptocurrency mining also has significant water and waste footprints that are rarely discussed.

On water: mining hardware generates constant heat. Cooling it requires water — directly at the mining sites, and indirectly through the thermal power plants providing electricity. Research published in Cell Reports Sustainability estimated that Bitcoin mining alone consumed over 1,600 gigaliters of water in 2021, rising to an estimated 2,237 gigaliters by 2023. A single Bitcoin transaction uses on average around 16,000 litres of water — roughly enough to fill a garden swimming pool.

On waste: mining relies on specialised hardware called ASICs that become unprofitable quickly as the network grows more competitive. The average lifespan of a mining chip before it is replaced is around 1.3 years — not because it stops working, but because it becomes too slow to turn a profit. Research published in Resources, Conservation, and Recycling estimated this generates around 30,700 metric tonnes of e-waste annually — comparable to the total personal computing waste produced each year in the Netherlands.

**STAT:** 16,000 litres — the estimated average water use per single Bitcoin transaction, according to research in Cell Reports Sustainability

**BODY:**

E-waste is not a benign problem. Many of the materials in mining hardware — lead, cadmium, lithium — are toxic. Less than 20% of global e-waste is currently recycled, and mining hardware is largely single-purpose, meaning few components can be repurposed.

## 2026 Update: Can Quantum Computing Clean Up Crypto Mining?

A 2026 experiment suggests quantum computers could one day slash the energy cost of cryptocurrency mining, though significant caveats remain.

Colton Dillion at Postquant Labs set up an experimental blockchain called **Quip**, running since April 2026. Unlike Bitcoin's proof-of-work, which involves brute-force mathematical racing, Quip's validation task is an **optimisation problem** — the kind of complex scheduling or portfolio calculation that quantum computers are better suited to than conventional machines. A D-Wave Advantage2 quantum computer joins the network alongside ordinary computers to compete for blocks.

The preliminary results are striking. Although Advantage2 is only available for about five minutes a day, it wins **92% of the blocks it competes for**. More importantly, it does so using roughly **12.5 watts** compared to approximately **1,334 watts** for a conventional machine tackling the same problem — about **100 times less power**. A conventional machine capable of beating Advantage2 would need around **300 times** more power than the quantum machine uses.

This points to a potential future where proof-of-work cryptocurrencies shift from energy-intensive brute-force computation to optimisation problems that quantum hardware can solve far more efficiently. Quip is also designed to be quantum-safe from the start, unlike most existing blockchains whose encryption could eventually be broken by sufficiently powerful quantum computers.

**The caveats matter:** Olivier Ezratty of the Quantum Energy Initiative points out that the capital and manufacturing costs of building and maintaining quantum hardware are very high, and energy savings per computation may not translate into lower total energy costs at scale. The experiment is also tiny — a single machine, a few minutes a day. Scaling this to a real financial network is a different challenge entirely.
