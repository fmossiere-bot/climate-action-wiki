## What is it

Every time we pull an iPhone out of our pocket, we are interacting with one of the most sophisticated pieces of technology on earth. However, behind the sleek glass and aluminum exterior lies a complex global footprint. The carbon footprint of an iPhone refers to the total greenhouse gas emissions generated throughout its entire existence. This spans from digging raw metals out of the earth and assembling the device in high-tech factories, to flying it across oceans, powering its daily charging cycles, and eventually disposing of it when it breaks or becomes obsolete.

While we often think of environmental pollution in terms of cars or smokestacks, consumer electronics are rapidly becoming a major driver of global emissions. When you look at an individual iPhone, its lifetime carbon footprint sits somewhere between 55 kg and 120 kg of $CO_2$, fluctuating based on the specific model and storage capacity. Although that may seem comparable to burning a few gallons of gasoline, multiplying that number by the billions of smartphones active worldwide transforms a seemingly small personal device into a significant global environmental challenge.

## Why it matters

For a non-expert, the carbon footprint of an iPhone is a stark reminder that digital convenience carries a physical cost. A staggering 75% to 95% of a phone’s lifetime emissions are released before you even open the box. The initial extraction of materials—including 16 of the 17 known rare-earth metals—destroys ecosystems, while the massive energy demand required to manufacture microchips mimics the annual emissions of entire countries.

Furthermore, standard manufacturer reports regularly underestimate the real-world impact of using these devices. Official data often fails to account for the immense energy consumed by cellular networks and cloud data centers every time we stream a video, back up photos, or load an app. As electronic waste piles up in landfills, it risks leaching toxic materials into local soil and water. Understanding this reality empowers consumers to break the cycle of upgrading devices prematurely. Simple behavioral shifts, like keeping a phone for five years instead of two or choosing brands designed for repair, can cut a user's annual smartphone carbon impact by over 30%.

## Key facts

- **Manufacturing Dominance:** Up to 95% of an iPhone's total carbon emissions occur during the initial production and manufacturing phases, rather than during its daily use [1].
    
- **Macro Comparison:** The global carbon footprint generated by smartphone manufacturing is roughly equal to the annual $CO_2$ emissions of the entire country of the Philippines, which houses over 100 million residents [1].
    
- **The Breakdown of Impact:** Based on an average four-year lifespan, an iPhone's emissions are distributed across production and packaging (81%), energy and charging usage (16%), global shipping and transportation (2%), and end-of-life recycling or disposal (less than 1%) [1].
    
- **Storage Scales Emissions:** High-capacity phones require more manufacturing energy; for instance, an iPhone 14 Pro with 128 GB of storage creates 65 kg of $CO_2$, whereas the 1 TB variant spikes to 116 kg of $CO_2$ [1].
    
- **The Cloud Network Underestimation:** Standard environmental reports often omit the broader digital ecosystem. While an official report may claim an iPhone 13 causes only 10 kg of usage emissions, accounting for the mobile data networks and cloud storage servers adds an estimated 50 kg of hidden $CO_2$ emissions per device [1].
    
- **Aggregated Charging Demands:** Global smartphone charging demands 8,088,324 tons of $CO_2$ equivalent every single year, an impact equal to having 1.7 million gasoline vehicles driving on American roads simultaneously [1].
    
- **Corporate Recycling Disparities:** Corporate efforts vary widely. Apple utilizes 100% recycled tin, tungsten, rare-earth elements, and gold in key parts alongside 35% recycled plastic. In contrast, its main competitor Samsung relies significantly less on certified recycled metals, using recycled ocean plastics primarily for small structural elements like the inner shell of its stylus pen [1].
    
- **Corporate Climate Goals:** Apple has publicly committed to a carbon-neutral target across its entire supply chain by the year 2030, while Samsung has not announced a definitive target date for its broader supply chain [1].
    
- **The Longevity Advantage:** Alternatives like Amsterdam-based Fairphone prioritize modular, easily replaceable components to combat planned obsolescence. Because manufacturing dominates emissions, simply choosing to maintain and use a smartphone for 5 years instead of 3 drops its annual carbon footprint by more than 30% [2].

## Are Cell Phones Bad for the Environment?

When considering the impact of smartphone manufacturing on a global scale, the question follows, are cell phones bad for the environment?

The answer is Cell phones, and in particular cell phone manufacturing, are far worse for the environment than initially believed.

More than 3.5 billion people around the world own a smartphone, and this number continues to increase.

The ongoing manufacturing and use of smartphones are causing a possibly irreversible environmental disaster because of the mining processes that are required to extract the metals used for phone manufacturing, as well as the toxic waste generated by not recycling old phones.

Almost all brands of phones, including iPhones, require the extraction of 16 out of the 17 known rare-earth metals.

![Bar chart showing carbon emissions from streaming in tablet.](https://8billiontrees.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/02/CO2-Emissions-of-Streaming-in-Tablet.png)

Furthermore, [smartphones are responsible for the most GHG emissions](https://www.epa.gov/sites/default/files/2015-06/documents/smart_phone_infographic_v4.pdf) when compared to all other electronic products and devices. When a smartphone reaches the end of its lifespan and eventually ends up in a landfill, it proceeds to pollute water and soil.

### Why Is the Carbon Footprint Announced in the iPhone 13 “Product Environmental Report” Underestimated?

There has been a lot of talk about the iPhone 13 Product Environmental Report and how much the carbon footprint described in it is being underestimated.

This is because the report states that only 10kg of the 64kg of carbon emissions related to iPhones are attributed to [usage](https://www.energy.gov/eere/energy-efficiency).[24](https://8billiontrees.com/carbon-offsets-credits/carbon-footprint-of-iphone/#ref-24) Considering that mobile data should factor into the equation, it is estimated that around 50 kg of carbon emissions need to be added to the report for the figures to be accurate.

This means that usage alone would emit 60 kg of CO2 per iPhone.

### What About the iPhone 14?

The carbon footprint of iPhone 14 is estimated to be:

- 79%: Source materials and production
- 2%: Package and ship
- 18%: Phone usage
- Less than 1%: End of lifespan process

But yet again, it seems that these figures have been underestimated and that the real figure is closer to an estimated 313kg of 4 years of usage.[3](https://8billiontrees.com/carbon-offsets-credits/carbon-footprint-of-iphone/#ref-3)

Looking at the above figures, it is clear that taking responsibility for our products at every stage is essential if we hope to recover some of the damage that cell phone production is causing to the global environment.



### Carbon Footprint of Making a Phone

The carbon footprint of making a phone includes an estimated 80 kg of CO2 emitted by a single device during manufacturing and production.

Extra CO2 is emitted during the transportation of newly manufactured phones and during the end-of-life processes.

The technology that accompanies the manufacturing of smartphones comes with massive energy requirements. For instance, using cloud storage and computing alone requires around 30 billion watts of electricity every year.

Fortunately, you can erase these emissions by using an [earth friendly web usage carbon offset](https://shop.8billiontrees.com/collections/carbon-offsets/products/earth-friendly-web-usage-carbon-offset) program.

### Carbon Footprint of Charging Phone

Data released in 2021 revealed that global smartphone charging is responsible for 8,088,324 tons of carbon dioxide equivalent every year. This figure is also the equivalent of providing a barbeque for around 1 and a half billion people.

Furthermore, global smartphone charging is also the equivalent of:

- A bullet train carrying 2 billion passengers.
- 1,758,311 [gasoline vehicles](https://www.epa.gov/ghgemissions/sources-greenhouse-gas-emissions) on the road in America.[27](https://8billiontrees.com/carbon-offsets-credits/carbon-footprint-of-iphone/#ref-27)
- The energy required to power the lights on the Eiffel Tower times 23,597,630.
- The energy required to produce 242,165,389 pairs of denim jeans.
- The energy required to cook 1,394,538,620 barbecue meals in the United Kingdom.
- The energy is released by 2,888,687 Australian cows.[7](https://8billiontrees.com/carbon-offsets-credits/carbon-footprint-of-iphone/#ref-7)
