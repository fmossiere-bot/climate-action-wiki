**Digital Sustainability for Climate Action**

## Objective
1. Understand the role of digital transformation in enabling, scaling, and accelerating measures for climate change mitigation and adaptation.
2. Appreciate linkages between digital transformation and climate change across key systems.
3. Understand how digital technologies themselves create negative environmental and climate change impacts.
4. Reflect on the next steps to unlocking the full potential of digital transformation for climate action, while minimising negative impacts.

In this module, we examine the relationship between digital transformation and climate change. We explore the transformational shift digitalization can bring to our approach towards climate action in both climate change mitigation and adaptation

###### The World Economic Forum's  [Exponential Climate Action Roadmap(opens in a new tab)](https://exponentialroadmap.org/exponential-roadmap/) estimates that digital technologies can help reduce global carbon emissions by up to 15%- around a third of the reduction required by 2030 – through solutions in energy, manufacturing, agriculture and land use, buildings, services, transportation and traffic management. 

> Digital technologies are crucial to climate adaptation as they support government agencies, humanitarian organizations and citizens in making better decisions by providing timely and effective information for effective early warning and enhanced disaster management.

Scalable new technologies coupled with nature-based solutions can enable us to leapfrog to a cleaner, more resilient world. In the following lessons, we will learn about the practical application of digital transformation for climate change adaptation and mitigation and also examine the potential pitfalls to avoid as we scale up the use of technology.

### Remote Sensing and mapping
Remote sensing involves acquiring data about objects or scenes from a distance using satellites or other instruments. Examples include aerial photography, satellite imagery, and radar.

This enables mapping, monitoring, and modelling of changes in land use, vegetation, and land cover. This information is crucial for understanding the impact of climate change on ecosystems and biodiversity

> See [VIDEO](https://youtu.be/5aDHQQE96YQ)

Compared to direct land observations, remote sensing techniques offer wider spatial coverage, making it possible to monitor larger areas

### Climate Resilient Infrastructures
The US needs to review probably most of its bridges. Most of them were built before the 80s, before climate change and climate adaptation was such a thing. 

> Our existing infrastructure — buildings, roads, bridges, airports and railway lines, power stations, or telecom towers – is vulnerable to today’s weather events

By integrating emerging and disruptive digital technologies like remote sensing, Internet of Things (IoT) devices and satellite imagery into infrastructure systems, engineers and planners can detect vulnerabilities, monitor structural integrity and enable the development of resilient infrastructure designs

### Community Engagement
Community engagement is vital for systemic change, as it puts people at the centre of societal transformation. **Grassroots-level actions are the initial sparks for transformative movements, complementing top-down mandates and political actions**. Engaging diverse community groups ensures equity by giving everyone a voice.

Isolated government actions can lead to poorly planned policies that face resistance. **Engagement fosters a strong social mandate, paving the way for transformative climate policies**. This encourages businesses, governments and civil society to align on choices that benefit the wider community.
