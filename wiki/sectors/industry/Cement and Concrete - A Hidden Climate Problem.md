Concrete is the most used material on Earth after water, and its production accounts for around 8% of global greenhouse gas emissions. The core problem is cement, its main ingredient: making it releases CO2 as a direct result of a chemical process, not just from the energy used. This makes it very hard to decarbonise.

Demand is still growing, driven largely by urbanisation in Asia, and the sector is currently off-track on its climate targets.

There are real solutions available, including replacing clinker with alternative materials, new low-carbon cement technologies, and carbon capture. But progress is slow, margins are thin, and large-scale adoption has not happened yet.

Timber is a promising alternative for construction, but it only works if the wood is sustainably sourced and properly protected.

Without stronger carbon pricing and clearer procurement standards, the gap between where the industry is and where it needs to be will remain difficult to close.
### Key Numbers

- **8%** of global greenhouse gas emissions come from concrete production
- **4 billion tonnes** of cement are produced each year
- **10 billion tonnes** of concrete are used every year
- **660 kg of CO2** are released for every tonne of cement produced
- If the cement industry were a country, it would be the **third largest emitter** in the world, after China and the United States
- Global demand for cement is forecast to **increase by 48%** by 2050
- The cement sector is currently **off-track** to meet Paris Agreement targets

### What Is the Difference Between Cement and Concrete?

People often use these two words interchangeably, but they are not the same thing.

> **Cement** is a binding agent made from limestone and clay. **Concrete** is a building material made by mixing cement with water, sand, and rock. Cement is the key ingredient in concrete, and it is also the source of most of its emissions.

Concrete is the most used material on Earth after water. It is hard to imagine modern construction without it.

---

### Why Is Cement So Polluting?

The problem with cement is not just the energy needed to produce it. The emissions are built into the chemistry itself.

To make cement, limestone is heated at very high temperatures in a kiln. This converts calcium carbonate into calcium oxide and releases CO2 as a direct byproduct. This chemical reaction alone accounts for 3 to 7% of global CO2 emissions, independent of the fuel used to power the kiln.

> The main component produced in this process is called **clinker**. More than half of cement's CO2 emissions come from clinker production. Today, it takes roughly 660 kg of CO2 to produce one tonne of cement.

This is what makes cement particularly hard to decarbonise. Unlike sectors where the problem is mainly about switching to cleaner energy, cement releases CO2 as part of the production process itself.

---

### The Scale of the Problem

A few facts help put this into perspective:

> - All the plastic produced in the past 60 years amounts to around 8 billion tonnes. The cement industry emits more than that every two years
> - China alone used more cement between 2011 and 2013 than the United States did in the entire 20th century
> - China currently uses around half of all the concrete produced globally
> - Production is still growing. The largest share of future demand is expected to come from India and other developing countries in Asia, driven by rapid urbanisation.

The emissions do not stop at the factory gate either. Concrete buildings absorb heat and radiate it back out long after the sun has set, contributing to the **urban heat island effect** in cities. This can make urban areas several degrees warmer than surrounding countryside, and it amplifies the extreme weather that buildings are supposed to shelter us from.

There are also significant indirect impacts: the global demand for sand to make concrete has led to serious environmental damage and, in some regions, violent conflict. Dust from cement production and construction sites worsens air quality and contributes to respiratory disease.

### What Are the Targets?

The Global Cement and Concrete Association (GCCA) has set targets of:

- **20% reduction in CO2 per tonne of cement by 2030**
- **25% reduction in CO2 per cubic metre of concrete by 2030**
- **Full decarbonisation by 2050**

These are ambitious goals. According to the World Resources Institute's 2023 tracker, the sector is currently well off-track to meet them.

Several structural barriers stand in the way. The industry is dominated by a small number of large producers who are cautious about adopting new materials or processes. Margins are very thin, which limits investment in change. Carbon pricing, where it exists, is not yet strong enough in most markets to close the cost gap between conventional and low-carbon alternatives. And engineers, architects, and clients are understandably cautious about using new building materials on large projects.

### How Can Cement Be Decarbonised?

There is no single solution. Progress will likely come from combining several different approaches.

**Clinker substitution** is the most established route. Replacing some of the clinker in a cement mix with other materials reduces the amount of CO2 released. These substitute materials include fly ash (a byproduct of coal combustion), ground granulated blast-furnace slag (GGBS), volcanic ash, ground limestone, and certain clays. The LC3 process, for example, uses calcined clay and limestone and can reduce CO2 emissions by up to 40% while maintaining performance.

**Alternative raw materials** offer another path. A UK company called Seratech has developed a process using olivine, an abundant mineral, to partially replace clinker. Replacing 35% of the regular cement with silica derived from this process could produce carbon-neutral cement. Replacing 40% or more could make it carbon negative.

**Carbon capture and storage (CCS)** is seen by many in the industry as essential for reaching full decarbonisation, particularly for the process emissions that cannot be designed out. In Norway, Heidelberg Materials is building a CCS facility at its Brevik cement plant, aiming to capture 90 to 95% of the plant's emissions. The captured CO2 will be transported and stored under the Norwegian seabed as part of the Longship project, a cross-border infrastructure initiative backed by the Norwegian government.

CCS is expensive and energy-intensive, and it has a mixed track record in other sectors. For cement, it is currently the most advanced technology available for the share of emissions that cannot be eliminated through material substitution alone.

**Low-carbon cement startups** are also making progress. Dublin-based Ecocem is developing a technology called ACT that aims to reduce cement's carbon intensity by up to 70% using widely available low-carbon materials, while working within existing cement plant infrastructure. In the United States, companies such as Brimstone, CarbonBuilt, and Fortera are commercialising approaches that either eliminate process emissions or capture and mineralise CO2 during production.

### The Role of Alternative Materials

Cement substitution is not the only lever. **Timber** is one of the most discussed alternatives to concrete in construction. Wood stores carbon for the lifetime of the building, and replacing concrete with responsibly sourced timber can reduce embodied emissions significantly.

> One estimate suggests that substituting one cubic metre of concrete with treated wood saves around 1.1 tonnes of CO2 in avoided emissions, on top of the 0.9 tonnes already locked into the wood itself. A 10% increase in the share of European homes built with timber structural components could reduce CO2 equivalent to roughly 25% of the reductions set out under the Kyoto Protocol.

This only holds if the wood is sustainably sourced and adequately protected so that the stored carbon is not released back into the atmosphere. Circular economy approaches, such as designing buildings for disassembly and reuse of modular components, can also extend the life of materials and delay the emissions associated with demolition and replacement.

---

### Where Does This Leave Us?

Cement and concrete are fundamental to how the world builds, and demand is still growing. The industry has set meaningful targets, and there are real technological options in development. But progress is slow, investment is limited, and the sector is not currently on a trajectory consistent with climate goals.

A stronger carbon price would help close the gap between conventional and low-carbon cement. Clearer procurement standards, particularly in public construction, could accelerate adoption of alternatives. And continuing investment in CCS and novel material science will be needed to address the portion of emissions that cannot be reduced through substitution alone.
