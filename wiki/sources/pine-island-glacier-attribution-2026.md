# Human activity has driven retreat of Antarctica's fastest melting glacier

**Source:** Phys.org press release based on King's College London / British Antarctic Survey research, 28 June 2026
**URL:** https://phys.org/news/2026-06-human-driven-retreat-antarctica-fastest.html
**Peer-reviewed study:** *The Cryosphere* (2026), led by Dr Alex Bradley (King's College London) and Mira Adhikari (British Antarctic Survey)

## What it is

A press release on the first study to **directly attribute the retreat of a major Antarctic outlet glacier to human activity**. The team modelled the **Pine Island Glacier** — which drains a large part of the West Antarctic Ice Sheet into the Amundsen Sea and is one of the biggest single contributors to global sea level rise — and compared scenarios with and without anthropogenic ocean warming.

## Key facts cited

- Greenhouse-gas-driven ocean warming **increased Pine Island Glacier's retreat by 18–20% since the 1940s**
- By 2015, simulations without human influence showed around **4 km less grounding-line retreat** — about one-fifth of the total observed retreat
- Pine Island began rapid retreat in the 1940s, driven by stronger intrusions of warm ocean water beneath its ice shelf
- Human-driven ocean warming in the region is thought to have begun in the 1960s and reinforced the retreat from then on
- The glacier may briefly stabilise later this century when it reaches a ridge in the bedrock beneath it
- If warming continues, the pause will be temporary and human influence is projected to be the **dominant driver of retreat in the 22nd century**

## Why it matters

Attribution studies are now routine for heatwaves, floods and mountain glaciers, but doing the same for an Antarctic outlet glacier has been technically much harder. This is the first time researchers have quantified how much of a major Antarctic ice loss event we can lay directly at the door of human emissions. The result reinforces that **the West Antarctic Ice Sheet is not a "natural" story anymore** — and that today's emissions will continue shaping Antarctic ice loss, and therefore sea level, **for centuries**.

## Notable quotes

- Dr Alex Bradley (lead author): *"Without sustained warming of the surrounding ocean since the mid-20th century, the glacier would not have retreated as far as it has."*
- Bradley: *"Ice sheets respond slowly. The impacts of today's emissions will continue to shape Antarctic ice loss for centuries."*
- Mira Adhikari (British Antarctic Survey): *"Our results add to growing evidence that human-driven climate change is likely affecting even the most remote regions of the planet."*

## Tone and angle

Sober, technical press release. The framing is "first quantitative attribution" rather than dramatic. The headline finding (18–20% extra retreat) is moderate in size but profound in implication: sea level rise from West Antarctica is no longer fully separable from human emissions.
