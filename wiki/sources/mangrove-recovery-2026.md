## What it covers
A study published in 2026 finding that global mangrove forests have been gaining more area than they have been losing since 2010, reversing decades of decline. The recovery is driven by natural regeneration once deforestation slows, stronger legal protections, and changed public attitudes following disasters like the 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami.

## Key facts
- From the 1980s to 2010, over 12,000 sq km of mangroves were cleared across Asia, Africa, and the Americas
- Since 2010, net losses have fallen to roughly 849 sq km — the world is now gaining more than it loses
- Mangroves store up to five times more carbon per hectare than land-based forests
- The proportion of closed-canopy (most carbon-dense) mangroves has grown by nearly 20% since the 1980s
- Indonesia and Myanmar have seen the most recovery; West and Central Africa (especially the Niger Delta) remain hotspots of destruction
- Tropical cyclones remain a major threat; oil pollution from pipelines is devastating in the Niger Delta
- New Landsat satellite mapping revealed far more regrowth than previous assessments captured
