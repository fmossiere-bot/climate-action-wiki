# Collapse of key ocean current may release billions of tonnes of carbon

**Source:** New Scientist, Alec Luhn, 13 April 2026 (updated 20 May 2026)
**Journal:** Nature Communications Earth & Environment — DOI: 10.1038/s43247-026-03427-w
**Lead researcher:** Da Nian, Potsdam Institute for Climate Impact Research; co-author Johan Rockström

## Summary

New computer modelling shows that if the AMOC (Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation) collapses, it would trigger a chain reaction in the Southern Ocean that releases massive quantities of carbon stored in deep water. **The total potential CO₂ release is up to 640 billion tonnes, which would heat the planet by an additional 0.2°C** on top of the direct climate impacts already projected from AMOC collapse.

The AMOC has already declined by about 15% and current CO₂ concentrations (430 ppm) are above the 350 ppm threshold above which the study finds AMOC collapse would be irreversible.

## Key facts

- AMOC is a system of ocean currents including the Gulf Stream. It brings warm, salty water to northern Europe, keeping winters mild.
- Greenland meltwater is diluting the AMOC, slowing the sinking process that drives the current.
- AMOC has already declined by ~15%. Collapse could occur anywhere from decades to centuries from now.
- At CO₂ above 350 ppm (current: 430 ppm), AMOC collapse would be irreversible once triggered.
- AMOC collapse would release convection of deep Southern Ocean water, which stores enormous amounts of carbon from dead plankton and atmospheric CO₂.
- Up to 640 billion tonnes of CO₂ could be released near Antarctica, adding 0.2°C of warming over 1,000+ years.
- Other known AMOC collapse impacts: Arctic cooling by 7°C; Antarctica warming by 6°C; disrupted monsoons in Africa and Asia; potential collapse of the East Antarctic Ice Sheet, causing metres of sea level rise.
- Rockström: "What matters is not the impact time, it is the commitment time. That commitment time may be within the next 25 to 50 years. It's literally now."
