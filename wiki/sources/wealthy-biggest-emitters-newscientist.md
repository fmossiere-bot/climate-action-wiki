# Wealthy people with environmental ideals are the biggest emitters

**Source:** New Scientist, by Alec Luhn

## Summary

A study by researchers at the University of Cambridge (Malte Dewies, Micha Kaiser) surveyed 5,000 people across Canada, France, Germany, Italy, the UK, and the US. It asked about income, wealth, education, and environmental values, then estimated a broad "ecological footprint" including diet, house size, vehicle use, and flying.

For most people, stronger environmental values correlated with lower footprints. But in the top 30% by socioeconomic status, those with the strongest environmental values had an even larger footprint than their high-income peers — mainly because they fly more frequently.

The researchers' conclusion: policy change matters more than attitude change. Targeting individual values and campaigns will not do the job of reducing emissions.

## Key facts

- Study covered 5,000 people across 6 countries: Canada, France, Germany, Italy, UK, US.
- In the top 30% by socioeconomic status: pro-environment people emitted more than their peers.
- Main reason: high-income environmentalists fly frequently — one of the most emissions-intensive individual activities.
- The term "carbon footprint" was popularised by BP to shift responsibility from producers to consumers.
- Researchers say price and policy interventions (aviation taxes, short-haul flight bans) are more effective than values campaigns.
- France banned short-haul flights in 2023, but loopholes meant no routes were actually cancelled.
- A note of nuance from Felix Creutzig (University of Sussex): environmentalism tends to correlate with openness and international social networks — which drives flying.
- Greta Thunberg's Fridays for Future pushed German climate legislation even though most demonstrators did not give up flying themselves. "Being a citizen with an active voice matters more than [consumer] behaviour."
