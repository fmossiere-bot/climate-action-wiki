# Source Summary: Air Conditioning in the UK — Green or Not?

## What this source covers

A Guardian explainer published during a UK heatwave in May 2026, examining the environmental trade-offs of air conditioning for homes. It covers types of cooling technology, energy costs, carbon emissions, the role of renewable energy pairing, and alternatives to mechanical cooling.

## Key facts and figures

- About **4 million UK homes** now have an air conditioner — double the figure from three years ago.
- The UK government's climate advisers (CCC) estimate **22% of British homes** would require active cooling with 2°C of global heating.
- Portable AC units (~1kW) running 8 hours/day produce **4.87kg CO2** in a summer — roughly equivalent to driving 18 miles.
- Round-the-clock portable AC costs **£5/day** vs **15–31p/day** for a fan.
- A wall-mounted unit (12,000 BTU) costs ~£750 plus ~£1,150 installation; portable equivalent costs ~£350.
- Air-to-air heat pumps produce about **3 units of cooling for every 1 unit of electricity** — far more efficient than straightforward AC.
- The UK government offers a **£2,500 grant** for replacing a fossil-fuel heating system with an air-to-air heat pump.
- A [2021 Lancet study](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanplh/article/PIIS2542-5196(21)00136-4/fulltext) found electric fans can be comparable to AC for preventing heat stress on hot days (2007–2019 data).

## Main arguments

- Straight grid-powered AC is not "green" until the grid is fully renewable.
- Pairing AC with solar panels or charging a home battery from renewable surpluses is the most responsible approach.
- Portable units are the least efficient type; air-to-air heat pumps (which can also heat in winter) are the best environmental choice.
- Passive cooling first — window shading, night ventilation, green roofs, insulation — can reduce or eliminate the need for active cooling.
- People should not feel guilty for using AC as a last resort during dangerous heat events; public health benefits are real.
