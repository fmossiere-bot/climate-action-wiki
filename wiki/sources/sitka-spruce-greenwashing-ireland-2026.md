# Source Summary: Sitka Spruce Industry Book — Greenwashing in Irish Schools

## What this source covers

An Irish Times article from April 2026 reporting on a children's book, *Sitka Spruce – the Amazing Timber Tree*, distributed to Irish primary schools. The book was produced by commercial forestry interests and contains a foreword by then-minister Michael Healy-Rae. The Irish Wildlife Trust (IWT) called for its removal, citing misleading and one-sided industry messaging presented as environmental education.

## Key facts

- The book was originally produced by **Morgan Sindall Construction** in the UK and adapted for Ireland.
- The Irish version is funded by the **Society of Irish Foresters**, the **Irish Timber Council**, and **SEEFA** (Social, Economic, Environmental Forestry Association).
- Foreword written by **Michael Healy-Rae**, recently resigned minister of state for forestry.
- The book depicts Sitka spruce plantations as ecologically rich, with wildlife present and trees removed individually while the "woodland flourishes."
- In reality, Sitka spruce plantations are widely described as **ecological dead zones**: they squeeze out most other plants, support few animals, acidify soils and waterways, and are routinely clear-felled.
- The book also promotes Sitka as a carbon sink — disputed in many Irish contexts where it is planted on **bogs that would store far more carbon if left intact**.
- The **Department of Education** distanced itself from the book, stating it has no role in approving or endorsing materials distributed directly to schools.
- The **IWT** called for immediate removal and revision of the book's messaging.
- The **Society of Irish Foresters** defended the book, arguing criticisms apply to older monoculture plantations and that new standards require 20% broadleaves and up to 15% biodiversity areas.

## Main arguments

- This is a clear example of **greenwashing in education**: commercially motivated messaging presented to children as environmental fact.
- The lack of any safeguard preventing industry actors from distributing promotional material directly to Irish schools is a systemic gap.
- The controversy highlights the broader tension in Irish forestry between **commercial Sitka plantations** and **biodiversity/native woodland** goals.
