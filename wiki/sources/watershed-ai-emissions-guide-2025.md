# Source: AI Emissions — A Practical Guide for Corporate Sustainability Leaders

**Original source:** Watershed (Eric Nevalsky, Sustainability Advisor; Steven Watson, PM Product Footprints), 2025
**URL:** https://watershed.com

## What this source covers

A 42-slide practical guide from Watershed aimed at corporate sustainability leaders trying to understand, measure, and reduce the emissions from their organisation's use of AI. Covers where AI emissions come from (GPU electricity, not chip manufacturing), four macro forces shaping AI's climate impact, how to evaluate AI vendors on sustainability, how to measure AI emissions despite the lack of standards, and how AI can itself accelerate decarbonisation.

## Key claims

- **Inference (using AI models) accounts for over 90%** of total lifecycle AI emissions; training is less than 10%.
- AI emissions break down as: ~80.9% GPU electricity, ~17.8% other data centre electricity, ~1.3% GPU manufacturing.
- A single LLM query uses ~0.24 Wh — roughly **0.1–0.3 g CO₂e** on the average US grid. A heavy user running 100 queries/day generates ~3 kg CO₂e/year — less than half a kilogram of beef.
- Google's Gemini saw a **33× energy reduction per query** from 2024–2025, but total AI energy demand is projected to **triple by 2030** — a classic Jevons Paradox.
- In 2025, 1,891 energy projects (266 GW) were cancelled in the US in a single year; 93% were clean energy.
- Goldman Sachs projects **60% of new data centre power this decade will come from natural gas**, not renewables.
- Choosing a lower-carbon data centre region can **reduce operational AI emissions by 30–80% instantly**.
- No globally accepted standard exists for measuring AI emissions; key data points are region/location (critical), model ID (critical), and token count (medium-high utility).
