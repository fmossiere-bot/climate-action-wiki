# Ariana copper and zinc mine — BankTrack Dodgy Deal dossier

**Source:** BankTrack, undated project dossier (accessed July 2026)
**URL:** https://www.banktrack.org/project/ariana_copper_and_zinc_mine

## What it is

A **BankTrack "Dodgy Deal" project dossier** on the Ariana mine, an underground copper and zinc project in Marcapomacocha district, Junín region, in the central Peruvian Andes. BankTrack collates the project background, the water and seismic risks flagged by Lima's water utility SEDAPAL, the legal history, and the financing that has enabled the project to advance. It is currently owned by the Peruvian company **Alpayana**, which acquired it from Southern Peaks in 2025.

## Key facts cited

- The mine sits in the **Marcapomacocha water system**, which supplies around **60% of Lima and Callao's dry-season water** for over 10 million people
- The system includes lagoons, canals, reservoirs, high-altitude wetlands (bofedales/peat bogs) and the **1960s Cuevas–Milloc Trans-Andean Tunnel** that pipes water west across the Andes
- Peru's water utility **SEDAPAL** identified four adverse impact pathways: reduced water collection due to interrupted groundwater flows; heavy metals contamination; vibration damage to the Trans-Andean Tunnel; and possible collapse of the tailings dam (located just 100 metres from the tunnel)
- The project's Environmental Impact Assessment was approved in 2016 and was due to enter operation in 2019; a constitutional appeal by Lima citizens has blocked operations ever since
- The **Constitutional Chamber of Lima ruled in 2025** that the project poses a certain and imminent threat to the fundamental right to drinking water; instead of nullifying the EIA, it ordered a Complementary EIA
- Alpayana acquired the project in 2025 for **US$200 million** (advised by LXG Capital) — a signal it intends to proceed
- **Banco Santander Perú S.A. and Banco Santander S.A.** provided a **US$100 million refinancing facility** to Alpayana in August 2025
- Peru has recorded **18 tailings dam ruptures since 1952**, mostly triggered by seismic activity — and the Ariana site is in a seismic zone

## Why it matters

The Ariana story is a concrete case of **bank finance colliding with local water rights**. It shows how a project can pass an environmental impact assessment yet still be judged by a constitutional court to threaten a fundamental right. It also shows how European banks — Santander in this case — remain financially exposed to controversial extractive projects on the other side of the world, and how the "critical minerals for the energy transition" framing can be used to justify projects with severe local consequences.
