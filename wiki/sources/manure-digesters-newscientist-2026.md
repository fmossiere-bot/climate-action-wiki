# Source: Are Manure Digesters a Real Solution to Dairy Farm Emissions?

**Publication:** New Scientist, Alec Luhn, 7 April 2026 (updated 20 May 2026)
**Journal reference:** Environmental Research Letters, DOI: 10.1088/1748-9326/ae4fe4

## Summary

A reported analysis of the research evidence on anaerobic digesters for dairy manure. The technology can cut methane from manure storage by up to 91%, but a California study of 98 dairies found that leaks, construction disruptions, and perverse financial incentives complicate the picture significantly.

## Key points

- Agriculture: ~1/3 of human-caused emissions; in the US, ~1/3 of agricultural emissions come from cattle belching, 14% from manure
- >17,000 digesters installed in the EU; ~400 each in the US and UK; millions in China (mostly small brick digesters)
- Manure through a digester emits **91% less methane** during storage — normally the headline figure
- California study (98 dairies): installing digesters cut point-source methane from 91 kg/h to 68 kg/h at 2/3 of dairies
- BUT: some leaks exceeded **1,000 kg/h** — turning some digesters into net emitters; emissions also briefly spiked during construction
- California notification programme (2023): 20% of leaking farms fixed leaks after notification
- Bulk of research: digesters can cut manure emissions by roughly **half** overall
- California Low Carbon Fuel Standard: grants $389m to digester construction + issues sellable credits; a study found receiving incentives increased dairy **herd size by 860 cows on average** (perverse incentive)
- Digesters accelerate ammonia formation from manure, raising concerns of "pollution swapping"
- Burning off biogas that cannot be sold can produce sulphur dioxide, a respiratory hazard
- Rebecca Larson (U. Wisconsin-Madison): "one of the highest performing mitigation measures we have" for livestock
- Brent Kim (Johns Hopkins): "taxpayer dollars are being used to inflate the value of manure"
