# 'Slough is like an experiment': Europe's largest datacentre hub leaves town sweltering

**Source:** The Guardian, Aisha Down, 26 June 2026
**URL:** https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2026/jun/26/slough-is-like-an-experiment-europes-largest-datacentre-hub-leaves-town-sweltering

## What it is

A feature on **Slough**, a Berkshire town 16 km west of Heathrow that has become **Europe's largest datacentre hub** — an estimated 30 to 40 huge facilities, hosting workloads for Amazon, Google, Oracle and Microsoft on a roughly **1 gigawatt** campus. The article asks whether the cluster is contributing to local heat during the UK's June 2026 heatwave, drawing on resident accounts and **new Cambridge research** suggesting datacentres create a measurable **urban heat island effect**.

## Key facts cited

- Slough hosts an estimated **30–40 large datacentres**, roughly **1 GW** of capacity in total
- A preprint from Cambridge (Andrea Marinoni et al., arXiv 2603.20897) finds datacentres push up local temperatures by **2°C on average and up to 9°C** in their immediate vicinity
- The study controls for urbanisation and the climate crisis and uses decades of satellite data
- Marinoni's data covers an older generation of datacentres (~100 MW max); Slough is roughly 10× larger and "almost like an experiment by itself"
- Operators in Slough: **Equinix and Digital Realty**, serving big tech tenants
- 24 June 2026 weather: 36.7°C at the station nearest the tech park, several degrees hotter than other Slough stations
- 2022 resident anecdote: 45°C in a Slough car park while it was 39°C in nearby Windsor
- UK government has proposed using **waste heat from datacentres to warm thousands of homes**

## Why it matters

If the Cambridge finding holds at scale, the UK's hyperscale buildout is not just an electricity and emissions problem; it is also a **local adaptation problem**. Communities near datacentre clusters — often less affluent — bear extra heat exposure on top of climate-driven heatwaves, while the benefits (jobs, tax) accrue more widely. The piece pairs with previous reporting on UK datacentres' grid demand and gas connections, deepening the case for siting standards and waste-heat recovery.

## Tone and angle

Combines on-the-ground residents' voices (a shop manager, an industrial-park worker, lifelong Slough residents) with measured caveats from the lead researcher. The article does not claim datacentres alone are responsible for Slough's heat; it presents the new evidence as a developing strand alongside climate change and concrete-driven urban heat.
