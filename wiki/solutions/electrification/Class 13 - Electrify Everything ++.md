## Cleaning up Buildings

#### Buildings account for a whopping 40% of all energy-related GHG
Source: https://rmi.org/our-work/buildings/
![[Screenshot 2023-10-17 at 11.18.59.png]]![[Screenshot 2023-10-17 at 11.19.04.png]]
Building emissions come from:

- **“Operational carbon emissions,”** which is just the day-to-day energy use for lighting, heating, and cooling. 
- **“Embodied carbon,”** or the emissions created through manufacturing building materials, transporting materials to construction sites, and the actual construction process.

#### Decarbonizing buildings can have favorable economics
We know the solutions:
_They involve energy efficiency measures and the deployment of decarbonized heating technologies (notably electric). More modern solutions have also recently emerged, among which are active energy efficiency (enabled by digital controls), smart electric heat pumps, and onsite solar (and storage)._

There is huge opportunity in building retrofit which will result in much less need for heat power. There is also room in the embodied carbon through electrified transportation, advancement in better Cement production processes or substitution for wood ertc ..

> _By 2030, we estimate that 60-70% of carbon emissions could be abated in the segment, generating savings around 15-50% on annual energy spend, with paybacks below 10 years (for retrofits) and an additional cost for new construction below 4%._

## Climat Solutions for industry
Class: https://textbook.terra.do/class/m-energy2-5
#### Heavy indsutry contributes to 24% of global GHG
It is a hard sector to decarbonize

> The industrial sector is very heterogeneous, as Rebecca Dell, who runs the industry program at ClimateWorks, [tells energy journalist Dave Roberts](https://www.volts.wtf/p/volts-podcast-rebecca-dell-on-decarbonizing#details)—but **two-thirds of emissions from industry are associated with four sectors**: steel, cement, plastics, and fertilizers. The remaining third is more diverse but also easier to decarbonize

These four sectors—steel, cement, plastics, and fertilizers—dominate GHG emissions because we make them in immense quantities globally. The first step in all these processes involves digging some raw material out of the ground and transforming it into a useful molecule; this is very, very energy-intensive compared to all the steps that follow.

Broadly, the typology of decarbonization solutions across industries are:   

- Improving materials efficiency: using less in the way of raw materials AND final products
- Carbon capture and storage
- Green hydrogen
- Direct electrification
- New types of storage, like thermal heat batteries
- Bioenergy—this will always be niche at best as we simply do not have enough sustainable biomass.

### About Fertilizers
#Fertilizer

The chemical industry is very diverse, with 100,000 different products. yet  80% of its ghg com from 20 products. 

> The largest are Nitrogen fertilizers and Plastic

#### Nitrogen fertilizers - upon which our food security relies on, account for some 5% of global emissions - more than aviation and shipping combined

Also, research found that 2/3 of fertilizer emissions take place after their deployment in droplands. 

Synthetic nitrogen fertilizers are made using hydrogen (made from fossil fuels)

> _"The rise in synthetic fertilizer use has been an environmental disaster in many regions of the world, contributing to water pollution and [associated health impacts](https://www.mdpi.com/1660-4601/15/7/1557), and [poor air quality and premature deaths](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41893-019-0261-y.epdf?shared_access_token=JQ8r_eSu5RRl7Zu-l1Ff-NRgN0jAjWel9jnR3ZoTv0Obt0HKn7VNHFalevA4bh2YH67zd3ud4nQsopjtEKhR61xPNkxd4kihNLpprrqSgUIyw7Vecb7fC84rgvhMTBIEgKINTeB2eAIWw1EXqT5JoQ%3D%3D). Fertilizer runoff and associated pollution has long been associated with the loss of oxygen and water life known as hypoxia, or the [Dead Zone, in the Gulf of Mexico](https://www.ncei.noaa.gov/maps/hypoxia/) and other waterways. [There is also new evidence](https://civileats.com/2021/07/21/excess-fertilizer-causes-a-new-challenge-low-crop-yields-during-drought/) that heavy fertilizer use can intensify the effects of drought on agriculture land."_


#### The Cambridge study above finds that by 2050, fertilizer emissions could be reduced by [80 percent](https://pulitzercenter.org/stories/nitrogen-fertilizer-releases-greenhouse-gases-throughout-its-life-cycle) using technology we already have, partly by decarbonizing the production process.
Source: [Research](The Cambridge study above finds that by 2050, fertilizer emissions could be reduced by [80 percent](https://pulitzercenter.org/stories/nitrogen-fertilizer-releases-greenhouse-gases-throughout-its-life-cycle) using technology we already have, partly by decarbonizing the production process.)

>Other solutions involve replacing synthetic fertilizers with [biochar-based fertilizers](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s42773-022-00160-3#Sec21) and even fertilizers made from, yes, [your excrement](https://blog.frontiersin.org/2023/01/16/frontiers-environmental-science-recycled-fertilizers-from-human-urine-feces/). And finally, there are food system approaches that eliminate synthetic fertilizers entirely, like regenerative agriculture. We cover these agricultural solutions in our class on food systems.

### About Plastic
Also See [[FACTS about Plastic]]
#Plastic 

Plastics are made from fossil fuel feedstock and are tremendously dirty—plastics will [outpace coal emissions](https://www.reuters.com/business/cop/plastics-outpace-coals-greenhouse-gas-emissions-by-2030-report-2021-10-21/) by 2030. For all the hype about recycling over the last few decades, only a fraction of plastics are _collected_ for recycling, and only a fraction of _that_ is actually recycled, as illustrated here. The recycling process itself also downgrades the plastic, yielding poorer quality materials.

 #### For all the hype about recycling over the last few decades, only a fraction of plastics are _collected_ for recycling, and only a fraction of _that_ is actually recycled
 

Left unchecked, a [2023 report](https://backtoblueinitiative.com/#signup-success) found plastic use could nearly double by 2050. And bioplastics are _not_ a meaningful alternative: [Research in 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2590332220303055#:~:text=which%20is%20larger%20than%20the%20total%20area%20of%20France) calculated the land it would take for bioplastics to replace all plastic packaging, and found you’d need an area bigger than France and "60% more than the EU's annual freshwater withdrawal."

 #### We need to use less, this is a large hope
 Beyond that, a [UN Report](https://www.unep.org/resources/turning-off-tap-end-plastic-pollution-create-circular-economy) on plastics outlines how we could reduce plastic pollution by 80 percent by increasing reuse, dramatically increasing recycling, and finally reorienting and diversifying.

And _again_, plastics, create environmental problems way beyond climate change. [Great garbage patches](https://marinedebris.noaa.gov/info/patch.html) keep getting greater in our oceans—and choking key rivers in places like [Thailand](https://www.thethirdpole.net/en/pollution/plastic-river-following-waste-choking-chao-phraya/). Also, some plastics are [much "dirtier"](https://rmi.org/clean-energy-201-all-plastics-arent-created-equal/) in terms of emissions than others.  
  

There is also a dream of [pulling carbon out of the air](https://www.sciencenews.org/article/plastic-carbon-dioxide-capture-recycling-climate-change) as an alternative means of making plastics—but in terms of cost and needed energy, doing so at scale may again be unrealistic. And maybe [dead flies could be a solution](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2023/aug/14/dead-flies-biodegradable-plastic-scientists), but we’re not holding out for this one.

Source: https://www.oecd.org/environment/plastic-pollution-is-growing-relentlessly-as-waste-management-and-recycling-fall-short.htm

Finally, while it's a good idea to minimize single-use plastics, keep in mind alternatives like reusable tote bags also have an impact—meaning they have to be [reused many times](https://ourworldindata.org/grapher/grocery-bag-comparisons-ghg) in order to actually _reduce_ emissions.
