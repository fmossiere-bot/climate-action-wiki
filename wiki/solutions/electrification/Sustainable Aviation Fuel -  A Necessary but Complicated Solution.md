Aviation is one of the hardest sectors to decarbonise. Electric planes remain far from commercial viability at scale, and hydrogen is still years away from widespread adoption. In the meantime, Sustainable Aviation Fuel, known as SAF, has emerged as the most realistic near-term option for reducing emissions from flying. It is promising, but it comes with real limitations that are worth understanding clearly.

---

### Key Numbers at a Glance

- Aviation accounts for around **2.5% of global CO2 emissions**, a figure expected to triple by 2050
- SAF can reduce lifecycle greenhouse gas emissions by up to **70% compared to conventional jet fuel**
- SAF reduces particulate matter emissions at cruising altitude by **50 to 97%**
- Today SAF makes up less than **1% of global aviation fuel**
- The EU required **2% SAF by 2025**, rising to **70% by 2050**
- The UK mandate required **2% by 2025**, rising to **22% by 2040**
- SAF currently costs roughly **double** the price of conventional jet fuel

---

### What is SAF?

SAF is a fuel that can power aircraft with similar properties to conventional jet fuel, but with a significantly smaller carbon footprint. Crucially, it is a drop-in fuel, meaning it can be blended with conventional jet fuel and used in existing aircraft engines without modification. Most current use involves a blend of around 50% SAF and 50% traditional fuel, though the goal is to reach 100% SAF over time.

The term SAF covers two broad categories. The first is **biologically-derived fuels, made from organic feedstocks** such as used cooking oil, agricultural waste, forestry residues, algae, municipal solid waste and dedicated energy crops. The second is **synthetic fuels, also called e-fuels**, electrofuels or power-to-liquid fuels, which are produced from renewable electricity, water and captured CO2 rather than from biological material.

---

### How is SAF Produced?

There are currently four main certified production pathways for SAF. The most common today is HEFA (Hydroprocessed Esters and Fatty Acids), which uses fats, oils and greases such as used cooking oil. Advanced biofuels use agricultural and forestry residues. Alcohol-to-jet processes convert ethanol into jet fuel. And the power-to-liquid pathway produces synthetic fuel from green hydrogen and captured CO2.

An important distinction is where the climate benefit actually comes from. The combustion of SAF produces similar emissions to conventional jet fuel at the tailpipe. The gain comes from the production process. A lifecycle analysis, which accounts for feedstock, production, transport and combustion, shows significant overall reductions compared to fossil-based fuel. Additionally, SAF reduces particulate matter emissions at cruising altitude by 50 to 97%, which also improves local air quality around airports.

One emerging and genuinely exciting development is solar SAF. Swiss company Synhelion has opened its first production facility in Germany and is building a 1,000-tonne capacity plant in Spain, expected to come online in 2027. The process uses concentrated solar heat at temperatures above 1,500 degrees Celsius to drive a thermochemical reaction that produces syngas, a mixture of hydrogen and CO, which is then converted into fuel. No electricity conversion is needed. It is the first solar fuel facility of its kind in the world.

---

### Why SAF Matters for Aviation

If global commercial aviation were a country, it would rank as the sixth largest emitter in the world, sitting between Japan and Germany. Emissions from the sector are projected to triple by 2050 as demand for air travel continues to grow, particularly in Asia.

Unlike cars or trucks, planes cannot simply be electrified with current technology. Battery energy density remains far too low for long-haul flights. Green hydrogen is a longer-term option being explored for shorter routes, but it requires entirely new aircraft and fuelling infrastructure. SAF, by contrast, works with the existing fleet and existing airport infrastructure, which makes it the only scalable option available in the near term.

---

### Regulatory Targets

Both the EU and the UK have introduced binding mandates to drive SAF adoption.

> The EU's ReFuelEU Aviation regulation requires 2% SAF at EU airports by 2025, rising to 6% in 2030, 20% in 2035 and 70% by 2050. Within this, there is a specific sub-mandate for synthetic fuels: 1.2% by 2030, rising to 35% by 2050. Airlines benefit from the EU Emissions Trading Scheme, under which SAF emissions are zero-rated, and SAF allowances allow airlines to reclaim the price difference between SAF and conventional fuel from 2024 to 2030.

> The UK's SAF mandate came into effect on 1 January 2025, requiring 2% of total jet fuel demand to be met by SAF in 2025, increasing to 10% in 2030 and 22% by 2040.

### The Controversies

SAF is not without serious problems, and it is important to look at these honestly.

**Land use and food competition**

The biggest concern surrounds feedstocks. When biofuels are produced from crops that require agricultural land, they can compete directly with food production, drive up food prices and, in the worst cases, incentivise deforestation to create new farmland. Converting land with high carbon stocks such as rainforests or peatlands to grow biofuel crops can release hundreds of times more CO2 than the fuel subsequently produced will ever save. This is why the EU has capped and is phasing out biofuels from food and feed crops under its renewable energy directives.

**The corn ethanol problem**

In the United States, corn ethanol has become a central part of the SAF conversation, with airlines signing deals with corn producers and ethanol manufacturers lobbying for clean energy tax credits. The problem is that nearly 40% of America's corn crop already goes to ethanol production, up from 10% in the mid-2000s. Reaching the US government's target of 35 billion gallons of SAF per year by mid-century would require doubling that production. This in turn would massively increase the use of groundwater for irrigation, putting already stressed aquifers at serious risk. Scientific studies have repeatedly questioned whether corn ethanol is genuinely more climate-friendly than fossil fuel once all the inputs are accounted for.

**Scale and cost**

Even with more sustainable feedstocks such as agricultural residues, cooking oil and algae, producing enough SAF to meet aviation's growing demand is a significant challenge. Supply is constrained, costs are roughly double those of conventional fuel, and reaching the 2050 targets would require a scale of production that does not yet exist. The 2030 targets appear achievable, but the path to 70% SAF by 2050 involves either a very large expansion of biomass production, with all the land use risks that implies, or a major scale-up of synthetic fuel production, which requires large quantities of green hydrogen and renewable electricity that are also needed elsewhere in the energy transition.

---

### The Bottom Line

SAF is a genuine and necessary part of the aviation decarbonisation story. It works in existing aircraft, reduces lifecycle emissions significantly and cuts harmful particulate pollution around airports. The regulatory frameworks in the EU and UK are pushing investment in the right direction.

But it is not a simple solution. The feedstock choices matter enormously, and not all SAF is equally sustainable. Corn-based ethanol, for example, carries real risks for water and land systems. The most promising long-term pathways, particularly synthetic fuels and solar-based production, are still at very early commercial stages.

SAF buys time for aviation while longer-term solutions develop. It should be treated as exactly that: a transition tool, not a destination.

### New Feedstock Pathway: Food Waste as Jet Fuel

A 2026 study from the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign, published in *Nature Sustainability*, has demonstrated a practical new pathway for turning food waste into jet-grade SAF. The process uses **hydrothermal liquefaction (HTL)**, which applies high heat and pressure to wet organic waste to produce a crude biocrude oil, mimicking the natural formation of crude oil but in a fraction of the time.

What makes this research notable is its emphasis on simplicity and cost. Rather than using heavy catalytic refining, the Illinois team focused on distillation, a more familiar and economical industrial process. The resulting fuel is lower in quality than fully refined SAF and needs to be blended with conventional jet fuel, but it meets ASTM and FAA jet fuel standards at a 50-50 blend. Even 10% or 20% blends would be useful given current SAF supply constraints.

Lead researcher Prof Yuanhui Zhang compared the approach to biodiesel blending for cars: "It would be very difficult to produce enough SAF to meet industry needs, so it makes sense to take a biodiesel approach with a percentage blend."

The lifecycle analysis found that both baseline and improved treatment scenarios achieve negative carbon emissions overall. The biggest practical hurdle remains logistics: collecting food waste from landfills and wastewater treatment plants at scale. HTL can use treated wastewater as feedstock, which opens a route through existing municipal waste infrastructure. A toxic byproduct (HTL aqueous phase) can be treated electrochemically to recover nutrients, though this currently increases costs significantly.

The research is still at small laboratory scale, with litre-scale fuel production being the next step before engine testing.
