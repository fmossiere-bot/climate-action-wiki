

Replace Short Car Journeys (under 2 miles) with Active Transport: Walking, Cycling etc.

Have you ever stopped to think about how many short car journeys you make in a typical week? You might be surprised to learn that studies show around 60% of car trips in the UK are actually under 2 miles [[3](https://www.bbc.com/future/article/20200317-climate-change-cut-carbon-emissions-from-your-commute)] 

This guide is here to empower you to become more "Human Powered" by replacing short car journeys with active transport options like walking and cycling. So, buckle up (metaphorically, of course!) and get ready to discover how active travel can be good for you, your wallet, and the planet.

## A Cleaner form of Locomotion

### Reducing Emissions and Oil Consumption

Transport is the largest emitting sector of GHG emissions, producing 24% of the UK’s total emissions in 2020 [[2](https://www.gov.uk/government/statistics/transport-and-environment-statistics-2022/transport-and-environment-statistics-2022)]. These emissions can be broken down into three main types:

- direct emissions: emissions produced by the vehicle itself
    
- indirect emissions: emissions produced by the extraction, refining, and transportation of the fuel used to power the vehicle. For electric vehicles, this includes the generation and transmission of electricity
    
- indirect effects: complex effects produced by greenhouse gases interacting with the atmosphere, for example, contrails produced by planes in the atmosphere, which reflect sunlight
    

  

The average diesel vehicle in the UK produced 169.8 g of CO2 per kilometre driven in 2023 [[1](https://www.statista.com/statistics/1233337/carbon-footprint-of-travel-per-kilometer-by-mode-of-transport-uk/#:~:text=After%20flights%2C%20cars%20typically%20had,grams%20of%20CO%E2%82%82e%20per%20km.)]. As cars grow larger, these emissions will only grow.  A decade ago SUVs made up 17% of global yearly car sales, but now account for 39%, and those larger cars emit up to 85% more emissions than a small vehicle [[3](https://www.bbc.com/future/article/20200317-climate-change-cut-carbon-emissions-from-your-commute)]

Environmental Champion: Transport is a major contributor to greenhouse gas emissions and air pollution (accounting for roughly 29% of greenhouse gas emissions in the United States according to the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA)).  
  
Did you know that in the US, if people walked or cycled for just half of their car trips under a mile, it could save an estimated $575 million annually in fuel costs alone?

### Connecting with Community and Environment

Reducing noise from car journeys can also have a huge environmental impact for both wildlife and humans. Noisy environments have been linked to decreased insect health, lower bird birthrates, loss of wildlife populations, and more [[6](https://education.nationalgeographic.org/resource/noise-pollution/)]. Beyond wildlife, noisy roads and airports are linked to stress, as well as impairments in memory, attention level, and reading skill in children [[6](https://education.nationalgeographic.org/resource/noise-pollution/)]. 

Active travel isn't just about getting from A to B; it's also a chance to reconnect with your surroundings. You might discover hidden gems on your route, bump into friendly faces, or simply enjoy the sights and sounds of your neighbourhood.  Walking can help improve your local economy by promoting downtown development and supporting small businesses. People who walk to the high street spend 40% more than those who drive [[8](https://www.gojauntly.com/blog/7-reasons-why-walking-will-save-the-world)].

A generation ago, 70% of British children walked to school, but now less than half do, despite studies showing children who walk or bike to school tend to be more focused and are less likely to be overweight. [[3](https://www.bbc.com/future/article/20200317-climate-change-cut-carbon-emissions-from-your-commute)] When we walk, we connect with nature, which has been proven to increase pro-social and pro-environmental behaviours, including developing kindness and empathy [[10](https://findingnature.org.uk/2020/07/08/pro-nature-behaviours/)]. Yet numbers show we rarely take time to connect with nature:

- Around 80% of people reported that they rarely or never watched wildlife, smelled wildflowers or drew/photographed nature.
    
- 62% of people rarely or never listened to bird song or took a moment to notice butterflies or bees
    

### Tips

- Look for local walking paths, gardens, or parks in your city. Utilise local resources like  [London's Great Green Routes](https://www.cprelondon.org.uk/discover-londons-countryside-2/greatgreenroutes/) that help you find green walking and biking trails.
- Look for the little joys in your journey. Stop and smell the flowers or admire the view. Take a picture of your favourite trail and share it. Spending a few minutes to reconnect with nature can re energise you for the rest of the day

## Big Savings for you and your community

### The Cost of Car infrastructure

In the Netherlands 26% of journeys are made by bike, followed by Denmark at 18% and 10% in Germany  [[3](https://www.bbc.com/future/article/20200317-climate-change-cut-carbon-emissions-from-your-commute)]. This translates into big savings, as a study in Copenhagen found that the individual cost of driving was 6x greater than biking [[4](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2015/05/150512104023.htm)]. And for society, the savings are even bigger, with if we only look at costs/benefits for society, one kilometre by car costs EUR 0.15, whereas society earns EUR 0.16 on every kilometre cycled.

  

Cars also degrade infrastructure much faster than biking or walking. The load (and therefore the damage) a vehicle imposes on a road follows the “fourth-power law” – in simple terms, a vehicle weighing twice as much per axle will do 16 times more damage [[5](https://www.cyclehamont.ca/cyclists-vs-drivers-who-pays-their-fair-share/)]. This means the more vehicles on the road and the larger they are, the more cities have to pay for road maintenance. The difference is striking when building as well- one study in CA calculated that a kilometre of road costs 13x more than a kilometre of biking infrastructure [[5](https://www.cyclehamont.ca/cyclists-vs-drivers-who-pays-their-fair-share/)].

it would take 10,000 cyclists to do as much damage to a roadway as a single car does in passing.

### Saving your wallet and Health

Financially Savvy:  Ditching the car for short trips means saying goodbye to petrol costs, parking fees, and even potential car maintenance expenses. Studies have shown that people who cycle regularly can save hundreds of pounds a year. 

A 2017 study found that if short journeys (less than five miles) currently taken by car were switched to bike, the average person in Scotland could save nearly £2,000 a year – the equivalent to a nearly 9% pay rise in take-home pay [[7](https://www.sustrans.org.uk/our-blog/get-active/2019/spring-and-summer/why-every-day-should-be-a-cycle-to-work-day/)]. Time is money too- motorists spend nearly five days a year stuck in traffic. As congestion only increases, switching to foot or bike for short journeys can often be faster than driving.

  

Health Hero:  Studies suggest that just 30 minutes of moderate-intensity exercise most days of the week can significantly reduce your risk of heart disease, the leading cause of death in the United States. Walking and cycling are perfect ways to achieve this. They strengthen your heart, improve blood circulation, and can help lower blood pressure.

The benefits extend beyond your heart. Regular physical activity can also help you manage your weight, reducing your risk of type 2 diabetes by up to 58%. It strengthens your muscles and bones, improving mobility and reducing your risk of falls, especially important as we age. Furthermore, air pollution has been linked to raised  cancer and dementia risks [[9](https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/government/uploads/system/uploads/attachment_data/file/1090376/COMEAP-dementia-report-2022.pdf)]

University of Glasgow research published in the British Medical Journal in 2017 found people who commuted by bike had a 41% lower risk of premature death, a 45% lower risk of developing cancer and a 46% lower risk of developing heart disease.  [[7](https://www.sustrans.org.uk/our-blog/get-active/2019/spring-and-summer/why-every-day-should-be-a-cycle-to-work-day/)]

According to the Royal College of Physicians up to 40,000 early deaths are attributable to air pollution each year in the UK – only smoking contributes to more early deaths.  [[7](https://www.sustrans.org.uk/our-blog/get-active/2019/spring-and-summer/why-every-day-should-be-a-cycle-to-work-day/)]

### Tips

- Work your way up. Start small with your closest and most frequent journeys, such taking the kids to school, visiting the bakery, or heading to the pub with friends. As you start walking more frequently, your stamina will increase
- Connect with neighbours and friends to discover the best routes and resources. Spread your own knowledge and advocate for pedestrian and biking friendly infrastructure in your HOA, neighbourhood, city, or regional governance. There are many groups advocating [in the UK](https://www.cyclinguk.org/cycle-campaigning) and elsewhere.


# Actions

### Week 1: Learning and Preparing

This week is about getting informed and setting yourself up for success.

- Planning Your Routes: Think about journeys that you could do without using the car. Use a map, app or website that highlights pedestrian and cycling paths. Look for quieter streets with good signage and consider potential obstacles like busy junctions.  
- Safety First: Brush up on your cycling (or walking!) safety skills. Familiarise yourself with local traffic laws and cycling etiquette. Here are some helpful resources:
- Transport for London (TfL) Cycling Safety: [https://tfl.gov.uk/corporate/safety-and-security/road-safety/cycle-safety](https://tfl.gov.uk/corporate/safety-and-security/road-safety/cycle-safety)
- Cycling Tips in Madrid: [https://www-madrid-es.translate.goog/portales/munimadrid/es/Inicio/Movilidad-y-transportes/Oficina-de-la-bici/Consejos-y-recomendaciones/Consejos-para-circular-en-bicicleta-por-la-ciudad/?vgnextfmt=default&vgnextoid=ca94292dc3f0b210VgnVCM2000000c205a0aRCRD&vgnextchannel=d74319927c278210VgnVCM2000000c205a0aRCRD&_x_tr_sl=es&_x_tr_tl=en&_x_tr_hl=en&_x_tr_pto=sc](https://www-madrid-es.translate.goog/portales/munimadrid/es/Inicio/Movilidad-y-transportes/Oficina-de-la-bici/Consejos-y-recomendaciones/Consejos-para-circular-en-bicicleta-por-la-ciudad/?vgnextfmt=default&vgnextoid=ca94292dc3f0b210VgnVCM2000000c205a0aRCRD&vgnextchannel=d74319927c278210VgnVCM2000000c205a0aRCRD&_x_tr_sl=es&_x_tr_tl=en&_x_tr_hl=en&_x_tr_pto=sc) 
- Baby Steps: This is about creating new sustainable habits, so start small and build up. Walk 10 minutes to the local shop instead of driving. You might find that a journey is not as essential as you first thought, and you can save some money on unwanted purchases too.  
- Grease The Wheels: If you plan to cycle, and haven’t done it for a while. Before spending money on a new bike, try out one of the rental bike schemes available. It’s a cheap and easy way to ease back in.  

## Week 2: Taking Action and Exploring Further

Week two is all about putting your learning into practice and expanding your horizons. 

- Explore Further: Once you're comfortable with your initial route, venture a little further afield. Maybe try cycling to a park you haven't visited in a while, or walk to meet a friend for coffee in a different neighbourhood.
- Advanced Techniques: If you're cycling, you might want to learn some basic bike maintenance skills – how to fix a flat tire, adjust your brakes, etc. There are plenty of online tutorials and workshops available.  
- Combine and Conquer: Consider integrating active travel with other modes of transport. For example, you could cycle to the train station and then take the train for a longer journey. This allows you to extend your reach while still reaping the benefits of active travel
  

## Week 3: Building Habits and Embracing the Benefits

- Integrate into Routine:  
    Make active travel a regular part of your daily routine. Plan your trips to see if you can walk or cycle instead of driving. Maybe schedule your grocery shopping trip for a day when you can walk or cycle there and back.  

- Discover Hidden Gems:  
    Active travel allows you to explore your surroundings at a slower pace. You might discover a hidden park, a charming cafe tucked away on a side street, or simply appreciate the architecture and details you miss when driving by.

## Week 4: Maintaining Momentum and Celebrating Success

- Stay Motivated: There will be days when the car seems like the easier option. Find ways to stay motivated. Listen to your favourite music or podcast while walking or cycling. Plan scenic routes to keep things interesting.

- Advanced Routes and Techniques:  
    If cycling is your chosen mode of transport, explore resources on more advanced routes and cycling techniques. Consider taking a cycling safety course to further enhance your confidence and skills.  

- Document Your Achievements:  
    Reflect on your journey and the progress you've made. Share your story and experiences on the social channel to inspire others.
