## Hydropower
> **THE BIGGER IS NOT THE BETTER - from an ecological pov**

While large dams have provided very cheap electricity and other vital development services (like irrigation water) to millions around the world, they are also the cause of significant deforestation, biodiversity loss, and forced human displacement. [The costs of mega dams can outweigh the benefits](http://news.mongabay.com/2018/11/mega-dam-costs-outweigh-benefits-global-building-spree-should-end-experts/). 

Note that large dams are not necessarily climate-saving—in tropical regions, large dams can [contribute more emissions](https://www.nature.com/articles/nclimate1540)than fossil fuel power plants for decades, as submerged forests decompose releasing potent methane. In spite of their downsides, [over 500 new dams](https://www.worldwildlife.org/magazine/issues/spring-2021/articles/wwf-study-finds-509-new-dams-planned-or-under-construction-in-protected-areas) are planned or under construction in protected areas around the world. Alarge part of those projects are in CHINA. 

 

Mini- and micro-hydro projects are an entirely different story. These smaller-scale systems can power local mini-grids and provide other services (e.g. water reservoirs for the dry season). These systems typically make the most sense in hilly regions where perennial streams and waterfalls exist. For more information, check out the [World Small Hydropower Development Report.](https://www.unido.org/our-focus-safeguarding-environment-clean-energy-access-productive-use-renewable-energy-focus-areas-small-hydro-power/world-small-hydropower-development-report) The Director of the [Hydro Empowerment Network](https://www.hpnet.org/) is a mentor with Terra, if you want to learn more about small-scale hydro.
