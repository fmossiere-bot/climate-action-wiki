Most cities are darker than they need to be. Black asphalt roads, dark rooftops and dense building materials absorb solar radiation during the day and release it slowly as heat, keeping urban areas several degrees warmer than the surrounding countryside even well into the night. This is the urban heat island effect, and it drives up energy demand, worsens air quality and accelerates local warming.

The solution, at least in part, is remarkably simple: make roofs lighter and more reflective.

---

### The Scale of the Opportunity

A 2010 study by Berkeley Lab using NASA data estimated that applying cool roof techniques to all rooftops and pavements across cities in the northern hemisphere could have a one-time carbon offsetting effect equivalent to removing more than 44 billion tonnes of CO2. That is more than total global annual carbon emissions. The figure comes from a combination of direct heat reduction and the knock-on effect of lower cooling energy demand in buildings.

That is a one-off benefit rather than an ongoing drawdown, but it illustrates just how significant the thermal impact of urban surfaces is at scale.

---

### How It Works

A cool roof works through two properties: high solar reflectance, meaning it reflects sunlight rather than absorbing it, and high thermal emittance, meaning it releases any absorbed heat back to the atmosphere rather than conducting it into the building below. A conventional dark roof scores poorly on both. A well-designed cool roof maximises both.

The practical benefits extend well beyond the building itself:

- **Cooler outdoor air.** By reducing the heat transferred from rooftops to the surrounding air, cool roofs help lower urban temperatures and mitigate the heat island effect.
- **Lower energy demand.** Buildings with cool roofs need less air conditioning, which reduces electricity consumption and associated emissions.
- **Better air quality.** Lower urban temperatures slow the formation of ground-level ozone, a key component of smog and a respiratory irritant.
- **Grid stability.** Reduced cooling demand during heatwaves lowers peak electricity load, reducing the risk of outages.
- **Improved indoor comfort.** In buildings without air conditioning, cooler roofs directly improve the conditions inside, which matters significantly in hotter climates and lower-income housing.

---

### The Technology Is Advancing

Painting roofs white is not a new idea. What has changed is how effective and durable these coatings have become. Researchers at Purdue University developed what Guinness World Records certified as the whitest paint ever made, reflecting more than 98% of sunlight. Buildings coated with it can stay cool enough to significantly reduce or eliminate the need for air conditioning. The challenge is that it can get dirty over time, which reduces its effectiveness, and producing it still requires mining of raw materials.

> A French company called Cool Roof France has taken a different approach. Funded partly through the Team For The Planet impact investment programme, it has developed a white roof coating made from sea shells. When applied to large flat industrial rooftops, it can cut roof surface temperatures by around 2 degrees Celsius. In France, where the electricity grid is already relatively low-carbon, the solution delivers an average saving of around 25 kg of CO2 per square metre over 20 years. The impact is considerably higher in countries with more carbon-intensive electricity grids: around 120 kg per square metre in Europe more broadly, 150 kg in the United States, 320 kg in China, and up to 460 kg in India.

This difference matters. The same cool roof delivers far greater climate benefit in a country that still relies heavily on fossil fuels for electricity, because every kilowatt-hour of cooling energy saved there avoids significantly more emissions.

---

### Worth Putting in Context

Cool roofs are most valuable in hot climates and densely built cities. For cooler, cloudier countries like Ireland, the direct energy saving is more limited, though the benefit to air quality and urban comfort remains relevant during increasingly warm summers.

It is also worth noting that cool roofs do not replace the need to decarbonise energy systems or improve building insulation. They are one layer of a much larger set of solutions needed for buildings, which remain one of the largest sources of global energy consumption and emissions. But as interventions go, cool roofs are low-tech, relatively low-cost, and can be applied to existing buildings without major renovation. In a sector where many solutions require long lead times and significant capital, that combination of simplicity and impact is worth taking seriously.
