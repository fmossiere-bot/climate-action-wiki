This is a selection of companies working on carbon removal, using different approaches. The underlying technologies will be covered separately. What these companies have in common is that they are trying to remove carbon that is already in the atmosphere or the environment, rather than simply reducing new emissions.

### Terra Fertilis - Biochar

Terra Fertilis is one of the leading producers of biochar, a form of charcoal made from plant-based organic material. When added to soil, biochar can serve several purposes at once:

- It improves soil health and fertility
- It helps soil retain water
- It stores carbon for a very long time

The carbon storage numbers are significant. Roughly 23 tonnes of CO2 can be captured per 30 tonnes of biochar applied per acre. Biochar has over 55 documented uses, which makes it unusually versatile. One example that stands out: when added to the grass that cattle feed on, it can reduce methane emissions from cows by around 10 to 15%.

Biochar also offers a potential alternative to synthetic fertilisers, which are responsible for around 5% of global emissions, more than aviation and shipping combined.

---

### Silicate - Enhanced Rock Weathering (Ireland)

Silicate is an Irish start-up with a distinctive approach: it uses cement dust, a waste product from the construction industry, to enhance soil and capture CO2. The process is based on mineral weathering, a natural phenomenon where certain rocks absorb carbon dioxide over time. Silicate accelerates this process by spreading fine mineral particles on agricultural land, which also improves soil fertility as a by-product.

It is worth highlighting because it comes from Ireland and because it combines carbon removal with a practical agricultural benefit, without requiring new or exotic materials.

---

### Heirloom Carbon - Direct Air Capture (USA)

Heirloom Carbon, based in San Francisco, uses limestone to absorb CO2 directly from the air. Limestone is a naturally occurring mineral, available in large quantities. Heirloom processes it so that it acts as a kind of sponge for atmospheric CO2. Once saturated, the carbon is extracted and stored permanently.

The company has signed an agreement with Microsoft as part of that company's carbon commitments. Its stated goal is to remove one billion tonnes of CO2 per year by 2035.

Heirloom is also explicit about how it operates. It does not resell captured CO2 to businesses that would return it to the atmosphere, and its founders have been openly critical of greenwashing in the carbon removal sector, particularly from oil and gas companies using vague capture commitments as a way to avoid reducing emissions.

---

### Research Direction: Electrically Charged Mist for Power Plants

This is not yet a company, but a research development worth noting. A team reported in New Scientist has designed a system that uses a fine mist of electrically charged droplets containing potassium hydroxide to capture CO2 from power plant exhaust. The CO2 reacts with the mist to form potassium carbonate, which can then be processed to extract and store the carbon. The researchers estimate the system could reach 95% efficiency. Cost remains the main barrier to adoption at this stage.

---

These examples show that carbon removal is not one single technology. It ranges from agricultural practices like biochar to industrial processes like direct air capture. Each comes with its own constraints, costs and potential. None of them, on their own, is sufficient. But together, they illustrate the range of tools being developed.
