Livestock farming and organic waste produce significant methane emissions as they decompose. Methane is around 80 times more potent than CO2 over a 20-year period, making it one of the most urgent greenhouse gases to address. Methane digesters offer a practical way to tackle this at source, while also generating real economic value for farmers.

---

### How They Work

A methane digester, or anaerobic digester, is a sealed tank in which microbes break down organic waste, such as animal manure, food waste or sewage sludge, in the absence of oxygen. This controlled decomposition process produces two useful outputs:

- **Biogas**: a combustible gas that can be used for heating, electricity generation, or as a vehicle fuel, replacing fossil fuels
- **Digestate**: a nutrient-rich material that can be used as a fertiliser, replacing synthetic alternatives and improving soil health

The process captures methane that would otherwise be released directly into the atmosphere during open decomposition. By converting it into usable energy, digesters turn a climate problem into a resource.

---

### The Potential at Scale

According to Project Drawdown, large methane digesters could grow to produce between 376 and 395 terawatt-hours of electricity per year by 2050, equivalent to around 1% of total global electricity generation. Over that period, widespread adoption could avoid between 6 and 7 gigatons of greenhouse gas emissions. The estimated net cost of achieving this sits between $138 and $182 billion globally.

Digesters are already in widespread use. China, the EU and parts of Southeast Asia have seen particularly strong uptake in recent decades. They can be installed at dairy farms, pig farms, wastewater treatment facilities and landfills, feeding electricity or gas either on-site or back into the grid.

---

### Why This Matters for Farmers

Beyond the climate benefit, methane digesters represent a genuine additional revenue stream for farmers. A digester on a farm can:

- Generate electricity or heat for use on site, reducing energy costs
- Supply gas or electricity to the grid, creating income
- Produce digestate as a substitute for purchased fertiliser, reducing input costs
- Help manage waste in a way that meets tightening environmental regulations

For dairy and pig farms in particular, where manure management is already a significant operational challenge, digesters address multiple problems at once. The feedstock, animal waste, is available in consistent quantities and does not need to be sourced separately.

The technology is proven and commercially available. The main barriers to wider adoption are upfront installation costs and the need for appropriate policy support to make the economics work for smaller farm operations.

---

Methane digesters will not solve the methane problem on their own, but they are one of the more practical and financially viable tools available, particularly for the agricultural sector. They turn an emissions liability into an energy and fertiliser asset, which is a rare combination in climate solutions.

---

## 2026 Update: The Limits of Digesters

A 2026 analysis by New Scientist (citing a study of 98 California dairies published in *Environmental Research Letters*) complicates the straightforward picture above. The headline figure — digesters cut methane from manure storage by 91% — holds up in controlled conditions. The real-world picture is messier.

**What the study found:**

Installing a digester reduced point-source methane emissions from 91 kg per hour to 68 kg per hour on average, at two-thirds of dairies in the study. But the remaining third saw no improvement, and at some sites leaks exceeded **1,000 kg per hour** — making those digesters net emitters rather than net savers. Emissions also briefly spiked during construction, when manure slurry had to be rerouted and agitated.

**The perverse incentive problem:**

California's Low Carbon Fuel Standard (LCFS) awards sellable carbon credits for biogas produced from digesters. A study found that farms receiving these incentives increased their dairy **herd size by 860 cows on average**. In other words, financial support for digesters has been encouraging factory farms to get bigger. As Brent Kim (Johns Hopkins) put it: "Taxpayer dollars are being used to inflate the value of manure so it begins to compete with the value of milk, and that creates a perverse incentive structure."

**Pollution swapping:**

Digesters accelerate the formation of **ammonia** from manure, raising concerns that they swap one pollutant for another. And if biogas cannot be sold and is burned off, the combustion can in some cases produce sulphur dioxide — a respiratory hazard.

**The overall verdict:**

The bulk of the research suggests that well-run digesters, with proper leak monitoring and repair, can cut manure methane emissions by roughly **half**. California's notification programme found that 20% of leaking farms fixed their digesters after being alerted. Researchers like Rebecca Larson (University of Wisconsin-Madison) call digesters "one of the highest performing mitigation measures we have" for livestock — but the key word is "well-run."

The bigger question is whether public money spent on digesters is more effective than comparable investment in solar panels or livestock reduction programmes. That comparison has not been adequately studied.
