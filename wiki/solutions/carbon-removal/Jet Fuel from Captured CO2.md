One interesting use for captured carbon is turning it into jet fuel. The process combines CO2 with green hydrogen to produce what is called a synthetic or electrofuel. These fuels can work on existing aircraft engines, which makes them more practical than a full technology switch, and they can be up to 70% less carbon intensive than conventional jet fuel.

The aviation sector is notoriously hard to decarbonise, and a few alternatives are being explored. Sustainable Aviation Fuel (SAF) is the broad category covering these options. Today, SAF represents only around 0.2% of aviation fuel, roughly 300 million litres, though some projections suggest it could reach 30 billion litres by 2030.

Not all SAF is the same. Most of what is produced today comes from biomass or ethanol, which can compete with food production and sometimes generate emissions of their own elsewhere in the supply chain.

Electrofuels, also called Power-to-Liquid fuels, are seen as a more scalable option because they are not limited by biomass supply. The process works by combining captured CO2 with hydrogen to produce syngas, which is then converted into fuel. The problem is the energy cost. Producing one litre of electrofuel requires three to four and a half times more energy than producing a litre of conventional fuel. At current levels of clean energy availability, doing this at scale remains a significant challenge.

It is a genuinely interesting direction, but the numbers make clear it is not a near-term fix.
