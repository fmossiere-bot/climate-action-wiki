Methane is a greenhouse gas around 80 times more potent than CO2 over a 20-year period. One of its largest sources is decomposing waste in landfills, which accounts for around 18% of all human-caused methane emissions. When organic waste breaks down without oxygen, it releases methane directly into the atmosphere.

Several solutions already exist to address this. Landfill operators can capture methane directly at the site and convert it to energy. Food waste can be processed through methane digesters, turning it into biogas rather than letting it decompose in open landfill. And reducing food waste at both production and household level cuts the problem at source.

A new approach is now being tested that could complement all of these.

---

### Methane-Eating Bacteria

Researchers are developing portable bioreactors filled with a specially selected strain of methane-eating bacteria. The bacteria consume methane as it leaks from landfill sites or coal mines, converting it before it reaches the atmosphere. The system is designed to be off-grid and deployable in locations where infrastructure for conventional capture does not exist.

If scaled up, these bioreactors could capture the equivalent of 24 million tonnes of CO2 per year by mid-century. That is not a transformative number on its own, but as one part of a broader set of methane reduction measures, it is a meaningful contribution.

The technology is still at an early stage, and real-world performance at scale remains to be demonstrated. But the approach is practical, relatively low-tech in its deployment, and targets a source of emissions that is genuinely difficult to manage through other means.
