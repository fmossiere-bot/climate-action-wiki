The ocean has absorbed roughly one third of all human CO2 emissions since the industrial revolution. It is one of the planet's most important carbon sinks, and researchers are increasingly looking at how marine ecosystems, particularly seaweed and kelp, could be actively used to remove more carbon from the atmosphere.

This is a genuinely interesting area, but also one where the science is still developing and caution is warranted.

---

### What Marine Algae Can Do

Seaweed, kelp and other marine algae absorb CO2 as they grow, storing carbon in their biomass. Studies suggest that kelp forests can sequester significantly more carbon per acre than land-based forests. Globally, seaweed is estimated to naturally sequester around 175 million tonnes of CO2 per year. According to Project Drawdown, scaling up seaweed farming could sequester between 2.5 and 4.7 billion tonnes of CO2 equivalent by 2050, representing roughly 1 to 2% of current global emissions.

Beyond carbon, marine algae offer a range of additional benefits:

- They help buffer ocean acidification by absorbing CO2 and releasing oxygen
- Kelp forests act as a natural barrier against coastal erosion and storm surges
- When farmed alongside shellfish like oysters and mussels, they help filter water and remove excess nitrogen
- They can serve as a sustainable source of food, animal feed, fertiliser, biomaterials and biofuel

Several companies are already working in this space. Greenwave supports a global network of small-scale regenerative ocean farms. Coast 4C, based in the Philippines, works with local seaweed farmers to combine climate benefits with community development. Keel Labs is developing biomaterials from seaweed. Others are exploring applications in animal feed, cosmetics and food additives.

---

### The Challenges Are Real

Despite the promise, there are significant uncertainties that need to be taken seriously.

**Keeping carbon captured is not straightforward.** Some species of kelp have gas bladders that allow them to float, meaning that when they break off, they can drift to shore and decompose in shallow water, releasing carbon back into the atmosphere rather than storing it on the seafloor. Seaweed ecosystems that attract plankton and shellfish also release carbon back into the water over time.

**The ecological impact of large-scale farming is not well understood.** Depositing large quantities of dead biomass on the seafloor could have significant consequences for marine ecosystems. Current seaweed farming is also concentrated on a small number of species, which reduces genetic diversity and makes crops more vulnerable to disease, pests and environmental stress.

**Measurement and verification are difficult.** As with forest-based carbon removal, confirming how much carbon is actually being stored, and for how long, remains a major challenge. Without robust monitoring, there is a risk of overestimating the climate benefit.

---

### Questions of Equity

One aspect of this topic that deserves attention is who benefits and who makes decisions.

Indonesia and the Philippines together produce over 32% of the world's seaweed, yet they receive far less funding and support for innovation than producers in Europe and North America. As of 2022, Europe had over 176 seaweed-related start-ups, compared to just 37 across Asia. This imbalance matters, both because it is unjust, and because the communities with the most experience growing seaweed at scale are being left out of decisions about how to develop this sector.

Ocean governance more broadly is fragmented, complex, and heavily reliant on voluntary commitments. This tends to favour larger and more powerful actors, while marginalising coastal and indigenous communities who depend on the ocean and have the most at stake.

Any serious effort to scale marine algae as a carbon removal solution needs to address these imbalances, not just the technical challenges.

---

### What to Take Away

Marine algae is one of the more promising and versatile tools in the carbon removal toolkit. It offers multiple benefits beyond carbon, it can support coastal livelihoods, and it does not compete with land-based food production. But the science on how much carbon it actually sequesters, and how permanently, is not yet settled.

This is an area worth watching and investing in carefully, with proper monitoring, diverse species selection, and genuine inclusion of the communities that know these ecosystems best.
