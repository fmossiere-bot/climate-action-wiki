### Overview: Marine Algae as a Solution to Climate

Marine algae, a broad term encompassing kelp, seaweed, and various other algae, stands as a multifaceted climate ally, capable of pulling significant amounts of carbon dioxide from the atmosphere. This diverse aquatic flora is not only pivotal for carbon sequestration, but also forms the basis of an array of sustainable products. Below are couple of examples on how marigen algae has been used across industries: 

  

Nutritious food options are derived from it by companies like Maine Coast Sea Vegetables, while Tate & Lyle explore its potential in food additives, transforming the way we enhance flavor and texture. In the realm of animal nutrition, Alltech and Cargill are utilizing kelp in livestock feeds, hinting at a dual benefit of nutrition and potential emission reduction. Beauty giants such as Aveda infuse marine algae into their products, capitalizing on its rich, nourishing properties. 

  

In the agricultural sector, organic fertilizers from the likes of Neptune's Harvest demonstrate how this biomass can return to the earth to enrich the soil. Energy sector innovators like AlgEternal are converting this aquatic vegetation into renewable biofuels, tapping into the untapped potential of algae. Furthermore, the pioneering work by Keel Labs in crafting biomaterials from seaweed underscores the versatile nature of marine algae, cementing its status as a cornerstone in the burgeoning sustainable economy.

### Question 1: Impact Estimate

Seaweed and kelp forest are multi-solving with a potential role to play in carbon drawdown and climate mitigation. According to Project Drawdown, seaweed farming could sequester [2.5-4.7 gigatons of carbon dioxide equivalent (CO2e) by 2050](https://drawdown.org/solutions/seaweed-farming). This would represent a reduction of 1-2% of global greenhouse gas emissions. 

- Seaweed absorbs carbon dioxide from the atmosphere and stores it in its biomass. Studies [from Harvard](https://sitn.hms.harvard.edu/flash/2019/how-kelp-naturally-combats-global-climate-change/) have shown that sea kelp forests can sequester up to 20 times more carbon per acre than terrestrial forests, which includes 1-10 billion tons of carbon dioxide per year. According to the [MIT Technology Review](https://www.technologyreview.com/2021/09/19/1035889/kelp-carbon-removal-seaweed-sinking-climate-change/), it is estimated that seaweed naturally sequesters nearly 175 million tons of carbon around the world each year.
    
- Seaweed helps to reduce ocean acidification. Ocean acidification is a major threat to marine ecosystems and is caused by the absorption of carbon dioxide from the atmosphere. Seaweed helps to buffer ocean acidification by absorbing carbon dioxide and releasing oxygen.
    
- Seaweed helps to protect coastal communities from erosion and storm surges. Sea kelp forests act as a natural buffer against waves and currents, which can help to protect coastal communities from erosion and storm surges.
    
- Seaweed combined with other sustainable farming like oysters and mussels help filter water and remove nitrogen. According to Bren Smith, founder of Greenwave, one acre of oyster reef can filter up to 140 million gallons of water per hour and remove three thousand pounds of nitrogen.
    
- Seaweed can be a great alternative source for many products, this includes fertilizers, biomass, biomaterials, biofuel, animal feed, cosmetics, food, and food additives. 
    

### Question 2: Major Challenges

- [Unknown ecological impact of large-scale kelp farming](https://www.technologyreview.com/2021/09/19/1035889/kelp-carbon-removal-seaweed-sinking-climate-change/) It is unclear what the ecological impact of depositing billions of tons of dead biomass on seafloor would be.  16 genera of seaweeds commonly grown in these farms only represent 19% of the seaweed species while account for >99% of the globally cultivated biomass. According to [Frontiers in Marine Science](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fmars.2019.00107/full), the decreased genetic diversity associated with the domestication of wild seaweed species makes crops more susceptible to abiotic stressors, disease and parasites.
    
- [Hard to ensure the captured carbon stays captured](https://theconversation.com/kelp-wont-help-why-seaweed-may-not-be-a-silver-bullet-for-carbon-storage-after-all-178018): Certain species of seaweed, like giant bladder kelp, have tiny gas bladders on their blades, enabling the macroalgae to collect more of the sunlight necessary to drive photosynthesis. The bladders can also keep the remnants or rafts afloat for days or longer depending on the species, helping currents carry dislodged kelp to distant shores. When the carbon in kelp decomposes on land, or turns into dissolved inorganic carbon dioxide in shallow seawater, it can return to the atmosphere.  In addition, seaweed ecosystems that attract planktons and shellfish release carbon back to the atmosphere on average around 20 tonnes per square kilometer every year. 
    

### Question 3: Impactful Solutions

- Take ecological impact into account for cultivation: Choose cultivation sites that minimize risk to sensitive marine features and maximize benefits for local ecosystems, such as filtering pollution for coral reefs.  [According to NOAA](https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/feature-story/habitat-benefits-shellfish-and-seaweed-aquaculture), diversify ecosystems by cultivating shellfish together with kelp to provide new habitats for fish and mobile invertebrate species. 
    
- Standardized Life Cycle Assessment to monitor ecological impact of seaweed farms: According to[MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2071-1050/12/9/3814) and [NCBI](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37673254/), we should have more quantitative research to quantify biodiversity impact of seaweed farms in order to continuously monitor the ecological impact of seaweed farms and incorporate them into standardize life cycle assessment for seaweed farms.
    

### Question 4: Equity and Justice

As we look into taking ecological impact into account for marine algae (kelp and seaweed) cultivation, we need to work with coastal and indigenous communities to ensure they have the proper funding and tools to engage in ocean and biodiversity conservation efforts. 

According to a research paper from [Food Production, Product and Nutrition](https://fppn.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s43014-022-00103-2), Indonesia and the Philippines has been producing over 32% of the world’s seaweed ,but yet, they haven’t gotten as much funding or tools to support innovative ideas and develop new applications beyond using seaweed as food production compared to North America and Europe. As of 2022, according to [Phyconomy,](https://phyconomy.net/articles/2022-seaweed-review/) Europe has the largest number of seaweed related startups (over 176) followed by 57 in North America, but only 37 in Asia.  

According to the [NPJ ocean sustainability article](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44183-022-00001-7#citeas), much of the existing ocean governance is complex and lacks strong accountability, relying instead on voluntary commitments and self reporting. This allows the people who have more power to maintain dominance on what they do with the ocean and its natural resources. Less powerful players are often further marginalized as a result.

As oceans and marine algaes play a key role in regulating global climate, we need to ensure that people who already experience pre-existing inequities and face disproportionate climate impacts including underserved individuals, women, black and indigenous people have a seat at the table and be part of the decision making spaces. According to the [High Level Panel for a Sustainable Ocean Economy Blue Paper](https://oceanpanel.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/Summary_Towards-Ocean-Equity.pdf), some of these transformative approaches include: (1) create a shared ocean economy that facilitates redistribution of wealth and benefits, (2) democratize ocean knowledge, (3) create inclusive governance process by including local voices and visions and 4) being aware of environmental and social limits on growth.

### Question 5: Specific Organizations in this Field

[Coast 4C i](https://coast4c.com/)s a social enterprise based in the Philippines that focuses on integrating the world's largest supply of regenerative seaweed while benefiting Communities, Commerce, Conservation and Climate. They focus on providing traceable and sustainable seaweed, increasing self sufficiency for underdeveloped communities, addressing environmental conservation and ensuring future food security. Some of their efforts include: 

1. Grow a network of small-scale farmers to increase sustainable and equitable supply 
    
2. Integrate seaweed into Marine Protected Areas to support habitat and fish stock recovery
    
3. Establish better fishing and cultivation practices such as removing old fishing nets 
    

[Greenwave](https://www.greenwave.org/) is a nonprofit that supports a global network of regenerative ocean farms to create jobs and protect the ocean and the planet. Their mission is to train and support ocean farmers through climate change and to build a blue economy that is led by farmers. Some of their efforts include:

1. Provide training, tools and support to regenerative ocean farmers to use seaweed and shellfish to yield meaningful economic and climate impacts
    
2. Use Greenwave’s commercial farm and hatchery as an innovation hub to develop and test next technologies and designs to help farmers scale their operations
    
3. Built an online Ocean Farming Hub to equip farmers with tools and network
    
4. Fund farmers for their climate positive role including carbon and nitrogen removal and habitat restoration 
    

### Question 6: Surprised We Learned

Governance of the ocean is a major challenge: Effective ocean governance hinges on creating a shared economy for equitable wealth distribution, democratizing marine knowledge, including local communities in decision-making, and respecting environmental and social limits to safeguard our oceans for the future.
