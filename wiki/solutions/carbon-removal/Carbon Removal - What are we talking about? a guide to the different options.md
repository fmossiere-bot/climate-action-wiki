Reducing emissions is essential, but it is no longer enough on its own. We have delayed action for long enough that we now need to also remove carbon that is already in the atmosphere. This article gives an overview of the main approaches, both natural and technological, and is honest about what each can and cannot do.

One thing is clear from the outset: carbon removal is not a substitute for cutting emissions. It is a complement to it, and a necessary one.

---

### What is Carbon Removal?

Carbon Dioxide Removal (CDR), also called carbon sequestration, is the process of taking CO2 out of the atmosphere and storing it in a way that keeps it from returning. It covers a wide spectrum, from protecting forests to industrial machines pulling CO2 directly from the air.

The IPCC acknowledges that CDR has to be part of the plan to stay within 1.5 degrees of warming. This is partly because we are not cutting emissions fast enough, and partly because some emissions, from certain industries and from gases like methane, will be very difficult or prohibitively expensive to eliminate entirely.

That said, the scale required is enormous. Reaching even 25% of the IPCC's removal target would require more than 100,000 times the current annual capacity. To put that in context, the largest carbon capture facility in the US aims to remove around 1 million tonnes per year. The IPCC scenarios require 10 to 20 billion tonnes removed per year. We are nowhere near that.

The EU has recognised this reality and has capped the role of carbon removal in its 2030 climate targets at around 2%, equivalent to 225 million tonnes.

---

### The Cost Problem

Cost is one of the biggest barriers across all CDR approaches. Today, removing one tonne of CO2 through direct air capture costs around $600 to $800. Some projections suggest this could fall to $200 to $300 by 2030, but experts are divided on whether even that is realistic.

By comparison, forest-based carbon credits trade at around $5 to $15 per tonne. That price difference explains why so many companies opt for tree-planting offsets. But cheaper does not mean equivalent. Forest carbon carries real risks: trees die, forests burn, and carbon stored in wood is not permanently removed. Permanence matters enormously in carbon accounting.

## Natural Approaches

### Forests

Forests are the most discussed natural carbon sink. In a typical year, forests and other vegetation absorb around 30% of human CO2 emissions. But that figure has been falling, and the science of measuring exactly how much forests absorb is more complex than it first appears.

Factors such as tree age, species, size, soil type, and regional climate all affect how much carbon a given forest stores. More recently, research has found that reforestation can create competing effects: tree canopies are darker than open ground, which means they absorb more heat, partially counteracting the carbon benefit.

One of the clearest findings in recent research is that **protecting existing forests is far more effective than planting new ones**. The net carbon impact of losing intact tropical forest is estimated to be six times higher than previously thought. Yet tropical forest carbon loss has roughly doubled over the past twenty years.

**On tree planting:** New plantations and reforestation projects have a role to play, but only when combined with deep emissions cuts, and only when done well. Monoculture plantations dominate current land restoration efforts, despite evidence that they are far less effective than diverse mixed forests. A large international study found that mixed-species forests capture up to 25% more carbon than single-species plantations, and are healthier overall.

Tree planting can also be done badly: poor species choices, habitat displacement, and displacement of forest-dwelling communities are documented problems in countries including India and Indonesia.

**On accounting risks:** Forest carbon credits are particularly vulnerable to overestimation and misrepresentation. Satellite-based estimates of above-ground biomass can overestimate carbon in tropical reforestation projects by up to ten times. Cases of inflated or entirely fictional forest credits have been documented across multiple countries. This is an area where caution is warranted.

---

### Peatlands

Peatlands are among the most underappreciated carbon stores on the planet. They store more carbon than all other vegetation types combined, including forests. Yet around 15% of the world's peatlands have already been drained, and drained peatlands emit roughly two billion tonnes of carbon per year.

Peatlands work by holding water, which prevents dead plant matter from decomposing fully. As the climate warms and peatlands dry out, they become more vulnerable to fire, creating a feedback loop: drying leads to burning, which leads to more drying.

The Congo Basin is home to one of the world's largest peatlands, but only 8% of its peat carbon sits within protected areas.

---

### Soils and Agriculture

Soils hold significant amounts of carbon, and better agricultural practices can increase the amount stored. Regenerative agriculture, cover cropping, and reduced tillage all contribute. As with forests, challenges around measurement, permanence, and additionality apply here too. But there are broader reasons to improve soil health beyond climate benefits, including food security and reduced dependence on synthetic fertilisers.

---

### Oceans

The oceans have absorbed around one third of all human CO2 emissions since the industrial revolution. They are a major carbon sink, but also under threat. Rising CO2 levels have increased surface water acidity by around 25%, which is damaging to marine ecosystems, particularly corals and shellfish.

Ocean-based carbon removal approaches include seaweed farming and enhanced marine alkalinity. Seaweed farming is an interesting area, but the science on its actual carbon removal potential is not yet settled. Ocean CDR remains an active area of research rather than a deployable solution.

---

### Key Risks Across All Natural Approaches

All nature-based solutions share a set of common risks that are worth understanding:

- **Additionality**: Would the carbon have been stored anyway, without the project? If yes, no new carbon is actually removed.
- **Leakage**: Protecting a forest in one location can simply push the damaging activity elsewhere, with no net benefit.
- **Permanence**: How long will the carbon stay stored? A tree that burns releases its carbon immediately. Rock storage lasts millennia.
- **Social harm**: Putting a financial value on natural land can exclude indigenous communities with historical rights over those spaces. This is a documented problem, not a theoretical one.

## Technological Approaches

### Point-Source Carbon Capture (CCS and CCU)

Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS) captures CO2 at the point of emission, such as the chimney of a power plant or factory, before it reaches the atmosphere. Carbon Capture and Utilisation (CCU) goes one step further, converting captured CO2 into useful products, including synthetic fuels.

These approaches are more economically viable than removing CO2 from open air, because concentrations at industrial sources are far higher than in the atmosphere. However, costs remain significant, and the technology has proven particularly difficult to implement at coal plants.

---

### Direct Air Capture (DAC)

DAC removes CO2 directly from the open atmosphere, regardless of where emissions occurred. It is the most flexible technological approach, and also the most energy-intensive.

The first commercial DAC plant, called Orca, was built by Swiss company Climeworks in Iceland. It is powered by geothermal energy, and the captured CO2 is injected into basalt bedrock, where it mineralises into rock within two to three years, a genuinely permanent form of storage. Climeworks has since begun construction on a larger plant called Mammoth.

In Texas, Occidental Petroleum is building a facility targeting 500,000 tonnes of CO2 per year.

To put the energy requirements in perspective: removing 40 billion tonnes of CO2 per year through DAC would require close to half of current global energy supply. That energy would need to be clean, or the process creates as many problems as it solves.

---

### BECCS (Bioenergy with Carbon Capture and Storage)

BECCS is a hybrid approach. Plants absorb CO2 as they grow. When used for energy, the emissions from burning are captured and stored underground, resulting in net negative emissions in theory.

In practice, BECCS at scale is considered a form of geoengineering. It requires enormous amounts of land and water. The Land Gap Report, produced by an international research team, found that the land area implied in national climate pledges for biological carbon removal is almost equivalent to all current global cropland. That is not a realistic proposition.

---

### Challenges Across Technological Approaches

- **Cost**: Current costs are high and the path to bringing them down is uncertain.
- **Energy demand**: DAC in particular requires large amounts of clean energy that does not yet exist at scale.
- **Standards**: There is currently a lack of agreed standards and regulatory clarity in this space.
- **Risk of misuse**: Some carbon capture technologies are already being used to support enhanced oil recovery, effectively extending fossil fuel operations. There is a genuine risk that carbon removal is used to justify delaying emissions cuts rather than complementing them.

---

## What This All Means

Carbon removal is necessary. It is also genuinely difficult, costly, and far from the scale required. No single approach comes close to being sufficient on its own.

The most important priorities, in order, are:

1. Reduce emissions as fast as possible
2. Protect what already exists, particularly primary forests and peatlands
3. Restore degraded ecosystems carefully and with proper safeguards
4. Invest in technological removal as a complement, not a substitute

Be sceptical of any plan, corporate or national, that relies heavily on future carbon removal to justify slower action today. The science does not support that approach, and the scale of removal being promised in many net-zero pledges is not grounded in what is currently achievable.
