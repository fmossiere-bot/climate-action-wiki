## Nature-Base Solutions: Land and Ocean Sinks
[LINK](https://textbook.terra.do/class/m-carbon-removal-3)

.. aka NBS

The IUCN [defines NBS](https://www.iucn.org/theme/nature-based-solutions) as "**actions to protect, sustainably manage, and restore natural and modified ecosystems that address societal challenges effectively and adaptively, simultaneously providing human well-being and biodiversity benefits."**

For both land and ocean systems, photosynthesis drives the process: Plants, phytoplankton and trees absorb CO2 from the atmosphere to make food and to grow. And when green, living creatures die, they add to the carbon stock of the soil (or the bottom of water bodies). Plants are thus existing and potential storehouses of carbon

### Risk Facing Nature-Based solutions
It is very important to have a grasp of what these risks mean as you dig deeper into these solutions. We will discuss specific examples of how these risks manifest in the different nature-based solutions areas that follow. 

#### **Additionality**
Additionality means the project would not have happened without new payments or finance. This is one of the toughest things to "prove" for a project yet one of the most important from a climate perspective. If the project would happen anyway, then _no additional carbon was actually removed._  Additionality is impossible to really ever actually guarantee, as one can never "prove" with certainty what would have happened anyway in the absence of the project funds.  We dig deeper into additionality in our modules on offsets. 
#### **Leakage**
A carbon removal project in one place (e.g. planting mangroves or trees on formerly deforested lands) could simply displace the original emissions (e.g. the ecosystem destroying activity) to elsewhere. This is especially likely if the consumer demand for forest products, for instance, remains unchanged, or even increases. This is the problem of leakage and is also very difficult to account for with some types of nature-based solutions. 

#### **Permanence**
This is a measure of how long, and how reliably, the carbon will be stored.  This can vary significantly between different types of projects.  As [Zeke Hausfather has argued](https://twitter.com/hausfath/status/1484246433502941184?s=20&t=aQJdAoO3LtNzFg7IvdHFgQ), there is a discernable difference in temperature impact for different periods of removal.  Permanence really matters.

#### **Additional social and environmental harm**
This should be obvious, but there is a major [concern](https://e360.yale.edu/features/a-big-new-forest-initiative-sparks-concerns-of-a-carbon-heist), and [some](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fclim.2022.831430/full#B42:~:text=if%20not%20done%20right%2C%20NbS%20can%20also%20have%20a%20significantly%20negative%20affect%20on%20indigenous%20peoples.%20For%20example%2C%20NbS%20such%20as%20protecting%20areas%20and%20forest%20plantations%20can%20both%20physically%20and%20economically%20displace%20people%20and%20can%20put%20restrictions%20on%20their%20livelihood%20and%20have%20negative%20impact%20on%20their%20culture) [evidence](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1877343517301410#:~:text=the%20locking%2Dup,for%20these%20schemes), that putting a financial value on nature can result in the [exclusion](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2023/jan/21/amazon-indigenous-communities-carbon-offsetting-pirates-aoe) of certain groups of people who have had historical—though not always legal—rights over certain natural spaces. Environmental [harms](https://news.mongabay.com/2023/01/biodiversity-human-rights-safeguards-crucial-to-nature-based-solutions-critics/#:~:text=For%20example%2C%20promoting%20monocropping%20or%20single%20species%20plantation%20forestry%20can%20be%20marked%20as%20afforestation%20but%20may%20create%20more%20%E2%80%A6%20ecological%20and%20socio%2Dcultural%20%5Bharm%5D) can also result, say, from practices like widely planting just one species. 

The above risks, when taken together, mean that the climate impacts of nature-based solutions can be easily misrepresented by accounting errors and tricks. This problem is _pervasive_ across this sector.


### Forests: How important are forests for climate stability?
#forest 

Also see [[FACTS about Forest, Land Use and Land management]]

It is not simple. Research below illustrate the complexity of evaluating real Carbon impact from Forest land based on a lot of different criterias. 
Forests are critical for climate stability, though the science of determining their true potential is still evolving and extremely complex. 

For example, climate change itself should be a net positive for forests (in higher latitudes), as more carbon dioxide effectively acts as a photosynthetic “stimulant” of sorts. In theory, this should help cool the planet as more trees absorb more CO2—but this is far from given. 

Even the basic mechanism for [how this occurs is not well modeled](http://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-020-19187-w). One study for the Southwestern United States found this fertilization effect on forest growth [could be 29 percent less than estimated](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-018-07800-y). This [effect may also be transitory](http://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-018-05705-4) for boreal forests, and [may have upper limits](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-018-05132-5) lower than estimated. 

And more forest cover can [create competing effects](https://www.science.org/content/article/trees-help-curb-climate-change-can-also-contribute-warming-reducing-earths-reflectivity): Studies have found that [trees also emit](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-019-00122-z) “a complex potpourri of chemicals, some of which warm the planet," and that "non-CO2 effects such as surface albedo [decreases associated with reforestation could counter](http://www.nature.com/articles/s43247-022-00391-z) almost half of the climate effect of carbon sequestration." Surface albedo is a measure of the reflectivity of the planet; darker leaves of trees absorb more heat than bare earth.

> [Research from 2021](http://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10584-021-03079-z) calculates that emissions reduction potential estimates for forests should be discounted by between 9 and 44 percent, underscoring the complexity of accurately measuring the climate impacts of forests.

#### Preserving what we have is best

> This paper](https://advances.sciencemag.org/content/5/10/eaax2546.abstract) by Dr. Yadvinder Malhi—a globally renowned expert on tropical forests and climate change—and his team found that the net carbon impact resulting from intact tropical forest loss is actually _six times higher_ than we previously thought. Our highest priority, therefore, should be preventing the destruction of this vital resource.

Sadly, we are still moving in the other direction. Tropical forest carbon loss is [estimated to have doubled](https://twitter.com/ciais_philippe/status/1498357733204103168?s=20&t=7_e54Cg8ftLPfCaLpoWRXg) over the past twenty years. [According to WRI](https://research.wri.org/gfr/latest-analysis-deforestation-trends?utm_campaign=treecoverloss2021&utm_medium=bitly&utm_source=PartnerTwitter&utm_source=twitter&utm_medium=anidasguptawri&utm_campaign=socialmedia&utm_term=0fb6dace-594d-4bde-aec2-923461b583fc), “Tropical primary forest loss in 2021 resulted in 2.5 Gt of carbon dioxide emissions, equivalent to the annual fossil fuel emissions of India.”

> [Research](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2021/jul/14/amazon-rainforest-now-emitting-more-co2-than-it-absorbs) indicates that the Amazon rainforest has recently switched from being a net sink to a net source of greenhouse gases, with deforestation rates at [record high levels](https://www.reuters.com/business/environment/deforestation-brazils-amazon-rainforest-hits-record-january-high-2022-02-11/). [Global meat (and associated leather) consumption](https://twitter.com/Quicktake/status/1485298053800243201?s=20&t=XdJd-cjibmYu69ADARtSYQ) is a key driver of this loss.

![[Screenshot 2023-12-11 at 18.07.08.png]]

#### Planting trees is not a panacea
While we are largely failing on preventing tropical deforestation, there is considerable interest in planting new trees and forests. It is important to keep in mind that new forests can never replace the biodiversity and range of environmental services that mature tropical forests provide.

A [2022 study](https://www.nature.com/articles/s43247-022-00391-z) finds that temporary carbon sequestration (e.g. planting trees) "has the potential to decrease the peak temperature increase, but _only if implemented alongside an ambitious mitigation scenario where fossil fuel CO2 emissions were also decreased to net-zero._" (Emphasis added.) And [the Land Gap Report](https://www.landgap.org/), produced by an international team of researchers, finds that the " total area of land needed to meet projected biological carbon removal in national climate pledges is almost 1.2 billion hectares – equivalent to current global cropland. _Countries’ climate pledges rely on unrealistic amounts of land-based carbon removal._" (Again, emphasis added.)

Monoculture plantations dominate as a land restoration activity, even though [their benefits are widely divergent and overestimated,](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-022-31380-7) and [this form of tree planting can be ecologically misguided.](https://e360.yale.edu/features/phantom-forests-tree-planting-climate-change)

There are many ways to do tree-planting right, but also, alas, [many ways to do it wrong](https://scroll.in/article/1025105/why-planting-more-trees-is-a-lazy-solution-to-the-crisis-of-climate-change). [Researchers from India's Ashoka Trust for Research in Ecology & the Environment outline](http://twitter.com/atree_org/status/1534006294910078976?s=21&t=IFo2KwNEGEBoUjlLGoYplA) how tree-planting efforts in India face problems from poor choices of species and habitat to the displacement of forest-dwelling communities.

#### Let's summarize all the challenges and risks:  

- As already discussed in the last few slides, how forests sequester carbon, and how _much_ they sequester, is a complicated question, and the science continues to evolve. It is [equally complicated](https://twitter.com/Peters_Glen/status/1438410749236416515?s=20) to calculate emissions from land-based sources like forests (e.g. when trees are cut down or a forest burns). Satellite imagery and AI/ML approaches do not easily solve the problem (though they are helpful tools). [One study finds](https://arxiv.org/abs/2107.11320) that "forest carbon estimates from satellite imagery can overestimate above-ground biomass by up to 10-times for tropical reforestation projects."  

- Partly because the science for measurement and verification is so complex, forest carbon accounting is [beset with problems](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10584-020-02653-1?ArticleAuthorAssignedToIssue_20200806). Examples of overestimation and/or misrepresentation of forest carbon credits, forest extents, and carbon sequestration potentials include cases in [California](https://carbonplan.org/research/forest-offsets-explainer), India (see [here](https://india.mongabay.com/2022/01/the-state-of-indias-forests-losing-forests-gaining-plantations/), [here](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41893-021-00761-z), and [here](https://twitter.com/trishuphigh/status/1483451884811784195?s=20&t=9H8QkkYfyPy4Mnqf3WBjNg)) [Indonesia](https://news.mongabay.com/2022/01/indonesian-proposal-could-redefine-palm-oil-driven-deforestation-as-reforestation/) and [China](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-021-04255-y.epdf). All verification protocols for offsets—even those considered "gold standard"—overestimate credits from forestry projects—we will dig into this in our class on offsets. [New approaches to accounting](https://carbonplan.org/research/ton-year-explainer) also have many problems and loopholes.

>  If there's one big lesson to take away from these slides, it's this: Beware of online schemes promising you some guaranteed long-term forest-based carbon storage in exchange for a handful of dollars. At the bare minimum, it's worth looking under the hood to see what's really going on. In some cases, entirely fictional forests are being [used for greenwashing](https://www.bbc.com/news/science-environment-61300708).

### Peatlands
#peatland

Peatlands are [a type of wetland. They include](http://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2022/02/21/headway/peat-carbon-climate-change.html?referringSource=articleShare) bogs, fens, pocosins, palsa, blanket bogs, and tropical peat swamp forests.

Peatlands are a tremendous natural carbon store. _They [store more carbon](https://www.iucn.org/resources/issues-briefs/peatlands-and-climate-change) than all other vegetation types in the world combined._ Damaged peatlands are a [major source of greenhouse gas emissions](https://www.iucn.org/resources/issues-briefs/peatlands-and-climate-change), responsible for almost 5 percent of global anthropogenic CO2 emissions.

Peatlands store carbon [as follows](https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2022/02/21/headway/peat-carbon-climate-change.html?referringSource=articleShare): Vegetation—chiefly sphagnum mosses—hold up to 26 times their weight in water, making peatlands very soggy places. As a result, as plants die and sink in, they cannot fully decompose, and the carbon in the plant body is mostly trapped. 

##### We have drained 15% of the world's peatland

> We have drained about **==15 percent of the world’s peatlands==**, and according to _[The New York Times](https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2022/02/21/headway/peat-carbon-climate-change.html?referringSource=articleShare)_, "Drained peatlands emit about a dizzying two billion tons of accumulated carbon every year. This number is only expected to rise as the world warms and peatlands dry out, priming the land to catch fire and burn. In other words, the world’s peatlands are becoming a vicious climate feedback loop: peat drying and burning and drying and burning."

One of the world's largest peatlands is located across the Republic of the Congo and the Democratic Republic of the Congo. [Only 8 percent of Congo’s peat carbon](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41561-022-00966-7) lies within nationally protected areas. The rest is threatened. [You can read more about it here](http://news.mongabay.com/2021/12/the-idea-uncovering-the-peatlands-of-the-congo-basin/).

## Technological, Geological and Hybrid removal solutions
[LINK](https://textbook.terra.do/class/m-carbon-removal-4)

Technological and geological CDR  involves the removal of carbon dioxide from the atmosphere (the technological part) and storing it in a permanent way, usually underground (geological).

### Industrial-point-source carbon capture and storage (CCS) or carbon capture and utilisation (GCU)
i_ndustrial-point-source carbon capture and storage_ (CCS) or _carbon capture and utilization (CCU):_ CCS is the process of capturing carbon dioxide from an emissions source, like the smokestack of a power plant, and storing it where it will not enter the atmosphere. CCU offers the dual advantage of turning emissions into [useful products](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2021/apr/18/vodka-toothpaste-yoga-mats-the-new-technology-making-items-out-of-thin-air), including [synthetic fuels](https://www.nationalgeographic.com/science/article/carbon-engineering-liquid-fuel-carbon-capture-neutral-science), and also enhancing the economics of carbon removal technologies, which to date remain very expensive. Different technologies, some with membranes, others involving solvents, can capture carbon dioxide. McKinsey estimates that over the next decade, most carbon removed will [need to be stored](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/sustainability/our-insights/driving-co2-emissions-to-zero-and-beyond-with-carbon-capture-use-and-storage). If you want to dig into the weeds of CCU, we recommend this [detailed article by David Roberts](http://www.vox.com/energy-and-environment/2019/9/4/20829431/climate-change-carbon-capture-utilization-sequestration-ccu-ccs).

### Technological solutions face their own challenges:   
 
- [Costs remain high](https://twitter.com/bataille_chris/status/1519017259364061185?s=21&t=2MkMF0zFpMSh10GFJ0Xn7w) and carbon removal will not scale as easily as semiconductors have. While the industry has coalesced around [a cost goal of $100/ton of carbon dioxide](https://www.protocol.com/bulletins/carbon-removal-cost-per-ton) removed, MIT research engineer Howard Herzog [thinks this is “pure fantasy.”](https://www.protocol.com/climate/carbon-dioxide-removal-cost-target) But innovative approaches like [Frontier’s advance market commitment initiative](https://frontierclimate.com/) combined with [generous support from the IRA law could change the calculation.](https://qz.com/carbon-capture-tax-credits-inflation-reduction-act-1849432789)   
     
- The process is typically very energy-intensive, and [problems with emissions still remain](http://web.stanford.edu/group/efmh/jacobson/Articles/Others/19-CCS-DAC.pdf). [Carbon capture from coal plants is proving to be especially elusive](http://twitter.com/joesmyth/status/1478406005843234817?s=20&t=4C4E64KIiegZLsMNEWpiiA). Plus, once stored, there can be issues of leakage. And there are [also other environmental trade-offs involved.](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-022-31146-1)   
     
- There is [a lack of standards and little policy clarity](https://carbonplan.org/research/cdr-scale-barriers) in this emergent area.

### Direct Air Capture (DAC)
This involves removing carbon dioxide _directly from the atmosphere_ as opposed to a point source of emissions. The carbon can either be stored or utilized. It is the most promising CCS approach. 

- -==Requires significant energy to operate. Energy needs to be clean==

> At a current world energy supply of 589 EJ (IEA WEO 2021), and 7 GJ/ton, it would take around 47% of current global energy use to remove 40 GtCO2/yr.
> *Source: [Zeke Hausfather on X](https://twitter.com/hausfath/status/1504947052752515072?s=20&t=WioDPh_R05WIBRaNhjvTGg)*

The first operation is there, in Iceland
> In fact, in September of 2021, Climeworks began operating one such machine, called Orca, in Iceland, [billing it as](https://climeworks.com/roadmap/orca) “the world’s first and largest climate-positive direct air capture and storage plant, making direct air capture and storage a reality.

Orca is powered by a nearby geothermal plant, and a chemical process turns the captured carbon into a kind of drinkable soda water, which is then injected underground into porous basalt bedrock. Within two to three years there, it turns to rock—a permanent form of carbon storage.

> Climeworks has also begun construction on another, bigger Iceland plant called [Mammoth](https://www.carbfix.com/the-new-plant-called-mammoth-increases-the-direct) which is expected to come online around 2025. And by late 2024, [Occidental Petroleum aims to dwarf](https://www.oxy.com/news/news-releases/occidental-1pointfive-to-begin-construction-of-worlds-largest-direct-air-capture-plant-in-the-texas-permian-basin/) that project with a facility in the Texas Permian Basin capable of pulling some 500,000 metric tons of CO2 out of the air each year.

## SUMMARY
- Carbon removal encompasses a diverse array of activities to physically withdraw carbon from the atmosphere. Importantly, it is not a substitute for _deep and rapid emissions reductions._ As we have delayed mitigation for so long, we are now in a situation where it is very likely we will need removal _in addition to_ deep emissions reductions to stabilize temperatures within 1.5C of global average warming.   
     
- There are natural, technological and hybrid solutions for carbon removal. They all present opportunities but both also face challenges.   
     
- Natural solutions encompass conservation of forests and afforestation and storing carbon in soils and in our oceans. All these solutions face risks related to additionality, leakage, permanence, and causing economic and political harm to forest-dwelling people. But new conservation policies and evidence from countries like Nepal provide hope for emerging best practices.
- - Technological and hybrid solutions include CCS, CCU, DAC and BECCS (so many acronyms!). Technological approaches like DAC seem promising and they avoid some of the risks of nature-based solutions. But they are currently expensive and energy-intensive. Hybrid solutions like BECCS is a type of geoengineering that carries many risks.   
     
- As a world, we need to rapidly reduce our emissions, protect and enhance nature, and technologically remove carbon from the atmosphere. And we need to do these all in ways that also address injustice, vulnerability, and poverty—or else we risk worsening existing injustices in the name of climate action.
