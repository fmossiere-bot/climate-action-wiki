## Summary

Livestock grazing covers a quarter of the world's land surface. How we manage that land, whether we overgraze it or give it space to recover, has a real effect on how much carbon it holds. Managed grazing approaches, including rotational grazing and reducing stocking density, have attracted significant interest as low-cost, nature-based climate solutions. Project Drawdown, one of the most respected organisations mapping pathways to reduce greenhouse gases, has been studying these approaches closely. Their latest assessment is nuanced: the potential is real, but the evidence is not yet strong enough to count on grazing management as a reliable climate fix. This article explains what managed grazing involves, why it matters for the climate, and why Project Drawdown currently places these approaches in a "Keep Watching" category rather than a "Deploy Now" one.


## What is Managed Grazing?

Managed grazing means carefully controlling how many animals graze a piece of land, when they graze it, and how long they stay. This is different from conventional grazing, where livestock are often left to roam the same area continuously without any planned rest period for the land.

Under the managed grazing umbrella, two main strategies are commonly discussed:

> **Alternative grazing** involves moving animals between paddocks on a planned rotation, so each section of land gets time to recover before being grazed again. This approach goes by many names, including adaptive multi-paddock, rotational, holistic, regenerative, or mob grazing. It uses multiple fenced paddocks, frequent animal movement, short grazing periods, and enforced rest periods for vegetation. The idea is to imitate the natural movement of wild herds, allowing plants and roots to regrow and feed more carbon back into the soil.

> **Reducing grazing intensity** takes a different angle. It involves lowering ruminant livestock stocking rates or shortening how long animals graze on a piece of land, with the goal of reducing pressure on grasslands and allowing soil organic carbon to recover.

Both approaches share the same underlying logic: give the soil and plants more breathing room, and the land will hold more carbon, resist erosion better, and support more biodiversity.


## Why Does This Matter for the Climate?

Soil is one of the largest carbon stores on the planet. Healthy grassland soil holds enormous amounts of carbon underground. When land is overgrazed, that carbon can be lost through erosion, compaction, and the breakdown of plant life that would normally feed carbon back into the ground.

> Research cited by Project Drawdown notes that livestock grazing already covers more than 3.3 billion hectares, roughly 25% of all land on Earth. Poor grazing practices have contributed to widespread land degradation and loss of soil carbon. If even a portion of that land could be better managed, the potential climate benefit is significant.

The appeal is clear: managed grazing is a low-tech intervention. No new equipment is needed. The methods already exist. And unlike reforestation, it does not require changing land use entirely. It just changes how livestock are managed on land already used for grazing.

## What Project Drawdown Says

Project Drawdown is a non-profit research organisation that evaluates climate solutions based on how much impact they can have, how ready they are, and how strong the evidence behind them is. They currently assess both alternative grazing and reduced grazing intensity as part of their Drawdown Explorer platform.

### The case for managed grazing

On the positive side, both approaches can deliver real local benefits. Reducing the number of animals on a given piece of land tends to reduce methane emissions, ease land-use pressure, and reduce threats to biodiversity. It requires no new technology or infrastructure, making it a relatively accessible intervention, though not necessarily cost-neutral for livestock producers.

In limited and context-specific cases, alternative grazing can also deliver localized ecosystem benefits, particularly where continuous grazing has caused erosion near waterways or where more strategic movement of livestock could help restore heavily degraded land.

### The evidence gaps

Despite the theoretical appeal, Project Drawdown's assessments highlight significant gaps in the evidence.

The available research suggests that alternative grazing practices do not consistently produce additional, durable soil carbon gains compared to continuous grazing, and that outcomes depend heavily on climate and land-use context. The best results tend to appear in wetter regions with high rainfall, and often on land previously used as cropland, where carbon gains may occur regardless of the specific grazing approach used.

For reduced grazing intensity, increases in soil organic carbon are similarly limited to wetter regions. In arid and semi-arid areas, which represent a major share of global grazing land, reduced grazing intensity often results in neutral or even negative soil carbon responses.

Many studies also struggle with methodological weaknesses. They often lack baseline measurements, proper controls, sufficient duration, or soil sampling at adequate depth, making it difficult to confidently attribute carbon gains to the grazing practice itself.

### The methane problem

A key challenge for both approaches is that ruminant livestock produce methane as part of their digestion, and no amount of grazing management changes that. Alternative grazing can in some cases result in reduced animal weight gains and associated increases in methane emissions per unit of beef protein produced, potentially offsetting any small, temporary soil carbon gains relative to conventional grazing.

The original assumption in managed grazing research was that soil carbon gains would more than compensate for ongoing methane emissions, at least until soil carbon reaches saturation. But that assumption only holds if the soil carbon gains are real, consistent, and lasting, and current evidence does not yet confirm this reliably.

### The leakage risk

A further concern is what researchers call leakage. Reducing grazing intensity often means smaller herd sizes, but under rising global demand for beef, this can simply shift production elsewhere, with no net benefit to the climate overall.

Project Drawdown also raises a broader concern about how managed grazing is sometimes framed. These practices are frequently positioned as a way to fix the climate impact of beef production, rather than encouraging a shift toward lower-emission food options. This framing can overstate what production-side changes can achieve and delay more effective demand-side action.

## Why "Keep Watching" Rather Than "Deploy Now"?

Project Drawdown places both approaches in a "Keep Watching" category. This means they believe the underlying idea is plausible and worth continuing to study, but the evidence is not yet strong enough to confidently recommend wide-scale adoption as a climate strategy.

The key reasons are:

**Inconsistent results.** Soil carbon outcomes vary too much depending on climate, soil type, land history, and grass species to rely on managed grazing as a predictable climate solution at global scale.

**Carbon gains may not last.** Soil organic carbon is a finite sink that approaches saturation within decades and can be lost through drought, warming, fire, or changes in management. Accumulation through reduced grazing intensity has been shown to be a temporary and fragile form of carbon storage.

**Stronger alternatives exist.** Project Drawdown notes that where reducing grazing intensity is possible, restoring those lands to more natural ecosystems such as native grasslands, forests, or wetlands would likely store more carbon and produce no ongoing methane emissions at all.

## Where Does This Leave Grazing Management?

Managed grazing approaches are not dismissed by Project Drawdown. They remain on the radar as potentially useful tools, and the practices can deliver genuine local benefits in terms of soil health, water retention, biodiversity, and reduced erosion. In heavily degraded areas, better grazing management is clearly a step forward.

But as a primary climate strategy, the science is not yet there, at least not consistently. The climate benefit depends too much on local conditions, and the carbon stored in soil is not as permanent or measurable as reductions in fossil fuel emissions.

What Project Drawdown is clear on is that managing grazing practices alone cannot offset the emissions from the livestock sector. The bigger lever is on the demand side: shifting diets, reducing beef consumption, and changing food systems so that less land needs to be grazed at all.

Managed grazing should be seen as one tool among many, and it is most valuable when it sits within a broader strategy that also asks what that land could contribute to the climate if it were restored rather than grazed.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Managed grazing covers two main approaches: alternative (rotational) grazing and reducing grazing intensity. Both aim to improve soil carbon and reduce land degradation.
- The potential is real, but evidence is inconsistent and largely limited to wetter climates with specific soil and land-use conditions.
- Project Drawdown classifies both as solutions to "Keep Watching": promising, but not yet proven to work reliably at scale as climate interventions.
- Methane from ruminant livestock continues regardless of how grazing is managed, and cannot be fixed by grazing methods alone.
- The most effective long-term climate strategy for grazing land is likely to reduce how much of it is used for livestock in the first place, through shifts in diet and food system investment.
