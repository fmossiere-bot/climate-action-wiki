## Key Facts at a Glance

Before diving in, here are the numbers that make this topic impossible to ignore:

- Agriculture produces about **12 Gt CO₂-equivalent per year, around 21% of all human-caused greenhouse gas emissions**. Animal agriculture accounts for more than half of that.
- Ruminant animals like cattle, sheep, and goats produce **methane, a greenhouse gas with 80 times the warming potential of CO₂** in the near term.
- Reducing ruminant meat consumption could mitigate between 1.4 and 2.8 Gt CO₂-equivalent per year, making it one of the fastest and most achievable climate solutions available today.
- A widespread shift to plant-rich diets could prevent around 5 million premature deaths per year by 2050.
- Animal agriculture currently **uses 80% of all agricultural land** and 70% of global freshwater.

These are not small numbers. What we eat, in particular how much red meat we consume, is one of the biggest levers any individual can pull on climate change. 

- A widespread transition to plant-rich diets could prevent about 5 million premature deaths and about 11 million total deaths annually by 2050.
- Plant-rich diets can improve health for people who do not rely on meat as an essential nutrient source and have excess red meat intake.
- Plant-rich diets are associated with improved nutrition and increased food security, which can reduce cardiovascular disease, reduce nutrient deficiency, reduce obesity, and improve mental health.
- Consuming less animal-based food can reduce agriculture's need for land and water


> By switching to a mostly plant based diet, you can:
> - Decrease emissions and slow deforestation by reducing meat demand
> - Improve your health by lowering the risk of heart disease, diabetes, obesity, and high blood pressure
> - Save money on food and healthcare costs

## Animal Agriculture Is a Major Driver of Climate Change

Ruminant animals produce methane through a digestive process called enteric fermentation. Clearing forests and grasslands for pastures and cropland to feed livestock also emits CO₂, while livestock manure releases both methane and nitrous oxide.

Ruminants make up 30% of food-related emissions but generate only 5% of global dietary calories. In other words, we are using enormous resources and producing huge emissions for a relatively small share of our food.

> **If cattle were a country, they would rank as the third largest emitter of greenhouse gases in the world**. Beef produces around 5 times more emissions than chicken, and up to 60 times more than legumes.

The EAT-Lancet Commission, an international team of scientists, estimated that red meat, beef, lamb, and pork, should ideally be limited to around 14 grams per day per person, or roughly 5 kg per person per year. Most people in high-income countries eat far more than that.

## Animal Agriculture Depletes Natural Resources

### Land and Water

Animal agriculture accounts for 80% of all agricultural land use and uses 70% of global freshwater. In the United States, livestock production is the largest source of freshwater consumption, and **producing 1 kg of animal protein uses 100 times more water than producing 1 kg of grain protein.**

By 2050, nearly half of the world's population is expected to live in water-stressed areas, in part as a direct result of food animal production. Each year, approximately 13 million hectares of forest — essential for biodiversity and carbon storage — are cleared for agriculture, primarily to support meat production.

In the European Union, research has estimated that halving consumption of meat, dairy, and eggs would result in a 23% reduction in per capita cropland use.

### Pollution and Emissions

The environmental footprint of animal farming goes well beyond land and water. Runoff from farms carries nutrients, antibiotics, and other contaminants into rivers, lakes, and coastal areas, causing dead zones where little aquatic life can survive.

Livestock production contributes 7.1 billion tonnes of CO₂-equivalent greenhouse gases, around **14.5% of global emissions**. Alongside CO₂, methane from animal agriculture has a warming potential many times greater than CO₂ over a 20-year period.

### Biodiversity Loss

Agricultural expansion for livestock production is a major driver of deforestation, which causes biodiversity loss through habitat degradation and fragmentation. Livestock farming can reduce the diversity of landscapes and contribute to the loss of large carnivore, herbivore, and bird species. Since 1970, human activities, notably livestock production, have led to a decline of over 60% of all wild species populations.

## Plant-Rich Diets Improve Health

The health case for eating less meat is well-established and growing.

Diets high in red meat have been linked to increased risk of overall mortality, cancer, cardiovascular disease, stroke, type 2 diabetes, colorectal cancer, and weight gain. Diets that incorporate other sources of protein such as fish, poultry, nuts, legumes, low-fat dairy, and whole grains are associated with a lower risk of mortality.

> Vegetarians in one study had a 24% lower rate of ischaemic heart disease deaths compared to non-vegetarians. A shift to vegan or vegetarian diets could reduce global mortality by 6–10% by 2050.

People who follow plant-based diets tend to have lower body weight, blood pressure, blood sugar levels, and cholesterol. There is also evidence that plant-based eating can reduce the number of medications needed to manage chronic conditions.

## It Also Makes Financial Sense

Plant-rich diets are not just better for the planet and your health, they can also save money.

> Diets that favour plant foods over animal products are up to 29% less expensive than diets heavy in meat. One study found grocery savings of around €1.60 per day on meat, and €0.65 per day on dairy. Add in savings from eating out, and the numbers add up quickly.

Research on the economic impact of plant-based eating in the UK found that if just 10% of the population shifted toward a diet high in vegetables and low in red meat, societal savings could reach £7.83 billion over two decades, from reduced healthcare costs and lower environmental restoration expenses.

## Small Changes, Big Impact

The good news is that you don't need to become fully vegan overnight. A diet shift away from ruminant meat can be adopted gradually, with small behavioural changes that together lead to globally significant reductions in greenhouse gas emissions.

Project Drawdown classifies improving diets as an "emergency brake" climate solution, meaning it can deliver rapid climate benefits and should be treated as a high priority.

Some practical starting points:

- **Limit red meat to a few servings per week**, working toward the EAT-Lancet guideline of roughly one small serving every six days.
- **Build meals around plant-based proteins** — lentils, chickpeas, black beans, tofu, tempeh, and quinoa are all satisfying, affordable, and low in emissions.
- **When eating out**, check the menu in advance and look for plant-based or fish options. If nothing fits, pick the least meat-heavy option.
- **Use herbs and spices generously** — cumin, coriander, smoked paprika, rosemary, and garlic can transform simple vegetables and legumes into flavourful, filling meals.
- **Shop seasonally and locally** where possible. Seasonal produce tends to be cheaper, fresher, and lower in emissions.



# Calculating the impact of a plant-rich diet
These calculation give a sense on how Project Drawdown came with the calculated impact of an improved plant-rich diet as a solution


> Using country-level data from the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations, Project Drawdown scientists estimated the growth in global food consumption by 2050, assuming that people in lower-income countries will consume more food and higher quantities of meat as economies grow. If 50–75 percent of people adopt a healthy diet of an average 2,300 calories per day and reduce meat consumption overall, they estimate at least 54.19–78.48 gigatons of emissions could be avoided from dietary change alone. Since agriculture, particularly for cattle and animal feed production, is the leading driver of tropical deforestation, reducing meat consumption can avoid additional forest loss and associated greenhouse gas emissions.
