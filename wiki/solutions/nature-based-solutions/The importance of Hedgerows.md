# SUMMARY
This article explore the importance of hedgerows play in supporting both biodiversity and climate mitigation. It plays a fundamental role in the EU land-use strategy and we explore in particular the state in Ireland and France. 

> Precious allies in the fight against climate change, hedgerows are now better protected and valued, with incentives for replanting. But uprooting continues.


# Why are Hedgerows so important? 
Planting hedgerows and trees in Irish grasslands offers multiple environmental, ecological, and agricultural benefits:

**1. Carbon Sequestration and Climate Mitigation**

- Hedgerows can sequester 50% more carbon than forestry, making them highly effective natural carbon sinks on farms
- Trees absorb CO2, helping to mitigate climate change by storing carbon in biomass and soils


**2. Biodiversity Enhancement**

- Hedgerows provide vital habitats, food, shelter, nesting, and hibernation sites for many native flora and fauna, including birds, bats, insects, and small mammals
- They act as wildlife corridors, allowing movement and genetic exchange between habitats
- A single mature tree can support hundreds of species, significantly boosting local biodiversity

**3. Soil and Water Quality Improvement**

- The root systems of hedgerows and trees stabilise soils, reducing erosion and preventing sediment runoff into watercourses
- They help filter pollutants and nutrients, improving water quality in adjacent rivers and aquifers
- Hedgerows intercept water flow, helping to control flooding

**4. Agricultural Benefits**

- Hedgerows provide shelter and protection for livestock and crops, reducing wind speed by up to 50%, which helps prevent heat stress in animals and crop damage
- They serve as cost-effective, long-term stock-proof barriers if well managed
- Shelter from hedgerows can improve animal welfare, especially during harsh weather and lambing seasons

**5. Cultural and Landscape Value**

- Hedgerows are part of Ireland’s cultural and historical heritage, often marking ancient boundaries and contributing to the distinct character of rural landscapes
- They enhance scenic appearance and provide natural screening and privacy

**6. Human Wellbeing**

- Exposure to trees and hedgerows reduces stress levels and promotes psychological wellbeing.
- Trees and hedgerows can increase property values and improve the aesthetic appeal of rural and urban areas

In summary, planting hedgerows and trees in Irish grasslands supports carbon capture, biodiversity, soil and water health, agricultural productivity, cultural heritage, and human wellbeing, making them a key nature-based solution for environmental and climate resilience in Ireland.


# What is the situation and plan in Ireland

> Climate Action Plan 2024 has a target to plant 40,000km of new hedgerows and improve management on 75,000km of existing hedgerows. However, there's been concern that this isn't being implemented fast enough with proper training and financial support for farmers.

**Current laws:** The Wildlife Act already protects hedgerows from cutting between 1st March and 31st August (to protect nesting birds), but there are lots of exemptions for agriculture, which means the protection isn't very strong.

> The situation: Ireland has about 689,000km of hedgerows, but they're being removed at an alarming rate. Less than one third of remaining hedgerows are in good condition. The Irish Government's Climate Action Plan explicitly integrates hedgerows into its broader goals to reduce emissions and improve land use.

# Hedgerows in EU climate and biodiversity policy

### The EU policy framework

The main EU policies that cover hedgerows are the **Biodiversity Strategy for 2030** and the **Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) 2023–27**, both part of the European Green Deal.

**Key EU targets:**

- At least **10% of agricultural land** across the EU should be under "high-diversity landscape features" — a category that explicitly includes hedgerows, along with flower strips, fallow land, ponds and fruit trees.
- The Biodiversity Strategy also calls for restoring 25,000 km of rivers to a free-flowing state, increasing organic farming, reducing pesticide use by 50% by 2030, and planting 3 billion trees by 2030.
- The EU also has a target for net greenhouse gas removals of **310 million tonnes of CO₂ equivalent** by 2030, and nature restoration — including hedgerows — contributes to reaching that.

There is no single EU-level target expressed in km of hedgerows specifically, but hedgerows are explicitly included in the landscape features target and supported through the CAP and the Nature Restoration Law (which entered into force in 2024).

# What about France in particular

### The scale of the loss

France had a historically rich bocage landscape (the French word for hedgerow countryside), but the damage since the mid-20th century is significant. France's total hedgerow length fell from around 1,244,000 km in 1975 to 707,000 km in 1987,  a 43% drop, mainly due to agricultural land consolidation in the 1970s.

More recently, losses have been running at around 23,500 km per year, a rate six times higher than what is being replanted each year. This pace has doubled compared to the 2006–2014 period.

The French biodiversity agency (OFB) estimates that only about 30% of the hedgerows that existed at their peak remain today.

### Why they matter in France specifically

Hedgerows are seen as a key part of the agro-ecological transition in France. Their benefits include improving agricultural production, helping tackle climate change, storing carbon, cleaning water, and reducing soil erosion.

Specifically, hedgerows filter runoff water, store organic carbon in the soil, protect against erosion and flooding, and serve as wildlife corridors for bats, insects and mammals.

**The northwest of France is the heartland of bocage**, with the highest hedgerow density:

- **61,813 km** in Manche (Normandy)
- **53,152 km** in Finistère (Brittany)
- **49,602 km** in Aveyron (South)

**A turbulent history:**

|Period|What happened|
|---|---|
|19th century|Farmers plant hedgerows to mark boundaries, contain livestock and produce firewood|
|1900–1950|Bocage reaches its peak|
|1950–1999|Massive uprooting due to agricultural consolidation and the decline of livestock farming — **70% of bocage hedgerows disappear**|
|2017–2021|23,500 km lost per year, while only **3,000 km are replanted**|

> **Today: only 1.55 million km of hedgerows remain in France.**

### What France has put in place

France has been quite active here, with several overlapping programmes:

**Pacte en faveur de la haie (Hedgerow Pact)** — launched in September 2023 as part of the _France Nation Verte_ plan:

- It sets a target of a **net gain of 50,000 km of hedgerows** between 2024 and 2030, building on an earlier "Plantons des haies" programme from the post-Covid recovery plan.
- The ambition is to protect, plant, manage and add economic value to hedgerows, through a coordinated effort involving all relevant actors.

**Longer-term target:** The agricultural orientation law (LOA) sets a more ambitious long-term goal of **500,000 km of sustainably managed hedgerows by 2048**, with the 50,000 km net gain by 2030 as a first step. A national observatory involving IGN (mapping agency), OFB (biodiversity agency) and INRAE (agricultural research institute) is being set up to track progress.

**Carbon credits and finance:** Some areas are experimenting with local carbon markets, where farmers are paid for the carbon stored in their hedgerows. Mature multi-layered hedgerows can store up to 3 tonnes of CO₂ per km per year.

**Label Haie (Hedgerow Label):** France has also developed a national certification scheme for good hedgerow management practices and the supply chains for wood coming from bocage.
