# Santa Marta Conference — Building a Coalition to Phase Out Fossil Fuels (2026)

## What is it

In April 2026, nearly 60 countries gathered in Santa Marta, Colombia for a landmark two-day climate conference focused on one specific goal: ending the world's dependence on coal, oil, and gas. The meeting was co-hosted by Colombia and the Netherlands, and came directly out of frustration with the annual UN climate summits, where fossil fuel-producing nations had repeatedly blocked any clear commitment to phaseout.

The key outcome was an agreement that countries would each develop a national **roadmap** — a plan setting out how they intend to wind down both the production and use of fossil fuels. These roadmaps are voluntary: there are no fixed deadlines, no required format, and no penalties for moving slowly. But the political signal is significant: this is the first time a substantial group of countries has explicitly organised around the goal of ending fossil fuels, not just reducing emissions in general.

The conference was prompted by a breakthrough at COP30 (Belém, November 2025), where — after petrostates blocked fossil fuel language from the formal agreement — the Brazilian presidency announced that the roadmap initiative would continue outside the UN process, merging with a plan backed by Colombia and around 90 countries.

## Why it matters

For decades, international climate negotiations have been hampered by a simple problem: the countries that produce and sell oil, gas, and coal have a financial interest in slowing any agreement to stop using them. Inside the UN system, where decisions require consensus, a small number of petrostates can block progress affecting the whole world. The Santa Marta approach sidesteps this by creating a separate forum for countries that are genuinely willing to commit to phaseout.

For the roughly 60 participating countries — who together represent more than half of global GDP and nearly a third of world energy demand — the conference is a way to coordinate action, share expertise, and put collective pressure on the rest of the world ahead of COP31 (November 2026). For small island nations like Tuvalu, which faces being submerged by rising seas caused by fossil fuel emissions they barely contribute to, having a dedicated coalition is a matter of survival.

## Key facts

- **~60 countries** attended the Santa Marta conference (April 28–30, 2026)
- The coalition represents **more than 50% of global GDP**, ~1/3 of world energy demand, and ~1/5 of fossil fuel supply [[colombia-santa-marta-fossil-fuel-talks-2026]]
- **Almost half** of participating countries are themselves fossil fuel producers — meaning they are agreeing to plan the wind-down of their own industry [[colombia-santa-marta-fossil-fuel-talks-2026]]
- **Major absentees:** US, China, India, Russia, Saudi Arabia, Qatar, UAE
- **France** was the first developed country to publish a national fossil fuel phaseout roadmap [[colombia-santa-marta-fossil-fuel-talks-2026]]
- **Colombia** published a draft roadmap during the conference; a new scientific panel was set up to advise all countries
- A **second conference** will be hosted in **Tuvalu** in early 2027, **co-hosted by Ireland** [[colombia-santa-marta-fossil-fuel-talks-2026]]
- Roadmaps are voluntary — no fixed deadlines, no enforced format — but countries are encouraged to publish them before the Tuvalu conference
- NDCs (countries' existing Paris Agreement pledges) are not sufficient substitutes for roadmaps because they only cover a country's domestic emissions, not the climate impact of fossil fuels it exports [[colombia-santa-marta-fossil-fuel-talks-2026]]
- Participants agreed to scrutinise fossil fuel subsidies and collaborate on trade policy and climate finance
