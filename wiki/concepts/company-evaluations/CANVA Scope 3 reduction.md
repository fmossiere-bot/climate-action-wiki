###### The global design platform is using partnerships and clean power projects to drive emissions reductions and set an example for all industries

> **Canva’s Scope 3 emissions are largely from the electricity used by print suppliers. Canva brings these suppliers into a VPPA so they co-fund new solar projects, and the suppliers receive RECs from those new projects. This reduces the suppliers’ Scope 2 emissions, which reduces Canva’s Scope 3.

## About CANVA

> Canva, an online visual communication platform with a mission to empower everyone in the world to design anything and publish anywhere, has 190 million monthly users who can also leverage the company’s global network of print supply partners to turn their designs into physical products—everything from posters and invitations to tote bags and mugs.

## Where do their Scope 3 emissions come from

Canva has made sustainability a core part of its mission and partnered with Watershed to measure and reduce its carbon emissions. It is addressing scope 3 through a Watershed-developed innovative fixed-price Virtual Power Purchase Agreement (VPPA), which reduces Canva’s supply chain’s environmental footprint through a series of new solar power projects funded in partnership with its print suppliers

For Canva, the largest portion of Scope 3 (Category 1 or Category 11 depending on accounting choice) comes from:  

### **📦 The energy used by Canva’s global print suppliers**

When you order printed materials through Canva, the printing happens at **hundreds of third-party facilities worldwide**.

These facilities consume electricity and fuel → which becomes part of Canva’s **Scope 3 upstream emissions**.


## What Canva is doing with Watershed

Canva is not just buying RECs on behalf of suppliers.
Instead, they’re doing something more material:

### **They are co-funding new solar projects together with their print suppliers.**

This is a **supply-chain VPPA**, meaning:
- Canva pays part of the VPPA cost.
- Print suppliers pay the rest.
- The VPPA gives the developer long-term revenue certainty.
- This revenue helps **build new community solar farms in Illinois**.

This is _much closer to true additionality_ than simple REC purchases.

### What suppliers get in return

Suppliers receive RECs (renewable energy certificates) from the VPPA, enough to cover _their own_ electricity use.

This lets suppliers claim:
- **Market-based Scope 2 emissions = 0 (or near 0)
- Therefore Canva claims:
    → **Reduced Scope 3 emissions** because their suppliers’ Scope 2 emissions are now lower.

So the causal chain is
**New solar farms** → **clean electricity + RECs** → **suppliers reduce Scope 2** → **Canva reduces Scope 3**.

> [!NOTE] RESULTS
> 
> - **Driving scope 3 emissions reductions with a solar initiative:** Based on the grid today, the project will avoid over 15,000 tons of CO2 annually, equivalent to 38 million gasoline car miles avoided, and an approximately 16% reduction of Canva’s 2022 footprint.
> - **Achieving widespread positive impact**: Canva’s sustainability initiatives, including the VPPA, will successfully reduce its scope 3 emissions while positively impacting local communities through creation of new jobs, lower electricity bills, and access to clean power––the excess generated that is unused by print supply partners.
> - **Leading by example and reducing its own footprint:** While helping print supply chain partners decarbonize, Canva is also driving change all industries can emulate, and inspiring global action on climate.

# Why this is credible (compared to typical Scope 3 reductions)? 

Many companies try to lower Scope 3 by:
- asking suppliers to buy RECs themselves (often non-additional), or
- pressure suppliers to reduce emissions without funding anything.

Canva is doing something stronger:
### **They are financing the actual build-out of new solar capacity, in partnership with suppliers.**

This aligns with:
- TCFD expectations (real decarbonization of the value chain)
- CDSB / ISSB guidance on material climate impacts
- Best practice for Scope 3 engagement under SBTi FLAG & supply chain enablement principles
It is one of the more robust forms of supply-chain decarbonization.

# What is a VPPA?


A **Virtual Power Purchase Agreement (VPPA)** is a financial contract between a company and a renewable energy project developer.

Key features:

### **It is a financial hedge, not a physical power delivery**

- Unlike a physical PPA, the company does **not** receive electricity directly.
- The company agrees to pay a fixed price for each MWh generated by a specific wind/solar project.
- Theroject sells its electricity on the wholesale market at the market price.

### **The company receives RECs**

- For each MWh generated, the company receives the associated **Renewable Energy Certificates (RECs)**.
- These RECs are used to claim **market-based Scope 2 reductions** (and sometimes are positioned—controversially—as influencing Scope 3 if they are tied to supplier enablement programs, which is usually not accepted by major standards).

### **Why companies enter VPPAs**

- Long-term hedge against electricity price volatility.
- Mechanism to support new renewable capacity (when well-designed).
- Ability to claim that the company “catalysed” the project’s development.


# Are VPPAs guaranteed to finance  new renewable projects (“additionality”)?


### **Short answer: No. VPPAs are not automatically additional**

VPPAs _can_ provide additionality—but only when structured carefully.
### **Why additionality is uncertain**
A VPPA provides additionality **only if**:
1. It is signed **before the project is financed or built**, and
2. The revenue certainty from the VPPA is **material to enabling the project’s construction**.

If a VPPA is signed with:
- an already-operational project, or
- a project that already secured financing without the VPPA,

→ **It does not create additional renewable capacity**.

In this case, the buyer is essentially purchasing RECs linked to an existing asset.  

### **Market standards**

- **GHGP (Greenhouse Gas Protocol)**: does not treat VPPAs with existing projects as additional.
- **SBTi**: emphasises _real emission reductions_, not market-based claims.
- **CDP & investor expectations**: look for evidence that a VPPA supported a _new_ project (“new build” RECs).
