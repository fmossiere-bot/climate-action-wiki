# Reflection

# Stena Line's Sustainability Efforts: Genuine Action or Communication Chaos?

I've been diving into Stena Line's sustainability initiatives, and I'm genuinely curious what others think about their approach. I travelled by ferry from Ireland to France this summer (like every summer), and while I usually would take Brittany, I used Stena on the way back. The difference between the 2 in terms of sustainability communication was striking. While Brittany seems to have a clear north star, values, commitment, Stena felt all over the place, listing CO2-e on the food menu, while making it hard to actually find recycling bins in some spaces, displaying its commitment to Sustainable fishing next to a shop full of plastic bottles, selling sustainable chocolate next to NEstle chocolate ... 

To the critical mind, it could appear as on the edge of greenwashing, when in fact, I don't think they are. Here's what I've found – and why I'm a bit conflicted.

## The Context: An Industry in Transition

First, some background: The maritime industry has set ambitious targets through the IMO's 2023 strategy:

- **40% reduction** in CO₂ emissions per transport work by 2030 (vs. 2008)
- **20-30% reduction** in total annual GHG emissions by 2030
- **Net-zero by around 2050**

The industry is responding with fascinating innovations – from sailing cargo ships to kite-assisted propulsion, batteries, and carbon capture. It's genuinely exciting to watch this space evolve.

## What Stena Line Is Doing

Stena Line appears to be taking real action. They've committed to:

- **30% reduction in total CO₂ emissions** from vessels by 2030, compared to 2019
- Zero fossil fuel emissions as a long-term goal
- Investment in e-methanol and battery-powered vessels
- Development of the Stena Futuro concept (promising 20% lower energy use)

They've also reported **11% reduction in carbon emissions per tonne of cargo** (May 2024), and external research from 2021 called them "a paradigm for shipping" with over $4.5 million/year invested in 300+ energy-saving initiatives.

## The Problem: Communication Chaos

Here's where I'm struggling. While the substance seems solid, their communication is... messy. Really messy.

Their website lists [hundreds of actions](https://stenaline.com/sustainability/the-list/) in what feels like a sustainability washing list:

- AI for fuel optimization ✓
- Single-use plastic reduction ✓
- Sustainable coffee ✓
- CO₂-e labels on all food items ✓
- MSC-certified sustainable seafood ✓
- LED technology, solar panels, propeller modifications ✓

It's overwhelming. And some of it feels more like box-ticking than meaningful change.

## My Concerns

**1. The "Everything Everywhere All at Once" Approach**  
When you list _everything_, it's hard to distinguish what actually matters. Are we meant to be equally impressed by sustainable coffee and methanol-powered ferries?

**2. Questionable Impact of Some Initiatives**  
Take the CO₂ labels on canteen food. It's a nice gesture, but we know from McDonald's calorie labeling that this doesn't drive behavior change. Is this meaningful action or performance?

**3. Verification Challenges**  
Claims are sometime hard to verify. The sustainable fishing claims are backed by a certification, which is good, but in general, I did find it hard to get clear numbers and outcome. 

**4. Information Accessibility**  
Finding their actual sustainability reports is unnecessarily difficult. Their annual review is sparse, and their detailed sustainability report is buried on Scribd. Why?

## The Greenwashing Question

Here's the thing: **I don't think Stena Line is greenwashing**. The external validation, the actual reported numbers, the substantial investments – it all points to genuine effort. But their scattershot communication approach _makes it look like greenwashing_.

When you overwhelm people with dozens of minor initiatives alongside major technological shifts, without a clear hierarchy or narrative, it creates suspicion rather than trust.

## Questions for the Community

I'd love to hear your thoughts:

- **Does listing every initiative** (no matter how small) help or hurt credibility?
- **What would better communication look like?** Clear priority ranking? Focus on the big-impact initiatives?
- **Am I being too harsh?** Maybe comprehensive transparency is better than selective reporting?
- **For those in maritime/logistics**: Is this level of detail standard, or is Stena Line's approach unusually fragmented?

I genuinely believe they're doing good work – and tackling a lot of areas, but my feeling on the boat did not align. At least this is how I felt. 

What do you think?
