> **Editorial summary:** A portrait of Yvon Chouinard, founder of Patagonia, tracing how a self-taught climber who never wanted to run a company built one of the most environmentally radical businesses in history — from forging pitons in the 1960s to suing the US President over national monument protections, to giving the entire company away to an environmental trust in 2022. The piece also acknowledges the tensions and contradictions in Patagonia's model honestly.

**The Reluctant Billionaire Who Gave It All Away**

Yvon Chouinard never wanted to run a business. He wanted to climb. Born in Maine in 1938 to a Franco-Canadian family, he was a poor student who felt out of place everywhere except outdoors. At 14, he joined a falconry club with friends, and climbing down cliffs to reach falcon nests turned into a lifelong obsession with mountains.

The climbing gear of the time was poor quality, so he started forging his own pitons and selling them from the boot of his car along the Californian coast. He lived on almost nothing;  in 1962 he even spent 18 days in jail in Arizona, arrested simply for wandering without money or a fixed address. That experience only made him more determined to live by his own rules.

His small workshop grew into Chouinard Equipment, the leading climbing gear maker in the US. Then in 1968, a road trip with friends to Patagonia changed everything. They opened a new route up Fitz Roy, a 30-hour climb that made mountaineering history and Chouinard fell in love with that wild landscape at the end of the world. It would later give his clothing brand its name.

**The moment that changed everything**

The real turning point came in 1970, on El Capitan in Yosemite. Climbing the famous Nose route, Chouinard saw the rock covered in hundreds of pitons and on one of them, his own company's name. His gear was destroying the nature he loved.

So in 1972, against all business logic, he stopped making pitons even though they were his best-selling product. Instead, he developed aluminium chocks that climbers could place and remove without damaging the rock. It was his first big lesson in business: profit comes second to the planet.

That same year, after discovering a sturdy rugby shirt in Scotland that all his climbing friends wanted, he launched a clothing line for outdoor people. Patagonia was born.

**Building a business that fights for the planet**

The start was rough: badly made products from outsourced factories, partners leaving, banks refusing loans. But Patagonia survived, and as it grew from 20 to 100 million dollars in five years, Chouinard used it as a tool for activism rather than wealth:

- From 1986, the company gave away part of its profits, eventually 1% of all sales, through **One Percent for the Planet**, a movement he co-founded.
- In 1994, Patagonia dropped conventional cotton, one of the most polluting crops, for organic cotton, hemp and recycled polyester, even though it hurt their margins.
- The company pushed people to **repair instead of buy**: free repair workshops, second-hand clothing lines, even a van travelling across America fixing jackets for free.
- In 2011, they ran the famous "Don't Buy This Jacket" advert in the New York Times, a clothing brand telling people to think twice before consuming.

**Taking on a president**

When Donald Trump pulled the US out of the Paris climate agreement in 2017 and shrank two protected national monuments in Utah to open them for mining and drilling, Chouinard did something no clothing brand had ever done: Patagonia sued the President of the United States. Their website carried one stark message: "The President Stole Your Land." The campaign went around the world, and in 2021 the protections were restored under the Biden administration.

**The final act**

In 2022, at 83, Chouinard made his most radical move. He gave Patagonia away, not to his children, not to investors. All shares went to a trust protecting the company's values and to a non-profit fighting the climate crisis. Every euro of profit not reinvested in the business, roughly 100 million dollars a year, now goes to defending the environment. As he put it, Earth became the company's only shareholder.

**The honest picture**

The story is not without contradictions. Patagonia still makes and ships clothes around the world, much of it produced in low-cost countries like Vietnam and Sri Lanka, and some critics call it polished [greenwashing](../key-ideas/Greenwashing.md). But even with these flaws, the company has done far more than most of an industry that ranks among the world's biggest polluters.

The lesson of Chouinard's life is a simple one: it's not the most powerful people who change the world: it's the most consistent. He built an empire he never wanted, then handed it to the planet.
