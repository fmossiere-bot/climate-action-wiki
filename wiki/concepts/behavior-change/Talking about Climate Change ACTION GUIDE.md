One of the many things we can do and have control over is to talk about Climate Change, and in particular about the Climate Actions that we are taking. Communicating about Climate Change is not easy, so here are a few guidelines on how best to communicate to your family, friends or peers. 


> "Never doubt that a small group of thoughtful, committed, citizens can change the world. Indeed, it is the only thing that ever has." - Margaret Mead

## The Need for Conversations

There is overwhelming survey evidence from Stanford University and PEW research center that people around the world feel concern for the climate but they are not talking about it, much less acting on it. A majority say they are concerned about climate change but they aren’t really talking to their family, friends, and colleagues about it.

Starting conversations about climate change with those around us is a powerful way of making a real difference. Talking about sustainability and climate action with family, friends, and colleagues, coupled with consistent action, is the groundwork for change. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) thinks shifting societal norms only requires 10% to 30% of individuals to be deeply committed.

This insight is supported by Damon Centola's research: once about 25% of a group adopts a new behavior or belief, it's likely the majority will follow. Meaningful conversations can build a community of support and spark positive change around you.

# Leading by Example

Taking action and being a positive role model are crucial to ensuring our conversations about climate change are more than just words. When we embody the change we want to see, our actions lend weight to our words, making them far more persuasive.

# More Science is Not the Answer

Entering a conversation with the mindset that you can scientifically prove climate change is real, urgent, and merits our attention and action may not be effective. If someone's identity is deeply connected to the opposing view, this approach can actually increase resistance. Attempts to educate can lead to more polarization. What does work is sharing information that connects climate science to its effects on us and the things we care about.

# Knowing Your Audience

In her TED talk, [Inspiring Climate Activism Stories](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-BvcToPZCLI), Katharine Hayhoe, a renowned climate scientist, underscores the importance of engaging in climate conversations that resonate on a personal level. She advises finding common ground by connecting climate change to what individuals value most, like family, work, or hobbies. For instance, discussing how extreme weather can impact local communities or how climate change affects outdoor activities like gardening or hiking. This approach, focusing on listening and understanding rather than lecturing, makes the issue more relatable and encourages deeper engagement.

# Using the Right Words

Changing how we talk about climate change can make a big difference. Using clear, positive language can inspire action and support for policies to address the crisis urgently. Instead of focusing on sacrifice, we should highlight the opportunities for better health and well-being in a world moving beyond fossil fuels. Avoid scientific jargon and misleading terms. For example, "natural disasters" are often influenced by human actions, making them not entirely natural. Clear communication can help shift perceptions and motivate collective action towards a sustainable future.

# Ignore the Outliers

Focus your efforts on the majority open to taking action, rather than trying to convert the dismissive few. Campaigns often target extremes, but targeting the middle 50% under the proverbial curve is where our efforts can make the biggest difference. Engaging the willing majority can build the political will and community support needed for meaningful climate action.


## Communicating Solutions

Highlighting solutions is as important as identifying problems. Focusing only on problems can overwhelm people, making them disengage. Many professionals highlight the need for hope, agency, and dignity in climate coverage. Most stories miss discussing solutions, leaving audiences feeling powerless. Highlighting positive steps and celebrating small victories can inspire action. It's important to balance fear with actionable hope and not to overemphasise sacrifice.

Showcase exciting aspects of climate action, like the benefits of electric vehicles, induction cooking, and vegetarian diets. The dropping prices of renewable energy and projected exponential adoption curves paint an enabling picture of the future. 

As critical as climatic tipping points are to the escalating crisis, positive social and economic tipping points to respond to it are underway and within our control. Encourage participation in grassroots efforts and local initiatives to demonstrate the many ways individuals can contribute to climate change mitigation.

## Guiding Children Through Climate Conversations

Although children are not the audience for this climate action, those are some of the most important climate conversations to be had. Explaining climate change to children requires a delicate balance of honesty and optimism. 

[How to Talk with Children About Climate Change](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Ym2uyC6kVIs) by BBC shares key strategies including using age-appropriate language to describe the challenges and emphasising actionable solutions that can make a difference. It's important to focus on the positive steps towards sustainability and involve children in these practices, fostering a sense of hope and agency.

## Calling out ‘Greenwashing’

Greenwashing is when companies falsely claim to be eco-friendly, misleading consumers into thinking they're making responsible choices. This practice, ranging from product labels to advertising, undermines the fight against the climate crisis by exaggerating positive impacts and hiding negative ones. Despite many consumers wanting to reduce their environmental footprint, greenwashing exploits this desire, damaging trust. Solutions like carbon offsetting often fail to deliver real benefits, with less than 5% effectively removing CO2 from the atmosphere. Recognising and calling out greenwashing can make issues relevant and personal to your audience.

## Misinformation

Counter misinformation with clear, factual information about the benefits of renewable energy and the tangible progress being made. Have a list of credible resources ready to share after real-life conversations to address The recent findings by the Center for Countering Digital Hate (CCDH) shed light on a concerning trend on YouTube, where the platform is profiting from a surge in climate misinformation. Alarmingly, 70% of this denial content is now aimed at discrediting climate solutions, employing sophisticated tactics to skirt YouTube's existing policies, as detailed in a Fast Company report ([YouTube's Profits from Climate Denial Content](https://www.fastcompany.com/91011266/youtube-making-millions-climate-denial-ccdh-says)). This revelation underscores the urgent need for more robust measures to combat the spread of misinformation.

# Checklist for Conversations

### Talking about climate change can lead to real change. Here's how to make those conversations count:

1. **Share Your Story**: Begin with your personal connection to climate change.
2. **Link to Priorities:** Connect climate change to what matters to the person you're speaking with, like family, economy, or health.
3. **Find Common Ground**: Use shared values to bridge differences.
4. **Discuss Immediate Impacts**: Highlight how climate change is affecting us now.
5. **Promote Clean Energy**: Talk about the benefits of renewable energy for the economy and health.
6. **Mention Cost Savings**: Point out the financial benefits of being sustainable.
7. **Emphasize Urgency**: Stress why we need to act quickly.
8. **Share Successes**: Use examples of effective climate action to inspire.
9. **Explore Futures:** Talk about how today's actions can create a better tomorrow.
