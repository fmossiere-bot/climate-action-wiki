## Animal Agriculture depletes natural resources

### The Detrimental Effects of Animal Agriculture on Land

By 2050, nearly half of the world’s population is expected to live in water-stressed areas as a direct consequence of food animal production [1]. Additionally,  The industry's water footprint extends beyond consumption, as the pollution and contaminants from livestock operations degrade water quality, affecting both human health and aquatic ecosystems. This extensive land use not only displaces native wildlife but is also a leading cause of deforestation. 

###### Each year, approximately 13 billion hectares of forest area—essential for biodiversity and as carbon sinks—are cleared for agricultural purposes, primarily to support the meat industry.

>  If 200 million individuals—roughly the population of Nigeria—cut meat from their diets for a single day, over 260 million tons of water and 46,000 hectares of land would be spared—the equivalent of about 104,000 Olympic swimming pools and the land area of the California Redwood Forests, respectively [1]

### Animal Farming and Its Role in Pollution and CO2 Emissions

The environmental impact of animal farming is not limited to land and water use. The runoff from feedlots and farms carries nutrients, antibiotics, and other contaminants into rivers, lakes, and coastal zones, leading to eutrophication and dead zones where few aquatic organisms can survive. One report found that livestock production contributes 7.1 billion tons of “CO2-equivalent” greenhouse gases, which amounts to 14.5 percent of the global emissions of these gases [2]. Alongside CO2, methane released from animal husbandry has a global warming potential many times greater than CO2 over a 20-year period, and the 1.5 billion cattle, raised specifically for meat production worldwide, emit at least 231 billion pounds of methane into the atmosphere each year [3].

- Take asparagus: in a report prepared for the city of Seattle, Daniel J. Morgan, now at Vanderbilt University, and his co-workers found that growing just half a pound of the vegetable in Peru emits greenhouse gases equivalent to 1.2 ounces of CO2—as a result of applying insecticide and fertilizer, pumping water and running heavy, gas-guzzling farm equipment. To refrigerate and transport the vegetable to an American dinner table generates another two ounces of CO2-equivalent greenhouse gases, for a total CO2 equivalent of 3.2 ounces [2]]
Understanding the environmental costs of our food is the first step in adopting a more climate conscientious diet. 

### Biodiversity and Ecological Loss Due to Animal Farming

Animal agriculture not only demands unsustainable levels of resources but also leads to substantial biodiversity and ecological loss. Since 1970, human activities, notably livestock production, have led to a decline of over 60 percent of all species populations. Intensive farming also leaves landscapes barren and susceptible to wildfire, floods, and other ecological disasters. Nearby habitats for wildlife suffer as a result.

### Tips

- When eating out, look for plant based, unprocessed options. Check the menu ahead of time and think about what looks both tasty and ecologically healthy. If there isn’t a plant based option available, pick the best option available.
-  Incorporate Plant-Based Proteins: Ensuring your meals are filling without meat involves focusing on plant-based proteins that add both substance and texture to your dishes. Legumes like lentils, chickpeas, and black beans are not only packed with protein but also fiber, making them incredibly satiating. Quinoa, a complete protein containing all nine essential amino acids, can serve as an excellent base for salads, stir-fries, and bowls. Tofu and tempeh, made from soybeans, offer a versatile protein source that can be marinated, grilled, or sautéed, absorbing flavors well and providing a meaty texture to your meals.

## Plant Based diets improve health and finances  

The evolution towards plant-based diets and industries offers more than just health and environmental perks; it also presents substantial economic advantages for individuals and societies at large. Diets that favor plant foods over animal products are up to 29% less expensive than diets that include a higher proportion of meat [4]. This is particularly noteworthy considering that beef, pork, and poultry account for about half of the recent increase in at-home food prices [5]. One study found grocery store savings to be $1.77/day for meat, and $0.74/day for dairy [6]. Add in savings from restaurants, and it adds up quickly.

> A model forecasting the economic impact of plant-based eating in the United Kingdom and Belgium demonstrated that if just 10% of the population shifted towards a diet high in vegetables and low in or devoid of red meat, societal savings could amount to 1.55 billion Euros and 7.83 billion Pounds over two decades. These savings stem from reduced healthcare costs associated with dietary-related diseases and lower environmental restoration expenses [7].

Employment within the plant-based sector not only contributes to a growing and vital part of the economy but also offers tangible financial benefits for workers. On average, individuals employed in the plant-based industry earn approximately $59,300 annually. This figure is notably $12,500 more than the average American income [8]

### Enhancing Health through Plant-Based Diets

The adoption of plant-based diets is increasingly recognized for its profound health benefits, particularly in the prevention and management of chronic diseases. Compelling scientific evidence underscores the effectiveness of plant-centric eating patterns in lowering the risk of heart disease, diabetes, obesity, and high blood pressure, thereby contributing to overall health and well-being.

- A healthy plant-based diet primarily focuses on the consumption of nutrient-dense plant foods while minimizing the intake of processed foods, oils, and animal products, including dairy and eggs. Such diets encourage the consumption of a wide array of vegetables (both cooked and raw), fruits, beans, peas, lentils, soy products, as well as seeds and nuts in moderation. These foods are rich in essential nutrients while generally being low in fat, contributing to a host of health benefits.  

Individuals who follow plant-based diets tend to have lower body mass index (BMI), blood pressure, HbA1C (a measure of blood sugar levels over time), and cholesterol levels. There is also evidence to suggest that adopting a plant-based diet can reduce the number of medications required to treat chronic diseases [9]. Vegetarians in one study had a 24% reduction in ischemic heart disease death rates when compared with non-vegetarians [8].

- A future scenario modeled for 2050 indicates that adopting vegan or vegetarian diets could not only reduce global mortality by 6-10% but also lead to annual savings of $1 trillion in healthcare and lost work productivity [7]

## Tips
- Infuse Flavor with Herbs and Spices: One of the secrets to creating tasty and satisfying meat-free meals is to experiment with a variety of herbs and spices. These can transform simple ingredients like vegetables, beans, and grains into a symphony of flavors. For example, roasting vegetables with thyme, rosemary, and garlic not only enhances their natural sweetness but also adds depth to the dish. Similarly, incorporating spices like cumin, coriander, and smoked paprika into bean stews or vegetable curries can bring a satisfying complexity that makes the absence of meat hardly noticeable. Many spices and herbs are also really good for!
- Look for vegetables and fruits that are in season and on sale. Choose produce that is fresh picked and ripe for maximum nutritional value.
- Shop your local farmers markets, coops, or join a CSA for nutrient dense, local food

# Actions

### Week 1: The Big Switch

- Focus on Whole Foods: when purchasing food at the grocery store, skip the meat and look for unprocessed whole foods.
- Change your relationship with meat: finish off any meat in your fridge by using it as a garnish or enhancement instead of the center point of your dish
- Check your favorite restaurants: look at the menus at your usual places and identify what meat free options sound good or you already enjoy. 

### Week 2: Expanding your repertoire

- Explore new recipes: check out vegan/vegetarian food blogs or recipe books. Libraries can be a great way to try a new cookbook before committing to buy it. Ask friends and coworkers for recommendations.
- Find new Restaurants: If you feel like your dining out options are limited, check out some new restaurants in your area. There are many new plant based options out there. 
- Experiment with meat alternatives like tofu, tempeh, or plant-based burgers for your protein source. Incorporate more vegetables into your meals and experiment with different cooking methods, such as roasting, steaming, or stir-frying.

### Week 3: Discover New Flavours
- Experiment with herbs, spices, and sauces to enhance the flavor of your plant-based meals. Try using fresh herbs like basil, cilantro, or mint.
- Explore international vegetarian cuisines, such as Indian, Middle Eastern, or Mediterranean. These cultures have a rich tradition of vegetarian cooking.
- Challenge yourself to create a meat-free version of a traditionally meat-centric dish, such as vegetarian lasagna or mushroom stroganoff.

### Week 4: Solidify Your Habits
- Plan and prep your meals for the week ahead. Stock up on pantry staples like beans, lentils, canned tomatoes, and spices to make cooking easier.
- Share your meat-free journey with friends and family. Host a plant-based dinner party or bring a vegetarian dish to a potluck to showcase delicious meat-free options.
- Reflect on your experience transitioning to a meat-free diet. Celebrate your successes and identify any challenges or areas for improvement. Set goals for maintaining your meat-free lifestyle going forward.

## Guardian article on Meat consumption in the US
Source: https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2023/oct/20/beef-usda-climate-crisis-meat-consumption

> [!US biggest consumer of meat]
> The US is the [biggest consumer](https://www.ers.usda.gov/topics/animal-products/cattle-beef/sector-at-a-glance/) of beef in the world, but, according to new research, it’s actually a small percentage of people who are doing most of the eating. A recent [study](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/15/17/3795) shows that on any given day, just 12% of people in the US account for half of all beef consumed in the US.


**So who is this 12% consuming all that beef?**

> Men and people between the ages of 50 and 65 were more likely to be in what the researchers dubbed as “disproportionate beef eaters”, defined as those who, based on a recommended daily 2,200 calorie-diet, eat more than four ounces – the rough equivalent of more than one hamburger – daily. The study analyzed one-day dietary snapshots from over 10,000 US adults over a four-year period.


- White people were among those more likely to eat more beef, compared with other racial and ethnic groups like Black and Asian Americans. 
- Older adults, college graduates, and those who looked up MyPlate, the US Department of Agriculture’s (USDA) online nutritional educational campaign, were far less likely to consume a disproportionate amount of beef.

> The USDA recommends eating no more than 4oz (113 grams) of all meat, poultry and egg products. On average, teenage boys consume more meat, poultry and eggs than is recommended by these guidelines, and for adult men, the distance from the recommendations is even greater, the study reveals.

Experts say there are deep historical and cultural reasons why beef intake is higher for men and boys.

“There’s this connection between meat consumption and masculinity,” said Joshua Specht, author of the book Red Meat Republic: A Hoof-to-Table History of How [Beef](https://www.theguardian.com/food/beef) Changed America. “Historically, to be a successful man in America meant eating beef.”

> Making a positive impact on the climate doesn’t necessarily mean giving up all meat – even reductions and substitutions can make a difference. A [study](https://ajcn.nutrition.org/article/S0002-9165(22)00147-2/fulltext) from last year showed that people who replaced chicken for beef decreased their carbon dioxide emissions from food by 48% each day.
