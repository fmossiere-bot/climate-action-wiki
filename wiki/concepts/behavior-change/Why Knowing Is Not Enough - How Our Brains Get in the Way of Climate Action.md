# Why People Know About Climate Change But Do Not Act

Most people across Europe and beyond accept that climate change is real. Many say it worries them. Yet the gap between awareness and action remains wide. The question is not whether people understand the problem. The question is why understanding rarely translates into meaningful behaviour change.

The answer lies, at least partly, in how the human brain works.

---

### Our Brains Were Not Built for This Kind of Threat

Human beings evolved to respond to immediate, visible dangers. A predator nearby. A rival tribe. A failed harvest. These are threats with a clear cause, a clear consequence, and a short time horizon.

Climate change does not fit that pattern. It is slow-moving, statistically complex, and geographically diffuse. It asks us to act now for benefits that will be felt decades from now, by people not yet born, in places we may never visit.

Psychologists have identified over 150 cognitive biases that shape human judgment and behaviour. Several of them are particularly relevant when it comes to climate inaction.

---

### Four Biases That Work Against Climate Action

**Hyperbolic discounting**

This is the tendency to value the present more than the future. If someone offers you 100 euros today or 120 euros in a month, most people take the money now. Applied to climate, this means the costs of acting today feel more real and immediate than the benefits of acting, which will only materialise over years or decades. Our evolutionary wiring simply did not need to account for that kind of trade-off.

**Limited concern for future generations**

Research in evolutionary psychology suggests that our sense of responsibility tends to extend only a few generations in each direction, from great-grandparents to great-grandchildren. Beyond that, the emotional connection fades. This does not mean people are indifferent to the future, but it does mean that long-range consequences rarely carry the same weight as near-term ones. Climate change, at its most severe, threatens generations well beyond that narrow window.

**The bystander effect**

In a group, the larger the number of people present, the less likely any individual is to take action. This is not selfishness. It is a cognitive default rooted in social efficiency: in small groups, roles are clear. Someone else will handle it. In the context of climate change, this effect is amplified to a global scale. With billions of people involved, the sense of personal responsibility becomes diffuse. Most people assume governments, corporations, or scientists are handling it.

**The sunk-cost fallacy**

Once significant time, money, or effort has been invested in a particular path, people find it very hard to change course, even when the evidence suggests they should. This helps explain the persistent resistance to moving away from fossil fuels. Entire economies, industries, and political identities have been built around them. Changing direction feels like admitting failure, even when continuing becomes the greater risk.

---

### The Problem of Scale

These biases interact with a structural challenge: climate change requires collective action at a scale that goes far beyond what human social cognition evolved to handle.

Anthropological research on social group sizes, often referred to as Dunbar's Number, suggests that individuals can maintain meaningful relationships with roughly 150 people. Beyond that, trust and coordination become much harder. Climate action, by contrast, requires coordinated behaviour across millions or billions of people, many of whom will never meet.

This does not mean collective action is impossible. It means the conditions for it need to be designed carefully.

---

### What Can Actually Work

**Small groups and local framing**

When people address climate issues in small, community-level settings, the dynamics shift. Initiatives that connect climate impacts to local concerns, such as agriculture, tourism, fishing, or public health, tend to generate more engagement than abstract global messaging. People respond to what they can see and relate to.

**Ownership of solutions**

When communities or individuals are involved in developing solutions rather than simply receiving instructions, they tend to value and follow through on those solutions more. This is sometimes called the endowment effect: we invest more in things we feel we helped create.

**Social comparison**

People adjust their behaviour based on what they see others doing. Research on energy consumption has shown that households reduce their usage when they can see how their consumption compares to their neighbours. The social signal carries more weight than the environmental argument alone.

**Positive framing**

How a message is framed matters. People respond better to messages that describe a desirable future than to ones that emphasise catastrophe. "A shift to clean energy could prevent tens of thousands of premature deaths" tends to motivate more than "we are heading towards collapse." This is not about avoiding the truth. It is about communicating in a way that opens people up to action rather than closing them down through anxiety or helplessness.

---

### A Note on Individual Action and Systemic Change

It is worth being clear about something often left unsaid in this kind of discussion. Individual behaviour change matters, but it is not sufficient on its own. The cognitive biases described above affect everyone, including the people in positions to make systemic decisions. Policies, regulations, and economic incentives shape the environment in which individual choices are made.

Focusing only on individual action risks placing an unfair burden on people who have limited power over the systems they live in. The most effective approaches tend to work at both levels at once: helping individuals act within their sphere of influence, while also pushing for the structural changes that make sustainable choices the default rather than the exception.
