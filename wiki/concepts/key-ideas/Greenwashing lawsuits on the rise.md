New York Attorney General Letitia James, known for her successful fraud case against Donald Trump and corruption case against the NRA, has now turned her attention to JBS, the world's largest beef producer.

Letitia James is perhaps best known as the New York Attorney General who recently won a US$450 million fraud case against former President Donald Trump and a landmark corruption case against the National Rifle Association.

> This case, though, is about something else: greenwashing. James claims in a [summons](https://t.dripemail2.com/c/eyJhbGciOiJIUzI1NiJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJkZXRvdXIiLCJpc3MiOiJtb25vbGl0aCIsInN1YiI6ImRldG91cl9saW5rIiwiaWF0IjoxNzEzMTkzNDk4LCJuYmYiOjE3MTMxOTM0OTgsImFjY291bnRfaWQiOiIxMjQwNTY4IiwiZGVsaXZlcnlfaWQiOiI2dHdoMGxtdHRtN25iY2V0MGQ3cSIsInVybCI6Imh0dHBzOi8vYWcubnkuZ292L3NpdGVzL2RlZmF1bHQvZmlsZXMvY291cnQtZmlsaW5ncy9qYnMtY29tcGxhaW50LnBkZj9fX3M9ejY4MmE3ODhzOGc2NzI4dGZmc3EmdXRtX3NvdXJjZT1kcmlwJnV0bV9tZWRpdW09ZW1haWwmdXRtX2NhbXBhaWduPU5ld3NsZXR0ZXIrLStBcHJpbCsxNSJ9.h_kgmhsWd3Yn6YBFcZpem1fLZ7jfaOkd7EzGQY-gA6c) that JBS is misleading consumers with claims it could create "bacon, chicken wings and steak with net-zero emissions." ("Net-zero" means overall they wouldn't produce more of the heat-trapping gases that are driving climate change.) Such ads, James says, "in effect, provide environmentally conscious consumers with a ‘license’ to eat beef."  

The problem, according to James, is that JBS is making these claims even though it knows that it has no feasible way of achieving them.  

JBS already carries a troubled track record. The company has paid hundreds of millions in fines in the US and Brazil for bribery and price-fixing, and more than a dozen US senators tried to block it from listing on the New York Stock Exchange, citing its "extensive international corruption record."

This case, however, is about greenwashing. James claims JBS misled consumers by advertising "bacon, chicken wings and steak with net-zero emissions" despite having no credible plan to achieve that goal. Such adverts, she argues, give environmentally conscious consumers a false licence to keep eating beef.

The scientific challenge behind her case is significant. Cattle produce methane, a potent greenhouse gas, through a digestive process called enteric fermentation. Attempts to reduce it, such as feeding cows seaweed, have not yet proven effective at scale. JBS is also the largest buyer of cattle from the Amazon region, making it a major driver of deforestation there. In 2021, the company reported emissions of 71 million tonnes of CO₂ equivalent, more than Ireland's total national emissions that year.

According to a 2023 FAO report, livestock and related land-use change accounted for around 12% of all human-caused greenhouse gas emissions in 2015. The lawsuit will ask a court to decide whether there is any technology or farming system that could ever bring those emissions to zero.

## 2025: A Landmark Year for Climate Litigation

2025 marked the tenth anniversary of the Urgenda case, when a Dutch court first ordered a state to cut its emissions faster to protect citizens from climate change. In the decade since, climate litigation has become a global legal architecture. The Guardian counted at least 13 significant wins in 2025 alone, spanning greenwashing verdicts, illegal fossil fuel approvals, and international court rulings.

Key cases that changed the rules:

- **Rosebank and Jackdaw (UK)** — Two North Sea oil and gas fields ruled illegal because the approval process did not account for scope 3 emissions from burning the extracted fuel.
- **TotalEnergies (France)** — A Paris court found TotalEnergies's "reinvention" marketing campaign made misleading net zero claims while continuing fossil fuel expansion.
- **Apple Watch (Germany)** — Frankfurt court ruled Apple could not call its Watch carbon neutral; Apple subsequently dropped the label in other markets too.
- **JBS and Tyson settlements (US)** — JBS paid $1.1 million to New York state; Tyson agreed to drop net zero 2050 claims. Both settled after lawsuits challenged false climate marketing in the meat industry.
- **EnergyAustralia (Australia)** — Settled with Parents for Climate; publicly acknowledged that carbon offsets do not prevent or undo greenhouse gas damage. First Australian greenwashing case against a utility.
- **Three Norwegian oilfields unlawful** — A Norwegian court of appeal declared licences for three North Sea fields illegal for not accounting for full climate impacts.
- **New South Wales coalmine expansion blocked** — Mount Pleasant Optimisation project annulled for failing to account for scope 3 (overseas combustion) emissions.
- **International Court of Justice advisory opinion** — Countries must prevent harm to the climate system; failure could result in compensation obligations.
- **Inter-American Court of Human Rights** — Found there is a human right to a stable climate and states have a duty to protect it.
- **UK forced to publish tougher climate plan** — After its original net zero strategy was ruled unlawful, the government published a revised carbon budget delivery plan committing to decarbonise electricity by 2030.

The pattern is consistent: courts are increasingly holding governments and companies accountable not just for what they emit, but for what they claim. Scope 3 emissions, once invisible in environmental assessments, are now a legal liability.
