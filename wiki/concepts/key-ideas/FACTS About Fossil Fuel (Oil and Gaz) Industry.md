## Global Contest - Fossil fuel finance is still booming

In the years since the Paris Agreement, the world’s 60 biggest banks have financed fossil fuels to the tune of $3.8 trillion. The nonprofit group [Influence Map](https://influencemap.org/report/Finance-and-Climate-Change-17639) reports that the 30 largest publicly listed financial institutions in the world have collectively enabled more than $740 billion in primary financing to the fossil fuel industry in 2020 and 2021 alone, all are members of industry associations lobbying against climate policy, and, _29 of the 30 have committed to net-zero._ Advocacy group Rainforest Action Network [details](https://www.ran.org/campaign/stop-banks-funding-climate-chaos/) which banks around the world have provided the most financing to the fossil fuel industry, as well as which have committed to phasing out fossil fuel financing. 

It's not only private money that's the problem. The [International Monetary Fund calculated](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2021/oct/06/fossil-fuel-industry-subsidies-of-11m-dollars-a-minute-imf-finds) that total fossil-fuel subsidies provided by governments in 2020 were **$5.9 trillion**, or almost 7 percent of global gross domestic product (GDP), and you can see where this funding went in the graph to the right. 

> But it’s not all terrible news. At least 53 countries reformed their fossil-fuel subsidies between 2015 and 2020, [according to the Global Subsidies Initiative](http://www.iisd.org/gsi/subsidy-watch-blog/53-ways-reform-fossil-fuel-consumer-subsidies-and-pricing) (GSI). If you're interested in understanding the nitty-gritty of fossil fuel subsidies, you can read [this paper](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-021-02847-2) in _Nature_.  We dive deeper into fossil fuel financing in the next class on risks. 

**Optional further reading:** As [_The Guardian_ reports](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2022/oct/31/two-thirds-of-us-money-for-fossil-fuel-pours-into-africa-despite-climate-goals), "US funding of fossil fuel energy projects in Africa  ramped up after the Paris agreement." And _Guardian_ [columnist George Monbiot argues](https://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2022/nov/30/amazon-public-money-earth-destruction-fossil-fuels-subsidies) "The more destructive the business, the more likely it is to enjoy political protection... We can protect the living world or we can protect the companies destroying it. We cannot do both."

## The TRANSITION Risk of the Fossil Fuel Industry

In a 2020 report titled [Decline and Fall: The Size & Vulnerability of the Fossil Fuel System](https://carbontracker.org/reports/decline-and-fall/), Carbon Tracker finds the following...

Globally, the three main fossil fuel assets are:  
 

- 900 billion tonnes of coal, oil, and gas, valued by the World Bank at $39 trillion
- Supply infrastructure of $10 trillion and demand infrastructure (electricity, transport, and heavy industry) of $22 trillion, and
- Financial markets with $18 trillion of equity, $8 trillion of traded bonds, and up to four times as much in unlisted debt.  
     

Each of these assets has an associated "flow." The key flows of the system are:  
 

- $1-3 trillion a year in economic rent to the petrostates from fossil fuels
- Capital expenditure of $1 trillion on supply infrastructure and $3-4 trillion on demand infrastructure, and
- Profits of $1-2 trillion.

![[Screenshot 2023-11-16 at 08.53.17.png]]

Carbon Tracker's analysis finds that the _flows_ of economic rents, capital expenditure and profit are damaged first, _before the assets_. And these losses are anticipated by equity markets long before they show up in the write-down of physical assets. Those companies, therefore, involved in expanding the fossil fuel system are deeply exposed to the consequences of peaking demand. The study identifies $6 trillion of equity in sectors which are expanding the system, from oil exploration to gas turbines to diesel cars.

Indeed, [some research suggests](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41560-021-00934-2) in a net-zero transition, half the world's fossil fuel assets would be worthless by 2036. As [_The Guardian_ puts it here](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/ng-interactive/2021/nov/04/fossil-fuel-assets-worthless-2036-net-zero-transition):

##### _"Countries that are slow [to decarbonise](https://www.theguardian.com/business/2021/oct/05/the-uk-2035-net-zero-electricity-target-how-could-it-be-achieved) will suffer but early movers will profit; the study finds that renewables and freed-up investment will more than make up for the losses to the global economy."_

Some other relevant analysis:

- Fitch Ratings, the credit ratings and financial research company, finds that stranded assets could [slash countries' credit ratings](https://www.reuters.com/article/us-climate-change-ratings-sovereigns-idUSKBN2AF1JO).
- While loan markets have started paying some attention and [penalizing coal](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2021-04-18/loan-markets-are-pricing-in-climate-transition-penalizing-coal), we need to be thinking about other fossil fuels as well: [Gas is the new stranded asset](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2021-04-17/gas-is-the-new-coal-with-risk-of-100-billion-in-stranded-assets?sref=FSjtyMb8) with $100 billion of investment at risk.
- A report from Finance Watch found [dozens of banks are exposed](https://twitter.com/forfinancewatch/status/1577198401867116547?s=20&t=QqGKXem2LlRnPc4391M3fQ) with some $1.35 trillion in fossil fuel assets that will crater in value in the transition to net zero.
