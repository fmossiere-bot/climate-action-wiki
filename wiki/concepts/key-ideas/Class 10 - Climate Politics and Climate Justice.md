### What about COP 28

The outcome of COP 28 at the end of 2023 might politely be described as a mixed bag.

**The good:** Countries [established](https://www.reuters.com/business/environment/cop28-summit-opens-with-hopes-early-deal-climate-damage-fund-2023-11-30/) a "loss and damage" fund—essentially a means of channeling funding from rich nations who bear most of the responsibility for climate change to poorer countries bearing the brunt of its impacts. (As the chart here illustrates, so far nowhere near enough money has flowed toward dealing with this problem. We discuss this in much more detail in our module on Countries’ Climate Responsibilities and Net-Zero Plans.) Delegates had earlier agreed to this idea in the waning hours of COP 27 in Egypt in late 2022, after years of pressure, but ironing out the details took considerable negotiation and represents a meaningful step.

**The bad:** A years-overdue [agreement](https://unfccc.int/documents/636608) eventually shook out calling for "transitioning away from fossil fuels… in a just, orderly and equitable manner." For comparison, in Glasgow in 2021, nations agreed to "phase down" (not phase out) coal (leaving natural gas and oil aside). But island nations were not impressed: “The process has failed us,” said Samoan negotiator Anne Rasmussen [in the _New York Times_](https://www.nytimes.com/2023/12/13/climate/cop28-climate-agreement.html), complaining that the deal in Dubai passed while dozens of island nations weren’t in the room: “The course correction that is needed has not been secured.” (Indeed, the nonbinding deal contains “a litany of loopholes,” [according to](https://www.reuters.com/business/environment/alliance-small-island-states-sees-a-litany-loopholes-cop28-text-2023-12-13/) the Alliance of Small Island States.)

**The ugly:** The news coming out of Dubai was repeatedly marred by reports that the president of the climate summit, Dr. Sultan Ahmed Al-Jaber—who is also head of the Abu Dhabi National Oil Company—had [denied the science](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/live/2023/dec/04/cop28-backlash-after-president-claims-no-science-behind-fossil-fuel-phase-out) behind phasing out fossil fuels—and that the host nation had [planned to make oil deals](https://www.bbc.com/news/science-environment-67508331) with other countries on the side. More than [2,400 people in the oil and gas industries](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2023/dec/05/record-number-of-fossil-fuel-lobbyists-get-access-to-cop28-climate-talks) were in attendance. (We discuss the significance of corporate responsibility in a subsequent module.)

### Context and caveats
#fossilfuel 
Even as countries need to vastly reduce their reliance on fossil fuels, they're on track to produce "around  110% more fossil fuels in 2030 than would be consistent with limiting warming to 1.5°C, and 69% more than would be consistent with 2°C," according to a [2023 report](https://www.unep.org/resources/production-gap-report-2023) from the U.N. Environment Programme.

This is a major concern because, ideally, we'd already be [_reducing_ emissions by 7 to 8 percent percent a year](https://www.unep.org/news-and-stories/press-release/cut-global-emissions-76-percent-every-year-next-decade-meet-15degc). This rate increases each year we don’t move in this direction. 

We should also note it's possible, as eminent climate scientist James Hansen and others argue, that [the climate is much more sensitive to carbon emissions](https://academic.oup.com/oocc/article/3/1/kgad008/7335889) than previously thought—meaning still more drastic action is needed, and faster.

## Defining Politics, Policies, and Justice: A Primer

small chapter ---  see [LINK](https://textbook.terra.do/class/m-politics-and-justice-3)  for details. 

- **POLITICS** - exercise and operation of Power - central role of power. 
- **POLICY** - principle of action adopted or proposed by a governement. Might not always turn into laws, or orders ...
- **JUSTICE** -  Climate Justice is concerned most deeply with Fairness. It relates to equality, Human rights, collective rights

### Politics
When I use the word "politics," I mean the exercise and operation of power. When I say "power," I mean the capacity or ability to direct or influence the behavior of others or the course of events.

In 2021, with global emissions still ticking up after decades of international meetings, frameworks, action plans, and so on, [a team of researchers asked _why_](https://www.annualreviews.org/doi/full/10.1146/annurev-environ-012220-011104)—and found that "a common thread that emerges across the reviewed literature is the **central role of power**, manifest in many forms, from a dogmatic political-economic hegemony and influential vested interests to narrow techno-economic mindsets and ideologies of control. " _[Emphasis ours.]_

Note that politics operates through concrete material acts, like taking or giving money. It also operates ideologically—if you believe one set of ideas of how a country should be run or how climate change should be solved, it's _always_ (yes, _always_) at the exclusion of other ways of thinking about a complex issue or problem.

### Policy

We briefly discussed policies in our modules on economics. Here's a quick refresher: A "policy," according to the Oxford dictionary, is "a course or principle of action adopted or proposed by a government, party, business, or individual." Yes, that's wondrously vague! So let's focus on what it means in terms of climate change and governments.

> Governments can adopt a range of different policies to reduce their country's greenhouse gas emissions. You can think of governmental climate policies as laws, though often they are just orders to other organizations like electric utilities to do something a certain way.

All policies have associated costs and benefits that tend to get distributed differently across society. This is very important to keep in mind—**there are always, _always_ equity issues embedded in any policy decision:** Who bears the costs and who gets the benefits is a critical question one should always ask when thinking about policies.

Again, there is no new policy _ever_ without winners and losers. It's amazing how often even smart analysts and researchers forget this, or try to showcase how they are creating "win-win" policies.

> [!WHAT IS NET-ZERO COMMITMENT]
> Per a [United Nations definition](http://news.un.org/en/story/2020/12/1078612), "net-zero means we are not adding new emissions to the atmosphere. Emissions will continue, but will be balanced by absorbing an equivalent amount from the atmosphere."  
> Achieving this involves two major components:
> 1.  _Reduce emissions_ to as close to zero as possible, and
> 2. Balance out any remaining emissions with carbon removal,_ through natural means like planting trees, as well as through technology like Direct Air Capture.

## SUMMARY
- Global climate negotiations have been underway since 1992. The most recent important treaty signed by 195 countries is called The Paris Agreement. Under the Paris Agreement, countries proposed their own plans for mitigation and adaptation, called Nationally Determined Contributions, or NDCs.   
- While this treaty is an incredible global achievement in some ways, it also falls short of getting us the types of emissions reductions we need to stay within a 1.5 C temperature increase target. The treaty also lacks "teeth" in that there is no global enforcement mechanism for ensuring that countries stick to their plans.  
- On the positive side, many countries have recently announced net-zero climate goals, and while the devil is in the details when it comes to meaningful action, recent action like the U.S. Inflation Reduction Act is encouraging.  
- At COP27, in November 2022, a pivotal issue was "loss and damage," or channeling funding from richer nations that bear more responsibility for climate change historically to help more vulnerable countries bearing the brunt of its impacts
- Central to global negotiations and to climate action in general is the idea of justice. Who is most responsible for climate change, who suffers the impacts disproportionately, and who should pay for mitigation and adaptation? These questions form the core of debates around climate justice. There is also the critically important issue of who gets a say in decision-making: Questions of representation in negotiations and policy-making are as important as the issue of what gets decided.  
- In the global negotiations, the UN climate framework adopted a principle called Common But Differentiated Responsibilities and Respective Capabilities (CBDR-RC) as the basis for dividing responsibility for global climate change. As per this principle, developed countries (called Annex I countries) bear a heavier share of responsibility for emissions reductions than developing countries (non-Annex I countries). This principle was watered down in the Paris Agreement, and the current global conversation around justice has shifted to one around "loss and damage."

###### ‘It’s a nightmare’: Landlords in a bind as France imposes tough new emissions rules

>Landlords across France are grappling with looming restrictions on renting out poorly insulated apartments and homes under strict new rules that phase in gradually from 2023. They aim to incentivise owners to renovate roughly two-thirds of France’s 37 million homes by 2050.

President [Emmanuel Macron](https://www.irishtimes.com/tags/emmanuel-macron/)’s government argues that widescale renovations are needed to address a big source of greenhouse gas emissions: buildings account for almost half of French energy consumption and a third of carbon emissions, while two-thirds of that total comes from homes. Experts say his government’s intervention is unique in Europe.


> [!What is the Policy Scope]
> To incentivise renovations, owners are required to get properties rated for energy efficiency, classifying them from A (best) to G (worst), before they can be sold or rented out. The regulations offer both carrot and stick: poorly insulated homes will gradually be excluded from the rental market over the next decade, but public money is available to help pay for insulation, new windows or upgraded heating.
> 
> Landlords of F- and G-rated homes may no longer raise rents, and as of this year, the worst of the G-rated properties cannot be rented to new tenants.
> 
> By 2028, 5.2 million homes rated F and G, or 17 per cent of total housing stock, will become ineligible for rental. By 2034, all E and D rated properties will be excluded, amounting to more than half of all homes.
