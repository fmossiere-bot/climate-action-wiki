## SUMMARY
**BASIC ECONOMY THEORY**
- The theory of free markets as defined by the self-regulation of supply and demand to produce efficient pricing and allocation of goods falls apart when it comes to environmental problems.  
     
- According to conventional economic theory, environmental problems like climate change are considered market failures.  
     
- In this particular type of market failure, the costs of climate change are not captured in the costs of the goods or services that produce greenhouse gases. These goods and services include things like electricity (from fossil fuels), food (from industrial agriculture, especially beef production), transportation (in cars or airplanes), etc.  
     
- Rather, these costs are imposed on society in the form of negative externalities (e.g. the cost of a disaster, or coastal erosion).

**POLICY RAMIFICATIONS**
- it is the job of government to solve Market Failure. Government can intervene through regulations or market-based approaches
- Market based are popular and work by setting a price for Carbon. Most common one is ETA

**ECONOMICS IS AN IMPERFECT, INCOMPLETE TOOL AGAINST CLIMATE**
- There are many carbon markets in operation or in design today, but the vast majority have a carbon price that's too low for us to stay within 1.5 C globally.  
     
- There are deeper critiques of market-based approaches too. These have to do with the difficulties of putting any price on potentially catastrophic outcomes, and how we value the future versus the present. Some environmentalists think you cannot use the same economic tools that created the problem (namely, market-based approaches to development) to solve it.  
     
- There is an emerging consensus among government officials, academics, and the private sector that we need both regulation and markets to achieve the massive GHG emissions reductions we need. Private actors, especially large corporations, can play a key role here

Carbon Pricing is a market response to a Market Failure, similar to articial deflation![[Screenshot 2023-10-19 at 15.56.26.png]]**On paper, the carbon price is a neat solution. But flicking a switch in EnROADS is a different story than implementing one in practice.  
Beyond all the problems with calculating the appropriate carbon price:  
- Countries and regions more reliant on fossil fuels are likely to face higher pressure to delay, seek exceptions (e.g. aviation and shipping) or implement a too-low price  
- Pressure can come both from companies/industry lobbyists as well as labour unions–see e.g. the Gilets Jaunes protests in France  
- Even if a price is implemented, there is the risk of “carbon leakage” whereby companies shift their polluting activities abroad  
- Then there are equity and justice issues–since the cost is often passed on to consumers, it can result in e.g. higher energy bills, which could hit the poorest hardest
