**Understand our Net-Zero target for 2050

# What is Net-Zero

Put simply, net zero means cutting greenhouse gas emissions to as close to zero as possible, with any remaining emissions re-absorbed from the atmosphere, by oceans and forests for instance.
Source: [https://www.un.org/en/climatechange/net-zero-coalition](https://www.un.org/en/climatechange/net-zero-coalition) 
Net zero refers to a state in which the greenhouse gases going into the atmosphere are balanced by removal out of the atmosphere. Or put it differently ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_zero_emissions)), all emissions human-caused emissions are compensated by human cause Carbon removals. 

- It is different from other terminology we might see like carbon neutral, climate neutral, GHG neutral. See this [Oxford article](https://netzeroclimate.org/what-is-net-zero-2/) for details on the terminology.
- Net-Zero It is the agreed framework for our climate ambitions. 
- Net-Zero is different from Zero: It is unlikely we can cut ALL emissions entirely, although this the the pledge some countries are taking. As well as deep and widespread cuts in emissions, we will likely need to scale up removals


Source: [https://netzeroclimate.org/what-is-net-zero-2/](https://netzeroclimate.org/what-is-net-zero-2/)

Net-zero will be achieved by de-carbonising most sector, especially the energy sector, while also increasing our ability to remove Carbon. 
Reaching net zero is characterised by the balancing of carbon flows into and out of the atmosphere, through the reduction of emissions and carbon removals.

To stabilise global temperatures in the long-term, we need to go further than this, in achieving durable net zero. This builds upon the original concept of net zero (which considers carbon flows into and out of the atmosphere only), and requires net anthropogenic flows between the lithosphere, atmosphere and the land and ocean biosphere to equal zero. This will require further emission cuts and for carbon removals to be further scaled up

# When we reach Net-Zero, we will stop global Warming

Source: [Carbon Brief](https://www.carbonbrief.org/explainer-will-global-warming-stop-as-soon-as-net-zero-emissions-are-reached/#:~:text=The%20best%20available%20evidence%20shows,as%20a%20new%20scientific%20finding.) 
YES. immediately. 
The reason for this is that the net-zero pledge requires us to remove all carbon we add, through long-term Carbon removal techniques such as Biomass and geological storage. 

All added GHG will in fact be removed, and the existing natural sink (natural Carbon Cycle) will continue to remove 5.5Gt per year, removing existing Carbon from the atmosphere, and lowering  the concentration. 

Net zero emissions will result in constantly decreasing concentrations of CO2 in the atmosphere because the ocean and land sinks will continue to absorb about 5.5 GtC per year. In other words 0 GtC - 5.5GtC = -5.5GtC, or about 2 ppm per year. Interestingly, in terms of temperature, this will be counterbalanced by oceans, so temperature change will stop

![](https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/LU8Q1IsqvqKNQWmh-mKxmoN9YoHKh75CVxE-PNY_9l-p43k2_Z3CKeaVUOd_n_XpsVP_Ms6soD8yNocYMXitbpAoc5u2aRD1ExiQDqNWpn00u0ovKSf-iYQnGnoA7dhtOn12PLhRqQNp5uOMrUngn7E)
  

The Earth is currently out of thermal equilibrium, meaning more energy from the sun is being trapped by the greenhouse gases in the atmosphere than is escaping back to space. Over 90% of this extra heat is going into warming the oceans. However, as the oceans continue to warm, they will take up less heat from the atmosphere and global average surface temperatures will rise further.

At the same time, the land and ocean are absorbing about half of the CO2 that humans emit each year. If emissions go to zero, these “carbon sinks” continue to take up some of the extra CO2 that was emitted in the past – quickly at first and then more slowly over time as they move toward a new equilibrium. This reduces the levels of CO2 in the atmosphere and, thus, the warming it causes. 

Constant concentration (meaning that we continue to emit as much as the natural sink can absorb) leads to an increase in temperature because we are not at equilibrium yet. Once the earth gets warm enough that it is emitting as much energy as it receives, then the rise in temp will stop

These “constant concentration” scenarios showed that there was additional warming “in the pipeline” as the oceans slowly warmed up to reach the same temperature as the atmosphere and brought the Earth back into radiative equilibrium. That is to say, where the amount of energy absorbed by the Earth from the sun is equal to the amount being reradiated back to space.

Models tended to suggest [0.4C](https://www.nature.com/articles/ngeo813) to [0.5C](https://static.scientificamerican.com/sciam/assets/media/pdf/hansen.pdf) or so of additional warming would occur over the next few centuries, if concentrations were kept at the same level. 
# The Global Carbon Cycle and Carbon Stock

The Global Carbon Cycle means that there are stock of carbon in the air, land and water, stocked for a varying lengths of time, and there are flux from one stock to another. 

- If a stock is emitting carbon into the atmosphere, it's called a "source:"
- If it removes, it is called a "sink"
- Today about 55% of Carbon is removed, or 5.5Gt of C per year. which leaves a Net extra Carbon of 5.2Gt, added to the existing stock of Carbon

Carbon and CO2 are different, while we often refer to CO2 emissions, this translate at the end of the day into Carbon which adds up into the atmosphere. As it adds up, the Concentration does too. The concentration of carbon in the atmosphere, along with other GHG, is what result in temperature increase, as the more concentration, the less Heat can escape, the more gets trapped and reflected back

1g C = 3.67g CO2

This means that the Carbon Cycle, and its negative overall impact, sinks about 20Gt of CO2e every year. 

Now, Carbon stays for hundreds of years in the atmosphere, it accumulates, and so as you add Carbon every year, the concentration increase.

We know how to convert concentration in CO2 into GT of Carbon: 1ppm = 2.13 GT C

Today, we have a 412ppm, which means we have a stock   878 Gt Carbon
# Carbon Budget

Today we estimate it to be < 420 Gt of CO2 over the next 10 years. But some argue it is much less than that. Number as low as 200 have been floating around.
