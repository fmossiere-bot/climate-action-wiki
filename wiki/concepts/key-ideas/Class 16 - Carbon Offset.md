## What are Carbon Offsets?
[LINK](https://textbook.terra.do/class/m-carbon-offsets-2)

_**"The terms**_ **carbon offset** _**and**_ **carbon offset credit** _**(or simply “offset credit”) are used interchangeably, though they can mean slightly different things. A**_ **carbon offset** _**broadly refers to a reduction in GHG emissions – or an increase in carbon storage (e.g., through land restoration or the planting of trees) – that is used to compensate for emissions that occur elsewhere. A**_ **carbon offset credit** _**is a transferrable instrument certified by governments or independent certification bodies to represent an emission reduction of one metric tonne of CO2, or an equivalent amount of other GHGs...**_

_**The purchaser of an offset credit can “retire” it to claim the underlying reduction towards their own GHG reduction goals... The key concept is that offset credits are used to**_ **convey** _**a net climate benefit from one entity to another. Because GHGs mix globally in the atmosphere, it does not matter where exactly they are reduced."**_

> The economics really matter here—the reason companies choose to do offsets (reduce or remove carbon _someplace else_) is because it is cheaper than achieving those same emissions reductions directly.

> " The projects that offset schemes support are vital: trees must be planted, existing forests and peatlands that hold and absorb carbon must be protected. Renewable energy and energy efficiency projects are critical and offset schemes can have a part to play in delivering sufficient funding and mechanisms at pace and scale:"

###### Carbon Offset in the form of Certified Emission Reduction (CERs - measured in CO2-e tonnes) can be bought and sold in markets

### Carbon Offset Projects
> Carbon offset credits are produced by discrete "projects" that reduce GHG emissions or remove carbon. Projects can be of any scale, and usually also claim to include "co-benefits," like improving local livelihoods, enhancing water quality, and protecting biodiversity.

In many cases, carbon offset projects produce social and environmental benefits beyond just GHG reductions. Depending on the project type, these “co-benefits” can include improvements to community employment opportunities; enhanced air or water quality; biodiversity and habitat conservation; improved energy access; and better access to community health and education services. Many offset credit buyers seek projects that yield a broad range of benefits. Carbon offsets can thus be part of a comprehensive strategy for corporate social responsibility, combining efforts to address climate change with contributions to other public goods.

### Carbon Offset Programs
> Carbon offset "programs" are types of markets that perform [one or more of these functions](https://www.offsetguide.org/understanding-carbon-offsets/carbon-offset-programs/): "(1) they develop and approve standards that set criteria for the quality of carbon offset credits; (2) they review offset projects against these standards (generally with the help of third-party verifiers); and (3) they operate registry systems that issue, transfer, and retire offset credits."


- Compliance offset programs are regulated by governmental organizations. These are the "market " used by companies who have to reduce their emissions
- Voluntary offset programs are run by businesses or NGOs.

### RECs, PPAs, Allowances and EECs

Some companies purchase other kinds of tradable instruments associated with GHG emission reduction claims or engage in transactions that include such claims. But, not all these options are equal in their effectiveness and environmental integrity.

In most cases, these purchases and transactions are _not_ subject to certification against standards required for effective carbon offset claims. Examples of some of these “emission reduction instruments” are listed below; follow the links for explanations of what they are and their suitability for making offset claims.

- [Renewable energy](https://www.offsetguide.org/understanding-carbon-offsets/other-instruments-for-claiming-emission-reductions/renewable-energy/)
- [Allowances](https://www.offsetguide.org/understanding-carbon-offsets/other-instruments-for-claiming-emission-reductions/allowances/)
- [Energy efficiency certificates](https://www.offsetguide.org/understanding-carbon-offsets/other-instruments-for-claiming-emission-reductions/energy-efficiency-certificates/)
- [Alternatives to purchasing](https://www.offsetguide.org/understanding-carbon-offsets/other-instruments-for-claiming-emission-reductions/alternatives-to-purchasing/)

> The findings in this guide are based on researched evidence. This evidence suggests that several commonly used instruments – including voluntary renewable energy certificates (RECs), green power purchases (PPAs), and energy efficiency certificates (EECs) – should be avoided by organizations whose primary goal is to achieve credible and quantifiable GHG reduction targets. See our [Green Power FAQ,](https://www.offsetguide.org/green-power-faq) for a detailed resource on voluntary purchasing green power claims and greenhouse gas accounting

## Challenges with Carbon Offsets
[LINK](https://textbook.terra.do/class/m-carbon-offsets-3)

> Scientists, activists and concerned citizens have started to voice their concerns over how carbon offsets have been used by polluters as a free pass for inaction. Annual emissions have to reduce by 29-32 gigatonnes of equivalent carbon dioxide (CO2e) by 2030 to maintain a fighting chance to stay below 1.5°C. This is a five-fold increase on current ambition.


The World of Carbon Offset faces 7 main challenges
### 1. The Atmosphere

###### To have a chance, we MUST stop 90% of our emissions. There is no substitute

We need ALL actors to remove carbon, no matter where they are. If one is financing another, doesn't matter, everyone needs to reduce. 

What this means for offsets is this: If the whole world needs to be reducing emissions _now,_  does it really make sense that some actors in one part of the world are claiming the right to continue to emit by stopping emissions elsewhere? Ideally, the actors at both ends of these transactions should be reducing emissions and storing carbon simultaneously. The only scientifically consistent net-zero program is one that follows what's depicted in the graph alongside: Reduce 90 percent of your emissions and then remove and store some carbon permanently on top of that

![[Screenshot 2023-11-06 at 10.36.17.png]]

### 2. Additionality
Additionality means that the project would not have happened without the offset program payments. 

It is very hard to prove that is the case. Here is an example of why:
> _Fundamentally, in order to believe in traditional offsets, one must buy into the validity and verifiability of a counterfactual. Let’s take an example: you own some trees containing 1 ton of carbon. I want to buy 1 ton of offsets so I can pollute 1 ton of carbon elsewhere. You sell me 1 ton of emission reduction traditional offsets, backed on the promise that not only you’ll protect the trees but that_ if I didn’t pay you, you would cut down the trees.

An offset, in theory, allows an organization to _continue to emit_ that same amount of emissions it claims that the offset project is reducing or removing. However, if the project is not really additional (the wind farm would have been built anyway as it's just good business, or the forest being conserved was in no danger of being cut down in the first place) it means that _no extra (additional) carbon was reduced or removed_ and so emissions on the whole INCREASED, instead of being canceled out.


### 3. Permanence
Permanence means that the offset project is likely to reduce emissions and store carbon in soils or trees for a long period of time. 
There are many ways in whcih a carbon offset porject might change, a windfarm is abandonned, a forest is destroyed:

> In the case of the 2020 U.S. West Coast wildfires, for instance, the Lionshead Fire in Oregon [destroyed one of the largest forest carbon offset projects](https://carbonplan.org/research/offset-project-fire) participating in California’s carbon market.

> Carbon removal costs are everywhere in climate discussions — this project costs 10, that one costs 200. Policymakers and corporate planners look to metrics to inform their decisions, and cost per ton of carbon dioxide ($/tCO₂) has become a near-universal unit of measure

	When two carbon removal projects each claim a $/tCO₂ cost, directly comparing their costs doesn’t tell a complete story if one has a temporary duration and the other is permanent

### 4. Double Counting

> If Brazil sells offset to Norway, it can't count the same forest protection towards it own national GHG accounting

The Paris Agreement represents the first time that every participating country pledged some level of emissions reductions. [This means](https://www.offsetguide.org/understanding-carbon-offsets/the-role-of-offsets-in-carbon-management-strategies/carbon-offsets-after-2020-the-world-under-paris/) "there are fewer opportunities for additional reductions – i.e., reductions that go beyond what countries have pledged."

While it is hoped that "robust accounting methods" will help solve this problem, in practice, there is no single registry that keeps tabs on every single climate project and claims on resulting carbon credits.  In fact, a [Bloomberg analysis found](https://twitter.com/AkshatRathi/status/1493230046970101766?s=20&t=EUvSKUwbYjnZ_ucOt6TxZw) that about half of all offsets sold in voluntary markets do not even have a buyer's name attached to them. And [there is no clarity](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/14693062.2021.1948384?src=recsys) on how the voluntary carbon market will align with the legal architecture of the Paris Agreement in a credible and legitimate way.
### 5 and 6: Leakage and Overestimation

> 5. Leakage: There is nothing to stop a project developer from taking funds to protect one piece of land and then using that money to deforest the next patch over to plan t oil palms or create a ranch. 

###### Leakage can be reduced by reducing our demand for Forest products like timber, palm oil and meat. 


> **6. Overestimation of credits** can happen due to the way the project baseline is set because emissions of the buyer are under-estimated, and due to some other reasons as well. [See pages 23-25 of this guide for more information](http://www.offsetguide.org/wp-content/uploads/2019/11/11.13.19.pdf).


### 7. Social and Environmental Harm

> Offset project should not jeopardize indigineous livelihoods or local culture and traditions, neither cause any harm on biodiversity and water resources

### Carbon offset projects = development projects?

While carbon offset projects may offer questionable climate benefits, those that are done well can improve local incomes, energy access, and other co-benefits that can improve the lives and livelihoods of communities around the world. 

Official funding from north to south for climate action is falling woefully short of where it needs to be. Money to help developing countries deal with the impacts of climate change has [historically fallen short](https://www.unep.org/resources/adaptation-gap-report-2022#:~:text=International%20adaptation%20finance%20flows%20to%20developing%20countries%20are%205%2D10%20times%20below%20estimated%20needs%20and%20the%20gap%20is%20widening.). Voluntary carbon markets are, in a small way, filling that gap while official bureaucracies drag their feet.

Even in the case of my own carbon offset project, the reality is that the funding alleviated a problem and allowed a small, cash-strapped solar company to serve its customers better.  

Voluntary projects also have some flexibility: The funds, when managed well, can go directly to poor communities and help them make a living protecting and enhancing natural resources. But proper management is crucial. A poorly conceived and badly run project can worsen the situation for communities on the ground if it leads to conflict between different groups—or if more influential actors capture the monetary flows and other benefits.

### The Oxford Offsetting Principles
introduce a Rigorous Standard, like the on from researchers in Oxford. You can [find the details in this PDF](http://www.smithschool.ox.ac.uk/sites/default/files/2022-01/Oxford-Offsetting-Principles-2020.pdf), but the general overview is this:
1. **Cut emissions, use high quality offsets, and regularly revise offsetting strategy** as best practice evolves. This means first, _prioritizing mitigation,_ thereby minimizing the need for offsets in the first place. It also means ensuring environmental integrity and maintaining transparency.
2. **Shift to carbon removal offsetting:** True offsets would _remove_ carbon from the atmosphere, so while emission reduction offsets can be important to support various projects, eventually we will need to concentrate on offsets which are truly negative. Over time, offsets should comprise more and more projects which remove rather than just reduce.
3. **Shift to long-lived storage:** Ultimately the carbon we remove from the atmosphere needs to remain stored for many years to be effective. Offsets that provide longer or permanent storage will need to be prioritized.
4. **Support the development of net-zero aligned offsetting:** Because the market for high quality offsets is immature, it needs support. Various strategies such as long term contracts provide certainty to offset developers, or enable sector-specific alliances. Further, this fourth principle states that a wide range of natural and semi-natural ecosystems should be protected and restored in their own right, not only for carbon sequestration

### Better "offsetting" in practice: Microsoft and Stripe

Microsoft and Stripe, as we mentioned, are two companies charting new paths around carbon offsets.

The first thing to note is that offsets are not central to either company's climate strategies. Microsoft has deep mitigation targets, including for its Scope 3 emissions, which it openly admits it is currently [struggling to reduce](https://blogs.microsoft.com/blog/2022/03/10/an-update-on-microsofts-sustainability-commitments-building-a-foundation-for-2030/). Stripe is not claiming to offset any of its own emissions. Instead, its climate strategy involves something [far more ambitious](https://stripe.com/climate): to transform the market for durable carbon removal by accelerating the early adoption of new technologies. ![[Screenshot 2023-12-22 at 10.59.37.png]]

Importantly, both companies are extremely transparent about their experience and have teamed up with researchers to share and publicize key learnings. You can read about Microsoft's experience and lessons on [CarbonPlan's website](https://carbonplan.org/research/microsoft-2021-insights) and also in [this article](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-021-02606-3) in _Nature_ co-authored by Microsoft staff and external researchers. And you can learn about Stripe's experience [here](https://carbonplan.org/research/stripe-2020-insights). 

Both companies have _struggled to find enough high-quality projects_ that meet their very stringent criteria—as seen in this graphic analyzing types of carbon removal proposals received by Microsoft, based on Oxford's permanence criteria). And additionality continues to be a problem, though as CarbonPlan's analysis of Stripe's projects [points out](https://carbonplan.org/research/stripe-2020-insights), "Additonality is essential to justify a carbon offset, but not particularly relevant to accelerating technological change.

### Alternatives for Smaller companies (not with the resources of Mivrosoft):
1. Start with the [Oxford Offsetting Principles](https://www.smithschool.ox.ac.uk/sites/default/files/2022-01/Oxford-Offsetting-Principles-2020.pdf), and get as close to these principles as possible. 

2. Use rigorous accounting protocols based on the latest science, like this [method](https://www.carbon-direct.com/insights/accounting-for-short-term-durability-in-carbon-offsetting) outlined by Carbon Direct for accounting for short-term durability in carbon offsets. ### A new approach for nature-based offsets: REDD+
In recognition of the critical role of tropical forests in mitigating climate change, the United Nations launched a program called [REDD+](https://redd.unfccc.int/) at COP 13 in 2007 as a way for developed countries to pay developing countries to protect their forests. This follows a previous initiative just called REDD.  
  
> REDD stands for "Reducing Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation." The "+" tacked on in 2007 extends the scheme to also include sustainable management of forests, as well as conserving and enhancing forest carbon stocks.

It's worth [noting](https://carboncredits.com/what-does-redd-plus-mean-everything-you-need-to-know/) that while "it hasn’t achieved its full potential yet as a large-scale funding mechanism to pay rainforest countries and communities for avoided forest emissions," as of early 2022 "more than 398 million REDD+ credits have been issued on the voluntary carbon market." And some research [suggests](https://news.mongabay.com/2019/09/redd-more-competitive-than-critics-believe-study-finds/) that, although profits from REDD+ might appear smaller than those from, say, leveling trees for logging and gold mining, the overall benefit to local communities from REDD+ could in fact be much greater.

**==What is REDD+?==** 
REDD+ has emerged as a framework and investment program to improve the way we do forestry-based projects that deliver credits for voluntary carbon markets. This program offers many opportunities for developing countries to conserve and expand their forests, but it also has challenges.

> REDD+ has the potential to channel funding to "countries that reduce emissions from deforestation and degradation, plus countries that continue to conserve large areas of intact forest, countries that plant new trees, and countries that use their forests sustainably," per [this helpful explainer on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?time_continue=129&v=8RBIz7SuAzY).

## SUMMARY
### Key takeaways:

- A carbon offset refers to a reduction in GHG emissions or an increase in carbon storage that is used to compensate for emissions that occur elsewhere. It is a controversial climate solution, yet the demand for carbon offsets is growing rapidly, as companies around the world commit to net-zero goals.   
     
- A carbon offset, in theory, equates a unit of emissions in one place with an equivalent unit of emissions avoided or removed elsewhere. The reality is much more complex. Offsets face significant challenges. At the highest level, one has to ask the question of whether there is any place for mitigation offsets in a world that needs to dramatically reduce emissions everywhere as soon as possible. Other practical challenges include additionality, permanence, double-counting, leakage, overestimation of credits, and additional social and environmental harm.

- There are better ways to do offsets, as outlined by the Oxford Offsetting Principles. The principles emphasize that organizations need to reduce their own emissions first, and that offsets should only comprise a small chunk of their carbon reduction goals. When offsets are used, they should be from storage projects that can deliver long-term durable carbon removal.  
     
- Microsoft and Stripe provide interesting examples of new approaches to "offsetting." But even they face a critical shortage of high-quality projects that meet their stringent criteria. Other alternatives include:
    - "Insetting"
    - Pursuing a "contribution" model
    - Pursuing "practice-based" credits  
         
- REDD+ has emerged as a framework and investment program to improve the way we do forestry-based projects that deliver credits for voluntary carbon markets. This program offers many opportunities for developing countries to conserve and expand their forests, but it also has challenges.   
      
- A bigger, more philosophical question is to ask what role carbon markets can and should play in climate action—and what alternatives exist. _What do you think?_

### Carbon-Offsetting schemes might not actually prevent deforestation
#forest 
*Source: [NewScientist](https://www.newscientist.com/article/2388848-carbon-offsetting-schemes-may-not-actually-prevent-deforestation/?utm_campaign=RSS%7CNSNS&utm_source=NSNS&utm_medium=RSS&utm_content=currents )*

> An analysis of 18 large carbon-offsetting projects has found that most don't significantly reduce deforestation compared with areas not covered by the schemes

- Those projects were all under the REDD+ Scheme, an international framework set up through the United Nations climate negotiations
- Overviewed by VERRA, the world leading provider of Carbon-Offsets

> A similar [investigation by the Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2023/jan/18/revealed-forest-carbon-offsets-biggest-provider-worthless-verra-aoe) also concluded that **94% of forest-related projects validated by Verra did not represent genuine carbon reductions**.

Carbon-offset schemes calculate their effectiveness based on predictions of how much forest would have been destroyed if the projects they support hadn’t been funded. Some researchers, including Kontoleon, suggest their methods are too simplistic and rely on outdated information.

The researchers looked at 18 projects across Peru, Colombia, Cambodia ... and compare to those usggested not included in the REDD+ scheme. It found that only 6% of the Credit issued were actually linked with saving trees. 

In fairness Verra also responded to the Guardian article (see [here](https://verra.org/verra-response-guardian-rainforest-carbon-offsets/)) explaining that their methodology is different from the one used by the research advanced by the Guradian of even newscientist, referred to as "Synthetic", trying to find a similar reference area to compare to, while Verra takes into account outside factors also, such as population, location, and select High risk areas. They estimate their impact more around the 50% mark. 

This just proves how complicated Carbon offsets are, and while not perfect in any way, it is very much a work in progress.
