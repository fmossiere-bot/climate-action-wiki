## What is Degrowth

> The term "degrowth" (or its French equivalent, "décroissance") was coined by French thinkers, but its academic and activist roots in Barcelona are strongly associated with **Joan Martinez-Alier**.

While the concept of "décroissance" originated in France in the 1970s with figures like André Gorz and Serge Latouche, it was brought to prominence in the English-speaking academic world and in Spain largely through the work of Martinez-Alier. He is a key figure in ecological economics and political ecology at the Autonomous University of Barcelona (UAB), where he helped establish the Institute of Environmental Science and Technology (ICTA-UAB).

Martinez-Alier's work, which focuses on environmental justice and the critique of endless economic growth, provided a strong academic foundation for the degrowth movement in Barcelona and beyond. He is widely recognized as a leading public intellectual and a major figure in the degrowth community, having influenced a new generation of scholars and activists who have made Barcelona a global hub for degrowth research and action.

> [!NOTE] Core of the Movement
> 
> > At its core, the **degrowth** movement is a political and academic framework that challenges the idea that endless economic growth is necessary or even desirable for human progress.
> 
>  It advocates for a **planned, democratic reduction of production and consumption** in high-income nations to achieve ecological sustainability and social justice. Instead of measuring success by GDP, degrowth proponents suggest focusing on other metrics of well-being, such as health, education, and leisure time.
> 

The movement's key principles include:
> 
> - **Ecological Limits:** It acknowledges that the Earth has finite resources and that perpetual economic growth on a finite planet is impossible and destructive.
> - **Social Justice:** Degrowth seeks to address social and economic inequalities by promoting the redistribution of wealth and resources, ensuring that basic needs are met for all.
> - **Reduced Throughput:** It focuses on decreasing the flow of materials and energy through the economy by promoting policies like circular economies, reduced consumption, and localized production.
> - **Redefining Well-being:** The movement prioritizes human fulfillment and quality of life over material accumulation, advocating for less work and more time for community and personal development.

### Public Support for Degrowth

While the term "degrowth" can be controversial and is often misunderstood, the core ideas behind it enjoy significant public support.8 A number of surveys and studies have shown:

- **Support for "Post-Growth" Ideas:** A survey of people in 34 European countries found that, on average, 61% favored a "post-growth" approach, which is a broader term for moving beyond economic growth.9
- **Prioritizing Well-being over Wealth:** Polls across multiple countries, including G20 nations, have found that a majority of people believe that the economy should prioritize the health and well-being of people and nature over a focus on profit and increasing wealth.10
- **Environmental Protection over Economic Growth:** A number of polls have shown that the public in the US and Europe believes that protecting the environment is more important than economic growth.11
- **Favorable Opinion of the Term Itself:** A French study found that 67% of respondents had a "favorable" opinion of the term "degrowth" itself, with support coming from across the political spectrum.12

Despite this, the movement still faces criticism, particularly concerning how a planned economic reduction would affect employment and public services.13 However, the growing popularity of its central ideas indicates a shift in public attitudes toward a more sustainable and equitable economic model.

### About GDP
#gdp 

> Gross Domestic Product (GDP) is a measure of the total market value of goods and services produced in a country over a period. It was institutionalized after World War II (and via structures born around the Depression era) as a convenient aggregate indicator of economic activity and recovery. Over time, governments and Western policymakers elevated GDP growth to the central benchmark of success.

  ###### *"Yet Kuznets himself repeatedly warned that GDP is not a measure of social welfare or well-being."*

The economist **Simon Kuznets** (1901–1985) played a foundational role in formalizing national income and output measurement in the 1930s and 1940s.  Yet Kuznets himself repeatedly warned that GDP is _not_ a measure of social welfare or well-being. He cautioned that “the welfare of a nation can scarcely be inferred from a measurement of national income.”  He urged that policymakers distinguish quantity versus quality of growth, and consider costs and distribution. 

  ###### "  *It ignores non-market activities (e.g. household care, volunteer work), externalities (pollution, resource depletion, environmental impact), and inequality in how income is shared"*

One major limitation is that GDP treats all market output equally: whether one produces $100 of teargas or $100 of potatoes, both add the same to GDP, even though their social impacts differ. It ignores non-market activities (e.g. household care, volunteer work), externalities (pollution, resource depletion), and inequality in how income is shared.  Because of these blind spots, many observers now argue that GDP has been overemphasized as the target of policy rather than being just one useful metric among others.

#### **The Jevons Paradox**

The thing about technology, is that, while it makes us more efficient, it does not reduce the energy needs. During teh industrial revolution, the English economist Willian Stanley Jevons noticed something rather strage. James Watt had just introduce its steam engine, which was significantly mor efficient than previous versions. Everyone assumed that this would hve reduce the overall coal consumption. But oddly enough, exactly the oppsostie happened.

Jevons discoverd that, with the effiecieny improvement, it saved money, and capitalist reinvested that moeny to expand production. This led to economic growth. The economy grew, and it used more coal as a result.
