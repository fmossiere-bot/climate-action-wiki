### First: Why do you want to communicate about climate change?

There are many possible answers, but one thing they likely have in common is this: **You want people to do something about it.**

This raises new questions:  
- Which people specifically do you hope will act?
- Why them?
- Are you focused on a person or group you share some connection with, or that you see some outsize value in persuading to act a certain way? 
- What action do you hope this audience will undertake after they've heard you speak or read your work?

Don't worry if you can't answer all of these just now. A key point to keep in mind is there is no universally correct way to communicate about climate change. Different methods will gain traction in different settings—so let's talk about how you can apply that knowledge deliberately.

### Who are you trying to reach—and why might they care?

There's a common pitfall in climate communications: Bombarding the audience with a ceaseless onslaught of facts. While we're quite fond of facts around here, [they're often insufficient on their own](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=nkMIjbDtdo0). The idea is you first want to connect with people, not start by lecturing them.

"It's nearly impossible to make someone care about climate change for the same reasons I do. But I don't have to, and you don't, either," Dr. Katharine Hayhoe writes in her 2021 book, [_Saving Us: A Climate Scientist's Case for Hope and Healing in a Divided World_](https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/Saving-Us/Katharine-Hayhoe/9781982143831).

Hayhoe, who has been [hailed by _The New York Times_](https://www.nytimes.com/2016/10/11/science/katharine-hayhoe-climate-change-science.html) as “one of the nation’s most effective communicators on climate change,” says that having a worthwhile climate conversation often hinges less on your delivery of compelling statistical evidence than on bonding over a shared value.

### Speak your audience's language.

What words you choose can say a lot about you, and affect how your audience perceives you. Per the aforementioned [2021 _New Yorker_ profile](http://www.newyorker.com/news/on-religion/how-to-talk-about-climate-change-across-the-political-divide):

> _With farmers, Hayhoe avoids using the term "climate change," since the phenomenon is frequently seen as a liberal hoax. "We use the words 'climate variability' and 'long-term trends,'" she said._

With this in mind, err on the side of speaking—or writing—in friendly, conversational terms. The table on the right gives a few examples of this in practice and comes from the [2019 Columbia University report _The Psychology of Climate Change Communication_](https://coast.noaa.gov/data/digitalcoast/pdf/psychology-climate-change-communication.pdf), which advises:

> _When talking to the general public, research shows that communicators should, whenever possible, avoid using jargon, complicated scientific terms, and acronyms. Instead, use words that will make sense to the audience... Sometimes only a scientific term is sufficient for getting a point across. In that case, it is important to thoroughly define the term for the audience. Communicators should remember, however, that stringing together too many scientific terms and acronyms may cause the audience to spend their time and mental energy deciphering vocabulary instead of absorbing the overall point._

## Making your case for Solutions

` "You don't want to paralyse your audience" `

- [ ] Interesting [Podcast](https://www.vox.com/podcasts/2019/12/16/21024323/ezra-klein-show-saul-griffith-solve-climate-change) from Saul Griggith on making solving climate change enjoyable

Climate messages that lay bare the problems of our warming world, while offering little in the way of solutions, have a nasty tendency to shut people down.

In fact, this problem isn't unique to climate—it's common in reporting about the news from around the world. But when journalist and author [Amanda Ripley reflected in the _Washington Post_](https://www.washingtonpost.com/opinions/2022/07/08/how-to-fix-news-media/)* on why the news often alienates people—herself included, she admitted—climate coverage gave a stark example

> _Last December, the New York Times published an ambitious [multimedia project](https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2021/12/13/opinion/climate-change-effects-countries.html) called “Postcards from a World on Fire,” chronicling how climate change has altered life in 193 countries. It led with a graphic of the Earth in flames, spinning in space, and the words, “Cities swallowed by dust. Human history drowned by the sea.” I kid you not. This was a well-intentioned effort, but it was simply not designed for humans. I don’t know what species it would work for, but it’s not one I’m familiar with._

> [!People need Hope]
> _Nowhere is the crying need for agency and hope more apparent than in climate coverage. Of all the climate stories aired on nightly news and Sunday morning shows in 2021, only a third discussed possible solutions, according to a [study by Media Matters](https://www.mediamatters.org/broadcast-networks/how-tv-coverage-climate-solutions-has-evolved-past-5-years) for America. What would agency look like? It might look like The Post’s April article detailing [six ways to halt climate change](https://www.washingtonpost.com/climate-solutions/2022/04/04/ipcc-climate-change-solutions/?itid=lk_inline_manual_38). Or it might look like the viral videos on TikTok, where non-journalists such as [@thegarbagequeen](https://www.tiktok.com/@thegarbagequeen) have started to fill the void, celebrating incremental environmental victories and debunking “climate doomers.”_


> The solution can be in more communication, and more grassroot work. If you're interested in grassroots work, check out the full piece: ["Climate change is all about power. You have more than you think."](https://www.vox.com/23152123/climate-actions-individuals-can-take) It also covers the "power-mapping" exercise seen here, which can help identify worthwhile people or groups to focus your efforts on.

### What about in corporate contexts?

Whether you're communicating with others within your organization, or thinking about how to tell the company's story, "keep it simple and clear," emphasizes Vance Merolla, global sustainability director for Colgate-Palmolive, which makes everything from deodorant to dog food.

Merolla is also a fan of visuals, like maps, and checklists that help distill down a lot of information so it's easily understood and skimmable. He gives the example of a short checklist for a complex set of green building standards: "Now behind this checklist is literally hundreds, maybe thousands of pages of guidance you have to follow if you're the engineer or the architect or the designer," but when you're persuading your organization of the value of building sustainably, it's essential to condense those details down to be manageable and meaningful.

While Merolla believes companies rarely lie about their environmental practices outright, he cautions you have to be careful with the exact words you choose—and make sure you're telling the whole story, rather than cherry-picking desirable results or anecdotes that give a misleading impression.

### What about if I need to communicate about climate change with a journalist?

If you take on a role publicly advocating for climate solutions, you may occasionally find yourself fielding questions from journalists. As with anyone else, it's wise to _ask them_ questions early on. Partly, this is just practical: You don't want to find out at the last minute that the podcast recording you thought would be audio-only will, in fact, feature video of you sitting in your car in an unwashed hoodie. (We know an author this happened to.)

Also, many reporters may not be as fresh or clear as you about what it means to limit warming to 1.5 C and why it matters. Stop short of quizzing them, but try to feel out what they're working on so you can add meaningful context. It can make a big difference in whatever story they end up putting together. 

[Another key question to remember is, "Can we talk on background?"](https://www.poynter.org/commentary/2021/on-background-is-common-journalism-lingo-what-does-it-mean/) This means for the moment, you don't want to be quoted verbatim or cited by name, but may be paraphrased. It's different from "off the record," which means you're telling the reporter something you don't want repeated at all. A good rule here is to assume everything is on the record except when the reporter agrees otherwise.

When possible, think through the key points you want to emphasize, but keep it conversational. Reporters would generally prefer to quote a person speaking organically rather than hear you read a statement that sounds calculated and lifeless.

Lastly, if you're uneasy talking to the press, remember that you don't have to—and that sometimes "not right now" is better than slamming the door outright. Many good reporters will still stay in touch and be open to your perspective on background, even if you don't prefer the spotlight.

### KEEP IT SIMPLE
When you're crafting a general message for a broad audience, you don't want people to feel confused or overwhelmed by a barrage of details, so it's often best to err on the side of simplicity. One impressive example of this is [this 2021 IPCC summary](https://www.ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg1/downloads/outreach/IPCC_AR6_WGI_SummaryForAll.pdf), which clocks in at just 16 pages cover to cover, and features as many cute doodles of cats as it does charts

### Talking about climate change can lead to real change. Here's how to make those conversations count:

1. ### Share Your Story: Begin with your personal connection to climate change.
2. ### Link to Priorities: Connect climate change to what matters to the person you're speaking with, like family, economy, or health.
3. ### Find Common Ground: Use shared values to bridge differences.
4. ### Discuss Immediate Impacts: Highlight how climate change is affecting us now.
5. ### Promote Clean Energy: Talk about the benefits of renewable energy for the economy and health.
6. ### Mention Cost Savings: Point out the financial benefits of being sustainable.
7. ### Emphasize Urgency: Stress why we need to act quickly.
8. ### Share Successes: Use examples of effective climate action to inspire.
9. ### Explore Futures: Talk about how today's actions can create a better tomorrow.

## Summary
- Productive climate conversations inspire action—and are tailored to specific audiences. This means learning a bit about the people or group you're trying to reach, and sharing a common value with them. Listen for where their pain points are. Is climate change affecting them financially, or doing harm to a place they love? How might this inspire them to act?  
- It's wise to speak in terms that won't confuse or distract your audience. This often means stripping away scientific jargon and acronyms, as the last thing you want to do is alienate people by being overly wonky.  
- There's little upside to arguing with people who are invested in dismissing the seriousness of climate change. Fortunately, such people are outnumbered by the many others who already care, or could be more easily swayed.
- Your message has to include solutions. Remember: hope, agency, and dignity. While fear can be a valid part of communicating about climate change, overdoing it risks driving people further away from action.  
- It's much easier for climate solutions to gain traction if they're not entirely about sacrifice and the things we need to give up. Give people something to run _toward_, not just _away from_.  
- Many of these ideas—know your audience, feel their pain, speak their language, emphasize solutions—have applications beyond individual conversations, whether you're speaking to your city council, your coworkers, or the press.  
- To close, as we began, with a word on humility: Sometimes the three smartest words you can say are "I don't know." This is especially true with a problem as big as climate change. You don't have to have all the answers to have a worthwhile conversation about it.

#### How brain biases prevent climate action
Source: https://www.bbc.com/future/article/20190304-human-evolution-means-we-can-tackle-climate-change
#behaviour #ucd 

- Human Brain don't understand long term statistical data
- “We have evolved to pay attention to immediate threats. We overestimate threats that are less likely but easier to remember, like terrorism, and underestimate more complex threats, like climate change"
- " “Cognitive biases that ensured our initial survival make it difficult to address complex, long-term challenges that now threaten our existence, like climate change,” says Seyle

Psychologists have identified more than 150 cognitive biases we all share. Of these, a few are particularly important in explaining why we lack the will to act on climate change.
- _Hyperbolic discounting_. This is our perception that the present is more important than the future. Throughout most of our evolution it was more advantageous to focus on what might kill us or eat us now, not later. This bias now impedes our ability to take action to address more distant-feeling, slower and complex challenges.
- _Our lack of concern for future generations_. Evolutionary theory suggests that [we care most about just a few generations of family members](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1090513810000905: our great-grandparents to great-grandchildren. While we may understand what needs to be done to address climate change, it’s hard for us to see how the sacrifices required for generations existing beyond this short time span are worth it.
- _The bystander effect._ We tend to believe that someone else will deal with a crisis. This developed for good reason: if a threatening wild animal is lurking at the edge of our hunter-gatherer group, it’s a waste of effort for every single member to spring into action — not to mention could needlessly put more people into danger. In smaller groups, it was usually pretty clearly delineated who would step up for which threats, so this worked. Today, however, this leads us to assume (often wrongly) that our leaders must be doing something about the crisis of climate change. And the larger the group, the stronger this bias becomes.
- _The sunk-cost fallacy._ We are biased towards staying the course even in the face of negative outcomes. The more we've invested time, energy or resources into that course, the more likely we are to stick with it – even if it no longer seems optimal. This helps explain, for example, our continued reliance on fossil fuels as a primary source of energy in the face of decades of evidence that we both can and should transition to clean energy and a carbon neutral future.
  

As individuals, [we know what we can do](http://www.bbc.com/future/story/20181102-what-can-i-do-about-climate-change)about climate change. But addressing the issue also requires collective action on a scale that exceeds our evolutionary capacities. The larger the group, the more challenging it gets. Remember the bystander effect?

**But in small groups, it’s a different story.**

Anthropological experiments show us that, on average, any one individual can maintain stable relationships with 150 other people – a phenomenon known as [“Dunbar’s number”](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/004724849290081J). Beyond that social relationships begin to break down, undermining an individual’s ability to trust and rely on the actions of others to achieve collective long-term goals.

Recognising the power of small groups, Exposure Labs, the film company behind [Chasing Ice](https://www.amazon.com/Chasing-Ice-James-Balog/dp/B00CNL78JA/ref=sr_1_1/137-1259956-7278039?s=movies-tv&ie=UTF8&qid=1546127648&sr=1-1&keywords=Chasing+Ice&) and [Chasing Coral](https://www.netflix.com/title/80168188), is using its films to mobilise communities to take local action on climate change. For example, in South Carolina, a US state rife with leaders who deny climate change, Exposure Labs shows a film to get a conversation started, inviting people from various interest groups – like the agricultural, fisheries, and tourism industries – to talk about how climate change affects them personally

These approaches use a couple of other psychological strategies, too. First, when small groups are involved in coming up with solutions themselves, they experience the [endowment effect](https://www.behavioraleconomics.com/resources/mini-encyclopedia-of-be/endowment-effect/): when we own something (even an idea), we tend to value it more. Second, [social comparison](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_comparison_theory): we tend to evaluate ourselves by looking at others. If we’re surrounded by other people in a group who are taking action on climate change, we’re more likely to do the same. This is also the impetus behind programmes like comparing energy consumption from household to household in a community. Research shows that [when people compare their energy use with their neighbours’ via statements on their energy bills they are more likely to reduce energy consumption](https://www.nber.org/digest/feb10/w15386.html).

Humans are more likely to change behaviour when challenges are framed positively, instead of negatively. In other words, how we communicate about climate change influences how we respond. [People are more likely to act in relation to a positive frame](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/280016724_Reframing_Environmental_Messages_to_be_Congruent_with_American_Values) (“a clean energy future will save X number of lives”) versus a negative statement (“we’re going to go extinct due to climate change”).
