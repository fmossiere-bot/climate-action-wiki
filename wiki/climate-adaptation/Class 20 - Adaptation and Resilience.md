## Introduction
[LINK](https://textbook.terra.do/class/m-adaptation-1?r=https://app.terra.do/app/learning/86/my-schedule#m-adaptation-1)

We talk about Climate Mitigations. But the CO2 levels have already reached high levels, and some weather and climate patterns are changed. It's easy to picture solutions in the form of huge infrastructure projects, like [floodwalls](https://www.npr.org/2023/08/09/1192996933/virginias-pricey-seawall-won-t-address-key-impacts-of-climate-change-critics-fea). Those are part of the answer, but only part. Adaptation will also look like "[defensible spaces](https://www.fire.ca.gov/programs/communications/defensible-space-prc-4291/)" around homes in wildfire-prone areas—and like new air conditioners in places that never needed them until recently.

And it's not just about "hard" infrastructure, either: We also need to consider how communities can safeguard themselves socially and economically in the face of worsening disasters.

> In this sense, resilience is a useful umbrella term for thinking about dealing with the impacts of climate change in varying systems, scales, and timeframes. It's inherently open-ended, so we'll spend some time unpacking its full meaning and laying out tools for how you can better understand a system and its resilience—and how those tools might apply to the challenges we face.

## Definitions, Contexts and Framing quesitons
[LINK](https://textbook.terra.do/class/m-adaptation-2?r=https://app.terra.do/app/learning/86/my-schedule#m-adaptation-2)

> [!Adaptation vs Resilience]
> The terms resilience and adaptation have their roots in psychology. “For psychologists,” according to [one study](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2212096315000273), “'Resilience' is a trait, reflecting a general ability to master challenges, whereas 'Adaptation' is a state, reflecting how individuals deal with specific stressors.”
> 
> The same framing could be extended to groups of people, cities, and regions. Resilience is a measure of capacity to withstand shocks, while adaptation is a set of actions taken by people in response to a shock, or to prepare for a shock or stressor. The UNFCCC [defines](https://unfccc.int/topics/adaptation-and-resilience/the-big-picture/what-do-adaptation-to-climate-change-and-climate-resilience-mean) adaptation as “adjustments in ecological, social, or economic systems in response to actual or expected climatic stimuli and their effects or impacts.” 
> 
> In practice, these terms are often used interchangeably.

### Remember? 
> **Disasters are produced when a _vulnerable_ population is _exposed_ to a particular _weather or climate event_**. 

_Vulnerability_ itself is often a function of poverty, injustice, and politics. When we say that the poor will be more severely affected by climate change, this is not because the rich won’t experience the same weather or climactic events. Rather, it’s that the poor may live on more exposed land (the “exposure” is different) and have fewer resources to draw on

### Resilience: The Common Threads

Resilience concerns how a system responds to a disturbance or change. A resilient response might mean one or more of these three types of behavior:   

- _Robustness/Resistance:_ absorbing the disturbance and maintaining specific features of interest; ability to resist change;
- _Stability/Recovery:_ recovering from the disturbance and returning to the original features of interest;
- _Adapting/Benefiting:_ adapting as a result of the disturbance and moving to a new situation that is at least as desirable as the original, or more so.

The only response to disturbance that is not considered resilient is when the system changes (either by adapting badly or degrading) to a condition seen as less desirable than before the disturbance.


### The IPCC 's working group II Report

> "Most observed adaptation is fragmented, small in scale, incremental, sector-specific, designed to respond to current impacts or near-term risks, and focused more on planning rather than implementation." The summary also points to a funding gap for adaptation that is on track to widen.

This underscores the urgent need to vastly expand and improve our systems for adapting and improving resilience along multiple lines at once. As the authors note: "Integrated, multi-sectoral solutions that address social inequities, differentiate responses based on climate risk and cut across systems, increase the feasibility and effectiveness of adaptation in multiple sectors.

A few other takeaways:
• The cost of adaptation, particularly in developing countries, will be substantial—many billions of dollars, as illustrated in [this Twitter thread](https://twitter.com/joethw8s/status/1498359200329748482?s=21).
• Success hinges on incorporating a diverse array of perspectives, from social scientists ([per _Vox_](https://www.vox.com/2022/3/1/22954724/climate-change-report-ipcc-adaptation-justice)) to researchers in Bangladesh working on salt-tolerant varieties of rice suitable for low-lying coastal areas ([per this opinion in _Scientific American_](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/adapting-to-climate-change-lessons-from-bangladesh/)).
• Lastly, adaptations that are poorly conceived can backfire; the IPCC authors term this "maladaptation,"

### Resilience of what?

The following questions also help determine what is interpreted as resilience, adaptability, vulnerability, or collapse, and so forth:  
 

- Where are system boundaries drawn?
- What is included in the analysis?
- Which features of the system are allowed to change and which must be preserved?
- What sorts of change constitute improvement?

### Resilience over what timeframe?

Increasing resilience or robustness to one type of disturbance can lead to increased vulnerability and fragility to other types of disturbance that may not yet have been considered.

There are parts of Australia where clearing of native deep-rooted vegetation and irrigation of farmland made farmers more resilient to water scarcity, by increasing the height of the water table and reducing soil salinization. But these measures also _decreased_ resilience to heavy rainfall. As rainfall patterns have changed, farmers now experience periods of heavy rain, and the system has become more vulnerable.

One takeaway from this example is that action taken now to build robustness or stability may reduce adaptive capacity to future changes.

### Resilience for whom?

We've talked a bit about resilience as a means of preserving—or even cultivating—desirable properties in disturbed systems. A key question here then becomes, _desirable for whom?_

In other words, resilience is normative: Judgement of what is desirable, and what constitutes improvement or detriment, is _observer dependent._ Changes that benefit one stakeholder may be detrimental to another. So who gets to define what is desirable—and how that gets negotiated—is critical.

### Resilience to what?

The type of disturbance is also important. Systems can be very resilient to one type of disturbance but vulnerable to another. For example, Mumbai slum dwellers have developed strategies for coping effectively with year-on-year flooding. However, they are completely vulnerable to the outbreak of a viral epidemic.

### A Framing Cycle

To summarize, resilience describes the nature of the response of a system to a particular disturbance, of a particular magnitude, from the perspective of a particular observer over a specified timescale.
Characterizing resilience hinges on specifying the following:  
 
- The boundaries of the system in question, and of the notion of improvement within those boundaries
- For whom the type and magnitudes of disturbance are to be considered, and
- The timescale to be considered
![[Screenshot 2024-02-27 at 18.58.55.png]]
  
In order to discuss resilience meaningfully, it is essential to talk about resilience **_of what, to what, from whose perspective and over what time frame._** These key issues represent a framing cycle, since each element has the potential to iteratively reframe all of the others.

### Different people make different system boundary judgements.

We touched on boundaries a moment ago with the example of the "collapse" of Mayan civilization, and it's worth considering further why this particularly matters.

Since everything is fundamentally interconnected and interdependent, when we consider the resilience of a particular system, we must define its boundaries. Because we each have different perspectives and interests it is likely that we will make different boundary judgments in the same situation, e.g. my idea of an "education system" will be different to yours because we see different elements, connections, and boundaries.

### Integrated Operational Framework

To do this, we can use an integrative operational framework with the following broad phases, each of which can itself involve the application of varied tools and multiple forms of representation:

1. Systemic boundary critique to explore the “of what.”
2. Visioning activities to explore and negotiate what constitutes desirable change and “for whom.”
3. Mobilization through asset-mapping and back-casting of plans that build on what exists, and also specifies over “what time- frame.”
4. Exploratory scenario development, exploring the “to what.” This can capture future uncertainties that we wish to be resilient to, thereby increasing our capacity to cope with them.
5. Implementation, with external support where necessary, guided by local knowledge.
6. Evaluation, learning and re-evaluation, and continuation of the process by the community in an iterative manner.

## Food Systems and Climate Change
[LINK](https://textbook.terra.do/class/m-adaptation-3)

#FoodSystems
§
To understand how food systems are vulnerable to climate change, it’s important to first grasp that we are talking about _systems._ 

These systems [include](https://www.annualreviews.org/doi/full/10.1146/annurev-environ-020411-130608) “the manufacturing and distribution of inputs (seed, animal feed, fertilizers, pest control); agricultural production (crops, livestock, fisheries, wild foods); primary and secondary processing, packaging, storage, transport and distribution; marketing and retail; catering; domestic food management; and waste disposal.”

> Climate change touches all parts of this system and the effects can reverberate up and down complex, often global, food chains. 

Every single human participates in food systems, though [we can all have different and multiple objectives for doing so:](https://www.annualreviews.org/doi/full/10.1146/annurev-environ-020411-130608) Beyond providing food, these systems yield profit, livelihood and employment, power and influence, innovation, and a range of environmental services.

==*Source: [IPCC Special report chapter 5](https://www.ipcc.ch/srccl/chapter/chapter-5/)*==

> **The current food system (production, transport, processing, packaging, storage, retail, consumption, loss and waste) feeds the great majority of world population and supports the livelihoods of over 1 billion people.** Since 1961, food supply per capita has increased more than 30%, accompanied by greater use of nitrogen fertilisers (increase of about 800%) and water resources for irrigation (increase of more than 100%). However, an estimated 821 million people are currently undernourished

> **About 21–37% of total greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions are attributable to the food system.** These are from agriculture and land use, storage, transport, packaging, processing, retail, and consumption (_medium confidence_). This estimate includes emissions of 9–14% from crop and livestock activities within the farm gate and 5–14% from land use and land-use change including deforestation and peatland degradation (_high confidence_); 5–10% is from supply chain activities (_medium confidence_).

> **Reduction of food loss and waste could lower GHG emissions and improve food security (_medium confidence_).** Combined food loss and waste amount to 25–30% of total food produced (_medium confidence_). During 2010–2016, global food loss and waste equalled 8–10% of total anthropogenic GHG emissions (_medium confidence_); and cost about 1 trillion USD2012 per year (_low confidence_).

> [!Interlinkage between the climate system, food system, ecosystems and socio-economics]
> These systems operate at multiple scales, both global and regional. Food security is an outcome of the food system leading to human well-being, which is also indirectly linked with climate and ecosystems through the socio-economic system. Adaptation measures can help
> ![[Screenshot 2024-02-28 at 10.17.57.png]]
> The graphic, from the IPCC’s Special Report on Climate Change and Land, captures the complex ways in which these are related. We briefly discuss this later in this module. As per the same IPCC Report, climate change can impact food security and human health in several ways. Climate change can:  
>  
> - Affect the amount of food, both from direct impacts on agricultural yields and indirectly, through climate change’s impacts on water availability and quality, pests and diseases, and pollination services.
> - Alter the CO2 in the atmosphere, influencing biomass and nutritional quality.
> - Worsen food safety risks during transport and storage.
> - Affect human health through the agricultural workforce’s exposure to extreme temperatures.


### How Climate Change Affects Food Systems

The IPCC report [finds evidence for all these effects](https://www.ipcc.ch/srccl/chapter/chapter-5/) (see Section 5.2), though the overall impacts are complex. 

While media articles can sometimes paint a rosy agricultural picture for [northern countries like Canada](https://www.cbc.ca/news/science/climate-change-farming-1.5461275), recent research tells a much less optimistic story. Future yield estimates remain uncertain, but there is evidence ([abstract here](https://www.nature.com/articles/s43016-021-00400-y), [preprint here](https://assets.researchsquare.com/files/rs-101657/v1_covered.pdf?c=1637595012)) that globally, maize, soybean, and rice yields will be both lower than previously estimated and that these effects will be felt sooner than previously predicted.

> In [a new review study](https://phys.org/news/2023-01-drought-significant-food-agricultural-production.html), scientists cite a study that finds that between 1980 and 2015, drought has reduced wheat and maize output by up to 40 percent globally. Global wheat yields are also projected to decrease due to heat and drought stress. 

 Other research on extreme climate events and food security ([link to the abstract here](http://www.nature.com/articles/s43016-021-00335-4)) predicts dramatically worsening food security absent significant adaptation and mitigation. In places like South Asia, offsetting a truly extreme event a few decades from now might require three times the region's current food reserves, the authors find.

While all world regions are affected in one way or another, the most vulnerable regions are South and Southeast Asia and Africa's Sudano-Sahelian Zone, “[where a high risk](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2590332221002360) of leaving these safe climatic conditions is combined with low resilience.”

In China, [agricultural output is already negatively affected by extreme heat](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0304387820301322), even after accounting for adaptation measures.

### Extreme Weather and Food Security
Our current food system is simultaneously more robust and more fragile than in the past. The developing world has seen a [significant decline in under-nourishment](https://ourworldindata.org/hunger-and-undernourishment#long-term-decline-of-undernourishment) from 1970 to 2015. Agricultural innovations such as the [green revolution](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.0912953109), combined with [massive land redistribution initiatives](https://www.annualreviews.org/doi/10.1146/annurev-environ-102016-060924) from 1945-1980 across nations of the Global South, created the conditions for large increases in food production and availability. 

At the same time, the entire system is increasingly vulnerable to shocks, and this trend is now _reversed_. The latest [UN report on The State of Food Security and Nutrition in the World](https://www.fao.org/state-of-food-security-nutrition/2021/en/) leads with the following sobering text:

> _"The world is in a very different place to where it was six years ago when it committed to the goal of ending hunger, food insecurity and all forms of malnutrition by 2030._ 
> _At the time, we were optimistic that with transformative approaches, past progress could be accelerated, at scale, to put us on track to achieve that goal._ 
> _Yet, the past four editions of [The State of Food Security and Nutrition in the World (SOFI)](https://www.fao.org/publications/sofi/2021/en/) revealed a humbling reality:_ 
> _The world has not been generally progressing either towards ensuring access to safe, nutritious and sufficient food for all people all year round (SDG Target 2.1), or to eradicating all forms of malnutrition (SDG Target 2.2)."_

The report identifies conflict, climate variability and extremes, economic slowdowns and downturns, and the COVID-19 pandemic as leading causes of this trend.

### Mitigation and Adaptation

Since the food system is both a source of greenhouse gas emissions and vulnerable to climate change, it’s useful to think of mitigation and adaptation through an integrated lens. 

 If we don’t, we run the danger of engaging in adaptation strategies that may worsen emissions, the most obvious being expansion of land under food production. It’s also possible to implement land-use strategies that could reduce emissions but make the food system more vulnerable to climate shocks, such as converting land to non-food uses (e.g. to manufacture plant-based plastics or biofuels). 
On the other hand, there are many win-win strategies that can both reduce emissions and enhance resilience, like agroforestry. The graphic alongside captures some of the synergies and trade-offs between mitigation and adaptation strategies around land use and food security.

### Food, Migration, and Climate Change

One adaptation strategy that doesn’t get nearly enough acknowledgment is _migration._ When people traditionally dependent on the land can no longer support themselves, they _move._ Scroll to watch a video that captures the intimate relationship between food security, climate change, and migration in a community in Honduras.

And for deeper insights, check out the award-winning series of stories from _ProPublica_ titled ["The Great Climate Migration."](https://www.propublica.org/series/the-great-climate-migration)
