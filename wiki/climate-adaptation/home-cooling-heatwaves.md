# Home Cooling During Heatwaves

## What is it

As global temperatures rise, heatwaves that were once rare are becoming regular events across Europe and the UK. Most British and Irish homes were built to keep heat in, not out — they lack shutters, cross-ventilation, or adequate insulation against summer heat. This mismatch is pushing millions of households to consider air conditioning for the first time.

There are several ways to keep a home cool: passive methods that require no energy, low-energy fans, and active mechanical systems like air conditioners and heat pumps. Each has a different carbon footprint and different effectiveness.

## Why it matters

Home cooling is a genuine public health issue. Extreme heat kills — especially children, older people, and those with health conditions. At the same time, widespread adoption of energy-hungry air conditioners could undermine climate goals if powered by fossil fuels. Finding the right balance between safety and emissions is one of the practical dilemmas of climate adaptation.

The UK's Climate Change Committee estimates that **22% of British homes** will require active cooling if global warming reaches 2°C — a level that current trajectories make likely within decades.

## Key facts

- **4 million UK homes** now have an air conditioner — double the number from three years ago. [[uk-air-conditioning-green-2026]]
- A portable AC unit running 8 hours/day produces ~**4.87kg CO2 over a summer** — equivalent to driving ~18 miles. [[uk-air-conditioning-green-2026]]
- Running a fan costs **15–31p/day** vs **£5/day** for portable AC (UK grid prices, May 2026). [[uk-air-conditioning-green-2026]]
- A 2021 Lancet study found electric fans can be **as effective as AC** at preventing heat stress on hot days. [[uk-air-conditioning-green-2026]]
- Air-to-air heat pumps deliver about **3 units of cooling per unit of electricity** — far more efficient than resistive AC. [[uk-air-conditioning-green-2026]]
- The UK government offers a **£2,500 grant** to replace fossil-fuel heating with an air-to-air heat pump. [[uk-air-conditioning-green-2026]]

## Types of cooling — from greenest to most energy-intensive

1. **Passive cooling** — free and zero-carbon. Window shutters or blinds (block solar gain before it enters), night-time ventilation, green roofs, added insulation, wet towels in front of fans.
2. **Electric fans** — very low energy (~15–31p/day). Most effective when air is below body temperature (~35°C). Less effective in extreme humid heat.
3. **Air-to-air heat pumps** — efficient and dual-purpose (heating in winter, cooling in summer). The best mechanical option if replacing a fossil-fuel heating system. Government grants available.
4. **Fixed split-system AC** — more efficient than portable units; right-sizing matters (think of it like a radiator — too big wastes energy).
5. **Portable plug-in AC** — the least efficient option. These lower indoor air pressure, drawing warm air back in through the exhaust gap. Useful as emergency backup.

## Can AC be green?

Not fully — until the electricity grid runs entirely on renewables. But you can reduce the impact:

- **Pair with solar panels**: run AC when your panels are generating, not from the grid.
- **Use a home battery**: charge it during renewable surpluses, power AC from stored energy.
- **Check grid carbon intensity**: sites like [mygridgb.co.uk](https://www.mygridgb.co.uk/) show when the grid is cleanest. Run AC during those windows.
- **Pre-cool rooms** before the evening peak in electricity demand, when the grid is often dirtier.
