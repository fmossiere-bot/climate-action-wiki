Climate change is no longer a future problem. Carbon dioxide levels in the atmosphere have already reached record highs, and shifts in weather patterns are visible everywhere. Floods are more frequent, droughts are longer, and heatwaves are breaking records season after season.

Most of the conversation in climate circles focuses on **mitigation: reducing emissions to slow down warming**. But there is a parallel and urgent challenge that often gets less attention. 

> Even if we stopped all emissions today, we would still need to adapt to the changes already locked in. This is what climate adaptation is about.

**Adaptation does not mean giving up on mitigation**. It means accepting reality and acting on it. It includes building flood barriers and fire protection around homes, but also installing air conditioning in regions that never needed it before, creating early warning systems for extreme weather, and making sure that communities have the financial and social resources to recover when disasters strike. Adaptation is both a physical and a social challenge.

## 2. Definitions and Context: What Do Adaptation and Resilience Actually Mean?

The two terms most used in this space are adaptation and resilience. They are related but not the same.

> **Resilience** comes originally from psychology, where it refers to a general capacity to handle challenges. In the context of climate, it describes how well a system, whether a city, a region, or a community, can absorb shocks and keep functioning.

> **Adaptation**, on the other hand, refers to the specific actions taken in response to climate impacts, or in anticipation of them. The United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change defines adaptation as "adjustments in ecological, social, or economic systems in response to actual or expected climatic stimuli and their effects or impacts."

In simple terms: resilience is a capacity, adaptation is action.

Why do these concepts matter? Because climate change does not affect everyone equally. The IPCC has a useful starting point: disasters happen when a vulnerable population is exposed to a climate or weather event. Vulnerability is often driven by poverty, inequality, and political exclusion. Poor communities tend to live in more exposed areas, such as flood plains or coastlines, and have fewer resources to recover when things go wrong. Understanding this link between vulnerability, exposure, and impact is essential for designing effective responses.

## 3. Resilience: For Whom, to What, and Over What Timeframe?

One of the most important things to understand about resilience is that it is not a simple, universal concept. It depends entirely on who you ask, what system you are looking at, and over what period of time.

### Three ways a system can respond to disruption

A resilient response to a shock can take different forms:

- **Resistance**: the system absorbs the shock and keeps its key features intact.
- **Recovery**: the system is affected but returns to roughly where it was before.
- **Adaptation**: the system changes in response and reaches a new, equally or more stable state.

The only outcome that is not resilient is when a system degrades to a worse state than before.

### Resilience for whom?

Resilience is not neutral. What looks like a positive outcome for one group can be harmful for another. A flood defence wall that protects a city centre might divert water towards poorer neighbourhoods on the outskirts. A policy that supports large agricultural businesses might leave smallholder farmers worse off. Deciding what counts as a good outcome, and whose interests are prioritised, is fundamentally a political question.

### Resilience to what?

A system can be highly resilient to one type of stress while being completely unprepared for another. Residents of informal settlements in Mumbai, for example, have developed very effective ways to cope with annual flooding. But those same communities are much more vulnerable to disease outbreaks or economic shocks. Resilience is always specific to the type of disruption considered.

### Resilience over what timeframe?

Building resilience for today's risks can sometimes create new vulnerabilities in the future. In parts of Australia, farmers cleared native deep-rooted vegetation and irrigated land to manage water scarcity. This raised the water table and reduced soil problems in dry periods. But it also made those same farms more vulnerable to heavy rainfall. As rainfall patterns have changed, the strategy that once protected them is now a liability. Short-term resilience can undermine long-term adaptive capacity.

This is why a clear framing is always needed. Any serious discussion of resilience needs to specify: resilience of what system, to what kind of stress, from whose perspective, and over what timeframe. Without these anchors, the concept risks becoming too vague to be useful.

## 4. Climate Change and Our Food System: A Serious and Growing Threat

Food is perhaps where climate change will be felt most directly by most people. Understanding this risk requires seeing food not as a product but as a system.

### A complex and deeply interconnected system

Food systems include everything from seeds, fertilisers, and animal feed, through crop production, fishing, and livestock, all the way to processing, packaging, transport, retail, and waste disposal. These systems cross borders, link continents, and involve hundreds of millions of workers and producers.

Climate change does not affect just one part of this chain. It disrupts all of it at once, and the effects ripple in all directions.

### Key numbers

The food system is both a victim of climate change and a significant contributor to it:

- **21 to 37% of all global greenhouse gas emissions** come from food systems, counting production, land use, transport, processing, and retail.
- Agricultural production alone accounts for 9 to 14% of emissions. Deforestation and land-use change add a further 5 to 14%.
- Food loss and waste represent 25 to 30% of all food produced globally, and account for 8 to 10% of total human-caused emissions.
- Despite feeding more people than ever, **821 million people remain undernourished** today.

### How climate change is affecting food production

The impacts are already measurable. Between 1980 and 2015, drought reduced global wheat and maize output by up to 40%. Future projections suggest that yields of staple crops including maize, soybeans, and rice will fall further than earlier models predicted, and sooner.

In South and Southeast Asia, as well as in Sub-Saharan Africa, the risks are particularly acute. These regions combine high climate exposure with low adaptive capacity. In some scenarios, managing a severe climate shock in South Asia within a few decades could require food reserves three times larger than what the region currently holds.

In China, agricultural output is already being affected by extreme heat, even accounting for adaptation measures already in place.

### Progress stalled, and potentially reversing

Significant progress was made in reducing hunger from the 1970s to around 2015, thanks to agricultural innovation and improved food distribution. But this trend has stalled and may be reversing. The 2021 UN report on food security was candid: the world is not on track to end hunger or malnutrition by 2030. Conflict, economic instability, climate variability, and the COVID-19 pandemic have all contributed.

### The adaptation challenge in food systems

Responding to these threats requires action at multiple levels: improving crop varieties suited to heat and drought, reducing food loss and waste, rethinking land use to balance food production with emissions reduction, and investing in local and regional food system resilience.

There are genuine trade-offs to navigate. Expanding agricultural land to grow more food increases emissions and drives deforestation. Converting land to grow biofuels or plant-based materials might reduce emissions but reduces food production capacity. At the same time, there are genuine win-win approaches, such as agroforestry, which can both sequester carbon and make farming more resilient to climate shocks.

One adaptation response that deserves more recognition is migration. When communities can no longer sustain themselves on land affected by drought or flooding, people move. This is not a failure of policy. It is a human response to an increasingly difficult situation, and it needs to be recognised and planned for as part of any serious adaptation framework.
