When you pick up a packet of fish in a supermarket and see a small blue label with a fish on it, that is the [MSC certification](https://www.msc.org). But what does it actually guarantee?


**1. What is the MSC?**
The Marine Stewardship Council (MSC) is a non-profit organisation that sets standards for sustainable fishing. Its mission is to use its ecolabel and certification programme to recognise and reward sustainable **fishing practices**. It was founded in 1996, following the collapse of the Grand Banks cod fishery off Canada, which wiped out one of the world's most productive fishing grounds and served as a stark warning about what unchecked overfishing can do.

> The MSC Fisheries Standard is grounded in the United Nations Code of Conduct for Responsible Fisheries and has been developed through a process involving fisheries, scientists, environmental NGOs, and the seafood industry.



**2. What does the blue label cover?**
The MSC label focuses on the environmental impacts of fishing, such as maintaining productive fish stocks, healthy ecosystems, and good fisheries management. It does not certify social or economic conditions, and it does not cover things such as the packaging or carbon footprint of the final product.

> The MSC Fisheries Standard has three core principles that every fishery must meet: first, are enough fish left in the ocean; second, what are the environmental impacts of the fishing activity; and third, are operations well managed and compliant with relevant laws.

It is also worth noting that the label covers the full chain of custody. Chain of Custody certification tracks fish and seafood from MSC-certified fisheries at each step of the supply chain, from fishery to table. This means that every company handling the product along the way, from processor to retailer, must also be certified.



**3. How does the certification process work?**

- Certification to the MSC Fisheries Standard is voluntary and is open to all fisheries that catch marine or freshwater organisms in the wild. Fisheries are assessed by accredited independent certifiers, not by the MSC itself.
- During the assessment, fisheries are scored across 28 performance indicators under the three principles. A score of 80 to 100 leads to full certification, a score of 60 to 79 leads to conditional certification requiring improvements, and a score below 60 results in failure.
- Assessment against the MSC Fisheries Standard can take around 12 to 18 months, and costs can range from around 15,000 to 120,000 US dollars depending on the complexity of the fishery. Once certified, fisheries are audited by independent assessors every year and must undergo full reassessment every five years.



**4. How widely is it adopted?**

Over **600 fisheries are engaged with the MSC programme**, supplying the world with over 20,000 sustainable seafood products. The label is recognised by major retailers across Europe, North America, and beyond, and is increasingly required by supermarkets as a condition of listing seafood products.

> It is not without criticism. The MSC has faced scrutiny over its ties to the fishing industry and a potential conflict of interest, given that the industry pays for its own certification. Critics have also raised concerns about some certified fisheries having high levels of bycatch, the unintended capture and killing of non-target species. The MSC has updated its standards over time in response to these concerns, including a revised Fisheries Standard published in 2024.



**Bottom line**

The MSC blue label is one of the most widely recognised sustainability marks in the food sector. It provides a meaningful, independently verified signal that wild seafood has been caught with environmental sustainability in mind. Like any certification, it is not perfect, but it remains the most rigorous and globally adopted standard for wild-capture fisheries available today.
