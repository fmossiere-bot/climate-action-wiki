Sustainability reporting has grown into a genuinely confusing landscape. **There are more than 600 different standards, frameworks, and initiatives in circulation worldwide**, but in practice only a handful matter for most companies. This article works through the main ones, groups them by region, and sets out which are legally required, which are voluntary but widely used, and how the picture has shifted through 2026, a year that brought real change on both sides of the Atlantic.

## 1. Why there are so many standards in the first place

Unlike financial accounting, which has settled on a small number of globally recognised systems, sustainability reporting grew up in a decentralised way: NGOs, investor coalitions, stock exchanges, and now governments each built their own frameworks to answer slightly different questions. Some are aimed at investors assessing financial risk. Others are aimed at regulators, customers, or the general public. That difference in audience is often the simplest way to tell two standards apart.

> The push toward consolidation is real but still in progress. The International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) has spent the past two years explicitly working to reduce this complexity, partnering with the Transition Plan Taskforce, the GHG Protocol, CDP, the Taskforce on Nature-related Financial Disclosures (TNFD), and the Global Reporting Initiative (GRI) to bring the various systems closer together. As of mid-2026, more than 30 jurisdictions, together accounting for a significant share of global GDP and market capitalisation, have adopted or are moving to adopt ISSB standards in some form. That is genuine progress, but it has not eliminated the underlying regional split described below.

## 2. The main standards, grouped by what they are for

Before going region by region, it helps to know what each standard is actually trying to do.

- **GRI (Global Reporting Initiative)**: the oldest and broadest standard, first published in 1997, aimed at all stakeholders rather than investors specifically. GRI has no central enforcement body, but companies can publish their reports through its database. It remains the most widely used voluntary standard globally, with roughly 10,000 organisations reporting against it.
- **ISSB / IFRS Sustainability Disclosure Standards (IFRS S1 and S2)**: created in 2022 by the IFRS Foundation specifically to serve investors and finance teams, tying sustainability disclosure to financial statements. IFRS S2 has effectively absorbed and replaced the older TCFD framework, and IFRS S1 has absorbed much of SASB's industry-specific approach.
- **TCFD (Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures)**: a climate risk disclosure framework rather than a full reporting standard. It has now been folded into the ISSB standards, but its four-pillar structure (governance, strategy, risk management, metrics and targets) still shapes how climate risk is reported almost everywhere, including inside California's state-level rules.
- **SASB (Sustainability Accounting Standards Board)**: industry-specific, investor-focused metrics. SASB merged into the Value Reporting Foundation in 2021, which was then absorbed by the ISSB, so SASB today survives mainly as the industry-specific backbone inside IFRS S1.
- **CDP**: a disclosure and scoring system (covered in more detail in a previous article) rather than a reporting standard as such. Companies, cities, and regions answer CDP's questionnaire and receive a score, and much of that data is now cross-referenced against ISSB and CSRD requirements.
- **EU CSRD / ESRS**: the EU's own mandatory reporting law and its accompanying disclosure standards, covered in detail below, since it now looks quite different from how it was designed in 2022.
- **TNFD (Taskforce on Nature-related Financial Disclosures)**: a newer framework focused specifically on nature and biodiversity-related risk, structured deliberately to mirror TCFD's approach so companies already familiar with climate risk reporting can extend the same logic to nature. TNFD and EFRAG (the body behind ESRS) have jointly published a mapping document showing where their concepts, materiality approach, and disclosure metrics align, specifically to help CSRD-scoped companies use TNFD without duplicating work.
- **B Corp**: a private certification (not strictly a disclosure standard) covering environmental, social, and governance performance, most widely used by small and medium-sized, often privately held, companies.

## 3. Europe: the EU CSRD, and why 2026 changed everything

The EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), and its accompanying European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS), have been the most comprehensive mandatory sustainability reporting regime in the world since they began applying in 2024. That description still holds, but the scope has shrunk considerably since the directive was first designed.

**What changed.** In February 2025, the European Commission proposed a simplification package known as "Omnibus I", in response to concerns from EU governments and businesses about administrative burden. After nearly a year of negotiation, the Omnibus I Directive (EU) 2026/470 was published on 26 February 2026 and entered into force on 18 March 2026. It substantially narrows CSRD's scope:

- Mandatory reporting now applies to EU companies with **more than 1,000 employees and over €450 million in net turnover**, up sharply from the original thresholds, which would have captured roughly 50,000 companies. Estimates suggest the new thresholds cut that figure by a large majority.
- Non-EU companies are only in scope if their EU turnover exceeds €450 million, applying from financial years beginning in 2028, rather than the original 2027 date.
- Sector-specific ESRS standards, which were meant to add detail for industries such as agriculture or mining, have been dropped altogether.
- Companies no longer have to adopt a formal Paris-aligned climate transition plan as part of their disclosure, only describe their approach to one.
- A simplified version of ESRS is being finalised by EFRAG (the technical body that drafts the standards) and is due for adoption in 2026, aiming to reduce the number of mandatory data points by roughly 60%.
- Member states have until 19 March 2027 to transpose these changes into national law, and companies that started reporting under the original 2024 rules (so-called "Wave 1") may or may not be able to step back out of scope, depending on how their own country implements the transition.

**Ireland specifically.** Ireland transposed the original CSRD on time, via the European Union (Corporate Sustainability Reporting) Regulations 2024, but the Irish transposition introduced some scope anomalies, most notably a rule that automatically treats certain listed SMEs as "large companies," pulling them into full CSRD reporting earlier and more comprehensively than the EU directive intended. According to Ireland's audit regulator IAASA, only around 20 Irish companies were required to report for financial year 2024 under the first wave. Most of these will likely remain in scope after Omnibus I; whether smaller Irish companies caught by the transposition anomaly get relief depends on how quickly the Department of Enterprise, Trade and Employment updates the Irish regulations.

**The double materiality principle.** Whatever the final scope ends up being, this core concept survives Omnibus I intact: in-scope companies must assess materiality from two directions at once, both how sustainability issues affect their own financial performance, and how their operations affect people and the environment. This distinguishes CSRD from most investor-only frameworks like ISSB, which use financial materiality alone.

**UK.** Outside the EU, the UK has taken its own path, formally endorsing UK Sustainability Reporting Standards (UK SRS) based closely on IFRS S1 and S2, positioning the UK as one of the more decisive adopters of the ISSB baseline among major economies, albeit distinct from the EU's CSRD.

## 4. United States: a state-level patchwork, with the federal rule in retreat

The regulatory picture in the US has moved in the opposite direction from Europe. The SEC's federal climate disclosure rule, finalised in March 2024, was tied up in litigation almost immediately and, in 2026, the SEC itself proposed to rescind it. A final rescission is unlikely before late 2026 or 2027, but for practical purposes the federal rule is not currently a live requirement companies need to plan around.

Instead, **California has become the primary source of mandatory climate disclosure for large US companies**, through two laws administered by the California Air Resources Board (CARB):

- **SB 253 (Climate Corporate Data Accountability Act)**: requires US companies doing business in California with revenue over $1 billion to report Scope 1 and Scope 2 greenhouse gas emissions, with Scope 3 following from 2027. The first reporting deadline, originally set for mid-2026, has been pushed to later in the year as CARB finalised its regulations.
- **SB 261 (Climate-Related Financial Risk Act)**: requires companies with revenue over $500 million to report climate-related financial risk, broadly along TCFD lines. Enforcement of SB 261 is currently paused pending a Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals ruling on a legal challenge, though SB 253 is unaffected by that injunction and remains in force.

Other states, including New York, New Jersey, Colorado, and Illinois, are considering similar legislation modelled on California's approach, so the pattern of state-level rather than federal mandates looks likely to continue. Outside of California, most large US companies that report on sustainability do so voluntarily, generally against GRI, SASB-derived metrics inside IFRS S1, or CDP.

## 5. Asia-Pacific and other regions: steady, varied ISSB adoption

Outside Europe and the US, the ISSB standards have become the default reference point, though the pace and form of adoption varies considerably by country:

- **Australia**: has adopted its own version, AASB S2, closely modelled on IFRS S2, phased in by company size from 2025.
- **Japan, Hong Kong, Malaysia, Singapore**: have formally aligned with ISSB standards, generally through their stock exchanges or national standard-setters, with Hong Kong's exchange mandating ISSB-aligned climate disclosure for all listed companies.
- **Brazil**: its securities regulator has required ISSB-aligned climate disclosure for listed companies on a phased basis from 2026, making it one of the most influential adopters in Latin America.
- **Nigeria, Kenya, Turkey**: among the earlier adopters outside the largest economies, often mandating ISSB standards directly or through close local translations.
- **Canada**: has published its own IFRS-aligned standards (CSDS 1 and CSDS 2), currently available on a voluntary basis, with mandatory adoption still pending regulatory action.

Taken together, more than 30 jurisdictions have now adopted or are actively moving toward ISSB standards, which is the clearest sign yet of a genuine, if slow, convergence around a single investor-facing global baseline, even as the EU continues to run its own separate, more expansive system alongside it.

## 6. Which standards are legally required, and which are just widely used

It's worth being precise about this distinction, since "widely used" and "legally required" get conflated often in sustainability marketing:

**Legally required, depending on where you operate:**

- EU CSRD / ESRS, for large EU companies above the revised thresholds, and large non-EU companies with sufficient EU turnover.
- California SB 253 and SB 261, for large companies doing business in California above the relevant revenue thresholds.
- ISSB-based national standards, in jurisdictions that have made them mandatory (Australia, Hong Kong, Brazil for listed companies, and a growing list of others).
- UK Sustainability Reporting Standards, once formally required for UK-listed companies under FCA rules.

**Voluntary, but expected by investors, customers, or supply chains:**

- GRI, the most broadly used voluntary standard across company sizes and sectors.
- CDP, particularly for companies with investor requests or supply chain pressure from large customers.
- TCFD-style climate risk disclosure, now mostly absorbed into ISSB but still referenced directly by name in several jurisdictions, including as an accepted format under California's SB 261.
- TNFD, for companies wanting to extend climate-style risk disclosure to nature and biodiversity, and increasingly relevant to CSRD-scoped companies given its formal mapping to ESRS.
- B Corp certification, mostly among smaller and privately held companies seeking a public commitment to social and environmental standards.

## 7. Bottom line

The honest short answer to "which standard should we use" is that it depends entirely on where a company operates and who is asking. A large EU company still needs to take CSRD seriously, even with its scope substantially reduced. A large US company doing business in California needs to take SB 253 and SB 261 seriously regardless of what happens to the federal SEC rule. Everyone else is choosing, largely voluntarily, between GRI for broad stakeholder reporting, ISSB-aligned standards if investors are the main audience, and CDP if the request is coming from investors or a large customer's supply chain programme. The genuine convergence effort underway between ISSB, GRI, CDP, TNFD, and the Transition Plan Taskforce is real progress, and the TNFD-ESRS mapping is a useful concrete example of it, but a single global standard is still some years away, and the direction of travel in 2026 has actually diverged in places, with the EU simplifying its rules just as several other jurisdictions extend theirs.
