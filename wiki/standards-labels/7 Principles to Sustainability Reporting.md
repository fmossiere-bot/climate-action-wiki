Sustainability reporting has moved well beyond a voluntary exercise. Stakeholders across the board, from investors to employees to local communities, are holding companies accountable for their environmental and social impacts. According to the 2022 Edelman Trust Barometer, 58% of consumers buy or advocate for brands based on their beliefs and values, 60% choose employers the same way, 64% invest based on values, and 88% of institutional investors now subject ESG to the same scrutiny as financial performance.

Getting sustainability reporting right matters. These 7 principles provide a practical framework for doing so.

---

### 1. Completeness

A sustainability report should not be limited to what happens inside the factory gates. Company impacts are often spread across entire supply chains, and a complete report reflects that reality.

This means describing the scope and boundaries of the report clearly, including which business segments and sub-operations are covered. It also means mapping the organisation's value chain and identifying where direct and indirect material impacts occur, including upstream suppliers and downstream customers.

A good example of this in practice is PTT Global Chemical, whose 2022 sustainability report maps affected stakeholders against every stage of its business activities, from feedstocks through to downstream production, giving readers a clear picture of where impacts occur and how the company is managing them.

---

### 2. Operating Context

A sustainability report that ignores the world around it is of limited use. Operating context refers to the external trends and conditions that could affect an organisation's strategy and performance: climate policy, regulation, sector-specific trends, and broader societal shifts.

Reports should identify the key megatrends relevant to the business, discuss how these could affect strategy and performance, and explain how they are factored into the materiality process. For companies with significant climate exposure, this includes discussing transition plans and the financial metrics linked to physical and transition risks, in line with TCFD recommendations or similar frameworks.

Holcim's 2022 Integrated Annual Report is cited as good practice here. It includes a detailed discussion of how climate policy regulation, including the rising cost of carbon and more stringent product obligations, is shaping the company's transition to a low-carbon economy, with concrete actions around materials, waste recovery and innovation.

---

### 3. Materiality

Materiality is the process of identifying and prioritising the ESG issues that matter most, both to the business and to its stakeholders. A materiality assessment looks at the relative importance of issues to different stakeholders and their actual or potential impact on the business, and it should do more than shape the report. It should inform strategy, goals and KPIs.

Key recommendations include describing the steps taken to identify and validate material topics, using a range of factors such as external trends, magnitude of impact, likelihood and alignment with enterprise risk management. The outcomes should be disclosed through a matrix or prioritised list, and the report's content should be aligned with those outcomes.

It is also worth noting that the concept of double materiality is gaining ground, particularly in the context of the EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD). Double materiality requires companies to consider not only how sustainability issues affect the business financially, but also how the company's activities affect people and the planet. Royal Philips's 2022 Annual Report is cited as an early example of a company undertaking a preliminary double materiality analysis in preparation for these requirements.

---

### 4. Alignment

Once a materiality assessment has been completed, the report should reflect it. Aligned reports ensure that disclosures support the outcomes of the materiality assessment and prioritise quality over quantity. A long report covering many topics superficially is less useful than a focused one that addresses the material issues in depth.

In practice, this means avoiding both over-disclosure, filling the report with issues that are not material, and under-disclosure, glossing over issues that are. It also means ensuring that the organisation's strategy and targets are visibly connected to the same material topics. Companies that do not undertake a materiality assessment at all are penalised in quality evaluations.

---

### 5. Stakeholder Engagement

Sustainability reporting should not be produced in a vacuum. Stakeholder engagement is an ongoing, open dialogue with the people and groups that an organisation affects or that affect the organisation, including investors, customers, employees, suppliers and local communities.

Engagement can range from routine mechanisms like surveys and questionnaires to more formal processes such as stakeholder forums, advisory committees and structured dialogues. What matters is that the report demonstrates how this engagement shapes the company's understanding of material issues, and how it influences decisions.

The key recommendations are clear: identify who you engage with and how, disclose the formal mechanisms in place, and show specifically what different stakeholder groups raised and how those concerns were addressed. A broad statement that "we engaged with stakeholders" is not sufficient. Readers need to see evidence that the process was substantive.

Neste's 2022 Annual Report is highlighted as good practice. It includes a detailed table of engagement mechanisms, key topics raised by each stakeholder group, and how those inputs fed directly into the materiality assessment and ESG risk evaluation.

---

### 6. Balance

A sustainability report that only presents good news is not a credible document. Balanced reports are transparent about risks, failures, missed targets and areas for improvement, alongside genuine progress and positive performance.

This does not mean dwelling on problems unnecessarily. It means giving an honest account of the reporting period, including concrete examples of challenges encountered, areas where targets were not met, and external voices that provide additional perspective. Graphics and narrative should reflect performance accurately, not selectively.

Balance is particularly important because it builds trust. A report that presents an unrealistically positive picture is more likely to invite scepticism or accusations of greenwashing than one that acknowledges difficulty alongside progress. IKEA's FY22 Sustainability Report is referenced as a good example: it uses a clear three-pillar structure and gives each material topic dedicated space with honest, balanced disclosure that makes it easy for readers to engage with the content.

---

### 7. External Assurance

The final principle addresses credibility. External assurance means engaging an independent third party to verify the sustainability information in the report, going beyond internal controls and audits to provide an external opinion.

The key recommendations are to engage an assurance provider with a broad scope, covering both the reporting process and the material key performance indicators, and to aim for a reasonable level of assurance so that sustainability information meets financial-grade standards. The assurance statement itself should be easy to find in the report or accessible via direct links, and should specify the scope, boundaries, the standard applied and the assurance provider's statement of independence.

As sustainability reporting becomes subject to increasing regulatory scrutiny, particularly under the CSRD in Europe, external assurance is moving from optional best practice to a requirement for many organisations.

---

### Taken Together

These seven principles form a coherent approach to sustainability reporting: start with a complete picture of where impacts occur, ground the report in the external context, identify what genuinely matters through a rigorous materiality process, align the report's content with those findings, demonstrate real engagement with the people affected, present performance honestly and with balance, and have the whole thing verified by an independent party.

Reports that apply all seven principles consistently are more useful to readers, more credible to investors, and more likely to withstand the growing scrutiny that sustainability disclosures now face.
