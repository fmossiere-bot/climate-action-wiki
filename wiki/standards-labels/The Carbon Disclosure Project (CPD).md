CDP (formerly the Carbon Disclosure Project) **is one of the most referenced names in sustainability reporting, alongside frameworks like the Gold Standard, GRI, or CSRD**. 

> Unlike a certification body for individual projects, CDP is best understood as a global disclosure system: it does not verify climate action itself, but it collects, scores, and publishes the data that companies, cities, and regions report about their own environmental performance.



## 1. What is CDP?

CDP is a UK-registered, non-profit organisation founded in 2000 by Paul Dickinson and Tessa Tennant. The original idea was simple: get investors to formally ask companies to disclose their greenhouse gas emissions, so that climate risk could be assessed the same way financial risk already was. Since then, the scope has widened well beyond carbon.

Today, CDP runs a standardised annual questionnaire covering three main areas:

- **Climate change**: emissions (scope 1, 2 and, increasingly, scope 3), climate risks and opportunities, and transition plans.
- **Forests**: exposure to deforestation risk in commodities such as timber, palm oil, soy, and cattle products.
- **Water security**: water use, risk, and management across operations and supply chains.

Organisations respond either because they are asked to (by investors through CDP's "Letter to the Board", by a customer through the Supply Chain programme, or by a city network), or because they choose to disclose voluntarily. Each response is scored on a scale from D- to A, based on four levels of maturity: Disclosure, Awareness, Management, and Leadership. Companies that reach the top band across all relevant themes make CDP's "A List", and those scoring A in climate, forests, and water together earn the rare "Triple A" rating.

Scale gives CDP its weight. In the 2025 cycle, over 22,100 companies disclosed, representing more than half of global stock market value, and CDP's data is used by financial institutions managing a significant share of the world's institutional assets. On the local government side, more than 1,000 cities, states and regions, together representing over a billion people, disclosed through CDP's dedicated CDP-ICLEI Track platform.

## 2. Latest updates

CDP's questionnaire and structure change most years, and the 2026 cycle brought some notable shifts:

- **A single integrated questionnaire.** Rather than separate forms for climate, forests, and water, companies now go through one combined questionnaire, with the specific modules shown depending on their size, sector, and risk profile.
- **A new Oceans module.** For the first time, companies can report on ocean-related risks, dependencies, and impacts, alongside the existing climate, forest, and water themes.
- **An SME pathway to a Leadership score.** Small and medium-sized companies can now achieve an "SME A" score for climate change, distinct from the full corporate A score, giving smaller businesses a clearer, more proportionate route to recognition.
- **Closer alignment with mandatory reporting.** CDP has continued to strengthen the overlap between its own questionnaire and frameworks such as the EU's CSRD/ESRS, the ISSB's IFRS S2, and TNFD, positioning a CDP response as a starting point companies can reuse across several reporting obligations rather than a stand-alone exercise.
- **Institutional restructuring.** CDP has announced it will split into two connected entities: a non-profit CDP Foundation and a commercial CDP arm, intended to keep the public-interest mission separate from the services and data products CDP sells to businesses and investors.
- **Key 2026 dates**: the questionnaire opened in Q2, the scoring deadline fell in mid-September, and the reporting window closes in late October, with A List results typically published in the following January.

## 3. Cities, states and regions

Alongside the corporate questionnaire, CDP runs a parallel system for local and regional governments, delivered jointly with ICLEI (an international network of local governments for sustainability) under the name CDP-ICLEI Track. To be scored, a city needs, at minimum, a clear greenhouse gas emissions inventory and a published climate action plan, and higher scores require evidence that action is actually being implemented, not just planned.

> This part of CDP's work has grown quickly. In the 2025 cycle, 738 cities were scored, with 122 receiving the top A grade, alongside a growing States and Regions A List now in its second year. Cities named on recent A Lists give a sense of the range involved, from major capitals to mid-sized towns: London, Barcelona, Mexico City, Cape Town, Auckland, Johannesburg, Denver, and Quito have all featured in different years, alongside a steady expansion of UK entries such as Birmingham, Brighton & Hove, Cambridge, and Sunderland.

Ireland's presence on the list is worth noting directly: **Belfast is currently the only city on the island of Ireland to hold an A rating**, having retained it for four consecutive years as of 2026. It is recognised alongside roughly twenty other UK local authorities. Belfast's submission points to a citywide emissions inventory, a published Net Zero Carbon Roadmap and Climate Action Plan, renewable electricity supply agreements for council buildings, and governance structures such as the Belfast Climate Commission. No city in the Republic of Ireland currently appears on the A List, though a number of Irish companies do (see below).

On the corporate side specifically, the CDP Ireland Network, a local affiliate that supports Irish companies through the disclosure process, publishes its own annual Ireland Climate Change report. Recent editions show steady growth in participation, a rising share of companies with externally verified science-based targets, and an increasing proportion providing third-party verification of their emissions data, alongside a recurring warning that the overall pace of decarbonisation still lags what is needed.

## 4. Credibility and controversy

CDP is widely regarded as the most established environmental disclosure platform in the world, and it is frequently referred to, including by CDP itself, as a kind of "gold standard" for reporting rigour. Financial institutions rely on it, and regulators increasingly reference it when designing mandatory disclosure rules. That said, a few points are worth keeping in mind.

**CDP scores disclosure quality, not environmental performance.** A high CDP score means a company has reported thoroughly and transparently, has credible targets, and shows evidence of managing environmental risk. It does not mean the company's overall emissions are low, or that its total environmental footprint is small. A large, carbon-intensive company with a serious, well-documented transition plan can score higher than a smaller company that discloses less rigorously but happens to pollute less. This distinction is often lost when CDP scores are used in marketing.

**The system depends on self-reported data.** CDP does not independently measure a company's emissions. It checks that data is complete, consistent, and increasingly cross-references it against a company's other public disclosures, but the underlying figures still come from the company itself, with third-party verification treated as a scoring bonus rather than a strict requirement for every data point.

**Participation is still uneven.** Even with more than 22,000 corporate disclosures a year, large parts of the global economy, particularly private and smaller companies outside CDP's investor and supply chain request networks, are not covered. The same is true geographically for cities: participation remains concentrated in Europe, North America and a limited number of Global South countries, so the A List is not a full picture of global climate leadership, only of climate leadership among those who chose or were asked to disclose.

**Scoring methodology changes make year-on-year comparison difficult.** Because the questionnaire is revised most years, sometimes substantially, a change in a company's or city's score is not always a clean signal that performance actually improved or worsened. It can also reflect a change in the questions asked.

None of this amounts to CDP being unreliable. The criticism is closer to "understand what the score measures" than "distrust the system." Used correctly, CDP data is one of the better publicly available proxies for how seriously an organisation treats environmental risk, and it is one of the few systems that gives cities the same kind of structured scrutiny normally reserved for companies.

## 5. Bottom line

CDP is not a certification like the Gold Standard or FSC, and it does not claim a project or product is "sustainable." It is a reporting and scoring system that makes environmental disclosure comparable across thousands of organisations, and its scale gives it real influence over investment and procurement decisions. For anyone assessing a company or a city through its CDP score, the most useful approach is to read the score as a measure of transparency and management maturity, check whether the underlying emissions data is third-party verified, and look at the direction of travel over several years rather than any single score in isolation.
