The UK's Sustainability Disclosure Requirements (SDR) are the Financial Conduct Authority's answer to a problem that has dogged sustainable investing for years: too many products marketed as "green," "ESG," or "sustainable" with very little standing behind the label. SDR is not a certification like the Gold Standard or FSC, and it is not a broad corporate reporting law like the EU's CSRD. It is a financial services regime, aimed first at asset managers and the products they sell, with wider corporate reporting still some way off.

## 1. What is SDR?

SDR is a package of FCA rules built around three main pieces, plus one rule that sits above all of them.

**The anti-greenwashing rule.** This is the broadest part of SDR, and it applies to every FCA-authorised firm, not just asset managers. Any sustainability-related claim a firm makes about a product or service, in marketing, official statements, or client communications, must be fair, clear, and not misleading, and must be backed by evidence. This rule has been in force since 31 May 2024 and is not optional in the way the labels are.

**A voluntary labelling system.** Asset managers can, if they choose, apply one of four labels to a fund:

- **Sustainability focus**, for products whose assets are primarily selected for their sustainability characteristics.
- **Sustainability improvers**, for products investing in assets that have the potential to improve their sustainability performance over time.
- **Sustainability impact**, for products aiming for a measurable environmental or social impact.
- **Mixed goals**, for products combining more than one of the above approaches.

To use a label, a fund manager has to notify the FCA, meet specific criteria on investment strategy, reporting, governance, and stewardship, and then maintain and document ongoing compliance. Funds that market themselves using sustainability-related terms without adopting a label are not exempt from disclosure; they still face pre-contractual and ongoing reporting requirements, plus a requirement to explain why they opted out of labelling.

**Sustainability entity reports.** These are annual reports required from larger asset managers, covering the firm's overall approach to sustainability risk and opportunity across its whole business, not just a single product. They follow the same four-pillar structure used by TCFD and ISSB: governance, strategy, risk management, and metrics and targets.

## 2. Who needs to comply, and when

SDR is being phased in gradually, and the timeline matters because different obligations apply to different sizes of firm:

- **The anti-greenwashing rule**: in force for all FCA-authorised firms since 31 May 2024.
- **Labelling and naming and marketing rules**: in force since 31 July 2024 for firms using labels, and since December 2024 for the naming and marketing restrictions more broadly.
- **Ongoing product-level disclosures**: came into force on 2 December 2025, applying to both labelled and unlabelled products that use sustainability-related terms.
- **Entity-level reports for the largest firms**: firms with £50 billion or more in assets under management have had to report since 2 December 2025.
- **Entity-level reports for mid-size firms**: firms with between £5 billion and £50 billion under management have until 2 December 2026.
- **Distributor rules**: platforms and advisers distributing labelled or sustainability-marketed products have had obligations to pass on labels and disclosures since 31 July 2024.

**Listed companies specifically.** Separately from the asset manager rules, the FCA is consulting on requiring listed companies to report against the UK Sustainability Reporting Standards (UK SRS), a UK-specific standard closely modelled on IFRS S1 and S2. UK SRS S1 and S2 were published in February 2026 and are currently available for voluntary use only. The FCA's proposal would make reporting mandatory for listed companies from 1 January 2027, with final rules expected in autumn 2026.

**Other non-financial companies.** Broader corporate reporting obligations for private companies, outside the listed company proposal above, are being considered through the UK government's Modernising Corporate Reporting programme, working within the existing Companies Act framework. A further consultation on this is expected later in 2026, so mandatory reporting for private companies is not yet close.

In short: SDR is live now, but its bite is currently concentrated in financial services. Everyone else, including most non-listed companies, is not yet legally required to report against a UK-specific standard, though that is expected to change from 2027 onward.

## 3. Latest developments

A few things have moved since SDR's rules were first finalised in November 2023:

- **FCA guidance on good and poor labelling practice, published February 2026.** Drawing on what the FCA has seen through the fund authorisation process, the regulator found that pre-contractual disclosures have generally improved as firms became more familiar with the rules, but flagged that it has not always been clear whether firms genuinely meet the criteria for the label they use, or whether disclosures accurately reflect what a fund actually invests in.
- **Minor rule amendments from December 2025**, following a consultation, tidying up some of the original 2023 rules.
- **A revised FRC Stewardship Code**, effective 1 January 2026, which introduced "sustainable" into its own definition of stewardship. The FCA has clarified this does not conflict with SDR's naming and marketing rules, since the two operate in different contexts (stewardship reporting versus product marketing).
- **The listed company consultation**, described above, which is the most consequential near-term change: if adopted as proposed, it would be the first point at which SDR-adjacent reporting becomes mandatory outside the asset management sector specifically.
- **No formal alignment with the EU's SFDR.** The UK's SDR was deliberately built as a separate framework from the EU's Sustainable Finance Disclosure Regulation, rather than a mirror of it, which means asset managers operating in both markets need to run two distinct compliance processes rather than relying on one to satisfy the other.

## 4. Credibility and limitations

SDR is generally well regarded as a response to a genuine market problem, but a few practical limitations are worth knowing.

**The labels are voluntary, which limits their reach.** A fund can continue to market itself using sustainability language without ever adopting one of the four labels, provided it meets the separate naming and marketing conditions and discloses why it has opted out. This means the labelling system, while useful where it is used, does not by itself guarantee that every fund using sustainability language has gone through the full labelling criteria.

**Self-assessment sits at the centre of the labelling system.** Fund managers validate and document their own adherence to a label's criteria internally, rather than through a fully independent certification process comparable to something like the Gold Standard's third-party verification. The FCA's February 2026 findings, that it isn't always clear whether firms meet the requirements they claim to meet, reflect this gap directly.

**Coverage is currently narrow relative to the wider economy.** SDR's mandatory teeth are concentrated in asset management and, prospectively, listed companies. Private, non-listed companies, which make up the bulk of the UK economy, are not yet in scope for mandatory UK-specific sustainability reporting, and won't be until at least the outcome of the Modernising Corporate Reporting consultation later in 2026.

**Divergence from the EU adds complexity rather than reducing it.** Because SDR is not aligned with the EU's SFDR, asset managers marketing products in both jurisdictions cannot simply reuse one regime's disclosures for the other, which works against the broader push toward international convergence described in CSRD and ISSB coverage elsewhere.

None of this undermines the basic logic of SDR. The anti-greenwashing rule in particular is a strong, broad backstop precisely because it applies regardless of whether a label is used at all. The main caveat is simply that SDR, as it stands today, is a financial services regime first, and a general corporate reporting standard only in prospect.

## 5. Bottom line

SDR is currently the UK's answer to fund-level greenwashing, not yet a general corporate sustainability reporting law. If you're assessing an investment product, check first whether it uses one of the four FCA labels and, if not, whether it has provided the required explanation for not doing so; either way, the anti-greenwashing rule means any sustainability claim attached to it has to be evidenced. If you're a listed company, the more relevant date to watch is 1 January 2027, when UK SRS reporting may become mandatory. If you're a private company, SDR itself does not yet apply to you directly, though the direction of UK policy suggests that will eventually change.
