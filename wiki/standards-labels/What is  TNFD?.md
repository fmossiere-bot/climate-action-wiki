_Nature-related financial risks are moving up the corporate agenda. The TNFD was built to give organisations a way to measure and disclose those risks. It is now entering a new phase._

**1. What is the TNFD?**

> The Taskforce on Nature-related Financial Disclosures (TNFD) is an international framework that helps companies and financial institutions identify, assess, and report on risks related to nature. That includes biodiversity loss, water stress, deforestation, land degradation, and other pressures on ecosystems that businesses both depend on and contribute to.

The TNFD published its final recommendations in September 2023. The framework is structured around four pillars that will be familiar to anyone who has worked with climate-related reporting: governance, strategy, risk and impact management, and metrics and targets. This design was deliberate. The TNFD was built to sit alongside the Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures (TCFD) and the ISSB's IFRS sustainability standards, giving organisations a coherent way to address both climate and nature in their reporting.

The framework also aligns with the Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework (GBF), the international agreement adopted in 2022 that sets targets for halting and reversing biodiversity loss by 2030.

---

**2. Who has adopted it?**

> Adoption has grown quickly since the recommendations were finalised. As of late 2025, over 700 companies with a combined market value of around 9.4 trillion US dollars have committed to TNFD-aligned reporting. This is up from 320 organisations at the start of 2024, when only 500 had signed on.

Early adopters come from a wide range of sectors and geographies, spanning 54 countries and 62 of the 77 industry sectors covered by SASB standards. Names include KPMG, Qantas, abrdn, and Tokyo Electric Power.

This growth reflects a broader shift. According to CDP data, biodiversity disclosures increased by 43% between 2022 and 2024, following the adoption of the GBF. Water-related reporting rose by 23%, and forest disclosures by 10%. That said, the gaps remain significant. Only around 10% of companies currently assess their dependency on biodiversity, despite estimates that nature loss could cost the global economy up to 20 trillion US dollars per year.

---

**3. What happens now?**

> In November 2025, the TNFD announced that it will wind down its operations by 2026 and hand responsibility for nature-related reporting standards to the International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB).

This is a significant step. The ISSB, which already oversees the IFRS sustainability disclosure standards, will develop a new standard based on the TNFD framework. A draft is expected before COP17 in October 2026, with final standards likely to follow in 2027.

The TNFD will complete its remaining technical work by the third quarter of 2026, including new guidance published in late 2025 to help companies integrate nature into their broader transition plans.

Emmanuel Faber, chair of the ISSB, has encouraged organisations already working with the TNFD framework to continue doing so. Using TNFD-aligned reporting now is seen as the most practical way to stay consistent with whatever the ISSB eventually publishes.

---

**Bottom line**

The TNFD has succeeded in putting nature on the corporate reporting agenda and in building a credible framework where none previously existed. Handing over to the ISSB signals that nature reporting is no longer a niche concern but part of the mainstream of sustainability disclosure. For companies already reporting on climate, the direction of travel is clear: nature-related risks will be expected to sit alongside climate risks in formal disclosures within the next few years.
