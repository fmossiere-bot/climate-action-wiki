# SBTi — Science Based Targets initiative

## What is it

The **Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi)** is a UK-based nonprofit that sets the rules for how companies can credibly claim to be pursuing net-zero emissions. It is widely regarded as the gold standard for corporate climate commitments. A company with an SBTi-approved target has had its decarbonisation plan reviewed against climate science — specifically, whether the company's emissions reduction trajectory is consistent with limiting global warming to 1.5°C.

In June 2026, SBTi published a major update to its corporate net-zero standard. The new framework is described as a **"best-efforts" approach**: companies must take every action within their control, but the standard now acknowledges that they cannot control everything.

## Why it matters

Thousands of large companies globally have signed up to SBTi. The rules SBTi sets determine what counts as genuine progress versus greenwashing. The 2026 update is significant because it expands what companies can count toward their targets, which has been welcomed by business as more practical but criticised by some environmentalists as a dilution of standards.

## What changed in the 2026 standard

Until 2026, SBTi required companies to focus their efforts on decarbonising their own supply chains. Buying external credits or offsets did not count toward SBTi-approved targets. The new rules change this in two important ways.

First, companies may now count **purchased environmental credits** in their footprint calculations. This includes instruments such as sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) credits, renewable energy certificates, and similar market-based mitigation tools purchased outside the company's own supply chain. These were previously excluded.

Second, **carbon removal technologies** — including direct air capture, reforestation credits, and similar approaches — will be allowed from 2035 onward, to cover the small share of emissions that are genuinely impossible to eliminate by then.

The changes are framed around the idea that companies face structural barriers they cannot overcome alone, and that penalising them for those barriers does not serve anyone. CEO David Kennedy described the transition as "a continuous improvement journey."

## Power purchase agreements and electricity matching

**Power purchase agreements (PPAs)** — long-term contracts that directly fund new renewable energy — can now be counted toward a company's clean electricity claims. Where structural barriers prevent local PPAs, companies can claim credit for PPAs in other regions.

On hourly electricity matching (calculating emissions at source each hour rather than annually), SBTi says hourly matching is preferable but will not mandate it for target-setting. Companies with significant electricity use, such as data centre operators, must at least report what share of their consumption is matched with low-carbon electricity on an hourly basis.

## Key facts

- SBTi's 2026 update allows **market-based environmental credits** to count toward net-zero targets for the first time.
- **Carbon removals** (reforestation, DAC) allowed from **2035** for hard-to-abate residual emissions.
- **PPAs** now count; local is preferred, but cross-regional PPAs are permitted where barriers exist.
- Hourly electricity matching is described as "generally preferable" but not required.
- Physical decarbonisation within the supply chain remains the primary expectation.
- Companies must be transparent about barriers that limited what was possible.
