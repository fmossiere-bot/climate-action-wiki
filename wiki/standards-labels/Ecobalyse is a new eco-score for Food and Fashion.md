A quiet but significant shift is underway in how clothing sold in France must be labelled. Ecobalyse is the tool at the centre of it. (source: See https://ecobalyse.beta.gouv.fr/ )

---

**1. What is Ecobalyse?**

Ecobalyse is a tool developed by the French government to help manufacturers understand and calculate the environmental impact of products sold in France. It was built under the direction of ADEME (the French ecological transition agency) and the Ministry of Ecological Transition, and is hosted on the government's open digital platform, beta.gouv.fr.

The tool uses predefined life cycle assessment data to calculate the environmental impact of each stage of a product's life, from raw material extraction and production through to distribution and disposal.

> The result is expressed as an impact score, called the "environmental cost." The higher the score, the greater the environmental impact. The score is not capped at a maximum value, so the intention is to compare products against each other rather than against a fixed threshold.

The tool is free to use and its source code is fully public on GitHub, which is relatively unusual in environmental regulation and makes the methodology open to scrutiny.

---

**2. What does it measure?**

A product's environmental cost is determined using **16 key indicators**, covering greenhouse gas emissions, water and land use, energy consumption, mineral resource depletion, and toxicity to humans and ecosystems. The scoring system is grounded in life cycle assessment principles and draws on the European Product Environmental Footprint methodology.

For textiles specifically, two additional criteria are included: one that penalises exporting used garments outside the EU, and one that scores materials based on microplastic fibre release during washing. This means that fast fashion brands, which tend to use synthetic fibres and encourage high volumes of purchases, can be differentiated from brands with more durable or sustainable practices, even when basic production metrics look similar.

---

**3. Which sectors does it cover?**

> Ecobalyse currently covers clothing, food products, and everyday objects, though the simulator for objects is still under development. The textile sector has been the primary focus, with the food sector already covered by a stable methodology.

The tool models the full life cycle of garments: from raw materials, through processing steps including spinning, dyeing, and assembly, to transport, distribution, use, and end of life.

---

**4. Why does it matter and what comes next?**

Ecobalyse is the official reference tool under France's environmental labelling scheme for textiles, introduced through the AGEC anti-waste law and formalised by the 2021 Climate and Resilience Law.

The timeline for compliance is now confirmed. From October 2025, brands can voluntarily calculate and submit their environmental costs to a public portal. **From October 2026, any third party, including retailers, NGOs, comparison platforms, or journalists, can publish an environmental cost score on behalf of a brand without the brand's prior approval**, using conservative default data assumptions if the brand has not done so itself. This is a meaningful pressure point: brands that do not calculate their own score lose control of how they are presented.

The scope may also grow beyond France. The European Union is preparing, under the Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation adopted in 2024, an obligation for environmental labelling of textiles across all member states by 2030, and Ecobalyse is being cited as a likely model for the European methodology.

---

**Bottom line**

Ecobalyse is a practical, government-built tool that translates life cycle assessment into a single number on a product label. For now it applies to France, but the direction is clearly towards broader European adoption. Brands selling clothing in France need to act before October 2026. Those who do not calculate their own score risk having it done for them, on less favourable terms.
