An **Environmental Product Declaration, or EPD**, is one of the more technical tools in the sustainability toolkit. Unlike a label such as FSC or the Gold Standard, an EPD does not tell you whether a product is "good" or "bad" for the environment. It gives you standardised, verified numbers, and leaves the judgement to whoever is reading it. That makes EPDs less visible to the average consumer, but increasingly central to how construction, manufacturing, and procurement decisions get made in Europe.

## 1. What is an EPD?

An EPD is a Type III environmental declaration under the international standard ISO 14025. In practice, this means it is a document that quantifies a product's environmental impact across its life cycle, from raw material extraction through manufacturing, use, and end of life, based on a Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) carried out under ISO 14040 and ISO 14044.

Three things distinguish an EPD from an ordinary sustainability claim:

- **It follows fixed rules.** Every EPD for a given product type is built using the same Product Category Rules (PCR), a technical document that fixes the functional unit, system boundaries, and impact categories to be measured. This is what allows two EPDs for, say, insulation panels or floor tiles, to be compared on a like-for-like basis, at least when they come from the same PCR and programme.
- **It is independently verified.** A qualified third-party verifier checks the underlying LCA and the resulting document before it can be published. This is different from a self-declared environmental claim (ISO 14021), which carries no independent check.
- **It does not rank or endorse.** An EPD reports figures such as global warming potential, water use, or resource depletion. It does not say a product is environmentally superior to another, even when a comparison is technically possible. That judgement is left to the reader, typically an architect, specifier, or procurement team.

In the construction sector specifically, the European standard EN 15804 sits alongside ISO 14025 as the common Product Category Rule, which is why EPDs are so widespread for building materials such as concrete, steel, insulation, and timber. Sub-standards, such as EN 16757 for concrete or EN 50693 for electrical products, add further detail for specific product families.

An EPD is typically valid for five years, after which it must be renewed or updated, and it usually costs several thousand euros to produce once the underlying LCA is in place, which is one reason smaller manufacturers have been slower to adopt them.

## 2. Who issues EPDs, and how the system is organised

EPDs are not issued by a single global body. Instead, a network of "programme operators" administers the rules, verification, and publication of EPDs within their own systems. The main ones active in Europe include:

- **EPD International (the International EPD System)**, based in Sweden, one of the largest and most widely used systems globally.
- **Institut Bauen und Umwelt (IBU)**, based in Germany, prominent in the construction sector.
- **BRE**, in the United Kingdom, which also runs its own Verified EPD scheme linked to BREEAM.
- **AENOR's GlobalEPD programme**, in Spain.
- **EPD Hub**, a newer, internationally active programme operator compliant with ISO 14025, EN 15804, and several other related standards.

To reduce fragmentation, the main European programme operators formed **ECO Platform**, which audits member programmes against EN 15804 and its own verification guidelines. An EPD carrying the ECO Platform mark is intended to be recognised across Europe, rather than only within the issuing programme, and is available in digital, machine-readable format through the ECO Portal.

Growth has been rapid. The International EPD System alone passed 18,000 valid, registered EPDs in 2025, and more than 9,000 new EPDs were published globally that year across all programme operators combined. Newer digital formats, such as EN ISO 22057 and openEPD, are being developed so that EPD data can be pulled directly into building design and Building Information Modelling (BIM) software, rather than read manually from a PDF.

## 3. Latest developments

A few regulatory and technical changes are shaping EPDs going into 2026 and beyond:

- **A new EU Construction Products Regulation** (Regulation (EU) 2024/3110) replaced the older Construction Products Regulation from January 2025 and starts applying to most construction products from January 2026. It ties environmental performance data more directly to the mandatory Declaration of Performance, which is expected to push EPD-style data from a competitive advantage toward a baseline requirement for construction products sold in the EU.
- **A common EU method for whole-life carbon in buildings.** From 2028, member states must report the whole-life global warming potential of new buildings above 1,000 square metres, extending to all new buildings from 2030. This creates direct, structural demand for the product-level carbon data that EPDs supply, since a building-level calculation is only as good as the EPDs behind its individual materials.
- **Digital Product Passports.** By 2028, construction product environmental data, including EPD-derived figures, is expected to feed into the EU's Digital Product Passport initiative, extending the life of this data well beyond individual sales documents.
- **A revised ISO 14025.** The underlying international standard is being updated for the first time since 2006, with a new edition expected to formalise some of the practices, such as digital and machine-readable formats, that have developed informally over the past decade.
- **The EU Green Claims Directive.** Once in force, this will require companies making environmental claims about a product to back them up with accurate, verified, life-cycle-based evidence, which is precisely what an EPD is designed to provide. This is likely to push more companies toward EPDs as the safest way to support a specific environmental claim.

## 4. Credibility and limitations

EPDs sit near the more rigorous end of environmental disclosure tools, largely because of the combination of fixed rules (PCRs) and mandatory independent verification. That said, several limitations are worth knowing before treating an EPD as a simple green or red signal.

**An EPD does not mean a product is good for the environment.** This is worth repeating because it is the most common misunderstanding. An EPD is a transparency document, not an endorsement. A product with high embodied carbon can still have a perfectly valid EPD; the EPD simply confirms that the high figure has been measured and verified, not that the figure is low.

**Comparability has real limits.** Two EPDs are only safely comparable when they share the same PCR, the same functional unit, and ideally the same programme operator. EPDs from different product categories, or built on different versions of a standard, are not directly comparable even though they look similar on the page. Readers who are not familiar with LCA methodology can easily draw the wrong conclusion from placing two EPDs side by side.

**Scope choices can flatter the result.** A "cradle-to-gate" EPD, which stops at the factory door, tells a different and generally more favourable story than a "cradle-to-grave" EPD, which includes use and end-of-life impacts. Both are legitimate under the standards, but a company can choose the narrower scope for a product where its use-phase or disposal impacts are less favourable, without technically breaking any rule.

**Verification quality is not uniform across all markets.** ECO Platform's audit process is generally well regarded in Europe, but EPD programmes outside this network, particularly in North America and Asia, vary more in how strictly PCRs are applied and how rigorously verifiers are vetted, which means the label "third-party verified" carries slightly different weight depending on the programme behind it.

**Cost remains a barrier for smaller manufacturers.** Because a full LCA and verification process typically costs several thousand euros per product, EPDs remain more common among larger manufacturers with the budget and technical capacity to commission them, which can put smaller or newer producers at a disclosure disadvantage even where their actual environmental performance is competitive.

## 5. Bottom line

An EPD is one of the more credible tools available for comparing the environmental footprint of individual products, precisely because it is boring by design: fixed rules, independent verification, and no marketing spin allowed in the figures themselves. Its value depends entirely on how it is read. Used correctly, an EPD supports genuinely informed decisions between products that do the same job. Used carelessly, or quoted out of context in marketing material, the same document can create a false impression of environmental superiority that the EPD itself was never meant to claim. For anyone assessing a supplier's EPDs, it is worth checking three things: which PCR and programme operator were used, whether the scope is cradle-to-gate or cradle-to-grave, and whether the EPD is being compared only against others built on the same rules.
