> Wetlands are critical ecosystems that provide numerous environmental benefits, including water filtration, wildlife habitat, and carbon sequestration. They are found all over the globe, from the tundra regions of the Arctic to the tropics. The distribution and abundance of wetlands can vary greatly by region, depending on factors such as climate, topography, and human activity.

Globally, wetlands can be categorized into several types, including:

- **Mangroves**: Found in coastal areas in tropical and subtropical regions.
- **Peatlands**: Including bogs and fens, found in both northern and southern hemispheres.
- **Marshes**: Including both freshwater and saltwater marshes, located in various climates.
- **Swamps**: Characterized by woody plants, found in many parts of the world.
- **Floodplains**: Associated with rivers and streams, found worldwide.

## Wetlands and Ireland
#Ireland 
Ireland has a variety of wetland habitats, including bogs (both raised and blanket bogs), fens, marshes, and swamps. These ecosystems cover a significant portion of the country, with **==peatlands==** (bogs and fens) being especially prominent. ## About Peatland
Peatlands are wetlands in which restoration typically has three main aims. 
1. Firstly, to manage vegetation more effectively to restore sites and their conservation features to favourable condition; 
2. secondly, where feasible, to restore peat formation; 
3. and finally appropriate management of water (chiefly peat wetness, but also water quality, runoff volume and timing) 

Vegetation management occurs on a continuum from the vegetation of bare peat, to projects aimed at restoring appropriate plant community composition. In UK peatlands, targets for restoration have largely focused on achieving favourable nature conservation status on protected sites. In recent years, with the involvement of water companies and the movement up the political agenda of peatlands as potential carbon stores, attention has focused on large area restoration projects in the uplands. Results suggest that rewetting has been achieved by the blocking of 1000s of kilometres of drainage ditches. Considerable gains to biodiversity and reductions in carbon loss have been made over large areas by reduction in grazing animal stock densities and removal of burning management. In upland and lowland peatlands, successful restoration following tree removal has been achieved where it has been possible to return the hydrological dynamics to the pre-plantation state. However in many afforested, drained and burnt over sites, oxidation and compression of surface peat layers has resulted in changes in hydrological function, increasing the risk of invasion from undesirable plant species and the loss of Sphagna.
