Freshwater is one of the most essential resources on the planet, and one of the most taken for granted. It underpins human health, food production, industry, and ecosystems. It is also finite, increasingly scarce, and under growing pressure from both population growth and climate change.

Freshwater is a recognised planetary boundary. Scientists now conclude that this boundary has already been crossed. We are withdrawing and disrupting water systems faster than they can regenerate, and the consequences are becoming increasingly visible around the world.
 
 > Fresh Water is part of the 9 Planetary Boundaries that we need to respect within a safe space in order to keep the Planet HABITABLE

### How Much Fresh Water Is There?

Only 3% of all water on Earth is fresh. Of that, around 2.5% is locked in ice, soil and the atmosphere. The amount of liquid fresh water available in lakes and rivers is remarkably small. If you collected all of it into a single sphere, it would measure only about 56 kilometres in diameter.

We are already withdrawing roughly 17% more water than nature can regenerate each year. In some countries the situation is far more severe. China extracts around 22% more than can be naturally replaced. India extracts around 52% more.

Global water demand is projected to grow by 55% by 2050, driven by population growth, agricultural expansion and industrial use.

### Where Does Water Actually Go?

Most people think of water use in terms of showers, taps and washing machines. In reality, household use is a relatively small part of the picture. **Around 87% of all the water that people consume globally goes to irrigated agriculture**. That is twenty-two times more than all household use by the world's 8 billion people combined.

This is where the concept of the **water footprint** becomes useful. Everything we consume has a water footprint, meaning the total volume of fresh water used to produce it, often in a completely different part of the world.

Some numbers that illustrate the scale:

- 1 kg of beef requires around 15,400 litres of water to produce
- 1 kg of chocolate requires around 17,200 litres
- 1 kg of roasted coffee requires around 18,900 litres
- 1 kg of nuts requires around 9,000 litres
- 1 kg of rice requires around 2,250 litres
- 1 kg of vegetables requires on average around 320 litres

The average consumer effectively uses around 5,000 litres of water per day through the food and products they consume, ranging from 1,500 to 10,000 litres depending on diet and location. Most of this water is invisible: it is embedded in what we eat, wear and use.

Switching from beef to chicken just a few times a week can reduce a household's annual water footprint by hundreds of thousands of litres. The choices we make at the supermarket have a real and direct impact on water systems, often in countries far from our own.

### Blue Water, Green Water and the Planetary Boundary

Scientists now distinguish between two types of water when assessing our impact on the global water cycle:

- **Blue water** is surface water and groundwater that we extract directly from rivers, lakes and aquifers.
- **Green water** is the moisture in soil that is absorbed by plants and returned to the atmosphere through evaporation and transpiration.

For a long time, assessments of water stress focused mainly on blue water extraction. More recent science has broadened this to include how human activities, particularly land use change, deforestation and climate change, are altering the green water cycle as well. This shift in understanding led scientists in 2022 and 2023 to conclude that the planetary boundary for freshwater has been transgressed. We are no longer operating within safe limits for the global water system.

This matters because disruptions to the green water cycle affect soil moisture, vegetation health, rainfall patterns and ultimately the stability of major ecosystems such as the Amazon rainforest.

### Climate Change Is Making It Worse

Climate change does not just reduce rainfall in some regions. It actively disrupts the water cycle itself. For each degree Celsius of warming, the atmosphere absorbs around 7% more water vapour, which intensifies rainfall events in some places and deepens droughts in others.

The communities that are most affected are typically those that have contributed the least to climate change. In the Tana watershed in Ethiopia, an area historically known for abundant water, communities are now experiencing shortages driven by a combination of local population growth, inadequate infrastructure and global forces entirely outside their control: rising temperatures, changing rainfall patterns and the demands of international trade.

---

### Virtual Water and Global Trade

One of the less visible dimensions of the water problem is what researchers call **virtual water**: the water embedded in goods and food that is traded internationally. Half of the virtual water imported by high-income countries comes from regions that are already facing high water stress. In other words, wealthy countries are effectively importing water scarcity from poorer ones.

Virtual water trade is projected to triple by the end of this century if current trends continue. This raises serious questions about equity, sovereignty and global responsibility for water as a shared resource.

At the UN Water Conference in 2023, the first such international gathering in nearly 50 years, the concept of water as a global common good was raised as a central theme. It remains far from consensus, but the direction of the conversation is shifting.

### Water in Ireland

Ireland has some of the best water quality and cleanest beaches in Europe. The country's climate has historically meant enough rainfall to meet most needs. However, Ireland's per capita water usage is significantly higher than the European average, and the combination of climate change and a growing population is expected to put that supply under increasing pressure in the medium to long term.

> The Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) plays a central role in managing and protecting Ireland's water. Its responsibilities include monitoring the ecological health of rivers, lakes, estuaries and coastal waters under the EU Water Framework Directive, as well as overseeing groundwater quality, regulating drinking water standards and inspecting wastewater treatment systems.

Groundwater in Ireland originates from rainfall that seeps through soil and bedrock, feeding rivers, wetlands and drinking water supplies. It is vulnerable to pollution from pesticide spreading, chemical spills and leaking septic tanks. The EPA runs a national groundwater monitoring programme to track quality and flag risks.

On drinking water, the EPA conducts audits, maintains a Remedial Action List for supplies that fall below standard, and works to eliminate risks from harmful bacteria, disinfection by-products, lead in old pipework and pesticide contamination. It publishes an annual report on the quality of both public and private water supplies.

Half of septic tank systems in Ireland fail inspection, which is a significant risk to both groundwater and public health in rural areas where connection to mains drainage is not available.

### What We Can Do

The scale of the problem can feel overwhelming, but there are real and practical actions at every level.

> At household level, simple habits matter: shorter showers, fixing leaks, full loads in washing machines and dishwashers, and being mindful of garden watering. A shower running at 10 to 15 litres per minute adds up quickly.

But the bigger leverage is in what we eat. Shifting towards less water-intensive foods, particularly reducing beef consumption, is one of the most impactful personal choices available. Reducing food waste also matters, because wasted food carries with it all the water used to produce it.

At policy level, the priority is to treat water not as a local utility issue but as a global commons that requires coordinated governance, fair pricing and genuine accountability, particularly for the industries and trade flows that drive the largest share of consumption.
