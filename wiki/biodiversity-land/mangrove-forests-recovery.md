# Mangrove Forests Recovery

## What is it

**Mangrove forests** are salt-tolerant trees that grow along tropical and subtropical coastlines. They are among the most productive ecosystems on Earth. For decades they were cleared rapidly for fish farms, agriculture, and urban development — but a 2026 study has found a genuine turning point: since 2010, the world has been gaining more mangroves than it loses.

The study used Landsat satellite imagery, which is more sensitive to canopy changes than earlier methods and revealed far greater regrowth than previous surveys had captured.

## Why it matters

Mangroves punch well above their weight for climate and people. They store **up to five times more carbon per hectare than land-based forests**, making them critical carbon sinks. Their tangled root systems absorb wave energy, protecting millions of coastal people from storm surges and tsunamis. They also serve as nurseries for fish and marine life, underpinning fisheries that coastal communities depend on. Losing them makes coastlines more vulnerable to climate impacts; restoring them stores carbon and rebuilds resilience simultaneously.

## The scale of the loss — and the reversal

From the 1980s to 2010, over 12,000 sq km of mangroves were cleared — an area the size of Jamaica. Fish farming (especially shrimp aquaculture) and coastal development were the primary drivers across Asia, Africa, and the Americas.

Since 2010, the picture has changed. Total net losses since the 1980s have now been reduced to around 849 sq km. The key driver of recovery is not planting campaigns but **natural regeneration**: once humans stop clearing, mangroves expand on their own wherever conditions allow. Indonesia has seen a marked slowdown in clearing, partly because the 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami changed public attitudes — communities observed that coastlines protected by mangroves suffered far less damage. A similar shift followed Cyclone Nargis in Myanmar in 2008, reinforced by a national logging ban in 2016.

The quality of remaining forests is also improving. The proportion of **closed-canopy mangroves** — the richest, most carbon-dense type — has grown by nearly 20% since the 1980s.

## Where the problems remain

The recovery is not uniform. West and Central Africa are hotspots of ongoing destruction. The Niger Delta stands out: oil pipeline networks cut straight lines through mangrove forests, and pollution is devastating local ecosystems. Tropical cyclones also remain a serious threat, causing some of the largest single-year losses recorded.

There is also a caution about the new growth in some regions. In Brazil, new mangrove stands have expanded along rivers fed by nutrient-rich sediment — but some of that nutrient load comes from upstream deforestation and mining, meaning the gains for mangroves partly reflect damage elsewhere.

## Key facts

- Mangroves store up to **5× more carbon per hectare** than land-based forests.
- From 1980s–2010: **12,000+ sq km** cleared globally — the size of Jamaica.
- Since 2010: world now **gains more than it loses**; net historical loss reduced to ~849 sq km.
- Closed-canopy (carbon-dense) mangroves have grown by **nearly 20%** since the 1980s.
- Indonesia and Myanmar lead the recovery; **Niger Delta** and West/Central Africa remain under threat.
- Oil pipeline pollution causes particularly severe damage in the Niger Delta.
- Natural regeneration — not replanting — is the main driver of recovery. ([BBC, 2026](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cn4pk07npvvo))
