- Honeybees are like the domesticated bees, wild and solitary bees are more essential for wild pollination that support biodiversity, not just food system

# Why bees keep coming up

Bees have become a symbol of nature under pressure. Wildflower patches, pollinator plans, rooftop hives — they are everywhere now. But it is worth stepping back and asking: why do bees matter so much? And is everything done in their name actually helpful?

The short answer is that bees sit right at the heart of both our food system and the natural world. When bee populations struggle, the effects ripple outward fast.

# Where does the 35% figure come from?

You may have heard that bees are responsible for around 35% of the food we eat. That figure does not mean bees physically produce 35% of our food. It means that roughly one in three bites of food on your plate depends on a pollinator, and bees are by far the most important ones.

Wind-pollinated crops like wheat, rice, and corn give us most of our calories, and bees play no role there. **But almost everything else, fruits, vegetables, nuts, oils — needs an insect to carry pollen from one flower to another. Without that, plants do not produce food.**

**Some crops are almost entirely dependent on bees. Almonds, for example**. Around 80% of the world's almonds come from California, and every year the industry trucks in billions of honeybees from across the United States just to cover the two-week bloom period. It is one of the largest, and most controversial, agricultural operations on the planet.

Beyond food, bees also pollinate the wild plants that feed birds, mammals, and insects further up the food chain. Lose the bees, and the effects go well beyond the supermarket.

STAT: Around 1 in 3 bites of food we eat depends on pollinators — and bees are the most important of all.

# Are all bees the same?

No. And this is one of the most important things to understand. There are over 20,000 known species of bees in the world. The honeybee, the one most people picture, is just one of them.

Honeybees are social insects. They live in large managed colonies, produce surplus honey, and can be moved by beekeepers to wherever pollination is needed. That makes them very useful for agriculture. But they are, in many ways, the exception.

More than 90% of bee species are solitary. They do not live in hives, they do not make honey, and they rarely sting. Each female builds her own nest in hollow plant stems, in mud, in dead wood, or underground, lays her eggs, and that is it. No queen, no workers, no surplus.

These solitary bees are often far more efficient pollinators than honeybees. A single mason bee can pollinate as many apple blossoms as 120 honeybees. Bumblebees use a technique called buzz pollination — vibrating at a specific frequency to shake loose pollen — that is essential for tomatoes, blueberries, and aubergines. No honeybee can do that.

When people talk about bees being in decline, it is mostly these wild, solitary species they are worried about. They have no beekeeper looking after them. They depend entirely on the local habitats around them.

# Are bees the only pollinators?

Bees do the heaviest lifting, but they are not alone.

**Flies** are the second most important group of pollinators. Hoverflies are especially useful in orchards. And if you enjoy chocolate, you have a tiny gnat-like fly called the biting midge to thank. It is the only insect that can pollinate the cacao flower, because bees are too big to get inside it.

**Bats** handle the night shift in tropical and desert regions, pollinating mangoes, bananas, and agave plants. No bats, no tequila.

**Wasps** have one very specific job: figs. A tiny fig wasp climbs inside the developing fruit to lay its eggs and pollinate it at the same time. It has been working like this for millions of years.

And **beetles**, one of the oldest pollinators on Earth, remain essential for some spice crops, magnolias, and macadamia nuts.

Bees are the main character. But the food system depends on a much wider cast.

# What about the honey?

Bees do not make honey for us. They make it to survive the winter.

A healthy colony produces far more than it needs, often two to three times its requirements. That surplus is what beekeepers harvest. Done properly, it does not harm the hive.

The process is more complex than most people realise. Forager bees collect nectar from flowers and store it in a dedicated honey stomach. Back at the hive, it is passed between bees, mixed with enzymes, deposited into wax cells, and then fanned constantly until most of the water evaporates. When the water content drops to around 18%, the bees seal it with a wax cap. That is honey. Properly sealed, it does not spoil.

It is a remarkable piece of natural engineering, and a byproduct of a system built entirely around survival, not production.

# What about rooftop beehives? Is that what we need?

Corporate rooftop beehives have become a popular sustainability gesture. A few hives on the roof, some local honey for staff, a line in the annual report. The intention is good. But the impact is often the opposite of what companies intend.

Here is the problem. A single honeybee hive contains up to 60,000 bees. Drop two hives on a city rooftop and you have unleashed 120,000 highly efficient foragers into a neighbourhood that already has limited flowers. Local wild bees, the solitary species that are actually under pressure, cannot compete. They get outcompeted for food and, in some cases, exposed to disease passed on by poorly managed hives.

Saying that keeping honeybees helps save the bees is a bit like saying keeping chickens helps protect wild bird species. Honeybees are managed livestock. They are not at risk.

**What cities and companies actually need to do is focus on habitat. Rooftop wildflower patches planted with native species are genuinely useful.** They act as stepping stones for insects crossing urban areas, and they provide the right kind of pollen for local solitary bees. Solitary bee hotels (small wooden blocks with hollow tubes for nesting) do more good than a managed hive.

The goal should be: more flowers, more nesting space, less pesticide. The honey is a bonus, not the point.

QUOTE: "Focus on the habitat, not the honey." — Conservationists studying urban pollinator health

# So what actually helps?

The core issue for wild bees is habitat loss. Wildflower meadows replaced by lawns, hedgerows cleared, roadsides sprayed. To a solitary bee, a perfectly mown grass verge offers nothing.

The good news is that small actions add up. A patch of native wildflowers in a garden, a few hollow stems left in a hedge, a strip of unmown grass at the edge of a field — all of these provide food and nesting space for the species that need it most.

At a policy level, the EU is taking this seriously. The EU Pollinators Initiative and the Nature Restoration Regulation set legally binding targets for reversing the decline of wild bees and butterflies by 2030. That pressure is pushing changes in how public land is managed and how farms are designed.

Bees matter because the food system and the natural world both depend on them. But protecting them is less about hives and honey jars, and more about giving wild species back the space they need to survive.

# What is Ireland Pollinator plan? 

It's a plan that brings together different groups across the whole island of Ireland to create a landscape where pollinators — bees, butterflies, hoverflies — can survive and thrive. It's coordinated by the National Biodiversity Data Centre.

**Why does it exist?**
Pollinators are in decline, mainly because of the loss of natural habitats. This is serious — it affects food production, the agricultural economy, and the health of the environment. 

> In Ireland, it is estimated that 1/3 of wild bees species are at risk of extinction due to loss of food and nesting sites

**How did it start?**
Ireland was one of the first countries in Europe to tackle pollinator decline. The first plan ran from 2015 to 2020, and all 81 actions were completed — with input from beekeepers, gardeners, students, farmers, businesses, and many other groups. 

**The current plan (2021–2025)**
The second plan is more ambitious — 186 actions across six objectives, developed by a 16-member steering group. It's voluntary, and the organisations involved agree to fund their own actions themselves. 

The six objectives cover: farmland, public land (parks, roadsides, etc.), private land (gardens, businesses), education, research, and policy.

**What's happening now?**
Pollinating insects are still in decline across Ireland overall, but local populations are increasing where actions have been taken. This shows the approach is working. 

A new All-Ireland Pollinator Plan for 2026–2030 is expected to be launched in spring 2026. 

**SPECIFIC ON POLLINATORS PLAN**
Here is some more detailed information regarding both the national **All-Ireland Pollinator Plan (AIPP)** and how **Dún Laoghaire-Rathdown (DLR)** is specifically implementing it.

### **1. The National Context: All-Ireland Pollinator Plan (AIPP)**

The AIPP is a framework coordinated by the National Biodiversity Data Centre to address a critical crisis: **one-third of Ireland's wild bee species are threatened with extinction**. This decline is primarily due to the reduction of food (flowers) and safe nesting sites.

The plan emphasizes specific "best practices" that contradict some common assumptions:

- **"Don't Mow, Let it Grow":** The plan discourages planting generic wildflower seed mixes, noting they can sometimes do more harm than good. Instead, the strategy is to allow common wildflowers to grow naturally by reducing mowing frequency.
- **Cross-Sector Action:** It organizes actions across farms, councils, businesses, and local communities to create a landscape where pollinators can survive.
    

### **2. DLR’s Specific Implementation**

Dún Laoghaire-Rathdown County Council has integrated the AIPP directly into its _Biodiversity Action Plan 2021–2025_ (specifically **Action 3.9**), committing to completing actions outlined in the national plan.

Here are the specific measures DLR is taking:

#### **A. The "Slow to Mow" Campaign**

DLR has moved away from "manicured" landscapes toward nature-friendly management.

- **Utilizing Soil Seed Banks:** Rather than buying commercial seeds, DLR aims to encourage seeds already lying dormant in the soil to germinate. This has resulted in native orchids (like Pyramidal and Bee Orchids) reappearing on roadside verges where they had not been seen for years.
- **Managed Meadows:** The council has designated areas in parks and roadside verges as "Slow to Mow" zones to allow dandelions, clover, and other native plants to flower, providing essential food for insects.

#### **B. Native Seed Harvesting (Fernhill Park)**

DLR is actively reducing reliance on commercial seeds to ensure genetic diversity remains local.
- **Fernhill Park & Gardens:** This park is managed as "chemical-free" and grazed by rare-breed Droimeann cattle during winter. In summer, staff harvest native seeds (after removing ragwort) to dry, store, and plant in other appropriate locations across the county.

#### **C. "Gardening for Biodiversity" (Community Action)**

DLR recognizes that private gardens, balconies, and patios are vital for connecting wildlife corridors.

- **Volunteer Project:** In 2020, DLR partnered with the Volunteer Centre to launch "Gardening for Biodiversity." This initiative encourages residents to take small steps, such as creating log piles, drilling holes in walls for hedgehogs, or leaving grass uncut.
- **Success Stories:** The plan highlights local success stories, such as a resident named Mark who created a pond and log pile, noting that "piles of earth... are being used by the birds as a dust bath" and potential nesting sites for solitary bees.

#### **D. Infrastructure and Planning**

- **Pollinator Areas:** DLR is establishing pollinator zones not just in parks, but in urban environments like shopping centers and on "brown roofs" (biodiversity roofs) on new developments.
