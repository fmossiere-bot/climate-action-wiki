Wetlands are land areas that are covered by water, or where the soil is soaked with water, for all or part of the year. The water can be fresh, salty, or a mix of both. This wet ground supports plants and animals that do not live anywhere else, and it does a lot of useful work for water, climate, and flooding.

Wetlands are found all over the world, from the cold tundra of the Arctic to the tropics. How many wetlands there are, and where they sit, changes from one region to another. This depends on the climate, the shape of the land, and what people have done to the land over time.

## Different types of wetlands

Wetlands are usually grouped into a few main types:

- **Mangroves**: coastal wetlands found in tropical and subtropical regions.
- **Peatlands**: this includes bogs and fens, found in both the northern and southern parts of the world.
- **Marshes**: both freshwater and saltwater marshes, found in many climates.
- **Swamps**: wetlands with woody plants and trees, found in many parts of the world.
- **Floodplains**: flat land beside rivers and streams that floods at times, found worldwide.

## Why wetlands matter

Wetlands give a wide range of benefits. They filter water, hold back floods, and provide a home for many plants and animals. They also take carbon dioxide out of the air and store it, which helps to slow down climate change and keeps the air and water cleaner. These areas hold a large variety of plant and animal life.

### Carbon storage

Wetlands store carbon in their plants and in the wet soil below them. Salt marsh is one example. The plants grow quickly and pull carbon out of the air. The soils sit under water and have little oxygen, so they break down the trapped carbon very slowly, over hundreds or even thousands of years.

A long running project at the Steart Marshes in Somerset, England, has measured this:

- The site has reached as much as **19 tonnes of carbon per hectare, per year**. The project team described this as the equivalent of charging 15 trillion phones each year, or heating 33,000 homes.
- Looking at all of the natural marshes in Britain together, they capture in the region of **46,500 tonnes of carbon a year**.

Carbon capture from wetlands is not huge compared to other climate actions, and it should not be treated as the main reason to protect them. But it is not small either.

### Flood protection

A healthy marsh acts as a natural barrier against flooding. It soaks up and slows the tides before they can reach further inland. At Steart, even during the wettest winter that local people could remember, the nearby village did not flood and the paths through the marsh stayed open.

This natural protection has been lost in many places. Over the centuries, marshland was often seen as useless land. Thousands of acres were drained and turned into farmland or built on. Since 1860, **Britain has lost 85% of its salt marshes**, according to the UK Centre for Ecology and Hydrology.

In England, a 10 year experiment at Steart is starting to show how marshland can be brought back to give natural flood protection, while also helping biodiversity and carbon capture. Britain has long used sea walls, sandbanks, and tidal barriers to hold back the sea, but many experts agree this may not be enough in future. The Steart project bought back land from farmers and let a natural habitat return. It was controversial at first, but it has gained wide support, including from farmers, because the marsh gives their cattle a place to feed on rich ground and produces traceable, free range meat.

### Wildlife and biodiversity

Wetlands are home to a large number of plant and animal species. They are some of the most diverse ecosystems on the planet, which is why they sit at the centre of both climate and nature plans.

### A note on methane

Wetlands also release methane, which is a strong greenhouse gas. This makes the full picture more complicated.

- About **40% of methane emissions come from natural sources** such as wetlands. The other **60% come from human sources**, such as cattle farming, fossil fuel extraction, and landfill sites.
- Rising temperatures can lead to _more_ methane, for example by speeding up the activity of the microbes in wetlands that produce it. At the same time, methane _removal_ is slowed down by the growing number of wildfires.


## Wetlands in Europe's climate and biodiversity plans

Wetlands, and peatlands in particular, are now a clear part of European policy on both climate and nature. They store large amounts of carbon, and restoring them is seen as one of the cheaper ways to cut emissions and bring back biodiversity at the same time.

The main tool is the **EU Nature Restoration Law** (Regulation (EU) 2024/1991). It was formally adopted on 17 June 2024 and has been in force since August 2024. It is the first law of its kind in the EU, and it forms part of the EU Biodiversity Strategy for 2030 and the wider European Green Deal. The background to all of this is that over 80% of European habitats are in poor condition.

Key targets in the law:

- Restoration measures must cover **at least 20% of the EU's land and sea by 2030**, and all ecosystems that need it by 2050.
- For habitats in poor condition, Member States must restore **30% by 2030, 60% by 2040, and 90% by 2050**.
- For drained peatlands under farming use, the targets are **30% restored by 2030, 40% by 2040, and 50% by 2050**, with part of this to be rewetted. Some flexibility is given to farmers, and rewetting on farmland is voluntary.
- Each Member State must prepare a **national restoration plan by 2026** that sets out how it will meet these targets.

Restoring a peatland mostly means rewetting it. When a peatland is drained, the dry peat reacts with oxygen in the air and releases its stored carbon as carbon dioxide. Keeping the ground wet stops this and lets the peatland store and capture carbon again.

## The role of Ireland

Ireland has many types of wetland, including bogs (both raised and blanket bogs), fens, marshes, and swamps. Peatlands are by far the most important.

- Peatlands cover around **1.46 million hectares, about 21% of the country** (roughly a fifth of all the land).
- They are mostly blanket bogs (around 66%) and raised bogs (around 33%), with fens making up less than 1%.
- Irish peat stores an estimated **1.6 billion tonnes of carbon**.
- Up to **85% of Irish peatlands have been degraded** by changes in land use, such as turf cutting, drainage, farming, and forestry. The Irish Peatland Conservation Council estimates that only about 25% are healthy enough to still filter water and store carbon well.

The largest restoration programme in Ireland is run by Bord na Móna, which has stopped all commercial peat harvesting:

- The aim is to rehabilitate about **33,000 hectares** of former industrial peatland across 82 bogs in the midlands.
- By the end of 2024, work was completed on **18,861 hectares**, which is 57% of the target.
- The scheme is funded by **€108 million** from the EU Recovery and Resilience Facility, through Ireland's National Recovery and Resilience Plan.
- It is expected to cut emissions by a net **3.3 million tonnes of CO2 equivalent**.

Ireland was one of the countries that helped get the Nature Restoration Law passed, and like all Member States it must publish its own national restoration plan by 2026.


### Restoring peatlands: three main aims
> 
> 1. Manage the plants and vegetation so that sites return to good condition.
> 2. Where possible, restart the natural process that forms peat.
> 3. Manage the water, mainly how wet the peat is, but also water quality and run-off.
> 
> Rewetting is the key step. In the UK, restoration has worked by blocking thousands of kilometres of drainage ditches, lowering grazing pressure, and stopping burning. Removing trees from drained sites has also worked where the natural water flow could be brought back.

## Solar farms on rewetted peatland

A 2026 study from the University of Greifswald found that placing solar panels on **rewetted** peatland can deliver a triple benefit: clean electricity income for landowners, carbon sequestration from the restored peat, and better biodiversity than the drained farmland it replaces. A 30-hectare pilot site in northern Germany run by Wattmanufactur attracted both wetland and woodland bird species absent from nearby hay fields, including the threatened meadow pipit.

The key word is rewetted. Solar farms on peat that is still drained can release more greenhouse gases from microbial decomposition than their electricity displaces. Of 165 solar farms on degraded German peatlands, only five are on genuinely rewetted peat. Germany has barred degraded-peat solar from minimum guaranteed electricity prices since 2023, but disclosure is not yet mandatory.
