The ocean covers most of our planet and does a huge amount of quiet work to keep the climate stable. It takes in heat and carbon, feeds billions of people, and holds a large share of life on Earth. It is one of our strongest allies against climate change, which is why protecting it has moved up the global agenda.

## Key facts

- The total surface area of the Earth is about **510 million square kilometres**, and more than **70%** of it (around 71%) is covered by water.
- The ocean absorbs about **90% of the extra heat** caused by human-driven global warming.
- It also takes in roughly **a quarter of our CO2 emissions** (around 25% to 30%).
- This makes the ocean one of the best natural allies we have in slowing climate change.

## How the ocean stores carbon

The ocean captures carbon through two processes, one physical and one biological.

1. **The physical process** works because CO2 dissolves in water. CO2 from the air dissolves naturally into the sea, and this happens more easily in cold water. Cold water is denser, so it sinks, carrying the dissolved carbon down with it.
2. **The biological process** is driven by photosynthesis in phytoplankton. These microscopic algae use sunlight to take in CO2 and turn it into organic matter and oxygen. When they die, some of them sink to the bottom of the ocean, taking their carbon with them.

We often hear that forests are the main store of carbon, but the ocean plays an equally large part through its "sea forest" of phytoplankton. As Cécile Guieu, a researcher at the Villefranche Oceanography Laboratory (LOV), points out, a key difference is speed: forests take a very long time to grow back, while algae can rebuild their biomass in a matter of days.

## Why protecting the ocean matters

Marine protected areas (MPAs) help the ocean keep absorbing carbon and give marine life space to recover. Limiting destructive and unsustainable fishing is part of the same effort.

- In December 2022, 196 countries agreed the Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework, which set the target of protecting **30% of land and sea by 2030** (often called 30x30).
- That target is still a long way off. Only about **8% of the ocean** is currently designated as a marine protected area, and just **2.8%** is considered effectively protected.
- The EU has set its own goal of protecting **30% of its sea area by 2030**.
- **France has the second largest maritime zone in the world**, largely thanks to its overseas islands, which gives it a big role and responsibility.

> A new era for the high seas The High Seas Treaty, formally the Agreement on Biodiversity Beyond National Jurisdiction (BBNJ), is the first legal framework able to create protected areas in international waters, which cover roughly two-thirds of the ocean. It was adopted in 2023, reached the 60 ratifications needed in September 2025, and entered into force on 17 January 2026.

The **UN Ocean Conference (UNOC)** is the main meeting where countries push for better protection of international waters by 2030. The third conference (UNOC3) was held in Nice, France, in June 2025. It produced the Nice Ocean Action Plan and more than 800 voluntary commitments, and helped build momentum for the High Seas Treaty and the 30x30 target

## Fishing pressure

### Progress on sustainable fishing

Fishing more sustainably means leaving enough fish in the sea for populations to renew themselves. France is a useful example, as one of the largest fishing nations in Europe.

- According to the research institute Ifremer, **51% of the 327,000 tonnes** of fish landed in mainland France in 2021 came from sustainably fished stocks, up from 48% the year before.
- This is a large improvement on the year 2000, when only around 15% of the catch came from sustainable stocks.
- It is still well short of the EU target of having all stocks fished at sustainable levels.

### Illegal fishing

Illegal, unreported and unregulated fishing (often shortened to IUU) is a major threat. It is hard to measure exactly, but estimates suggest it makes up roughly a fifth of the global catch, with some estimates around 15%.

- **China has the largest fishing fleet in the world.** Its distant-water fleet accounted for about **44% of the world's visible fishing activity** between 2022 and 2024, and it is widely ranked as the top offender for illegal fishing.
- Monitoring is improving. Norway has developed satellite tracking for illegal fishing that it can share to help patrol vulnerable areas.
- For more on how large fleets fish unseen in so-called "dark zones", see [this YouTube video](https://youtu.be/2tuS1LLOcsI?si=5r_3nSgdV7KTMNHI).

> A notable mangrove restoration effort in Costa Rica has focused on the Térraba-Sierpe National Wetland, which lost about 40% of its mangroves after the 1980s banana boom collapse. An invasive fern species took over the degraded areas, preventing natural regrowth. The project involved clearing the fern, planting mangrove saplings, and ongoing maintenance.

Mangrove forests are remarkable coastal ecosystems that thrive in environments where the land meets the sea.1 They possess a host of unique adaptations that allow them to survive in conditions that would be lethal to most other plants.2 Here are some interesting facts about mangroves:

### Incredible Adaptations

- **Saltwater Specialists:** Mangroves are able to live in salty or brackish water.3 Some species have specialized root systems that filter out as much as 90% of the salt from the seawater, while others excrete excess salt through glands on their leaves, which can sometimes be seen as tiny salt crystals.4
- **"Breathing" Roots:** The soil where mangroves grow is often low in oxygen.5 To overcome this, many species have developed unique aerial roots.6 These roots, which include stilt roots and pneumatophores (roots that stick up out of the mud like snorkels), allow the trees to take in oxygen directly from the air.7
- **Sturdy Support Systems:** The dense, tangled root systems of many mangroves anchor the trees in soft, muddy soil, allowing them to withstand the daily ebb and flow of tides and the powerful forces of waves and storms.8 This gives some species the appearance of standing on stilts.9
- **Unique Reproduction:** Mangroves have a special way of reproducing called vivipary. Their seeds germinate and begin to grow while still attached to the parent tree.10 These seedlings, known as propagules, can float in the water for long distances and for up to a year before finding a suitable spot to take root and grow into a new tree.11

### Vital Ecosystem Services

- **Coastal Protection:** Mangrove forests act as natural barriers against storms, tsunamis, and coastal erosion.12 Their dense root systems absorb the energy from waves, reducing their height and power.13 It's estimated that a wide stretch of mangrove trees can reduce storm wave heights by as much as 80%.14
- **Biodiversity Hotspots:** Mangrove forests are incredibly diverse ecosystems.15 The intricate root systems provide crucial nursery habitats for a wide variety of marine life, including commercially important fish, crabs, and shrimp.16 The canopies and surrounding areas also provide homes and feeding grounds for many birds, reptiles, and mammals, including some endangered species.17
- **Natural Water Filters:** The roots of mangroves trap sediments and pollutants from land runoff, helping to maintain and improve water quality.18 This filtering process is beneficial for adjacent ecosystems, such as coral reefs and seagrass beds, which can be damaged by excess sediment.19
- **Carbon Sinks:** Mangroves are exceptionally good at storing carbon, often referred to as "blue carbon."20 They can absorb and store up to 10 times more carbon per hectare than terrestrial forests, making them a powerful tool in the fight against climate change.21 The carbon is stored in their leaves, roots, and the soil beneath them.22


> Where can Mangroves be found? 
> - Mangrove forests can be found on tropical and subtropical coastlines in over 100 countries
> - Indonesia has the largest amount of mangrove coverage in the world, with almost a fifth of the global total.
> - Globally, as much as 80% of the world's fishing catch may depend directly or indirectly on mangrove habitats.
> - The loss of mangrove forests is a significant concern, with much of the loss being due to human development, aquaculture (like shrimp farming), and agriculture.

> Did you Know? The inspiration of Paphanaumokuakea
> Papahānaumokuākea Marine National Monument, located in the Northwestern Hawaiian Islands, is one of the world's largest marine protected areas and a UNESCO World Heritage site.1 It's a place of profound significance, valued for its unique natural ecosystems and its deep cultural importance to Native Hawaiians.2

> **Importance for Biodiversity** : Papahānaumokuākea is a global hotspot for biodiversity, largely due to its isolation and relatively pristine condition.3

- **High Endemism:** The monument is home to over 7,000 marine species, with about 25% of them **endemic**, meaning they are found nowhere else on Earth.4
- **Apex Predator Dominance:** Its coral reefs are among the last in the world to be dominated by **apex predators** like sharks and jacks, which indicates a very healthy, intact ecosystem.5
- **Critical Habitat:** The area provides essential breeding and nesting grounds for numerous endangered species. This includes over 90% of the world's Hawaiian monk seals, nearly all of the critically endangered Laysan ducks and Nihoa finches, and a vast number of seabirds.
- **Coral Reefs:** The monument contains the deepest and northernmost coral reefs in the world and covers about 70% of the coral reef area under U.S. jurisdiction.7

>**How it is Protected** Papahānaumokuākea is managed through a multi-layered system of protection.

- **Marine National Monument:** Established in 2006, it was the first marine protected area of its kind. Its boundaries were significantly expanded in 2016 to create one of the largest conservation areas globally.
- **Co-Management:** It's co-managed by four "co-trustees": the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service, the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA), the State of Hawaiʻi, and the Office of Hawaiian Affairs.
- **No-Take Policy:** The core of the monument has a strict **no-take policy**, which prohibits fishing and the extraction of any resources. Access is highly restricted and requires a permit.
- **National Marine Sanctuary:** The marine portion of the monument was also recently designated as a National Marine Sanctuary, which adds another layer of legal protection and promotes a long-term conservation framework.

> Why it Matters: Papahānaumokuākea is significant for several reasons that go beyond its impressive size and biodiversity.

- **Cultural Heritage:** To Native Hawaiians, the islands are a sacred place of great cosmological and traditional importance. They are believed to be the origin of life and the place where ancestral spirits return after death.
- **Scientific Reference Site:** Its near-pristine condition makes it an invaluable reference site for scientists. It allows them to study healthy marine ecosystems and better understand the effects of climate change and other human impacts on less-protected areas.
- **Global Conservation Model:** The monument's successful co-management and strong protections serve as a model for large-scale marine conservation around the world, inspiring the creation of other similar protected areas. It demonstrates that it is possible to effectively safeguard immense stretches of ocean.


#### Seamounts are underwater mountains that rise from the seafloor but do not reach the surface. While they are often volcanic in origin and conical in shape, the term can also refer to any isolated rise of 1,000 meters or more.

**How many seamounts are there?**

> The number of seamounts is not precisely known, but estimates suggest there are **over 100,000** of them worldwide. However, only a tiny fraction, less than 1%, have been explored in detail by scientists. Most are found in the Pacific Ocean, with some of the largest and most significant examples being the Emperor Seamount chain, an extension of the Hawaiian Islands.

**Why are they important?**: Seamounts are critical for ocean life and are considered biodiversity hotspots for several key reasons:6

- **Habitat for Deep-Sea Life:** They provide a rare, hard surface in the deep ocean for marine organisms to attach to, such as corals, sponges, and other invertebrates. These organisms form dense, complex communities that provide shelter and food for countless other species.
- **Nutrient Upwelling:** Seamounts can alter ocean currents. As water flows over and around them, it is forced upward in a process called upwelling. This brings nutrient-rich water from the deep sea to the surface, fueling plankton growth and creating a food source that attracts a wide variety of marine life, from small fish to large predators like sharks and whales.
- **"Oases in the Desert":** Because of the upwelling and varied habitat, seamounts are often "oases of life" in an otherwise sparsely populated deep-sea environment. They serve as feeding grounds, spawning sites, and migratory stepping stones for numerous species.
- **High Endemism:** The isolation of many seamounts has led to the evolution of a high number of endemic species—plants and animals found nowhere else on Earth.

**Can they be protected?**: Yes, seamounts can and are being protected, but it's a significant challenge.

- **Marine Protected Areas (MPAs):** Some seamounts fall within national marine sanctuaries and MPAs, such as those in the Papahānaumokuākea Marine National Monument. These areas often have strict regulations against fishing, mining, and other extractive activities.
- **International Efforts:** Many seamounts are located in the high seas, outside of any single country's jurisdiction. This makes protection difficult. However, international agreements and organizations are increasingly recognizing seamounts as "Vulnerable Marine Ecosystems" (VMEs). This designation can lead to restrictions on damaging activities like deep-sea bottom trawling, which can be highly destructive to fragile seamount ecosystems.
- **New Treaties:** The newly established UN High Seas Treaty aims to create a framework for establishing MPAs in international waters. This could be a game-changer for seamount conservation, allowing for the protection of these critical habitats on a much larger scale.

## 2026 Update: UN World Ocean Assessment III

The UN's third World Ocean Assessment, published on World Oceans Day 2026 and drawing on nearly 600 scientists from 86 countries, found the oceans under "severe and accelerating" pressure. The findings are stark:

- **Sea level rise has doubled in rate** — from 2mm per year before 2015 to 4.3mm per year by 2023.
- **16% of all ocean heat absorbed since 1955 occurred after 2018 alone** — the pace of warming is accelerating sharply.
- The ocean has now absorbed 90% of the excess heat and 30% of the CO₂ released by human activity since industrialisation.
- 52.1 million tonnes of plastic enter the ocean every year, contributing to 24.4 trillion microplastic particles that affect more than 4,000 marine species.
- Ocean currents, including the AMOC (Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation, which includes the Gulf Stream), are changing — their future impacts are described as "poorly understood" but potentially severe. See [AMOC Collapse and Carbon Release](../climate-science/amoc-collapse-carbon.md).
- Only 27% of the ocean floor has been mapped as of 2025. Deep-sea ecosystems remain largely unknown.
- The high seas treaty came into force in 2026, giving legal protection to the two-thirds of ocean outside any country's jurisdiction.
- Ocean governance remains "fragmented" across sectors and regions; strengthening coordination is described as critical.

More than a third of the world's population lives within 100km of coastlines. Eleven per cent live on land less than 10 metres above sea level — directly exposed to rising seas.
