We have known for a long time that freshwater is a scarce and limited resource – only 3% of the world’s water is fresh and drinkable, and 2.5% of that is trapped in ice, soil, and the atmosphere. We generally have water-treatment systems for well-connected cities and suburban areas, but many underdeveloped countries still do not. Not only is our own society heavily reliant on the resource of fresh water, but so are millions of species of wildlife.

## Freshwater Boundary

> [!Freshwater Use] What is the freshwater (or “freshwater change”) boundary?
> 
> Originally (in 2009/2015) the freshwater boundary was formulated as a global “blue water” (i.e., surface + groundwater withdrawal) limit. The idea: there is a volume of freshwater humans can withdraw globally while still maintaining river flows, groundwater recharge, aquatic ecosystem health, etc., such that the Earth-system remains in a Holocene-type stable state. 
> 
> A commonly-cited number from earlier work: ~4,000 km³/year of global consumptive freshwater use (i.e., usage that removes water from the system or significantly alters flows) was proposed.  The thinking was that if humans withdrew more than that, you risk destabilising global hydrological cycles.
> 
> However, that simple “4,000 km³/year” has always been subject to caveats: spatial heterogeneity, temporal seasonality, and the fact that water systems are very locally/regional and the global aggregation hides many risks.



![[Screenshot 2025-10-07 at 15.13.35.png]]


Some countries have already surpassed their freshwater use, taking out more than can be naturally regenerated. 
#### **What’s new: “Green water” + a shift from use to change in the freshwater cycle**  

> [!NOTE] Most Recent Reassessment consider both BLUE and GREEN water "disruption"
> In the most recent reassessments, scientists have moved from simply “how much water do we extract” to a broader lens of “how much are we perturbing the freshwater cycle” — including both blue water **and** “green water”.
> 
> - **Blue water**: surface water + groundwater that is extracted (rivers, lakes, aquifers).
> - **Green water**: the portion of rainfall that infiltrates soils and is used by plants (soil moisture, evapotranspiration). 


> The 2022/2023 studies argue that human activities — land use change (deforestation, irrigation), climate change (altering precipitation, evapotranspiration), infrastructure (dams) — are already substantially altering the green-water component and thus the global freshwater cycle. 

###### Hence the term “freshwater change” rather than “freshwater use” is now being used: it’s not just how much we withdraw, but how much we are altering flows, soil moisture, seasonality, distribution, and regenerative capacity.

One summary from the Stockholm Resilience Centre states: “In April 2022, a reassessment of the planetary boundary for freshwater indicated that it has now been transgressed. This conclusion is due to the inclusion of “green water” – the water available to plants – into the boundary assessment for the first time.” 

Another key article: “Human Disruption to Earth’s Freshwater Cycle Has Exceeded Safe …” indicates green water changes are now causing Earth-system-level risks. 

#### **Has the boundary been exceeded? What the latest science says**

Yes — the consensus from recent work is **yes**, the freshwater (or “freshwater change”) boundary has been transgressed. Key points:

- A news release: “Freshwater planetary boundary has been crossed since the mid-twentieth century.” 
- The 2022/2023 update: “A reassessment … indicates that it has now been transgressed … due to inclusion of green water.” 
- The study in _Nature_ (2023) on “Safe and just Earth system boundaries” sets two spatially defined safe Earth-system boundaries for freshwater and says they have been passed. 
- In the 2023 mapping of all nine boundaries, the framework authors conclude that six of nine planetary boundaries are transgressed, including freshwater. 


Hence, your understanding is correct: although earlier versions suggested we might still be “within” the freshwater boundary (or at least close) under the old metric, the new, more comprehensive framing says we are **already outside** the safe operating space for the freshwater cycle.

#### **Why it matters:**

- The shift to include green water acknowledges that beyond direct extraction, land-use change and climate change affect soil moisture, evapotranspiration, vegetation health, even rainfall recycling. For example, the stability of the Amazon Rainforest depends heavily on soil moisture and vegetation-hydrology coupling; changes there can push toward tipping points. 
- From an ESG/investor perspective, this means water-related risk is not only about “how much a region withdraws” but also about the broader hydrological resilience and regime stability.
- Crossing the boundary means we are in a zone of increased Earth-system risk (higher probability of non-linear changes, tipping points, regime shifts). These globally aggregate risks filter down to regional/asset risks: water supply constraints, ecosystem collapse, altered rainfall patterns, agricultural yield volatility.


#### **Caveats and things to keep in mind:**

- Although the boundary is said to be “transgressed”, that does not mean _everywhere_ is failing or that water crises are uniform globally. Much depends on geography, watershed management, climate, land use. Some basins remain within relatively safe limits; others are well outside. 
- The boundary definition and quantification come with substantial uncertainty. Previous critiques note that setting a global number for water is problematic because hydrological systems are spatially heterogeneous and locally managed. 
- The fact that water issues are often local/regional means the global boundary is a _signal_ rather than a rule for local investment decisions. For ESG screening, you’ll still need basin-level/regional data.
- The boundary is framed in terms of Earth-system stability (Holocene-type conditions) not simply “no regional water stress” — meaning the objective is more ambitious and systemic.

## Access to Water is more and more Inequitable
Source: https://www.wri.org/insights/inequity-water-scarcity-causes?apcid=0065aea19f4a6d4198ca0d00&utm_campaign=wridigest&utm_medium=email&utm_source=wridigest-2024-03-21 

> While the Scarcity is more felt Locally, the cause are more and more Global

Here is the example of Ethiopia, an area called the Blue Nile, alwasy known for its abundance of Water, the water scarcity is starting to be felt. Here the water cycle is out of sync. 
Here women and children walk miles and wait hours to collect water

> As is the case elsewhere, the causes of water insecurity in the Tana watershed are complex, driven in part by **local conditions like a growing population, an expanding economy, inadequate water governance and insufficient investment in infrastructure like wells and sewage systems**. But there are also bigger sources far outside the control of Tana’s farming communities.

While water has always been considered a LOCAL good, one that shoiuld be managed locally. it is more and more referred to as a "global common good"

> At the UN 2023 Water Conference — the first-such international conference in nearly 50 years — UN General Assembly President Csaba Kőrösi described the water cycle as a [global common good](https://enb.iisd.org/un-2023-water-conference-summary) that transcends culture and borders. The idea is still far from consensus — as anything deemed part of the global commons might elicit questions about sovereignty and accountability — but it still emerged from the proceedings as a central theme."

###### Global Forces Are Increasingly Driving Local Water Problems
- That is climate change, increasing water vapour in the air, increasing density of strom, drought, and those impacting more those that less contributed. Greenhouse gas emissions, polluting and warming our shared atmosphere are [changing the water cycle itself](https://www.weforum.org/agenda/2022/05/climate-crisis-is-speeding-the-water-cycle-satellite-data-reveals/).
- And then there’s another dimension of water in the global commons: the water used to produce foods and goods (also known as “virtual water”) that moves around the world via international trade. In fact, [50%](https://fairwaterfootprints.org/2023/03/22/shocking-new-data-shows-50-of-water-use-to-supply-high-income-economies-is-unsustainable/) of the virtual water imported by high-income countries comes from places of high water scarcity. Trade of virtual water is projected to [triple](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-020-17400-4) by the end of the century.
