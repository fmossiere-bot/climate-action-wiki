Reaching net zero by 2050 will require more than switching to renewable energy. We also need to stop adding carbon to the atmosphere faster than natural systems can absorb it. Forests and land play a central role in this, and yet they are under increasing pressure.

---

### Why Forests Matter for the Climate

Forests are not just important for biodiversity. They are one of the planet's main mechanisms for absorbing CO2. In a typical year, forests and other vegetation absorb roughly 30% of the carbon humans emit from burning fossil fuels. That is a significant buffer, and one we tend to take for granted.

How much carbon a tree stores depends on several factors:

- **Age** - the longer a tree lives, the more carbon it holds
- **Growth stage** - younger trees absorb carbon faster; older trees slow down but hold more
- **Species** - different trees have different wood density and growth rates
- **Size** - larger trees generally store more carbon
- **Soil type** - affects growth rate and long-term health

What happens when a tree dies also matters. Carbon is gradually released back into the soil or atmosphere. Trees cut for timber continue to lock up carbon until the wood begins to break down. Trees burned for fuel release their carbon immediately. This is why how we manage forests, not just whether we plant them, makes a real difference.

---

### Diversity Makes Forests More Effective

A large international study, covering 273 forests, 243 species and 255 plantations worldwide, confirmed that mixed forests significantly outperform monocultures. Trees planted alongside at least one other species grow taller by around 5%, wider by nearly 7%, and capture up to 25% more carbon than single-species forests. They are also healthier overall and produce more biomass. This is relevant not just for conservation, but for any reforestation or afforestation programme aiming to maximise carbon sequestration.

---

### The Situation is Getting Worse

Despite the importance of forests, their role as a carbon sink is weakening. In 2023 and 2024, extreme fires caused forests to absorb only around a quarter of the carbon they do in an average year. That is a dramatic drop, and it is not just a temporary spike. It is part of a longer-term trend where the gap between forest carbon absorption and forest carbon emissions is narrowing.

The 2024 figures are particularly alarming:

- 6.7 million hectares of primary rainforest were lost, nearly double the 2023 figure
- This is the equivalent of 18 football pitches every minute
- Forest loss in 2024 alone caused 3.1 gigatonnes of greenhouse gas emissions, roughly equivalent to India's annual fossil fuel emissions

Brazil accounted for 42% of all primary rainforest loss in the tropics. Bolivia ranked second for the first time, with primary forest loss nearly five times higher than in 2020. In the Democratic Republic of Congo and Congo-Brazzaville, loss of pristine rainforest reached record levels.

At COP26 in Glasgow, more than 140 world leaders pledged to halt deforestation by 2030. Based on current trends, that target requires forest loss to fall by 20% per year from 2024 levels. Countries are not on track.

---

### From Carbon Sink to Carbon Source

The risk is no longer theoretical. Some forests are already becoming net emitters of carbon rather than absorbers. If this trend continues at scale, one of the planet's most important natural climate buffers will begin working against us.

The clearest priority is to protect what already exists. Old-growth and primary forests store far more carbon than replanted or managed forests, and they cannot be replaced on any meaningful timescale. Reforestation and afforestation are valuable, but they are not a substitute for preservation.

---

### A Note on Timber and Construction

Forests also have a role to play in reducing emissions from the construction sector. Timber, used in place of concrete and steel, locks up carbon for the lifetime of a building. This connection between land management and the built environment is worth keeping in mind when looking at the full picture of what forests contribute.

---

### Forest Cover Across Europe

While the global picture is one of loss, Europe is an exception. According to the State of Europe's Forests 2025 report and World Bank data, European forest area has generally expanded over the last three decades, driven by sustainable forestry policies, reforestation programmes, and natural regrowth on abandoned agricultural land.

Finland leads Europe by a wide margin, with roughly 73% of its land area covered by forests. Sweden is close behind. Montenegro and Slovenia also rank near the top. At the other end, Iceland, Ireland, and the UK have some of the lowest forest shares on the continent. Ireland's historically low forest cover reflects centuries of intensive agricultural land use and the absence of a major reforestation tradition until recent decades.

That said, more forest area does not automatically mean healthier forests. The European Environment Agency notes that forest resilience is becoming a policy concern as temperatures rise and extreme weather becomes more frequent. Logging, insect outbreaks, drought, and storm damage are all increasing. Europe's forests are growing in total area, but they are doing so under growing pressure.

---

The bottom line is straightforward: forests are doing essential work for the climate, and that work is being undermined faster than most people realise. Land management is not a side issue in climate policy. It is central to it.
