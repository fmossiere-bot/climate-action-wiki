# Ocean-Based Climate Solutions

## What is it

The ocean covers more than 70% of the planet and already does enormous climate work — absorbing 30% of human CO₂ emissions and 90% of excess heat. A growing number of **ocean-based climate solutions** aim to protect and enhance this role, or to cut emissions through ocean energy. Project Drawdown reviewed more than 14 such approaches in 2026, ranking them by climate impact, readiness, and risk.

The results are more mixed than the hype suggests. A handful of ocean solutions are ready to deploy at scale now. Others are worth investing in for research. Some are not recommended at all because they risk causing more harm than good.

## Why it matters

With land-based emissions cuts proving difficult to scale fast enough, ocean solutions have attracted growing attention and funding. But as Project Drawdown researcher Christina Richardson notes, some of the highest-impact climate actions still available today are on land — utility-scale solar, onshore wind, diet change, forest protection. Ocean solutions are a vital part of the toolkit, not a substitute for urgent action elsewhere.

## What is ready now: offshore wind and coastal protection

**Offshore wind** is by far the top ocean-based climate solution. By replacing fossil fuel energy, it could deliver an estimated 1.90–3.04 Gt CO₂-equivalent per year — one of the most impactful mitigation options available anywhere, on land or at sea. Siting matters: offshore wind must avoid disrupting carbon-storing ecosystems such as seagrass beds and mangroves.

**Protecting coastal wetlands** — salt marshes, seagrasses, and mangroves — is the next best ocean opportunity. These ecosystems are being lost at roughly 1.2–3.5% of their global area annually. Halting that loss prevents stored carbon from being released and preserves coastal storm protection. Collectively, protecting coastal and ocean ecosystems could deliver 0.21–0.30 Gt CO₂-eq/year. Restoring these habitats adds additional but smaller benefit.

## What is worth pursuing but limited in scale

Reducing overfishing, protecting the seafloor from bottom trawling, improving fishing vessel efficiency, and improving aquaculture practices can all contribute to climate goals. But each delivers under 0.1 Gt CO₂-eq/year and should be pursued primarily for their biodiversity and food security benefits rather than as major climate solutions.

## What remains uncertain: novel marine carbon removal

Ocean alkalinity enhancement and ocean electrochemistry aim to increase the ocean's capacity to absorb CO₂ by altering seawater chemistry. Seaweed farming for food is another emerging approach. These could eventually matter, but none has yet demonstrated reliable large-scale effectiveness, cost efficiency, or ecological safety.

## What is not recommended

**Ocean biomass sinking**, **artificial upwelling**, and **ocean fertilisation** are not recommended. Artificial upwelling appears likely to be ineffective. The others manipulate ocean biology in ways that could trigger widespread, hard-to-reverse ecological harm if deployed at scale.

## The funding gap

Ocean protection currently receives around $1.2 billion per year globally, against a scientifically estimated need of $16 billion per year to meet the 30x30 target (protecting 30% of the ocean by 2030). Offshore wind investment — despite clear commercial momentum — still falls well short of the $100 billion needed by 2050. Meanwhile, novel mCDR research needs roughly $2 billion over five to ten years just to answer basic feasibility questions.

One warning from Project Drawdown: mCDR funding should not divert money from proven, deployment-ready solutions that are already severely underfunded.

## Summary findings

- Offshore wind: 1.90–3.04 Gt CO₂-eq/year achievable — the single largest ocean climate opportunity.
- Coastal wetland and seaweed protection: 0.21–0.30 Gt CO₂-eq/year combined.
- All other ocean actions individually deliver under 0.1 Gt CO₂-eq/year.
- Novel mCDR (alkalinity enhancement, electrochemistry): uncertain, early-stage, requires significant R&D.
- Ocean biomass sinking, artificial upwelling, ocean fertilisation: not recommended.
- Ocean protection funding gap: ~$1.2bn/year actual vs ~$16bn/year needed for 30x30.
- Dismantling ocean science observatories (a trend under the Trump administration as of 2026) undermines the ability to evaluate any of these approaches.
