## Latest NDC Update from COP30
[[COP30]]

The Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) were a central element of the context surrounding COP30, as the conference took place ten years after the Paris Agreement, which required countries to submit renewed and more ambitious pledges. Discussions regarding NDCs focused on the severe lack of global ambition, widespread failure to meet submission deadlines, and political disagreement over establishing a mechanism to review and strengthen them.

#### The NDC Deadline and Status of Submissions

This year was designated as key for countries’ climate pledges, as it marked the deadline for nations to submit new NDCs outlining their ambitions for slashing emissions out to 2035.

• **Missed Deadline:** The formal deadline for countries to submit new NDCs to the UN was **10 February**, but **95% of countries missed that deadline**.
• **Progress at COP30:** Despite the failure to meet the deadline, nations continued submitting new pledges during the talks. On the first day of COP30, the UNFCCC executive secretary noted that **22 new NDCs** had been submitted since the previous synthesis report. By the end of the summit, **some 122 countries** had submitted their new pledges.
• **Key Emitters:** The **world’s biggest annual emitter, China**, announced its NDC at an event in September, ahead of the summit. There was much speculation at COP30 over whether the world’s third biggest emitter, **India**, would come forward with its new NDC; its climate minister clarified that this would happen **“by December”**. The **US NDC was effectively void** after Donald Trump resumed power and signed an order to withdraw the nation from the Paris Agreement.
• **Final Call:** The final overarching agreement, the **“global mutirão”** decision, praised the 122 submitted pledges and **“urges”** those that have not yet made new pledges to do so **“as soon as possible”**.

#### The Ambition Gap

The NDCs submitted were deemed **“grossly inadequate”** to meet the Paris Agreement temperature goal.

###### Projected Warming:** Based on the available plans, the world remains on track for **2.3-2.5°C of warming by 2100**.

• **Emissions Shortfall:** A UN NDC synthesis report, based on the plans submitted up to October, found that the latest NDCs would achieve a global emissions drop of about **10% by 2035** from 2019 levels. 

######  After 22 additional NDCs were submitted at the start of COP30, this projected drop increased slightly to **12%**. This figure is considered **“drastically short”** of the **60% cut needed to keep 1.5°C in sight**.

• **Required Action:** Sources state that outstanding NDCs should **align with 1.5°C**, drive bold sectoral shifts in key areas (food, forests, ocean, energy, transport), and prioritize resilience.


#### Political Conflict over Review Mechanisms

Discussions at COP30 included how to address the collective emissions shortfall arising from these insufficient NDCs.

• **AOSIS Proposal:** The Alliance of Small Island States (AOSIS) proposed a **“dedicated space” on the COP agenda** to “assess collective progress…and drive ambition toward 1.5C”. Palau’s ambassador, who chaired AOSIS, stressed the need for the conference to resolve measures to improve inadequate NDCs, stating, **“The 1.5C target is our north star”**.

• **Opposition:** This proposal was expected to face severe opposition from the **Like-Minded Developing Countries (LMDCs)** group (including China, India, and Saudi Arabia), which prefers to interpret the Paris Agreement goal as allowing temperatures to rise toward 2°C.

• **Failed NDC Discussion:** An early draft of the key outcome text, the **“global mutirão,”** included an option that would have created **annual NDC discussions** linked to a “transition away from fossil fuels.” However, this option was **opposed by the LMDCs and the African group** and subsequently dropped from the final versions.

• **Implementation Focus:** Instead of explicit annual reviews, the final _mutirão_ decision **“calls on” countries to “accelerat[e] the full implementation” of their climate pledges** and encourages them to draw up **“implementation and investment plans”** to align climate strategies with economic development.

#### NDC Content on Specific Sectors

The scope and quality of NDCs were also examined in relation to specific sectors:

• **Agriculture and Food Systems:** A report found that **93% of new NDCs** included at least one measure concerning **agriculture or food systems**. However, an assessment ranked the NDCs of **Brazil and New Zealand as “weak”** in this regard, while those from Somalia and Switzerland were deemed **“very strong”**.

• **Ocean and Water:** More than **90% of new NDCs submitted by coastal and island countries** included **ocean-based climate actions**. Brazil and France had previously launched the **“blue NDC challenge”** to encourage nations to integrate ocean measures into their climate pledges.

The overall outcome on NDCs and ambition reflects the political reality that while the majority of countries recognize the need for NDCs to align with 1.5°C, political consensus proved impossible for establishing a binding or mandatory system to rapidly accelerate and enforce those commitments.

#### What are NDCs?

NDCs, or **Nationally Determined Contributions**, are climate action plans that countries develop under the Paris Agreement. They lay out how each country intends to reduce greenhouse gas emissions and adapt to the effects of climate change. These plans are crucial because they reflect global efforts to limit warming to **well below 2°C, ideally 1.5°C**. NDCs have prompted countries to set more **ambitious climate targets** and adopt cleaner technologies. While progress is uneven, there’s evidence they’re steering investment and policy shifts toward sustainability. Still, much more needs to be done to meet global goals and protect vulnerable communities and ecosystems
