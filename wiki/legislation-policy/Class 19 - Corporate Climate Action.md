## Key Terms
[LINK](https://textbook.terra.do/class/corporate-2)
In this module, we cover key terms in corporate climate action. 

There are a lot of terms around climate action that you have likely heard: **ESG** (Environment and Social Governance), **CSR** (Corporate Social Responsibility), Social Impact, and Corporate Sustainability are some of the most common ones. Each of these speaks in some way to how organizations can do more good in the world, by focusing on sustainability, social impact, and good governance and being more accountable to stakeholders, not just shareholders. They’ve all moved conversations forward and also revealed many issues. 

See [[What is ESG?]]
 
Most recently, ESG has taken the lead. As covered in our modules on Finance, ESG is a corporate governance and investment framework. Companies that adopt ESG principles consider, measure, report, and—in theory—improve the environmental, social, and governance aspects of their business alongside its financial considerations (profit, expenses, growth, accounting). You can read more about the history of ESG and the terms before it [here](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/esg/esg-environmental-social-governance/). 

[ESG](https://twitter.com/lynaldencontact/status/1483216986498908165) [has](https://ssir.org/articles/entry/the_world_may_be_better_off_without_esg_investing) [many](https://seekingalpha.com/article/4505323-esg-what-a-mess-how-did-you-get-it-so-wrong) [problems](https://hbr.org/2022/03/an-inconvenient-truth-about-esg-investing), perhaps the core of which is that [ESG Investing Isn’t Designed to Save the Planet](https://hbr.org/2022/08/esg-investing-isnt-designed-to-save-the-planet).

Climate action requires four elements that are defined by the [We Mean Business Coalition](https://www.wemeanbusinesscoalition.org/business/), a global nonprofit accelerating corporate climate action: 

- **Ambition:** Setting a goal to align the organization with a net-zero world.   
- **Action:** Innovating and transforming business operations, products and services to deliver on this ambition.   
- **Advocacy:** Influencing political systems, stakeholder groups, and supply chains to align with your ambition and action  
- **Accountability:** Clear and consistent disclosure of plans, progress, risks, and opportunities.

### What is net-zero? 
The concept of net-zero carbon emissions emerged from physical climate science. As we have covered earlier in this course, we have a finite budget of carbon dioxide (and other greenhouse gases) we can emit, beyond which any further release needs to be balanced by removal into sinks (everything below the horizontal axis in the graph to the right). 

To meet a 1.5 C goal with a 50 percent probability means we have a remaining carbon budget of 400–800 GtCO2. To stay within this carbon budget, we need CO2 emissions to peak before 2030 and fall to net-zero (that is, the sum of emissions and removals being zero) by around 2050. If you're still wrapping your head around this, [here is a short primer](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Qc1zfRPQ2zA) and [here is a deeper summary](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41558-021-01245-w?utm_source=twitter&utm_medium=social&utm_content=organic&utm_campaign=NGMT_USG_JC01_GL_NRJournals).

Applying net-zero to a company means applying this same logic to an organization, instead of the planet. Organizations need to see their emissions peak and drop to a point where they are removing whatever (few) emissions they still have by 2050. To get more specific, the Science Based Targets Initiative (SBTi)  [defines](https://sciencebasedtargets.org/resources/files/Net-Zero-Standard.pdf) corporate net-zero as:  
 

- _Reducing scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions to zero or to a residual level that is consistent with reaching net-zero emissions at the global or sector level in eligible 1.5°C-aligned pathways_ 
- _Neutralizing any residual emissions at the net-zero target year and any GHG emissions released into the atmosphere thereafter._

### Science-Based Targets
The next key term to understand is science-based emission reduction targets.

The aforementioned Science Based Targets Initiative (SBTi) defines science-based targets as "clearly-defined pathways for companies to reduce GHG emissions in line with what the latest climate science deems necessary to [limit] global warming to well-below 2°C above pre-industrial levels and pursuing efforts to limit warming to 1.5°C.

### Other Terms
Here are a few more terms that you may come across in these slides, and in the world of corporate climate action. You can find more expansive definitions of these terms on the [Oxford Net Zero website](https://netzeroclimate.org/what-is-net-zero/).

- **ABSOLUTE ZERO**: No GHG emissions are attributable to an organization’s activities across all scopes.  
- **CLIMATE NEUTRAL**: An organization’s activities result in no net effect on the climate system. Any emissions or activities with warming effects are fully compensated by GHG reductions, removals, or activities with cooling effects.   
- **CARBON NEUTRAL**: An organization’s net contribution to global CO2 emissions is zero.  
- **1.5 C ALIGNED**: A target is aligned with scenarios that yield a long-term warming outcome of below 1.5 C.
- **SCIENCE-BASED / PARIS-ALIGNED**: A target is aligned with what the latest climate science deems necessary to meet the goals of the Paris Agreement.  
- **OFFSETTING**: Reducing GHG emissions (including through avoided emissions), or increasing GHG removals through activities _external_ to an organization, in order to compensate for GHG emissions.  
- **INSETTING**: Reducing GHG emissions (including through avoided emissions), or increasing GHG removals _through an organization’s scope 1, 2, or 3 emissions_ (that is, _internal to an organization_) in order to compensate for GHG emissions.

### Greenwashing
If we’re defining climate action, it’s important to bring up key terms related to what is not climate action. In 2023, these two terms are offsets and renewable energy credits, and are now mostly labeled “junk RECs” or “junk offsets.”  
  
What constitutes "junk?"

**Offsets** are promissory notes that buy you a project that should avoid or remove your organization's emissions. However, it only works if the money you paid for the offset was the only way the project could have been built. If you can’t verify that, then there’s no way of knowing if any carbon was actually avoided elsewhere. If you want to go down the rabbit hole of this, consider [this thread](https://twitter.com/akshatrathi/status/1594674898881896449) by Bloomberg Green reporter Akshat Rathi.

**Renewable Energy Credits** (RECs) are purchased credits for the generation of MWh renewable electricity generation–you can read more about them [here](https://www.epa.gov/green-power-markets/renewable-energy-certificates-recs). [Reporting by](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2022-11-01/intel-p-g-cisco-among-major-companies-exaggerating-climate-progress#xj4y7vzkg) Ben Elgin and Sinduja Rangarajan at _Bloomberg Green_ assessed 1,318 companies that reported combined reductions of 31 percent from 2015 to 2019. Without junk renewable energy credits, however, their emissions actually fell only 10 percent. For example, For just $600,000—or 28 minutes worth of its annual profits—Cisco re-branded its environmental image while actually increasing its emissions through the purchase of junk RECs. 

**Forest carbon offsets** are also credits, but represent the extraction of 1 ton of CO2 from the atmosphere via a natural source or the protection of forests from deforestation. We’ve already covered the problematic nature of these offsets in our offsets modules, but it’s worth adding that [recent research into Verra,](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2023/jan/18/revealed-forest-carbon-offsets-biggest-provider-worthless-verra-aoe) the world’s leading carbon standard for the rapidly growing $2 billion voluntary offsets market, found that more than 90 percent of their rainforest offset credits are likely to be “phantom credits” and do not represent genuine carbon reductions.

### Financial Risks and Opportunities
Corporate climate action is being driven by a mix of risks and opportunities. On the right, you can see a pretty standard list of these defined by the [Task Force on Climate Disclosure](https://www.fsb-tcfd.org/), a guidance framework that helps companies disclose climate-related financial risks to investors, lenders, and insurers.
![[Screenshot 2023-11-21 at 14.39.33.png]]

The TCFD recommendations are focused on governance, strategy, risk management, metrics, and targets. While this framework is fundamentally for risk management (which makes sense, given the TCFD’s focus on investors), it is also useful for us to understand why organizations are taking climate action.

The TCFD may be familiar from some of our modules on finance, along with these terms:

- **Transition Risks** are risks inherent in changing strategies, policies, or investments as society and industry work to reduce reliance on carbon and impact on the climate; think about the implications of economy-wide changes discussed in solutions modules.  
- **Physical Risks** are risks to infrastructure and supply chain from climate change itself (i.e. implications identified in modules on climate impacts).  
- **Opportunities** are the mirrors of these risks: when an organization orients around climate and takes these risks seriously, they may also identify real opportunities for cost savings, risk reduction, and innovation to get ahead of competitors. 

 ### Compliance
 Various governments are at different stages of mandating climate-related financial disclosures of companies, financial institutions, and governmental agencies. Examples of movement in this direction include the [2022 Rules to Enhance and Standardize Climate-Related Disclosures for Investors](https://www.sec.gov/news/press-release/2022-46) from the US Securities and Exchange Commission and the work by the TCFD. The growing expectation is that this will become standard practice.
![[Screenshot 2023-11-21 at 14.41.23.png]]
So the trend of regulations is clear. Would it not be an advantage to get ahead of it?

### Technology Risks: Cement Case Study
#concrete 
Technology risks to an organization come from competition of lower emissions options replacing demand for your product/service. A great example of technology risk is happening right now in the cement industry. A major focus turned on cement emissions after the IEA reported that cement produces more emissions than all the trucks on roads. Since then, the major cement producers have all set emissions reductions targets, including:  
- CEMEX (40 percent less by 2030 from 1990 baseline)
- Heidelburg (33 percent less by 2030 from 1990 baseline) 
- Laforge (20 percent less by 2030 from 2018 baseline)

![[Screenshot 2023-11-21 at 14.43.57.png]]
  
At the same time, new entrants are claiming abilities to reduce the embedded emissions of cement by 85 percent ([Material Evolution](https://www.materialevolution.com/)), 70-90 percent ([Ultra High Materials)](https://www.ultrahighmaterials.com/), 30-40 percent ([Solidia](https://www.solidiatech.com/)), and 100 percent carbon neutral ([Brimstone](https://www.brimstone.energy/)). Each of these companies is both (a) accelerating our path toward 1.5 C, (b) threatening the bottom line of these incumbents, and (c) making their net-zero pledges look weak.

### Market Risk: Changing Customer Demands
#research #individualsentiment
Market risks include changes in the demand or supply of goods and services driven by climate impacts. Consider the following consumer sentiments research:  
 
- In 2020, [57 percent of consumers](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/retail/our-insights/survey-consumer-sentiment-on-sustainability-in-fashion) in Germany and the UK made significant changes to their lifestyles to lessen their environmental impact. (McKinsey)
- [79 percent of consumers](https://www2.deloitte.com/ch/en/pages/consumer-business/articles/shifting-sands-sustainable-consumer.html) in 2020 have changed their purchase preferences based on sustainability. (Deloitte)
- [81 percent of consumers](https://nielseniq.com/global/en/insights/analysis/2018/global-consumers-seek-companies-that-care-about-environmental-issues/) feel strongly that companies should help improve the environment. (Nielsen)

Not all organizations buy into this research; some doubt consumers are willing to make a change in their choices based on social or environmental impact. But many disagree. A 2020 [Capgemini survey](https://www.capgemini.com/gb-en/wp-content/uploads/sites/5/2022/05/Final-Web-Report-Sustainability-In-CPRD-1.pdf) found 77 percent of organizations believe that their sustainability approaches increase customer loyalty.

Indeed, in the US consumer packaged goods (CPG) sector, [50 percent of the sector's growth from 2013 to 2018](https://www.stern.nyu.edu/sites/default/files/assets/documents/NYU%20Stern%20CSB%20Sustainable%20Share%20Index%E2%84%A2%202019.pdf) came from sustainability-marketed products, and sustainability-marketed products grew faster than their conventional counterparts in almost every category.  
 
There are customers to be acquired and value to be realized with sustainability strategies now.

### Reputational Risk: Climate Talent
Reputational risks are captured by changes in customer preferences or the increased likelihood of confrontational stakeholders. One such stakeholder is an organization’s employees.

Every good organization knows that it is only as valuable as its ability to attract and retain top talent. **Talent risk** means that organizations who do nothing on climate will lose talent to other organizations, and will struggle to attract more—consider how many fellows at Terra are considering changing their job because their current organization is not doing enough. Consider these statistics:   
 

- [40 percent of millennials](https://www.forbes.com/sites/forbesbusinesscouncil/2021/02/10/why-corporate-strategies-should-be-focused-on-sustainability/?sh=62507e367e9f#:~:text=to%20attracting%20talent.-,Nearly%2040%25,-of%20millennials%20have) have taken a job because of the company's sustainability commitments, and they are even willing to take a pay cut to work at an environmentally responsible company.
- [65 percent of UK office workers](https://www.unily.com/insights/guides/future-of-the-sustainable-workplace-in-the-age-of-covid-19-and-climate-change) said they were more likely to work for a company with strong environmental policies.
- Employee discussion on environment-related topics [increased by 52 percent](https://heartbeat.peakon.com/reports/employee-expectations-2020/) and environmental concern rose by 128 percent among Gen Z employees between 2019 and 2020.

  
These surveys are corroborated by [research](https://www.mmc.com/insights/publications/2020/may/esg-as-a-workforce-strategy.html) on ESG data, which found that top employers, as measured by employee satisfaction and attractiveness to talent, have significantly higher ESG scores than their peers.  
 

Talent Risk is not limited to "Western" economies, either. The desire by employees for organizations to act on climate change [is higher](https://press.hp.com/content/dam/hpi/press/press-kits/2019/earth-day-2019/HP%20Workforce%20Sustainability%20Survey.pdf) in India and China than in the US, UK, or Europe. 

For more on climate risks, the UK Task Force on Climate Disclosures has put together a good summary [here](https://www.tcfdhub.org/Downloads/pdfs/E06%20-%20Climate%20related%20risks%20and%20opportunities.pdf). If you want to dive deeper into the talent argument, check out MarshMcLennan's report [ESG as a Workforce Strategy](https://www.mmc.com/insights/publications/2020/may/esg-as-a-workforce-strategy.html).  
  
If you want the best people, you need to have a climate strategy.

### Acute Physical Risks
**Acute physical risks** refer to those that are event-driven, including increased frequency or severity of extreme weather events such as cyclones, hurricanes, or floods. Consider the following: 

The Rhine River is Europe’s most important commercial waterway. Any organization that relies on the Rhine was impacted by multiple acute risks in 2022. First, heavy rainfall and snowmelt in February caused some banks to begin to burst, triggering a halt in river shipping for several days. Then a long-term drought caused water levels to drop so low that cargo ships were forced to load no more than half their usual capacity to avoid running aground.

Semiconductors are one of the main physical components of an increasingly digital economy. Flooding from a 2022 typhoon severely damaged Klang, a port in Malaysia. Semiconductors from Taiwan, (the world’s largest manufacturer) often pass through Klang for packaging at Malaysian factories, and this breakdown contributed to global semiconductor shortages and caused some U.S. automobile manufacturers to suspend operations. [Research by McKinsey](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/sustainability/our-insights/could-climate-become-the-weak-link-in-your-supply-chain) found that the probability of a hurricane of sufficient intensity to disrupt semiconductor supply chains may grow two to four times by 2040, and that the difference of being well-prepared for this type of disruption event would amount to some 30 percent of sales for the year. 

You can read about many more examples of these in this [Yale E360 article](https://e360.yale.edu/features/how-climate-change-is-disrupting-the-global-supply-chain) on acute supply chain disruptions.


### Chronic Physical Risks

**Chronic physical risks** refer to longer-term shifts in climate patterns (like sustained higher temperatures) that may cause sea-level rise or chronic heat waves. 

Long-term temperature change will significantly impact the growing seasons and regions for many staple crops. Any organization that has invested significantly in long-term suppliers is at risk of those investments being lost as yields change over time.

Water availability affects many beverage companies, such as AB InBev, which needs access to a large volume of clean water to produce alcoholic beverages. Any company operating in increasingly water-stressed regions needs to address local water resilience. For this reason, AB InBev established a goal (**Ambition**) to “reduce water risks and improve water management in 100% of [its] key barley-growing regions.” 

You can find other examples of these risks in [this 2016 report](https://mitsloan.mit.edu/sites/default/files/2018-10/Colgate-Report-2016.pdf) done by MIT Sloan and Colgate-Palmolive.  

### Care

While it's easy to get lost in the analytical nature of climate risks and opportunities, it’s important to note that more and more people, from employees to CEOs, simply care about climate change. In fact, care may be driving more climate action than any of these risks we mentioned combined. 

Care can come from a CEO, like when Ray realized that his organization must behave sustainably—or from employees, like when former Terra.do Fellow Tim Falls organized a hackathon to measure his organization’s emissions.  
  
Care is present—and often needs to be acknowledged. If it is not, it can be a "silent majority." More and more employee-driven action is happening. In 2019, over [4,200 Amazon employees](https://www.nytimes.com/2019/04/10/technology/amazon-climate-change-letter.html) called on the company to rethink how it addresses and contributes to a warming planet and make firm commitments to reduce its carbon footprint across its operations.  
 

Care can also form a fundamental part of an organization’s purpose and strategy. Paul Polman, the former CEO of Unilever who restructured the entire organization through a [Sustainable Living Plan](http://www.sustainable-living.unilever.com/), [argues](https://www.theguardian.com/sustainable-business/paul-polman-unilever-sustainable-living-plan) that "if we focus our company on improving the lives of the world's citizens and come up with genuine sustainable solutions, we are more in synch with consumers and society and ultimately this will result in good shareholder returns."  
 

There are probably more people in your organization who care about climate change than you may think—and they definitely have kids who do!

### Sometimes you just need data.

Rapid emissions reduction is essential in organizations, but many are “flying blind” to their energy use, especially in energy-intensive industries. Many factories in supply chains are in countries with dirty grids that have little ability to influence policy and access to renewable energy. However, they still face high energy costs, and many lack the data they need to make informed decisions about where to invest.

#carbonaccounting #carbonfootprint 
Enter [Guidewheel](https://guidewheel.com/about-us), who leverages trends in AI, big data, and machine learning to measure every energy-consuming part of a factory. When energy costs become highly visible and detailed, operators are able to use this data to buy more efficient machines, move production to more efficient lines, reduce machine idle time, reduce scrap, improve processes, and train operators. This has led to 10-25 percent energy use reductions (**action**) all while also providing real “scope 3” emissions data to suppliers and enhancing transparency (**accountability**).

### Case Studies in Sustainable Business Model Innovation

Consulting firm BCG analyzed 500 sustainability initiatives worldwide and found only 102 cases where companies changed some or all of their core business model to generate environmental and societal benefits and business value. Very few stand out as practitioners of what BCG calls "Sustainable Business Model Innovation," which you can read more about [here](https://www.bcg.com/en-us/publications/2021/keys-to-being-a-leader-in-sustainable-business-model-innovation). Some examples include:

- [**Schneider Electric**](https://www.se.com/us/en/) reimagined its business model from a traditional B2B electrical distribution equipment manufacturer to becoming a recognized global leader in environmental sustainability solutions. Its annual revenues have grown accordingly, from €15 billion ($18 billion) in 2007 to €27 billion ($33 billion) in 2019.  
- **[Yara](https://www.yara.com/)**, a Norwegian agricultural products company, diversified beyond its traditional fertilizers business to launch a digital “smart” agriculture arm to enhance customers’ farming yields and reduce water and fertilizer use.  
- [**Fairphone**](https://www.fairphone.com/en/) produces and sells a more ethical and durable smartphone composed of sustainable materials (including responsibly sourced and conflict-free tin and tungsten and recycled copper and plastics) and with a modular design that can be upgraded and repaired by swapping out components.

To more and more business leaders and entrepreneurs, these business models are obvious. Consider [this TerraTalk](https://youtu.be/ziufcA8MPNw) with a pioneer in the space and Terra.do academic board member, Harish Hande, about how he built out SELCO, a solar company in India.

### Corporations and Climate: A History
#fossilfuel 

From one lens, corporations have been involved in climate change since day one, and so have we as members of the global economy. There's a lot out there on corporate lobbying, culpability, and accountability. A [2017 report](https://cdn.cdp.net/cdp-production/cms/reports/documents/000/002/327/original/Carbon-Majors-Report-2017.pdf?1501833772) by the Carbon Disclosure Project named 100 investor- and state-owned fossil fuel companies to be responsible for 70 percent of all human emissions.
![[Screenshot 2023-11-21 at 15.08.59.png]]
There has also [been](https://graphics.latimes.com/exxon-arctic/) [significant](https://www.climatechangecommunication.org/wp-content/uploads/2019/10/America_Misled.pdf) [reporting](https://insideclimatenews.org/book/exxon-the-road-not-taken/) on how the fossil fuel industry has funded and led disinformation campaigns about the reality and severity of climate change to confuse the public and decision-makers and delay climate action.

A recent [report](https://www.ceres.org/practicingRPE) by Ceres found that 1 in 5 of the companies in the Fortune 100 still actively lobby against climate legislation, even if they **had** set or committed to set their own emissions reduction targets. These behaviors have led many activists to express justifiable concern that net-zero pledges are more of the same smoke and mirrors

### A Rising Tide of Pledges & Targets

A [2021 analysis](https://ca1-eci.edcdn.com/reports/ECIU-Oxford_Taking_Stock.pdf?mtime=20210323005817&focal=none) by Oxford and the Energy & Climate Intelligence Unit found that one in five of the world’s 2,000 largest public companies has now committed to a net-zero emissions target. The companies represent nearly $14 trillion in sales.

[SBTi's October 2020 progress report](https://sciencebasedtargets.org/sbti-progress-report-2020) showed that the companies pledged to setting science-based targets had a combined market capitalization of over $20.5 trillion, which equates to almost 20 percent of the global market. The 1,374 companies with approved targets are responsible for approximately 4 percent of global emissions, but if current growth rates continue, the SBTi predicts that by 2030, SBTi companies’ scope 1 and 2 emissions could cover almost a quarter of global emissions from energy and industry (you can see the latest numbers [here](https://sciencebasedtargets.org/companies-taking-action)). 
![[Screenshot 2023-11-21 at 15.11.46.png]]
The UNFCCC [reported](https://unfccc.int/news/commitments-to-net-zero-double-in-less-than-a-year) that from 2019-2020, the total number of net-zero commitments by companies and governments doubled in under a year. Also consider [this graph,](https://www.southpole.com/uploads/media/climate-active-certification-chart-south-pole.png) which shows the growing number of carbon neutral certifications given by Climate Active, the Australian Government’s carbon neutral certification network.

### Science-Based Targets

The outcomes from science-based targets are encouraging. SBTi's 2020 progress report found that **companies who set science-based targets reduced their combined scope 1 and scope 2 emissions by 25 percent since 2015**—compared with a 3.4 percent increase in global emissions—and that the median SBTi company is reducing annual **scope 1 and scope 2 emissions** at a rate that _exceeds_ the rate needed to limit warming to 1.5 C.

### But Scope 3 Emissions Missing

Note that SBTi only mentioned progress on scope 1 and 2 emissions. These are relatively easy emissions for a company to impact. Scope 3 emissions present a much larger challenge, and often the majority of an organization's operations fall under this category. For example, the image to the right shows the breakdown of scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions for Vale, one of the largest mining companies in the world. 

Vale is not alone. Unilever has stated that due to scope 3 emissions, they’re not sure if they can meet their net-zero targets (**Accountability)**. A [study](https://t.co/lEsp2dcqWP) found that while 49 percent of suppliers, 36 percent of manufacturers, and 31 percent of retailers from across the food and beverage industry claim to be very confident that they will meet their carbon-reduction goals related to their own carbon emissions, there is a more than 50 percent drop-off (to 27 percent, 13 percent, and 6 percent, respectively) regarding scope 3 emission reductions in value chains. ### Case Study of Going Green: Microsoft

A great example of a net-zero challenge is Microsoft. The company committed to being climate negative by 2030 **(Ambition)**. The company has supported the development of emissions disclosure rules for public companies and [recently announced](https://www.nytimes.com/2022/03/10/business/microsoft-climate-carbon-emissions.html) that it would no longer do specialized work for energy companies involved in extracting fossil fuels unless they had a net-zero target **(Advocacy)**. In its most recent reporting, Microsoft showed that while it reduced its scope 1 and 2 emissions by 16.9 percent, its total emissions grew 21.4 percent (**Accountability)**. 

“Our emissions outputs took place against a backdrop of significant business growth in 2021," [noted](https://blogs.microsoft.com/blog/2022/03/10/an-update-on-microsofts-sustainability-commitments-building-a-foundation-for-2030/) president and vice chair Brad Smith. "In this time period our business revenue grew by 20 percent," driven by growth in Xbox sales and usage as a result of the pandemic, and the company significantly expanded its global data center footprint to meet the increased demand for cloud business.

Microsoft is facing a challenge all businesses must address: how can a business grow without emissions growing?

### Microsoft and Scope 3 emissions

Microsoft presents a good example of **accountability**: that is, they publish the data publicly, and also speak about the **action** that they aim to take to address their challenges. This includes:

- Adapting how they set carbon targets (**Ambition**)
- Increasing the frequency and scope of our internal reporting **(Accountability)**
- Updating their Supplier Code of Conduct to require in-scope suppliers to report emissions (**Advocacy)** 
- Accelerating work to set business-group-specific annual carbon intensity targets based on fundamental business drivers and restructuring and increasing an internal carbon fee to help incentivize more aggressive measures to reduce Scope 3 emissions and better match the underlying cost of carbon abatement. For example, increasing the Scope 3 business travel fee to $100 per mtCO2e to better support the purchase of sustainable aviation fuel. (**Action**)

You can read more about other actions and advocacy initiatives Microsoft has committed to [here](https://blogs.microsoft.com/blog/2022/03/10/an-update-on-microsofts-sustainability-commitments-building-a-foundation-for-2030/). ### So, What To Do.

There is plenty of pressure to take climate action, and while lots of organizations are struggling to do so effectively, there are some leaders out there. So what are some best practices you can take back to your organization? 

This module includes best practices to get an organization’s scope 1, 2, _and_ 3 greenhouse gas emissions to zero.

### Set science-based targets.

The current growing norm is for organizations to set science-based targets to reach net-zero. The entire SBTi Net-Zero standard is 65 pages long (you can read it [here](https://sciencebasedtargets.org/resources/files/Net-Zero-Standard.pdf)) and guides organizations to do four things: 

1. **Focus on rapid, deep emission cuts:** Meaningful cuts to value-chain emissions are the most effective and scientifically sound way of limiting global temperature rise to 1.5 C. 
2. **Set near- and long-term targets:** Set both near-term and long-term science-based targets, i.e. making rapid emissions cuts now to half emissions by 2030 and planning to produce close to zero emissions by 2050. 
3. **No net-zero claims until long-term targets are met:** A company is only considered to have reached net-zero when it has achieved its long-term science-based target. 
4. **Go beyond the value chain:** Make investments outside science-based targets to help mitigate climate change elsewhere.

### Addressing Scope 1 and 2 Emissions

Once you measure, you can manage. 
For scope 1 emissions, some common strategies are:  
- Reduce consumption and increase energy efficiency (e.g., heat pumps, better insulation). 
- Replace fossil fuels with cleaner alternatives (e.g., overhauling a vehicle fleet to EVs). 

For scope 2 emissions, some common strategies include:   
- Purchase "unbundled" renewable energy certificates.
- Enter into off-site power purchase agreements.
- Generate renewable energy on-site.
- Participate in Utility Green Tariffs and Utility Green Power Programs.

The leaders in scope 2 emissions reduction are aiming for 24/7 carbon-free electricity, which you can read more about [here](https://www.volts.wtf/p/an-introduction-to-energys-hottest?s=r) and [here](https://www.canarymedia.com/articles/clean-energy/google-and-others-have-committed-to-24-7-carbon-free-energy-what-does-that-mean). ### Internal Carbon Pricing

We mentioned in a previous module that Microsoft is working to reduce its emissions; part of that involves increasing the company's internal carbon price. Internal carbon pricing is when companies assign a cost to each ton of carbon used, restructuring the costs of current or new initiatives to reflect the cost of their embedded emissions as well. This can help make more low-carbon investment decisions as they look at new business lines. 

  
[An analysis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0148296322002028) of 500 U.S. publicly listed firms found that robust internal carbon prices reduce carbon emissions per employee and carbon emissions per revenue by 13.5 percent and 15.7 percent, respectively. [Research](https://twitter.com/natbullard/status/1570446174024519680?s=46&t=Xin4LM3rIBeWlSeOQKpmdA) from _Bloomberg_ has found that explicit internal carbon prices and internal trading has the most impact on organizations' decision-making (vs. offsets, which have no impact on changing business activities/practices).  
  
While [more companies are planning to implement carbon prices](https://twitter.com/natbullard/status/1570445735551991809?s=46&t=Xin4LM3rIBeWlSeOQKpmdA) (**ambition**) the translation of ambition to action with internal carbon prices has been lower, as the percentage of companies since 2015 who have implemented internal carbon prices against those who reported intending to implement an internal carbon price in the “next two years” has been, on average, l[ess than 15 to 20 percent](https://twitter.com/greenmetric/status/1601396303984209921/photo/1). Also, these prices [are all over the place](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/strategy-and-corporate-finance/our-insights/the-state-of-internal-carbon-pricing). The High-Level Commission on Carbon Prices [recommends](https://www.carbonpricingleadership.org/report-of-the-highlevel-commission-on-carbon-prices) having an internal carbon price between $50 and $100 per metric ton by 2030 to be in line with standards set in the Paris Agreement.

### Good Carbon Removal

If you’re focusing on carbon removal, keep in mind the importance of durable carbon removal. Engineered solutions like direct air capture may have greater durability.  
  
The argument for investing in carbon removal credits in the present is that doing so will create a future supply of carbon removal options by sending a demand signal as a buyer now to help carbon removal solutions get to the scale, and at a price that organizations will need to reach net-zero targets—_and_ also lock in that removal for your organization at a lower price. Because of the immaturity of carbon removal technologies and the fact that they are not a replacement for rapid emissions reduction, it’s best to think of these engagements as **advocacy** more than **action**.  
  
You can find a good guide for how to approach carbon removal engagement in [this report](https://cdn.shopify.com/static/sustainability/Shopify-Carbon-Removal-Buying-Guide.pdf) by Shopify.

### Action happens collectively.

Corporate climate action is as much a numbers question as anything else. The more individuals at an organization are informed, engaged, and bringing climate action into their daily work, the more likely an organization is going to take effective action, meet targets, and truly transform. Climate action will not happen solely as a top-down initiative from management; it must be seeded and grown in teams throughout an organization. Three essential steps that underlie any sustained **ambition, action, advocacy,** or **accountability** are: 


**1. Find Your Champions.**   
In any organization, there are champions—individuals (like yourself!) who are inspired to go above and beyond in their careers to have (and make) climate impact. ‘Climate Champions’ need to be able to understand their organization’s climate profile and get buy-in from and be accountable to senior managers and members of the board. They should live across many different functions of an organization.

**2. Educate.**
“Talent development drives the sustainability journey” is the first conclusion of [Microsoft's 2022 report on sustainability transformation](https://query.prod.cms.rt.microsoft.com/cms/api/am/binary/RE5bhuF). Employees need access to education to help them understand how climate change may impact their organization and how to think about solutions that they can bring into their organization and daily work. Most important is (1) the development of a common language around climate action and (2) effective diffusion of this language across teams and functions.  
 

**3. Evolve.**
Climate action is a living process. Our knowledge about the science and climate impacts is constantly evolving (which is why we update Terra.do classes as frequently as we do) and new technologies and business solutions are constantly changing the landscape of what’s possible for businesses. This means climate action needs to have the space to be dynamic and responsive to these changes.

## Key Takeaways:

- Corporate climate action requires four elements: Ambition, action, advocacy, and accountability.  
- SBTi defines net-zero as:
    - _Reducing scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions to zero or to a residual level that is consistent with reaching net-zero emissions at the global or sector level in eligible 1.5°C-aligned pathways_ 
    - _Neutralizing any residual emissions at the net-zero target year and any GHG emissions released into the atmosphere thereafter._  
- SBTi defines science-based targets as "clearly-defined pathways for companies to reduce GHG emissions in line with what the latest climate science deems necessary to [limit] global warming to well-below 2°C above pre-industrial levels and pursuing efforts to limit warming to 1.5°C."  
- Corporate climate action is being driven by a mix of risks and opportunities, ranging from regulatory compliance issues to technology and market risk to the need to attract and retain talent.
- Scope 1 emissions are those directly produced by an organization, say by burning fuel for heating buildings or manufacturing processes.  
- Scope 2 emissions are less direct—for instance if the organization keeps its lights on using electricity from a nearby coal plant.  
- Scope 3 emissions indirectly result from up and down the company's supply or value chain; these are the biggest part of any company's emissions.  
- Successful employee climate action is most likely to succeed if you start small, build a team, recognize the larger challenges (and challengers), relate to decision-makers, focus on how your business can do its part to reduce emissions, and give careful thought to your risk tolerance.
