### Summary

More companies than ever have made climate commitments. But pledges and real action are not the same thing. Most targets focus on the easier emissions to measure, ignore the larger picture, and rely on tools like carbon offsets that do not deliver what they promise. The gap between ambition and actual progress remains large. And in 2025, a visible retreat by some of the world's largest companies raised serious questions about whether the corporate climate movement is holding together.

---

### Key Numbers

- Around **60%** of all Forbes 2000 companies now have net-zero pledges, and more than **8,500 companies** have had their targets validated by the Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi)
- By early 2026, **9,764 companies** hold validated near-term targets and **2,325** hold validated net-zero targets
- **72% of firms** in a large US sample are not on track to meet their climate targets
- Companies with approved science-based targets reduced their scope 1 and 2 emissions by **25%** between 2015 and their most recent reporting, compared to a **3.4% increase** in global emissions over the same period
- Only **10%** of assessed companies have net-zero targets that include their most material scope 3 emissions
- **40%** of millennials have taken a job because of a company's sustainability commitments
- Over **50%** of CEOs surveyed either do not believe they produce meaningful emissions or lack the tools to measure them

### The Basic Framework: What Corporate Climate Action Actually Means

Before looking at what companies are doing, it helps to be clear on the key terms, because they are used loosely and often misleadingly.

> **Net-zero** means that an organisation has reduced its emissions to a level close to zero, and that whatever small residual emissions remain are balanced by verified carbon removal. It is not the same as being "carbon neutral" through purchasing offsets.

**Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions** define where a company's emissions come from:

- **Scope 1** are direct emissions from the company's own operations, such as fuel burned in boilers or company vehicles
- **Scope 2** are indirect emissions from the electricity the company buys
- **Scope 3** are all other indirect emissions up and down the supply chain, including purchased goods, employee travel, product use, and waste disposal. These are typically the largest category for most organisations, and the hardest to address

> **Science-based targets** are emissions reduction goals aligned with what climate science says is needed to limit warming to 1.5 degrees Celsius. They are set through the SBTi and require companies to follow a defined methodology rather than setting their own benchmarks.

**Offsetting** means paying for emissions reductions or carbon removal elsewhere to compensate for your own emissions. The quality of offsets varies enormously, and many have been shown not to deliver the reductions they claim.

### Why Companies Are Acting

Corporate climate action is driven by a mix of pressure and opportunity.

**Regulation.** Governments are increasingly requiring companies to disclose climate-related financial risks. In Europe, the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) is now pushing tens of thousands of companies to measure and report their emissions more rigorously, including scope 3. CSRD's obligations can act as a catalyst, and scope 3 emissions measuring and reporting is now a prerequisite for net-zero target setting in line with SBTi requirements. The direction of travel is clear, even if the exact scope of CSRD has been revised as part of the EU's broader simplification process in 2025 and 2026.

**Market and technology risk.** In many sectors, lower-carbon alternatives are entering the market. In cement, for example, startups are claiming the ability to reduce embedded emissions by 70 to 100%, directly threatening the business models of large incumbents. Any company whose product or service has a high carbon footprint faces the risk of being displaced.

**Customer pressure.** Consumer research consistently shows that sustainability influences purchasing decisions for a significant share of buyers. In the United States consumer goods sector, around half of all growth between 2013 and 2018 came from sustainability-marketed products.

**Talent.** Organisations that do nothing on climate risk losing employees to competitors who are doing more. This is particularly visible among younger workers and is not limited to Europe and North America.

**Supply chain disruption.** Extreme weather events are already disrupting global supply chains, and the risk is growing.

**Care.** Beyond the business case, a growing number of employees and leaders simply care. Employee-led pressure has become a real force in large organisations and is increasingly common.

### The Current State: A Mixed and Deteriorating Picture

The number of corporate climate commitments has risen sharply. Net-zero pledges are now common among the world's largest companies, yet halfway through what the United Nations calls the critical decade for climate action, progress on the ground remains limited. Many companies still lack credible plans to achieve their targets, and evidence of structural changes in business models is scarce.

Research from the University of Birmingham, analysing over 1,200 sustainability reports from Fortune Global 500 companies, found that net-zero narratives often serve as symbolic reputation management rather than a blueprint for transformative climate action.

**The retreat of 2025.** What made 2025 particularly concerning was a visible rollback of commitments by some of the world's largest companies. Oil and gas companies drove the most visible retreat. BP scrapped its pledge to cut output by 40% by 2030 and increased production targets while halving investment in renewables. Shell expects fossil fuel output to rise annually through 2030, and TotalEnergies cut its clean-energy spending from $5 billion to $4.5 billion. Several major banks also backtracked. HSBC pushed back its net-zero target to 2050 citing economic changes, and Wells Fargo scrapped plans to green its lending activities entirely.

Coca-Cola abandoned its 2025 goal to cut virgin plastic use by 3 million tonnes, pushing recycled-content targets to 2035. PepsiCo cancelled its 2030 reusable packaging target.

**Quiet progress in the background.** Not all news is negative. PwC's 2025 decarbonisation report describes an era of "quiet progress", where companies avoid publicising climate pledges that can open them up to scrutiny and instead focus on making progress away from the spotlight. Some companies are continuing to invest in durable carbon removal, tighten supplier requirements, and make operational changes, without making public announcements about it.

**Most targets still do not cover scope 3.** Research from Climate Action 100+ found that only 10% of assessed companies have net-zero targets that include their most material scope 3 emissions. Since scope 3 is typically the majority of a company's footprint, targets that only cover scope 1 and 2 remain incomplete.

**Offsets remain problematic.** A wide variety of accounting practices are blurring the real status of corporate progress, with cross-sector use of market-based electricity accounting and carbon credits. Many companies still claim to be carbon neutral based on the purchase of carbon credits, and many fail to disclose crucial information about those purchases.

### What Is Changing: SBTi Version 2.0

The standards that define what counts as a credible corporate climate target are themselves being updated. SBTi published a revised draft of its Corporate Net-Zero Standard Version 2.0 in November 2025, introducing scope-specific target setting, a more flexible framework for scope 3 emissions, and a new recognition mechanism for taking responsibility for ongoing emissions. Version 2.0 is expected to become mandatory for new targets from January 2028.

Scope 3 targets under the new standard will focus on significant emissions categories above 5% of total scope 3, and the range of acceptable methods has been expanded to include supplier engagement, emissions intensity benchmarks, and circularity targets.

Critically, the new standard maintains the principle that reductions come first. Carbon credits can only be used for neutralising residual emissions at the net-zero point, estimated at around 10% of a company's remaining emissions by 2050, and for voluntary beyond-value-chain mitigation. Credits do not count toward reduction targets.

SBTi is also proposing a five-year cyclical review process, aligned with CSRD, that requires companies to assess progress at the end of their target period and set new targets that account for previous performance. SBTi will also conduct spot checks at any point in the cycle to confirm compliance.

### What Good Practice Looks Like

Despite the overall picture, some companies are setting a higher standard. The common elements of credible climate plans include:

**Setting science-based targets through SBTi.** This provides an externally verified, consistent methodology aligned with the 1.5 degree pathway.

**Covering all three scopes.** A plan that ignores scope 3 is not a complete plan. Companies need to work with suppliers, customers, and partners to address the full footprint of their products and services.

**Setting interim targets.** Meaningful near-term milestones are essential for accountability and prevent the hard work being deferred indefinitely.

**Using internal carbon pricing.** Putting a price on carbon inside the organisation, applied to investment decisions and budgets, changes behaviour. Research shows this reduces emissions per employee by around 13 to 15%. The recommended range is 50 to 100 US dollars per tonne by 2030.

**Being transparent.** Publishing data, including when results are disappointing, is a mark of genuine commitment. Disclosing clearly how offsets are being used, and in what volume, is equally important.

**Using offsets sparingly.** Offsets should be used only for residual emissions that cannot be eliminated, and only where additionality can be verified. High-durability carbon removal such as direct air capture or biochar is more credible than short-lived forestry credits.

---

### What This Means in Practice

Corporate actors have been successfully mobilised to develop climate strategies, with a focus on setting targets and disclosing emissions. However, these targets have yet to translate into meaningful shifts in companies' operations and business models that align with limiting global warming to 1.5 degrees Celsius.

The standards are becoming more rigorous, and the regulatory environment in Europe is pushing disclosure requirements further down the supply chain. At the same time, some of the world's most prominent companies are stepping back from their previous commitments, citing economic and political pressure.

The organisations that will be best positioned in the years ahead are those that treat climate action as an operational and strategic priority rather than a communications exercise, that set targets grounded in science, measure honestly, and report transparently on both progress and shortfalls.
