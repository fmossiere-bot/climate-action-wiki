_What they are, why they exist — and how to use them well_

---

## What is it?

A carbon footprint is the total amount of greenhouse gas emissions that come from your lifestyle in a given year — your travel, what you eat, how you heat your home, what you buy. It is usually measured in **tonnes of CO₂ equivalent (CO₂e)**.

Carbon footprint calculators are online tools that estimate this number based on a series of questions about your habits. Most take between 5 and 15 minutes to complete.

> ⚠️ **A word of warning on origins.** The term "carbon footprint" was not invented by environmentalists. It was popularised in **2004 by BP**, the oil company, as part of a major PR campaign designed by the advertising firm Ogilvy & Mather. The goal was to shift the conversation about climate change away from the fossil fuel industry and towards individuals. BP even launched one of the first carbon footprint calculators as part of this campaign. That context matters. Climate change is primarily a systemic problem caused by large corporations and governments — not recycling habits.

That said, the tool itself has become genuinely useful — even if its origins were manipulative. Used with the right mindset, a carbon footprint calculator can give you a rough sense of where your personal impact is biggest. The key word is _rough_. These calculators rely on national averages and broad assumptions. They are approximations, not precise measurements. Think of them as a compass, not a GPS.

---

## Why it matters

The main value of a carbon footprint calculator is not the number itself — it is the perspective it gives you. Most of us have a surprisingly distorted view of what actually matters when it comes to our personal emissions.

> 💡 Research consistently shows that people tend to **underestimate the impact of flying** and overestimate the impact of everyday actions like recycling or switching off lights. A single long-haul return flight can produce more emissions than several months of average car use.

So if you are vegetarian, cycle to work, and buy second-hand — but fly to a holiday destination every few months — your focus should clearly be on your flights, not on which bin to use. A calculator helps you see that clearly.

It is also useful for understanding which areas of your life you _do_ have control over versus those that depend on structural choices: public transport availability, housing options, the energy grid in your country. That distinction matters for where you direct your energy — both personal choices, and advocacy for bigger change.

## Key Facts

### Benchmarks by country

To make sense of your own result, it helps to know where different countries stand.

|Country / Region|Tonnes CO₂e per person/year|Notes|
|---|---|---|
|🌍 Global average|~4.7|Average across all countries|
|🇮🇪 Ireland|~14.0|2nd highest in the EU (consumption-based, 2023)|
|🇪🇺 EU average|~9.0|Consumption-based, 2023|
|🇺🇸 USA|~17.6|More than twice the global average|
|🇮🇳 India|~2.2|Below global average|
|🌍 Sub-Saharan Africa|~0.1|Chad, Niger, CAR — 150x lower than the US|

**The target:** Climate scientists say we need to get to around **2 tonnes per person per year** to limit global warming to 1.5°C. For most people in Ireland, that means cutting emissions by roughly 85%.

### Production vs consumption: an important distinction

Most country figures you see are **production-based** — they only count emissions generated within a country's borders. But this can be misleading. If Ireland imports goods manufactured in Asia, those emissions don't show up in our national statistics.

**Consumption-based figures** are more honest — they count emissions wherever they were generated, as long as they produced things we consume. Ireland's consumption-based footprint is significantly higher than its production-based one. Eurostat data shows that the EU's total footprint is about 21% higher when measured on a consumption basis.

### Where do individual emissions actually come from?

Research from Our World in Data, the IEA, and the UN shows a consistent pattern for people in wealthier countries:

|Sector|Approximate share|
|---|---|
|🚗 Transport (car + flights)|~30–35%|
|🥩 Food & diet|~20–25%|
|🏠 Home energy|~15–20%|
|🛍️ Goods & shopping|~10–15%|
|🔧 Services & other|~10%|

In Ireland specifically, **transport** is the single biggest factor — the average Irish person drives roughly 16,000–17,000 km per year. **Food** — particularly beef — is also a much bigger factor than in many other European countries. **Flying** adds significantly on top for many households.

## The main carbon footprint calculators

There are several solid tools available. They vary in depth and approach. Here are the main ones worth trying.

### ⭐ ClimateHero _(recommended)_

**[climatehero.me](https://climatehero.me/)**

Takes about 5 minutes and covers housing, travel, and consumption. Uses over 30 public data sources. Works well for Ireland and the UK. Gives you one of four clear archetypes — Climate Hero, Friend, Consumer, or Villain — rather than obsessing over exact decimal points. It also lets you commit to a reduction pledge and see how your score compares to others. Over 2 million people have used it.

### Carbon Footprint Ltd

**[carbonfootprint.com/individuals.html](https://www.carbonfootprint.com/individuals.html)**

One of the more detailed tools available. Goes deeper on household energy, travel, shopping, and secondary footprint. Also covers business and event calculations. Good if you want more granular results and guidance on reducing emissions.

### WWF Footprint Calculator

**[footprint.wwf.org.uk](https://footprint.wwf.org.uk/#/)**

Run by the World Wildlife Fund. Simple questionnaire format, focused on the UK but useful elsewhere. Good for a quick first overview — less detailed than ClimateHero, but friendly and accessible.

### SEAI — Sustainable Energy Authority of Ireland

**[seai.ie](https://www.seai.ie/)**

Not a full personal calculator, but the best Irish source for understanding your home's energy use and where you can make changes. Useful for heating, insulation, and electricity — which together make up a large chunk of most Irish households' footprint.
