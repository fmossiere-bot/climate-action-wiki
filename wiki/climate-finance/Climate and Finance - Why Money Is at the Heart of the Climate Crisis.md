## Is Finance the Problem, the Solution, or Both?

When we talk about addressing climate change, the conversation often turns to **technology, policy, or individual behaviour**. But there is another dimension that is just as important and far less discussed: **money**. Where it comes from, where it goes, and who controls it.

> Finance is not neutral. **The banking system, investment funds, government budgets, and private capital markets all play a direct role in either accelerating or slowing down climate change**. The same institutions that have the tools to fund a clean energy transition have also spent decades financing the fossil fuel industry that caused the problem in the first place.

This article explores that tension. It looks at why finance matters for climate, what is currently happening with public and private climate investment, where the gaps and risks are, and what individuals can do with their own money.

---

## Why Does Finance Matter for Climate?

Addressing climate change at the scale and speed required is enormously expensive. 

> According to McKinsey, **reaching net zero by 2050 will require around 9.2 trillion dollars every single year** between now and then. 

To put that in context, the IPCC estimates that staying below 1.5 degrees of warming requires roughly:
- 1 to 2 trillion dollars per year to clean up the power sector
- 1.8 trillion dollars per year in buildings
- 1.7 trillion dollars per year in industry
- At least 2.7 trillion dollars per year in transport between 2015 and 2035
- Around 300 to 350 billion dollars per year in land use and agriculture

These figures do not even include adaptation, meaning the investments needed to help communities cope with climate impacts that are already locked in.

No government can fund this alone. Private finance must be part of the solution. But finance is also a lever that can be pulled quickly, even when political progress is slow. And critically, financial institutions have not been passive bystanders. They have actively shaped the energy system we have today, often by directing capital toward the most carbon-intensive parts of it.

There are also compelling economic arguments for climate investment. The World Bank estimates that investing in resilient infrastructure in developing countries could deliver 4.2 trillion dollars over the lifetime of that infrastructure. 

> Every dollar invested in resilience generates, on average, four dollars in benefits. A shift to low-carbon economies could also create over 65 million net new jobs globally by 2030.

## The Current State of Climate Finance

### Public and private finance are both growing, but not fast enough

Climate finance comes from two main sources: 
- Public finance includes money from governments and public institutions such as development banks. 
- Private finance comes from businesses, investment funds, and individuals.

According to the Climate Policy Initiative, **both forms of finance grew at around 7% per year in the decade from 2011 to 2020.** Today, public and private climate finance are roughly equal in size. Public finance often leads the way by taking on early risk, which then attracts private capital. There are also blended finance structures, where public money is used to reduce the risk of an investment enough for private investors to come in.

Despite this growth, there remains a significant funding gap. Current investment falls well short of what is needed to stay on a 1.5 degree pathway.

### Fossil fuel finance is still very large

The more uncomfortable truth is that while climate finance is growing, fossil fuel finance is not shrinking fast enough. Since the Paris Agreement was signed in 2015, the world's 60 largest banks have provided 3.8 trillion dollars in financing to fossil fuel companies. In 2020 and 2021 alone, the 30 largest financial institutions collectively provided more than 740 billion dollars to the fossil fuel industry. Of those 30 institutions, 29 had publicly committed to net zero.

Governments are also part of the problem. The International Monetary Fund calculated that global fossil fuel subsidies in 2020 reached 5.9 trillion dollars, nearly 7% of global GDP. This includes direct subsidies and the broader economic costs, such as pollution and health impacts, that are not priced into the cost of fossil fuels.

There is some progress. At least 53 countries reformed fossil fuel subsidies between 2015 and 2020. But the pace of change remains slow relative to the scale of the challenge.

## Public Finance: Key Instruments and Examples

### Government spending and green investment

Governments are increasingly deploying large-scale public finance for the climate transition. In the United States, the Inflation Reduction Act passed in 2022 committed 370 billion dollars over ten years to clean energy and climate solutions, alongside a 1.2 trillion dollar infrastructure deal with significant climate components. A national green bank was also established to direct 27 billion dollars toward clean energy projects.


### Debt-for-climate swaps

One creative mechanism gaining traction in developing countries is the debt-for-climate swap. Many low-income countries carry very high levels of debt, with annual repayments sometimes consuming 30% or more of their total export revenues. This leaves little room to invest in climate mitigation or adaptation.

In a debt-for-climate swap, a country agrees to redirect money it would have paid in foreign debt repayments toward domestic climate projects instead. Belize, for example, reduced its debt in exchange for committing to protect 30% of its marine areas and fund marine conservation for twenty years. Costa Rica used similar arrangements to plant over 60,000 trees and reverse deforestation. In principle, the same model could be used to fund renewable energy, flood defences, or other climate-critical infrastructure.

### The Green Climate Fund

The Green Climate Fund, set up under the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change, is the world's largest fund focused on helping developing countries reduce their emissions and adapt to climate impacts. It has funded more than 100 projects since it was established in 2015, though it remains underfunded relative to needs.

### Criticisms of aid-based climate finance

Not all assessments of public climate finance are positive. Oxfam has produced highly critical analysis showing that the actual value of climate finance flowing from wealthy to developing countries is much lower than what donors report. Assistance for adaptation in particular has been insufficient. And rather than being additional to existing development aid, the majority of climate finance appears to be replacing it.

There is also a mismatch in where money goes. Funding for some of the most impactful interventions, such as cooling systems in tropical regions, represents a tiny fraction of total climate finance, despite the fact that cooling could account for 13% of global emissions by 2030.

## Private Finance: ESG, Green Bonds, and Venture Capital

### ESG investing

Environmental, Social, and Governance investing, commonly known as ESG, refers to an approach that considers a company's environmental and social performance alongside its financial returns. ESG investing has grown dramatically over the past two decades. ESG assets were projected to reach 165 trillion dollars globally by 2025, representing around half of all professionally managed assets. Europe remains the largest player in this space.

However, ESG investing comes with significant challenges.

The most fundamental is that ESG ratings have historically been designed to measure risk to the company, not risk to the world. A company might score well on ESG metrics because climate change poses limited financial risk to its specific business model, even if that business model is contributing to environmental damage. As one financial journalist put it: ESG ratings are about corporate profits, not planetary health.

There is also a lack of consistent standards. Different rating agencies use different methods, making it very difficult to compare ESG performance across companies or funds. This has led to some well-documented absurdities. In 2022, Tesla was removed from the S&P ESG index not for environmental reasons, but due to concerns about workplace conditions and racial discrimination. At the same time, Exxon remained on the index and another oil company was added.

### Greenwashing

Closely linked to ESG is the problem of greenwashing: making claims about environmental performance that are misleading or not backed by evidence. Regulators are beginning to act. The European Union has passed legislation specifically targeting greenwashing in investment products. In the United Kingdom, HSBC had advertising banned for making misleading climate claims. In Germany, Deutsche Bank's asset management arm faced legal challenges over similar issues. In the United States, the SEC created a dedicated Climate and ESG Enforcement Task Force, and BNY Mellon paid a 1.5 million dollar fine for misleading ESG claims.

### Green bonds

Green bonds are debt instruments where the proceeds are committed to environmentally beneficial projects. The market has grown to around 2 trillion dollars as of late 2022, and demand continues to outpace supply. For issuers, bonds typically offer cheaper capital than bank loans. For investors, they provide relatively stable returns while supporting climate goals.

Green bonds are not without problems. Standards and accountability have historically been weak, creating conditions for greenwashing. A well-known example from 2015 saw a US college building authority issue a 94 million dollar "green bond" to fund, among other things, a car park. Standardisation is improving, driven in part by organisations like the Climate Bonds Initiative, which certifies bonds against a defined standard.

### Climate tech venture capital

Venture capital has moved significantly into climate technology in recent years. In 2022, climate tech venture capital investment reached 70 billion dollars globally, up 89% from the year before. This covers a wide range of sectors including renewable energy, electric vehicles, sustainable food systems, and climate data and analytics.

Climate tech now accounts for around 14 cents of every venture capital dollar, according to PwC.

However, most of this investment remains concentrated in areas that address less than 20% of the potential emissions reductions available. Hard-to-abate sectors such as heavy industry, shipping, and agriculture receive much less attention despite their large contribution to total emissions.

There is also the question of cryptocurrencies. Bitcoin alone consumed an estimated 121 terawatt-hours of electricity in 2023, more than the total electricity consumption of countries like Hungary, Ireland, and Switzerland combined. A single bitcoin transaction uses roughly the same energy as an average household in the United States consumes over three weeks. Bitcoin accounts for around 0.16% of global greenhouse gas emissions. By contrast, other cryptocurrencies like Ethereum have made dramatic changes to reduce their energy use, cutting consumption by over 99% following a change to their underlying technology.

## What Can You Do With Your Own Money?

Finance is not only a matter for governments and large institutions. Individual choices about where to save, invest, and borrow also have a measurable climate impact.

The most direct and high-impact action most people can take is switching to a greener bank. As the Project Drawdown report on personal banking makes clear, banks use deposits to fund loans across the economy. The average deposit of 1,000 dollars in a carbon-intensive bank generates roughly the same annual greenhouse gas emissions as a return flight from New York to Seattle. Moving from a carbon-intensive bank to a climate-responsible one could reduce the climate impact of personal banking by up to 76%.

For investors, it is worth scrutinising the ESG credentials of any funds held in pension schemes or personal investment accounts. Given the inconsistencies in ESG rating systems, this requires going beyond labels and looking at what companies are actually included in a fund.

Resources such as bank.green, BankTrack, and Banking on Climate Chaos provide independent assessments of banks and their fossil fuel financing. In the UK, Make My Money Matter campaigns actively on the issue and offers practical guidance for individuals.

## Conclusion: Finance Must Work for the Climate

The relationship between finance and climate is not simple. Finance has helped fund real progress on clean energy and climate solutions. It also continues to channel enormous sums into the fossil fuel industry, often with the same institutions doing both at once.

Closing the climate finance gap requires action at every level: governments directing more public funds toward climate and away from fossil fuel subsidies, private investors applying genuine environmental standards rather than marketing labels, regulators enforcing accountability, and individuals making more informed choices about their own money.

The scale of the challenge is real. But so is the scale of the capital available. The question is not whether we have enough money to address climate change. It is whether we have the will to direct it properly.
