# Ariana Mine and Santander: When a $100m Loan Meets Lima's Water Supply

## What is it

Ariana is an underground **copper and zinc mining project** planned for the high Andes of central Peru, in the Marcapomacocha district of Junín region. The site sits directly on top of the water system that supplies **around 60% of the dry-season drinking water for Lima and Callao — a metropolitan area of more than 10 million people**. The Peruvian company **Alpayana** bought the project in 2025 and is trying to move it into operation. In August 2025, **Banco Santander lent Alpayana US$100 million** in a refinancing facility that campaigners say is what makes construction realistic. The mine has not yet started operating, thanks in large part to a constitutional case that Lima residents brought and that has been running for years.

## Why it matters

The Ariana story is a small, sharp example of a much bigger question: **should a European bank finance a mine that threatens the drinking water of 10 million people?** Peru's water utility SEDAPAL has flagged four ways the mine could go wrong: reduced groundwater flows, heavy metals contamination, vibration damage to a decades-old trans-Andean water tunnel that runs a hundred metres from the planned tailings dam, and the risk that the tailings dam itself could fail in an earthquake. Peru has already recorded 18 tailings dam ruptures since 1952. Santander has an [Environmental, Social and Climate Change Risk Management Policy](https://www.banktrack.org/download/environmental_social_climate_change_risk_management_policy) on paper. What happens with Ariana is a test of whether that policy actually binds the bank in the field.

## How the project got here

The mine's Environmental Impact Study was approved in 2016 and it was meant to open in 2019. A group of Lima citizens filed a constitutional appeal that year and the project has been legally frozen ever since. In 2025, after six years of proceedings, the **Constitutional Chamber of Lima ruled that Ariana does pose "a certain and imminent threat" to the fundamental right to drinking water and a suitable environment**. But instead of striking down the EIA, the court ordered a Complementary Environmental Impact Study. In the same year, Alpayana bought the project from Southern Peaks for US$200 million (advised by LXG Capital) — a clear signal that it intends to press ahead. A few months later, Santander stepped in with the US$100 million loan.

## The water system at stake

The **Marcapomacocha water system** is not just a set of lakes. It is a mix of natural high-altitude wetlands — locally known as *bofedales*, essentially Andean peat bogs — combined with lagoons, canals, reservoirs, and the **Cuevas-Milloc Trans-Andean Tunnel** built in the 1960s to carry water from the eastern slopes of the Andes across to the arid Pacific coast where Lima sits. Lima is one of the largest desert cities in the world, and this water system is its lifeline in the dry season. The proposed tailings dam would sit **just 100 metres from the tunnel**, in an active seismic zone. If either failed, the alternative water supply for a metropolitan region of 10+ million people would be limited at best.

## The campaign and the transition-minerals defence

Copper and zinc are on many "critical minerals" lists for the [energy transition](../solutions/energy-transition/santa-marta-fossil-fuel-conference-2026.md) — copper for electricity grids, wiring, EVs and heat pumps, zinc for batteries and galvanising. Alpayana's defenders have framed Ariana in those terms. The Ekō petition targeting Santander's Executive Chairman **Ana Botín** rejects that framing directly: it argues that people using the "metals for green growth" line rarely volunteer their own drinking water for the trade. The campaign asks Santander to use its influence as lender to persuade Alpayana to drop the project, citing precedents where similar public pressure moved banks away from the [Carmichael coal mine in Australia](https://www.stopadani.com/) and the Pebble Mine in Alaska.

## Key facts

- Ariana would sit inside the water system that supplies **around 60% of Lima's dry-season drinking water**
- Peru's water utility **SEDAPAL** flagged four adverse impact pathways: reduced groundwater flows, heavy metals contamination, vibration damage to the Trans-Andean Tunnel, and possible tailings dam collapse just 100 m from the tunnel
- Peru has had **18 tailings dam ruptures since 1952**, mostly due to seismic activity
- **Constitutional Chamber of Lima (2025)**: the project is "a certain and imminent threat" to the right to drinking water; a Complementary EIA is required
- Alpayana bought the project in **March 2025 for US$200 million**; LXG Capital advised
- **Banco Santander** provided a **US$100 million refinancing facility** to Alpayana in **August 2025**
- Ariana is untouched by construction so far — the current window is when public and financial pressure can still matter
