### Global context: Public and private finance is trending upward.

Public finance is capital (money) provided by public institutions. This includes direct finance from governments, as well as through public sector intermediaries, such as [development financial institutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Development_finance_institution) (DFIs). 

Private Finance comes from individuals, businesses, companies, partnerships, and for-profit and non-profit organizations.
Just to make things complicated, government and government-linked organizations, such as pension funds held on behalf of government workers, or sovereign wealth funds, are often managed by private financial institutions, so the distinction between public and private money can be difficult to define precisely.

According to [Climate Policy Initiative](https://www.climatepolicyinitiative.org/publication/global-landscape-of-climate-finance-2021/), public and private finance increased at a rate of about 7 percent in the decade starting in 2011.

Right now, there is about as much public as private finance supporting climate change. Public sector finance often blazes a trail for private sector finance: As public sector finance de-risks an investment, more and more private finance moves in. There are also blended structures: combinations of public and private finance, where public money helps “de-risk” an investment sufficiently for private money to come in (though private philanthropic money also sometimes plays that role.)

### Global context: Fossil fuel finance is still booming.
#fossilfuel  [[FACTS About Fossil Fuel (Oil and Gaz) Industry]]

In the years since the Paris Agreement, the world’s 60 biggest banks have financed fossil fuels to the tune of $3.8 trillion. The nonprofit group [Influence Map](https://influencemap.org/report/Finance-and-Climate-Change-17639) reports that the 30 largest publicly listed financial institutions in the world have collectively enabled more than $740 billion in primary financing to the fossil fuel industry in 2020 and 2021 alone, all are members of industry associations lobbying against climate policy, and, _29 of the 30 have committed to net-zero._ Advocacy group Rainforest Action Network [details](https://www.ran.org/campaign/stop-banks-funding-climate-chaos/) which banks around the world have provided the most financing to the fossil fuel industry, as well as which have committed to phasing out fossil fuel financing. 

It's not only private money that's the problem. The [International Monetary Fund calculated](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2021/oct/06/fossil-fuel-industry-subsidies-of-11m-dollars-a-minute-imf-finds) that total fossil-fuel subsidies provided by governments in 2020 were **$5.9 trillion**, or almost 7 percent of global gross domestic product (GDP), and you can see where this funding went in the graph to the right. 

But it’s not all terrible news. At least 53 countries reformed their fossil-fuel subsidies between 2015 and 2020, [according to the Global Subsidies Initiative](http://www.iisd.org/gsi/subsidy-watch-blog/53-ways-reform-fossil-fuel-consumer-subsidies-and-pricing) (GSI). If you're interested in understanding the nitty-gritty of fossil fuel subsidies, you can read [this paper](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-021-02847-2) in _Nature_.  We dive deeper into fossil fuel financing in the next class on risks. 

**Optional further reading:** As [_The Guardian_ reports](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2022/oct/31/two-thirds-of-us-money-for-fossil-fuel-pours-into-africa-despite-climate-goals), "US funding of fossil fuel energy projects in Africa  ramped up after the Paris agreement." And _Guardian_ [columnist George Monbiot argues](https://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2022/nov/30/amazon-public-money-earth-destruction-fossil-fuels-subsidies) "The more destructive the business, the more likely it is to enjoy political protection... We can protect the living world or we can protect the companies destroying it. ### Debt for Climate Swap
is a very interesting case, one that has allowed Belize and Costa Rica to protect areas and re-forrest, by stoping paying so much debt in US dollars, instead 
==See [here](https://theconversation.com/how-debt-for-climate-swaps-can-help-solve-low-income-countries-crushing-debt-and-environmental-challenges-at-the-same-time-191161#:~:text=Similarly%2C%20Costa%20Rica%20has%20carried%20out%20two%20debt%2Dfor%2Dnature%20swaps%20with%20the%20United%20States.%20Under%20the%20swaps%2C%20Costa%20Rica%20agreed%20to%20allocate%20%2453%20million%20for%20conservation%20projects.%20It%20has%20already%20planted%20more%20than%2060%2C000%20trees%20and%20reversed%20its%20deforestation.)==

Many developing countries face considerable debt—often 40 percent of their gross national income, [_The Conversation_ reports](https://theconversation.com/how-debt-for-climate-swaps-can-help-solve-low-income-countries-crushing-debt-and-environmental-challenges-at-the-same-time-191161), continuing: "For countries in Latin America and the Caribbean, the annual payments just to service that debt [averaged 30% of their total exports](https://openknowledge.worldbank.org/bitstream/handle/10986/36289/9781464818004.pdf)." 

Such high payments crimp developing nations' abilities to mitigate and adapt to climate change, even as they grow more vulnerable to it, thus further driving up their borrowing costs. 

Enter "debt-for-climate swaps." Here's how Climate Policy Initiative explains them:

> _"Debt for Climate (DFC) swaps are a type of debt swap in which the debtor nation, instead of continuing to make external debt payments in a foreign currency, makes payments in local currency to finance climate projects domestically on agreed upon terms. DFC swaps can reduce the level of indebtedness as well as free up fiscal resources to be spent on green investments."_

Similar arrangements have been around for decades in the form of "debt-for-nature swaps." For instance, [Belize](https://theconversation.com/how-debt-for-climate-swaps-can-help-solve-low-income-countries-crushing-debt-and-environmental-challenges-at-the-same-time-191161#:~:text=Belize%2C%20for%20example%2C%20was%20able%20to%20lower%20its%20debt%20in%20exchange%20for%20committing%20to%20designate%2030%25%20of%20its%20marine%20areas%20as%20protected%20areas%20and%20to%20spend%20%24US4%20million%20a%20year%20for%20the%20next%20two%20decades%20on%20marine%20conservation%20under%20a%20complex%20debt%2Dfor%2Dnature%20swap.) was able to shrink its debt in exchange for committing to protect large marine areas and spend millions annually on marine conservation. Other deals in [Costa Rica](https://theconversation.com/how-debt-for-climate-swaps-can-help-solve-low-income-countries-crushing-debt-and-environmental-challenges-at-the-same-time-191161#:~:text=Similarly%2C%20Costa%20Rica%20has%20carried%20out%20two%20debt%2Dfor%2Dnature%20swaps%20with%20the%20United%20States.%20Under%20the%20swaps%2C%20Costa%20Rica%20agreed%20to%20allocate%20%2453%20million%20for%20conservation%20projects.%20It%20has%20already%20planted%20more%20than%2060%2C000%20trees%20and%20reversed%20its%20deforestation.) have helped halt deforestation and get tens of thousands more trees planted.

In theory, the same basic model could drive mitigation actions like building wind farms, or adaptation measures like sea walls.

### Criticisms of global (aid-based) climate finance
Oxfam, the global development and disaster aid NGO, conducted a review of climate finance flows from developed to developing countries in 2020, and produced a [highly critical report](https://www.oxfam.org/en/press-releases/true-value-climate-finance-just-third-reported-developed-countries) of the sector. Among various problems, they report that:  

- Actual delivered climate finance is much lower than what donors are reporting.
- Assistance for climate adaptation especially is too low.
- Climate finance was supposed to be additional to development aid, but instead, the vast majority is replacing development aid.
 
There is also the problem of getting funding to where it’s needed most. [This article](https://ccacoalition.org/en/news/bridging-gap-climate-finance-untapped-potential-investing-short-lived-climate-pollutant) reports that "there is a gap in the ambitions of financial institutions to invest in the most impactful projects and the ability of the countries most vulnerable to climate change to attract available funding."

And there is a mismatch in sectoral allocation. A [study](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/14693062.2019.1605330) in the journal _Climate Policy_ found that "climate finance for sustainable cooling, in particular, represents just 0.04% of total ODA [overseas development assistance], despite cooling projected to represent 13% of global emissions by 2030."

### ESG investing
#esg 
Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) investing is, according to [NerdWallet](https://www.nerdwallet.com/article/investing/esg-investing/), a form of investing "that considers environmental, social and governance factors to judge an investment’s financial returns and its overall impact."

ESG investing often relies on ESG scoring by third-party research groups, who have developed sophisticated rubrics and scoring systems to aggregate company performance on a wide range of topics, from the use of recycled materials in products to board diversity to GHG emissions and labor rights in the supply chain. 

ESG criteria can be applied across the vast investment landscape, from the stock market to private equity to bonds and other financing methods. But the term ESG investing is used most frequently regarding investments in  publicly listed companies (i.e., companies that are listed on a stock market like the NASDAQ or Bombay Stock Exchange, and whose shares can be bought by almost anyone, with no income or credential requirements.) 

ESG investing, especially in public equities,  has grown dramatically over the last 20 years, [including](https://citywire.com/pro-buyer/news/esg-funds-enjoy-another-record-breaking-year-winning-69bn-of-new-money/a2379110) through 2020 and 2021. And investors have proven loyal through the market upheaval of 2022, [according to Morningstar](https://www.morningstar.com/articles/1104758/us-sustainable-funds-see-outflows-for-the-first-time-in-five-years). ESG assets are expected to hit [$165 trillion globally by 2025](https://www2.deloitte.com/us/en/insights/industry/financial-services/esg-investing-and-sustainability.html), or half of professionally managed assets. Europe continues to be the largest player in ESG investing, and sustainability-linked bonds could grow to $15 trillion in the next few years.

### ESG investing challenges: ESG for whom? 

Historically, ESG ratings have focused on how ESG concerns affect the company’s bottom line, and not on the company’s effects on the environment and on society, as discussed in this [article](https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/2021-what-is-esg-investing-msci-ratings-focus-on-corporate-bottom-line/). A [Twitter thread](https://twitter.com/akshatrathi/status/1469224831019192320) by one of that story's reporters puts it this way: "ESG ratings are all about risk to companies and profits, rather than risk to the world."

That same reporter, Akshat Rathi, also notes that many ESG funds remain tied to Russian assets. Amid the war in Ukraine, [he asks](https://twitter.com/AkshatRathi/status/1500495805895131138?s=20&t=wU03fjNXXsyTDKLW4QyxDg), "What are you even doing if your funds committed to environmental, social, governance issues are funding an autocrat's war?" 

He’s not the first to raise this point: some pacifist investors such as Quakers have long shunned US Treasuries, widely considered the safest of safe assets, because of the US’s enormous military budget. Even the US Congress has enacted laws to force investors to divest from companies with links to [Sudan](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-10-742), Iran, Russia, and other countries whose governments’ policies and actions aren’t aligned with international law. 

 [An analysis by _Mint_](https://www.livemint.com/money/personal-finance/esg-investments-are-fast-gaining-traction-in-india-11605111323843.html) puts it well: "there are no uniform standards for ESG yet and fund houses have adopted their own methods to determine which stocks make the ESG cut. Alternatively, you can construct your own ESG portfolio, but this will require you to devote a significant amount of time and energy towards researching these companies."

Many of the factors considered in ESG ratings are subjective and/or [political](https://www.forbes.com/sites/chriscarosa/2022/10/03/are-others-trying-to-convince-you-to-play-politics-with-your-retirement-savings/?sh=1705ee2d4016), as we’ve seen with politicians in [Texas](https://www.npr.org/2022/09/01/1120457153/texas-ban-on-firms-who-dont-invest-in-firearms-and-fossil-fuels-are-cost-taxpaye) and [Florida](https://citywire.com/ria/news/florida-gov-bans-esg-from-state-pension-funds/a2395656) taking aim at investment firms that claim to use ESG criteria. 

### Additional ESG investing challenges

A related challenge is that ESG considerations can mash together such disparate topics as renewable materials in physical goods, employee diversity, and data and privacy risks. So what ESG is supposed to mean concretely can be [contentious](https://time.com/6273398/esg-explained/).

To give one example, in 2022 Tesla was kicked off the S&P ESG index, not for sustainability reasons but because, as an S&P senior director put it in a [blog post,](https://www.indexologyblog.com/2022/05/17/the-rebalancing-act-of-the-sp-500-esg-index/#:~:text=are%20many%20reasons.-,Talking%20Tesla,-First%20and%20foremost) Tesla's score had suffered amid "claims of racial discrimination and poor working conditions" and other investigations. "While Tesla may be playing its part in taking fuel-powered cars off the road, it has fallen behind its peers when examined through a wider ESG lens," she wrote. At the same time, Exxon was allowed to stay on the index, and another oil company, Phillips 66, was added, raising questions about the "sustainability" quotient of the ESG label.

There's also some perception of underperformance: Some ESG funds perform better than non-ESG funds, and some don’t. That’s the nature of investing. Of course, if environmental performance always led to better business performance, all investors would take the environment into account all the time. 

A strict interpretation of some of the tenets of [modern portfolio theory](https://corporatefinanceinstitute.com/resources/wealth-management/modern-portfolio-theory-mpt/) posits that non-financial factors (as ESG factors are often considered) would limit diversification and therefore lead to lower financial returns. Given the investment industry’s focus on financial performance, this perception—though it has been [disproven](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/20430795.2015.1118917) [repeatedly](https://www.stern.nyu.edu/experience-stern/about/departments-centers-initiatives/centers-of-research/center-sustainable-business/research/research-initiatives/esg-and-financial-performance) [over the last decade](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/20430795.2022.2106934)—is still prevalent. 

### Greenwashing
One other ESG challenge is the problem of [greenwashing](https://www.forbes.com/advisor/investing/greenwashing-esg/), "a form of advertising and marketing spin in which green PR and green marketing are deceptively used to persuade the public that an organization's products, aims and policies are environmentally friendly," [per Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenwashing).

It's widespread enough that the E.U. has [passed regulation](https://fortune.com/2021/03/10/eu-esg-sustainability-investing/) in an attempt to stamp it out, and early signs show that [it is working](https://www.fa-mag.com/news/new-esg-fund-numbers-plunge-as-regulators-probe-for-greenwashing-70261.html). Other regulators are also stepping up enforcement: Two of the bank HSBC’s ads were recently [banned](https://www.bbc.com/news/business-63309878) by the UK’s Advertising Standards Authority for being misleading about the firm’s climate change work; DWS in Germany has faced [similar challenges](https://www.bankingdive.com/news/deutsche-banks-dws-sued-over-confusing-esg-claim/634759/). The SEC announced the creation of its new Climate and ESG Enforcement Task Force in March 2021, and [BNY Mellon paid a $1.5M fine](https://www.reuters.com/legal/legalindustry/greenwashing-wave-hits-securities-litigation-2022-09-22/) to the regulator in May 2022. 

Of course, in an ideal world, there shouldn't be a separate activity called ESG investing, because by definition then it means all other investment is going into activities that do not integrate environmental and social factors. Surely all investments should account for ESG!

### Green and sustainability-linked bonds

A green bond is a type of debt security that links finance to projects that claim environmental benefits. Green bonds are reportedly flying off the shelves, with demand exceeding supply. The market has [grown to $2 trillion](https://www.climatebonds.net/resources/reports/5-steps-5trillion-2025) as of October 2022—and has grown to include more loosely defined [sustainability-linked bonds](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2022-09-20/the-booming-esg-bond-that-s-facing-growing-skepticism-quicktake), with blockbuster issuances from Starbucks and Apple.  

Green bonds act as a fixed-term IOU between the borrower (the bond issuer) and their lenders (the bond investors). Investors loan money to issuers by purchasing their bonds. In return, investors receive regular interest payments until the bond "matures" and the issuer repays the principal in full. The interest payments provide relatively safe returns so they typically provide fairly secure sources of revenue in an investment portfolio. 

On the issuer side, bonds typically provide cheaper capital than bank loans. This makes them an attractive option for financing capital-intensive projects like renewable energy or public transport infrastructure.

### Green bonds: Challenges

Green bonds are not without issues. Like much of ESG investing up to now, green bonds are relatively new. Green bonds can suffer from lack of accountability, consistency, and be susceptible to [greenwashing](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2022-10-04/greenwashing-enters-a-22-trillion-debt-market-derailing-climate-goals). For example, in 2015, the Massachusetts State College Building Authority issued a US$94 million bond for an “environmentally sustainable” project on university campuses, [which included the construction of a parking lot](https://www.climatechangenews.com/2015/01/13/green-bond-to-fund-multi-storey-car-park/). 

As of 2020, close to 40 percent of global green bonds were issued by [China](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-39204-8_19) and Chinese companies. The _Financial Times_, which did a series of stories on China Green Finance, [reports](https://www.ft.com/reports/china-green-finance) that "China’s domestic green bond market has been a minefield for global investors, who worry they might inadvertently buy green debt that does not meet international standards. But the growth of the Chinese market in recent years has been too fast for the world’s largest investors to ignore, and many experts say standards are improving." 

The [Climate Bonds Initiative](https://www.climatebonds.net/about) is a nonprofit that acts both as a cheerleader and a watchdog for the industry. It has created and encouraged the adoption of a [climate bond standard](https://www.climatebonds.net/climate-bonds-standard-v3) and developed a certification scheme. It also provides a list of approved verifiers who confirm that an issuance meets CBI’s standard, and tracks the growth of the industry around the world, providing crucial data and analysis for this promising source of financing. 

### Venture capital: the climate tech boom

Venture capital woke up to the importance of climate action en masse relatively recently: Climate tech fundraising and deals boomed in the last few years, especially in 2020 and 2021. It initially focused mostly on mobility and energy solutions but has broadened into climate-focused applications of almost any kind of venture-backable tech, such as data and analytics, food and ag, the built environment, carbon credits, and related financial services. 

A [2021 PWC report](https://www.pwc.com/climatetech) found a sharp uptick in climate tech investment over the year prior: 
##### "Climate tech now accounts for 14 cents of every venture capital dollar," 

the report said, with particularly strong funding in the US and in the area of electric and low-emissions vehicles. [Reports](https://www.svb.com/trends-insights/reports/future-of-climate-tech) show the trend tapering but remaining strong, showing sustained interest from a kind of capital that seeks out fast-growing, transformational businesses. 

Indeed, [an early 2023 report](https://www.holoniq.com/notes/2022-climate-tech-vc-funding-totals-70-1b-up-89-from-37-0b-in-2021) says venture capital for climate tech "had an exceptionally strong 2022 delivering $70.1B of investment, defying gravity in a broader market that has fallen significantly."

### Project Drawdown report
Source: https://drawdown.org/publications/saving-for-the-planet 

As our planet heats up, more and more people are working to reduce their personal climate impact. Few, however, are aware of one of the most impactful actions they can take: assessing and reducing the climate impact of the money they hold in their savings or checking accounts.

Through their lending and investing power, banks play a tremendous role in determining our climate future, including by financing infrastructure that locks in greenhouse gas emissions or climate solutions that yield reductions in emissions for decades to come. Because banks differ in the extent to which they invest consumers’ deposits in carbon-intensive or climate-friendly enterprises, where individuals choose to save can make a huge difference in whether their hard-earned money contributes to or helps mitigate climate change.

**Saving (for) the Planet: The Climate Power of Personal Banking** is a new, comprehensive guide to assessing and reducing the climate impact of consumer banking. Its eye-opening analyses and actionable advice will help you ensure that your dollars help build the future you want for yourself, your loved ones, and our planet: renewable energy, sustainable agriculture, green buildings, public transportation, healthy ecosystems, flourishing communities, and other climate-friendly enterprises.

> [!**Key Takeaways]
> 
> - For the average person in the U.S., personal banking may constitute a large source of indirect greenhouse gas emissions
> - Every US$1,000 a person has in savings is roughly equivalent to the direct emissions generated by flying from New York to Seattle every year
> - Eleven of the largest U.S.-based banks lend around 19.4% on average – and as high as 30% – of their portfolios to carbon-intensive industries
> - Moving from a carbon-intensive bank to a climate-responsible bank could reduce the personal banking emissions of an average person in the U.S. by 76%
> - Switching banks can be a powerful, relatively easy, and affordable climate action
