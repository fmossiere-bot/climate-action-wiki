## Context and Types of Climate Risk

Top risk for the next 10 years according to the World Economic Forum are: 
1. Failure to mitigate Climate Change
2. Failure to adapt to Climate Change
3. Natural disasters and extreme weather events
4. Biodiversity loss and Ecosystem Collapse

There are various types of Climate Risks:
1. Physical risk - destroys hours, affect infrastructures. 
2. Tranistion Risks - risks posed by the financial firms as countries move away from Carbon Intensive infrastructures

## Beyond What Is Counted—the Human Impacts

The toll caused by air pollution from fossil fuels is even higher. [Research](https://www.hsph.harvard.edu/c-change/news/fossil-fuel-air-pollution-responsible-for-1-in-5-deaths-worldwide/) finds that air pollution caused by the burning of fossil fuels was responsible for 8.7m deaths globally in 2018, a shocking one in five of all people who died that year, and more than global Covid-19-related deaths in 2020.

A [2020 study](https://storage.googleapis.com/planet4-southeastasia-stateless/2020/02/21b480fa-toxic-air-report-110220.pdf) by the Center for Research on Energy and Clean Air (CREA) finds that if you put a $ value to the health and human costs of air pollution from burning fossil fuels, calculated in terms of work absences, years of life lost, and premature deaths, it adds up to 3.3 percent of global GDP, or about $8 billion per day.

These costs are primarily borne by children, families, and communities, who face [devastating effects](https://twitter.com/dwallacewells/status/1463517703226937348?s=20) on their health, quality of life, cognitive development, and life expectancy.


## Bank financing the Fossil Fuel
- The [2023 "Banking on Climate Chaos" report](https://www.bankingonclimatechaos.org/) on fossil finance begins this way: "Fossil fuel financing from the world’s 60 largest banks has reached USD $5.5 trillion in the seven years since the adoption of the Paris Agreement, with $673 billion in fossil fuel financing in 2022 alone."
- This comes at a time when "the world cannot afford any fossil fuel expansion: no new oil and gas fields, no new coal mines, no new or expanded oil and gas pipelines, no new LNG terminals, no new coal-fired power plants," the report notes.
- And banks in the United States have been particularly egregious actors, as the chart here shows.
- While leading the charge in some ways, like the development of Aladdin Climate, BlackRock still [holds major fossil fuel companies](https://fossilfreefunds.org/blog/2021/04/20/blackrocks-carbon-transition-fund-packed-big-oil.html).
- The insurance industry [does not seem well prepared](https://climateai.medium.com/how-insurance-is-preparing-for-climate-change-risks-cb4f9e352e10) to deal with climate risks. 
- A [December 2022 Carbon Tracker](https://carbontracker.org/oil-and-gas-companies-invest-in-production-that-will-tip-world-towards-climate-catastrophe/) study found oil and gas companies are still committing billions to projects "that will only be needed if oil and gas demand grows to the point where it pushes global temperatures beyond 2.5°C." 
- A March 2022 study finds that [India's banks are completely unprepared for climate change](https://www.thethirdpole.net/en/climate/india-banks-unprepared-climate-breaking-points/).

## Companies, non-profit organisations helping Climate Risks
The **Financial Stability Board** (FSB) is comprised of G20 finance ministers and central bank governors who work collaboratively to promote international financial stability.

The **Task Force on Climate-Related Financial Disclosures** (TCFD): Created in 2015 by the Financial Stability Board to "develop recommendations for more effective climate-related disclosures that could promote more informed investment, credit, and insurance underwriting decisions and, in turn, enable stakeholders to understand better the concentrations of carbon-related assets in the financial sector and the financial system’s exposures to climate-related risks."

The TCFD is arguably the most important "formal" global initiative around climate risk, seeking to standardize approaches and policies across countries and institutions.

The **Climate Disclosures Standards Board** (CDSB) provides companies and policymakers with the practical tools and technical expertise they need to implement the recommendations of the TCFD. See 

Similar to CDSB, the **Carbon Disclosure Project** (CDP) is a not-for-profit charity that runs the global disclosure system for investors, companies, cities, states, and regions.[[The Carbon Disclosure Project (CPD)]]

The **Sustainability Accounting Standards Board** (SASB) is an independent nonprofit organization that sets standards for disclosure for the subset of environmental, social, and governance (ESG) issues most relevant to financial performance across 77 industries.

And the **Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi)** is a partnership between the aforementioned CDP, World Resources Institute (WRI), World Wide Fund for Nature (WWF) and the United Nations Global Compact, which encourages businesses to adopt sustainable policies. SBTi defines and promotes best practices for lowering emissions, and provides expertise as well as independent assessment of targets.
