## The climate lever hiding in your wallet

Most people who care about climate change have already made some changes to how they live. Fewer flights, less meat, better energy choices at home. These actions matter. But there is one lever that most people have never considered, and it is sitting quietly in their wallet.

It is their bank.

Where you keep your money has a direct and measurable impact on the climate. Not in some abstract, distant way. Your deposits, right now, are most likely being used to finance oil fields, gas pipelines, and other carbon-intensive industries. And the scale of this is larger than most people realise.

> **Did you know?** 
> Changing to a greener bank could be the most impactful action you take. 10,000€ in a "dirty" bank could generate the equivalent of 2.4 tCo2-e a year! 

---

## How banking and climate are connected

Banks do not just hold your money. They use it. Every euro or pound you deposit can be lent out, many times over, to businesses and projects across the economy. The amount a bank can lend is not limited to what you have sitting in your account at any given moment. It is based on the total flow of deposits passing through.

And a significant share of that lending goes to industries driving climate change. On average, eleven of the largest US banks direct around 19% of their lending to carbon-intensive industries. **For some, that figure reaches 30%.**

The numbers translate into real impact at the personal level. According to research by Project Drawdown, every 1,000 euros or dollars held in a carbon-intensive bank generates roughly the same greenhouse gas emissions as a return flight from New York to Seattle, every single year. For most people with a current account and savings, their banking choices produce more indirect emissions than their flights.

---

## It is not just fossil fuels

Banks also finance other forms of environmental destruction that receive less attention. In 2024, Santander provided more than 600 million dollars in financing to companies linked to beef, palm oil, and soya supply chains driving deforestation in South America. Since 2011, the bank co-arranged over 1.3 billion dollars of financing for a single Argentine agricultural company, Cresud, which has been linked to large-scale forest clearance in the Gran Chaco, one of South America's most important and biodiverse ecosystems.

This happened despite Santander having introduced a deforestation limitation policy in 2018 and committing to net-zero emissions in its portfolio by 2050. It is a clear illustration of the gap that often exists between bank commitments and bank actions.

## The impact of switching

The good news is that switching banks is one of the most effective climate actions you can take, and it requires no ongoing effort once done.

> Project Drawdown estimates that moving from a carbon-intensive bank to a climate-responsible one could reduce the climate impact of your personal banking by up to 76%. That is a bigger annual reduction than most people achieve through years of dietary changes or travel cuts.

It is a one-time decision with lasting consequences.

---

## What to look for in a greener bank

Not all banks that claim to be sustainable are equally serious about it. When evaluating your options, a few key questions are worth asking.

On climate commitments:

- Has the bank committed to stop financing fossil fuel expansion?
- Does it provide more financing to climate solutions than to carbon-intensive sectors?
- Does it have a net-zero commitment that includes its financed emissions, not just its own operations?
- Does it publish short-term targets, not just a 2050 goal?
- Does it measure and disclose its full emissions in line with recognised accounting standards?

On practical considerations:

- Does it offer the services you need day to day?
- Are you satisfied with its app and digital tools?
- How is it rated by existing customers?

Resources like [bank.green](https://bank.green/), [BankTrack](https://www.banktrack.org/), and [Banking on Climate Chaos](https://www.bankingonclimatechaos.org/) provide independent assessments of major banks and their financing of fossil fuels and high-risk sectors. In the UK, [Make My Money Matter](https://makemymoneymatter.co.uk/) runs an active campaign on the issue and offers practical guidance.
If you are in France,[ Team for the Planet](https://team-planet.com/fr/bank-for-the-planet) is now partnering with Credit Cooperatif to provide a credit card financing climate innovation, or with Good Vest for green pensions

---

## A small act with a large footprint

Switching banks does not require cutting back on anything. It does not cost money. It does not change your daily routine in any meaningful way. And yet its climate impact, measured over years, can be greater than many of the sacrifices people make in other areas of their lives.

The climate movement spends a great deal of energy encouraging people to fly less or eat differently. Those things matter. But where your money sleeps at night matters too, perhaps more than most people know.
