# Wildfires as a Climate Feedback

## What is it

Wildfires both respond to climate change and accelerate it. As the climate warms, conditions for wildfires — hot, dry, windy weather — become more frequent and more extreme. The fires that result then release carbon that was stored in vegetation and soils, adding more CO₂ to the atmosphere and driving further warming. This is a **climate feedback loop**: warming causes fires, fires cause more warming.

Two threads make this more alarming than it might appear. First, Europe is the world's fastest-warming continent, warming twice as fast as the global average since 1980, and 2025 saw its worst wildfire season on record. Second, new research on Arctic and boreal fires shows that current climate models are almost certainly underestimating wildfire emissions — because those fires are burning not just recent plant growth, but ancient carbon locked in Arctic soils for hundreds to thousands of years.

## Why it matters

Climate models use wildfire emissions as an input to project future warming. If those models assume fires only burn recent organic material, they will underestimate both current emissions and the speed at which permafrost and peatland carbon stores are being destabilised. Getting this right matters enormously for understanding how much time remains to act.

## Europe in 2025: the fastest-warming continent at its limit

Europe suffered unprecedented wildfires and heatwaves in 2025. A three-week heatwave reached **30°C inside the Arctic circle** — a region that would typically see zero to two days of strong heat stress in a year. The same event stunted plant and animal growth and encouraged the spread of invasive species, compressing decades of biodiversity change into a single summer.

In August, climate change made the extreme hot, dry, and windy conditions that triggered wildfires in Portugal and Spain **at least 40 times more likely**. More than 10,000 sq km burned. Fires approached Madrid. The Camino de Santiago pilgrimage route was shut down in sections. Smoke drifted to the UK.

Across Europe, wildfires emitted **47 million tonnes of carbon** in 2025, a record amount. Spain, the UK, the Netherlands, Germany, and Cyprus all broke their previous fire-emission records. Soils were the driest in 33 years of observations; more than a third of Europe suffered extreme agricultural drought.

The pattern that drove the worst blazes was a volatile weather whiplash: a wet spring that boosted vegetation growth, followed by record summer heat that dried it out, creating a tinderbox. Scientists warn this pattern will become more common.

## The Arctic: burning carbon that is thousands of years old

Arctic and boreal soils are not just grassland or forest floor. In cold conditions, organic matter accumulates in the soil rather than decomposing, building up over centuries and millennia into deep layers of **peat**. These soils have been acting as a net carbon sink, slowly removing CO₂ from the atmosphere over geological timescales.

When a wildfire burns across the surface, it does not just consume the living vegetation. In many places, the rapid burning of surface plants triggers a much **slower smouldering of old organic matter deep in the soil** — releasing carbon that has been locked away for hundreds to thousands of years, along with black carbon (soot).

Research by Meri Ruppel of the Finnish Meteorological Institute, using soil cores from fire-affected Arctic and boreal regions, confirmed this across multiple environments:

- In Canada's Northwest Territories, fires are burning several centimetres into the soil, releasing carbon stored up to **400 years ago**.
- In Greenland, fires burn 10 cm down on average, releasing carbon **1,000 years old**; some burns reach 15 cm.
- In Quebec's boreal forest, fires in some locations have released carbon from **5,000 years ago**.

Current climate models do not account for this ancient carbon release. They assume fires burn only recent plant growth. This means **wildfire emissions are being systematically underestimated** in the very models used to project future warming and inform climate policy.

## Black carbon: the hidden double effect

Wildfires also release **black carbon** — fine soot particles. Black carbon has two warming effects. In the atmosphere, it absorbs solar heat directly, warming the air. When it settles on snow or ice, it darkens the surface and reduces its reflectivity (albedo), causing ice to melt faster than it otherwise would. In high-latitude regions already losing ice rapidly, this is a significant additional forcing.

## Summary findings

- Europe is warming twice as fast as the global average; 2025 was the hottest year on record in the UK, Iceland, and Norway.
- European wildfires emitted 47 million tonnes of carbon in 2025, a record; five countries broke their all-time fire-emission records.
- Climate change made the August 2025 Iberian wildfires at least 40 times more likely.
- Arctic and boreal wildfires are burning carbon up to 5,000 years old, trapped in peat soils.
- Climate models assume only recent plant growth burns — this means they underestimate actual wildfire emissions.
- Black carbon from wildfires accelerates ice and snow melt at high latitudes.
- The ocean around Europe set a new annual sea surface temperature record for the fourth consecutive year; 86% of European seas suffered marine heatwaves.
