# AMOC Collapse and Carbon Release

## What is it

The **Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC)** is a vast system of ocean currents, including the Gulf Stream, that acts like a giant conveyor belt. It carries warm, salty water from the Gulf of Mexico northward toward Europe, then cools, sinks, and returns southward along the ocean floor. Without it, European winters would be far colder, monsoons across Africa and Asia would shift, and sea levels along the US East Coast would rise faster.

Human-caused warming is already slowing the AMOC. Fresh meltwater from the Greenland ice sheet is diluting the salty surface water that drives the current's sinking mechanism. The AMOC has already declined by around 15%, and new computer modelling published in 2026 in *Nature Communications Earth & Environment* adds a further alarming dimension: if the AMOC collapses, it could trigger a chain reaction in the Southern Ocean that releases up to **640 billion tonnes of CO₂** into the atmosphere — heating the planet by an additional 0.2°C on top of all other collapse impacts.

## Why it matters

The AMOC is one of the most watched potential climate tipping points. Its collapse would be a global-scale event with cascading effects, not a regional concern. The new finding from Potsdam Institute researchers Da Nian and Johan Rockström makes the stakes higher still: collapse would not only disrupt climate patterns, it would act as a slow-burning carbon bomb, releasing CO₂ from deep ocean waters over a thousand years.

The window to prevent this is narrowing. Current atmospheric CO₂ is around 430 parts per million. The study found that above 350 ppm, AMOC collapse becomes irreversible once triggered. We are already past that threshold.

## The Southern Ocean carbon feedback

The deep waters of the Southern Ocean store enormous quantities of carbon, absorbed from the atmosphere and from the sinking of dead plankton over millennia. Normally, a layer of fresher surface water keeps this deep, carbon-rich water trapped below.

When the AMOC runs, it sends less-salty water southward from the northern Atlantic, keeping that layering stable. If the AMOC shuts down, that stabilising effect disappears. The Southern Ocean convection strengthens, deep water reaches the surface, and much of its stored carbon escapes to the atmosphere.

The study modelled this across different future climate scenarios and found that the feedback would unfold over more than 1,000 years — but the decision that locks it in could happen within the next 25 to 50 years.

## Other AMOC collapse impacts

Beyond the carbon feedback, AMOC collapse would:

- Cool the Arctic by around 7°C, freezing large areas of Canada, Scandinavia, and Russia.
- Warm Antarctica by around 6°C, threatening the stability of the East Antarctic Ice Sheet — the largest ice mass on Earth, whose collapse could raise global sea levels by dozens of metres over centuries.
- Disrupt monsoon systems across Africa and Asia, threatening food and water security for hundreds of millions of people.
- Raise sea levels along the US East Coast faster than the global average.

## Summary findings

- AMOC has already declined by ~15% due to Greenland meltwater diluting the driving mechanism.
- Collapse is projected anywhere from decades to centuries away; exact timing remains uncertain.
- At CO₂ concentrations above 350 ppm (current: 430 ppm), collapse appears irreversible.
- New 2026 modelling: collapse could release up to 640 billion tonnes of CO₂ from the Southern Ocean.
- Additional warming from this carbon release: 0.2°C, playing out over 1,000+ years.
- AMOC collapse would cool the Arctic by 7°C and warm Antarctica by 6°C simultaneously.
- Potential domino effect: East Antarctic Ice Sheet destabilised, causing metres of sea level rise.
- Rockström: "The commitment time may be within the next 25 to 50 years. It's literally now."

## AMOC Monitoring Under Threat

A 2026 Guardian commentary by oceanographers Penny Holliday, Femke de Jong, and Sjoerd Groeskamp warns that the world's ability to track AMOC could be lost just as the stakes are rising. In a collapse scenario, Europe could experience climate change **up to 10 times faster** than the current rate of change.

Systematic AMOC monitoring only began around two decades ago, stitched together from individually funded national research projects. That fragile foundation is now under acute pressure. The Trump administration has proposed budget cuts to NASA, NOAA, and the National Science Foundation — agencies that together provide around 50% of total AMOC monitoring funding. The US also moved to descope the Ocean Observatories Initiative, which formed part of the tracking network.

The European Commission launched the **OceanEye** initiative in 2026, committing €50 million to ocean observation. But OceanEye is not yet operational, and the research vessels servicing current monitoring need bridging funding right now.

The total cost of all AMOC monitoring globally is roughly **€25 million per year** — about five cents per EU citizen. The authors urge the EU, the UK, and international partners to treat AMOC monitoring as an urgent global priority before continuity is broken and the data record is permanently lost.
