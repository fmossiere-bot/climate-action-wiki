Power (W) = Energy (Wh) / Time (h)
In 4 h, a 7W light bulb consumes 7x4= 28Wh of energy

Energy generated for various type of fule can be found easily
1. **Energy:** capacity to do work. Units: joules, KWh, Btu, calories
2. **Energy content of fuels:** energy per mass or volume of fuel
3. **Carbon intensity of fuels:** CO2 per unit of energy of various fuels
4. **Power:** energy over time (i.e., the rate of energy use). Units: Watts, hp, Btu/hour
5. Much of energy analysis is **unit conversions.**

## Load Curve & Load Duration Curve

> **Load curve** = Graphical Representation of actual demand for power, ina chronological time sequence by hours


![[Screenshot 2024-10-31 at 14.32.13.png]]

If you sum up all those individual demands, you end up with the demand per day, in GWh. Intuitive since we know energy = power x Time

- Load curve can vary by country, and by season

> [!NOTE] Solar Power turns load curves into ducks
> When solar generation is at its highest in the middle of the day, it reduces the net load on systems, which climbs again around sundown. 
> ![[Screenshot 2024-10-31 at 14.36.07.png]]


> **Load Duration Curve(LDC):** 
> A load duration curve (LDC) is another graph used heavily in electricity sector planning for traditional power systems. The LDC is similar to a load curve but the demand data is ordered in descending order of magnitude, rather than chronologically

> [!NOTE]  Capacity planning for power systems with high levels of variable resources
> Regulators, utilities, policymakers, and others need a way to plan out investment decisions to minimize costs to consumers, while ensuring reliable electricity, all in the context of shifting rapidly to a grid powered entirely by renewables. To achieve this type of planning, a new generation of capacity planning models are now available. 
> 
> These models underpin [over a thousand studies](https://twitter.com/ChristianOnRE/status/1686180508529512449?s=20) that demonstrate how you can operate a grid economically and reliably with very high levels of renewable energy.

## Economics: The cost of producing electricity
There are five types of cost associated with building and running any power generating facility:

1. **Capital costs**
    These are _fixed costs_, though they depend on location and context. They include all the costs of building a facility, including purchasing or leasing the land, materials and labor costs, as well as "regulatory costs," like getting siting permits, environmental approvals, etc.  
2. **_Fixed_ operation and management costs**  
    These are the costs to keep the plant running, irrespective of whether it's generating a lot of electricity, a little, or none at all. They include, for example, the costs of labor that needs to always be present, and some types of routine maintenance.  
3. **_Variable_ operation and management costs**  
    These costs scale according to the amount of power being produced. They are higher if the plant is producing a lot of electricity, and lower if the plant is not running that often. They can include labor, customer service, maintenance and replacement costs. They do not include fuel costs, but do include things like wastewater management and disposal, chemicals and lubricants, spare parts, and other similar materials costs.  
4. **Fuel costs**  
    These include the cost of the fuel source for the power plant. They are also a type of _variable_ cost as they scale with power production for conventional fossil-fuel power generators.  
5. **Transmission costs**  
    These are very context specific. The addition of a new power generating facility can sometimes require upgrades to the existing transmission system. Distributed renewable energy sources can also add new types of costs to transmission, as the traditional system was designed to only handle power flows in one direction—from power plants to consumers. Handling two-way flow of electricity—from, say, hundreds of grid-connected rooftop PV systems on homes to offices and factories in the middle of the day—requires the use of new power electronics, and some transmission and distribution upgrades.

###### There are a lot of variables

> To get around this problem, we combine all these different costs into a single metric called the **levelized cost of energy** or the **levelized cost of electricity (LCOE)**. The LCOE is any given power generation facility's cost to generate a single unit of electricity averaged over the lifetime of the plant.

When you see cost values for different electricity generating technologies in the media or elsewhere represented in terms of $/kWh or INR/MWh (or any currency / unit of electricity), these are almost always LCOE costs.
