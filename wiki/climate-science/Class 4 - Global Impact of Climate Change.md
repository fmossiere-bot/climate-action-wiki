One reason why impacts do not track linearly with temperature relates to tipping points and carbon feedback loops, which we'll discuss later in this class. Notably, the potential for abrupt shifts increases significantly as temperatures rise, per this [UNFCCC thread](https://x.com/UNFCCC/status/1413147508566495236) ([archived PDF here](https://drive.google.com/file/d/13qR_6_kpWr2RmhCFJ_xsAydYmmNr7jxL/view?usp=drive_link)).

For example, a [2023 study](https://iccinet.org/statecryo23/) on the state of the cryosphere (the frozen water part of the earth) finds that “2°C will result in extensive, potentially rapid, irreversible sea-level rise from Earth’s ice sheets,” and other, related irreversible and devastating impacts.  We discuss this further in the optional module in this class titled Global Impacts Today.  

Zooming back out, while atmospheric carbon levels may increase linearly over time, there may be points where the consequences suddenly become much more drastic, leading to relatively faster rates of sea level rise, and processes that release even more carbon, like wildfires and perhaps melting permafrost. The danger of this occurring is higher above 1.5 C. There are, of course, uncertainties around all these risks—they could be worse or less intense than we are currently able to predict. 

  
As we noted earlier, the world is now at least 1.1 C warmer, _on average,_ above the pre-industrial average, with 2023 crossing 1.4 C of warming.

### Why did we change from an initial pledge of 2C to now a pledge of 1.5c? 
Some island nations felt that 2 C was a "death sentence" for their countries due to projections that sea level rise would put them underwater. The image here is of Olhuvelifushi Island, Maldives, and gives you a sense of how threatened such nations are by sea level rise. 

This political momentum in turn [influenced a new focus](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1462901119315588) on 1.5 C from the scientific community, leading to the research you are reading about today, and to the goal enshrined in the landmark 2015 global climate agreement, the Paris Accord. But whether 1.5 C is a realistic goal anymore has since become a matter of increasing doubt. Later in this class, and in our Politics class, we discuss the likelihood of staying within 1.5 C of average warming over pre-industrial temperatures. 

For now, a key takeaway is that 1.5C is not a “hard” threshold so much as a sobering approximation of a threshold we should not cross. But every tenth of a degree counts. “There is nothing magical about the 1.5 number, other than that is an agreed aspirational target. Keeping at 1.4 is better than 1.5, and 1.3 is better than 1.4, and so on,” [says MIT research scientist Sergey Paltsev](https://news.mit.edu/2023/explained-climate-benchmark-rising-temperatures-0827#:~:text=There%20is%20nothing%20magical%20about%20the%201.5%20number%2C%20other%20than%20that%20is%20an%20agreed%20aspirational%20target.%20Keeping%20at%201.4%20is%20better%20than%201.5%2C%20and%201.3%20is%20better%20than%201.4%2C%20and%20so%20on).

### When will warming stop? 
Warming is likely to stop once CO2 emissions reach zero, so we have the power, and this is something that was debated for a long time actually.
See full explanation here in the Carbon Brief article:[ Will global warming stop as soon as net-zero emissions are reached]()

In other modules, we discuss how carbon dioxide can persist for a long time in the atmosphere, potentially contributing to warming for centuries. So even if we stop emitting more carbon, will the world keep getting hotter?

Fortunately, no. While there was some scientific debate about this even in the last few decades, the answer now appears to be, as _[Carbon Brief](https://www.carbonbrief.org/explainer-will-global-warming-stop-as-soon-as-net-zero-emissions-are-reached)_ puts it, that "warming is likely to more or less stop once carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions reach zero, meaning humans have the power to choose their climate future." 

Here, let’s differentiate between two concepts:

- stabilizing CO2 concentrations at a given level
- reducing CO2 emissions to zero or net-zero

First recall the difference between _concentrations_ of CO2 in the atmosphere, which is a measure of the total amount of CO2 up there at any given time, and _emissions_, which measure of how much  CO2 we are _adding_ to the atmosphere each year.    

If we—our cars, planes, factories, power plants, and so on—can stop emitting carbon altogether, atmospheric concentrations of CO2 will quickly _drop_, and will stabilize at a lower level., thanks to our Carbon Sinks.  As the _Carbon Brief_ piece above explains in detail, this would stop _further_ warming. The world might be hot, but it would stop getting hotter.

If, on the other hand, we just keep CO2  concentrations from getting higher—so they flatten around the current level of roughly 420 parts per million—warming will continue. In this scenario, we’d continue emitting carbon at around 30 percent of current levels, and over the next few centuries, temperatures would climb another 0.5 C or so.

This underscores the significance of reducing CO2 emissions, if not to “absolute” zero, then to a point where what few emissions continue can be balanced out by additional carbon removal efforts, so no net CO2 is added to the atmosphere. This concept is known as “net-zero,” and we discuss it much more elsewhere.

As illustrated here, some amount of sea-level rise will continue well after we hit net-zero, though how much and for how long depends largely on us. Indeed, the takeaway is the _faster_ we get to net-zero _emissions_—not concentrations—the less damage will be already baked in.

This is very important to remember and is a point we will return to in later modules: **Actions that prioritize emissions reductions** **_today_** **are more impactful than reducing emissions tomorrow or a year or ten years from now.**

While much of the focus of climate mitigation efforts is on CO2, [human emissions of other GHGs and aerosols](https://www.carbonbrief.org/analysis-why-scientists-think-100-of-global-warming-is-due-to-humans) also have a large impact on global surface temperatures. And whereas global temperatures will stabilise once CO2 emissions fall to zero, the same is not true for zero GHG or aerosol emissions.

### Sea-level rise is accelerating.

From 2021 to 2022, sea levels rose by 0.27 centimeters, or 0.11 inches, according to [satellite data and NASA](https://sealevel.nasa.gov/news/259/nasa-uses-30-year-satellite-record-to-track-and-project-rising-seas/): "That’s the equivalent of adding water from a million Olympic-size swimming pools to the ocean every day for a year and is part of a multidecade trend of rising seas."

Over the last three decades, NASA says sea levels are up an average of 9.1 centimeters (3.6 inches). And the annual rate of expected sea level rise has roughly doubled in that period.

Importantly, even if we halt all global carbon emissions at this very moment, some amount of sea level rise is now "locked in" for many years to come, due to a combination of melting ice and thermal expansion, as the ocean slowly catches up to the changes more readily observed in our atmosphere: 

"As the heat sinks into the deep ocean, it takes centuries to be moved around and for a new equilibrium to be reached. Ice sheets also have a response time, so that if you change the thermometer tomorrow, it can take hundreds to thousands of years to reach an equilibrium," British physicist Jonathan Bamber [tells the](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2023/jun/26/its-absolutely-guaranteed-the-best-and-worst-case-scenarios-for-sea-level-rise#:~:text=%E2%80%9CThe%20atmosphere%20changes%20quite%20rapidly%20but%20deep%20ocean%20circulation%20takes%20centuries%2C%E2%80%9D%20says%20Prof%20Jonathan%20Bamber%2C%20director%20of%20the%20Bristol%20Glaciology%20Centre%20at%20the%20University%20of%20Bristol.) _[Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2023/jun/26/its-absolutely-guaranteed-the-best-and-worst-case-scenarios-for-sea-level-rise#:~:text=%E2%80%9CThe%20atmosphere%20changes%20quite%20rapidly%20but%20deep%20ocean%20circulation%20takes%20centuries%2C%E2%80%9D%20says%20Prof%20Jonathan%20Bamber%2C%20director%20of%20the%20Bristol%20Glaciology%20Centre%20at%20the%20University%20of%20Bristol.)._ "Taken together, you’re talking multiple centuries to reach an equilibrium with the new temperature we’ve established." (Other research [supports this finding](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2023/oct/23/rapid-ice-melt-in-west-antarctica-now-inevitable-research-shows).)

### Climate change could also slow ocean currents—with dire results for weather.

Ocean currents in the Atlantic have slowed considerably in recent decades, and it's [possible](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2021/feb/25/atlantic-ocean-circulation-at-weakest-in-a-millennium-say-scientists) that increasing rainfall and melting ice from Greenland [could](https://www.pik-potsdam.de/en/news/latest-news/gulf-stream-system-at-its-weakest-in-over-a-millennium) make this trend much worse. (That said, other research [questions](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41558-022-01342-4) if we can attribute this to climate change.)

At issue is the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation, also called AMOC. This system carries warm water from the equator to the North Atlantic, where water cools and gets denser before flowing back south. But with more fresh water flowing in, the water there becomes less salty and dense, meaning it won't flow back south as fast.

If this trend worsens, it could reach a "tipping point"—an idea we detail in another module—unleashing widespread and dangerous disruptions to weather patterns. The IPCC's [2023  Synthesis Report](https://www.ipcc.ch/report/ar6/syr/downloads/report/IPCC_AR6_SYR_SPM.pdf) puts it this way: 

![[Screenshot 2024-02-08 at 11.57.46.png]]

"There is medium confidence that the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation will not collapse abruptly before 2100, but if it were to occur, it would very likely cause abrupt shifts in regional weather patterns, and large impacts on ecosystems and human activities." Past IPCC reports have suggested those effects could include drying in Europe and changes to regional monsoon patterns.

As always in science, there are uncertainties. The German oceanographer and climatologist Stefan Rahmstorf [wrote in July 2023](https://www.realclimate.org/index.php/archives/2023/07/what-is-happening-in-the-atlantic-ocean-to-the-amoc/#:~:text=even%20rather%20worrying%20for%20the%20next%20few%20decades) that the risk of a dramatic shift in the system is "far greater" than 10 percent this century, and "even rather worrying for the next few decades." And [other scientists question](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsta.2022.0193) how well we can trust any of these projections.

### Shared socio-economic pathways: visions of the future

What emissions trajectory—and what temperature we end up at globally—depends on many factors. How might populations and behaviors change over the next few decades? How might economic growth evolve globally? How fast might we decarbonize our economy?

There are thousands of plausible ways these and other socio-economic factors may come together to influence emissions. Climate scientists, economists, and energy systems modelers collaborated on five such scenarios of how global society, demographics, and economics could plausibly change over the next century. These are collectively known as the “[Shared Socioeconomic Pathways](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0959378016300681)” (SSPs).

These SSPs then became important inputs to a class of climate models called **Integrated Assessment Models** or IAMs, to help us figure out how different climate goals can be met. These IAMs form the basis of the IPCC 2023 Synthesis Reports and provide the scientific justifications for the global climate agreement called the Paris Accord, which we discuss more in a later class.

It simply captures the fact that different societal choices, demographic and economic futures, and decarbonization choices influence emissions in different ways, which then influence the climate in different ways, which drives impacts and risks.

The only SSPs that keep warming to 1.5 C by 2100 call for deep emissions cuts year after year. Barring a drastic and unexpected change of course, this seems out of reach. Even the most optimistic scenarios considered in a report by the United Nations Environment Programme ([PDF here](http://wedocs.unep.org/bitstream/handle/20.500.11822/43923/EGR2023_ESEN.pdf)) put the likelihood of achieving this goal at 14 percent.

Our next best hope is something known as "1.5C with overshoot," where we temporarily exceed the 1.5 C mark, but manage to achieve the emissions cuts needed to stabilize temperatures slightly above 1.5 C. We then bring temperatures back _down_ with by sucking massive amounts of carbon dioxide out of the air and storing it permanently. (This path appears difficult and may not be realistic; we discuss it more in our Carbon Removal class.)

### What are tipping points?
We started this class with a video about thawing permafrost in the far north. Thawing permafrost is a source of serious concern because it creates carbon cycle feedbacks (more on these in a minute) and risks bringing the climate system to a tipping point. It creates Carbon Feedback, and risks bringing the Climate System to a Tipping Point

> A tipping point is a threshold beyond which a tiny change could push a system into a completely new state, causing dramatic, irreversible, and typically catastrophic change. Note that tipping points are not a scientifically measurable concept as such. Rather, they provide a conceptual framework for understanding sources of potentially catastrophic risk—and uncertainty—in our climate system.

 Carbon Brief has put together these [9 tipping points](https://www.carbonbrief.org/explainer-nine-tipping-points-that-could-be-triggered-by-climate-change/), illustrated below

![[New Note.png]]

So far, as the Earth has warmed, its systems have responded "proportionate to the rate of recent temperature change," IPCC authors explain in their AR6 summary ([PDF](http://www.ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg1/downloads/report/IPCC_AR6_WGI_SPM_final.pdf)). But that may not remain the case if one or more of these systems reaches a tipping point: "a critical threshold beyond which a system reorganises, often abruptly and/or irreversibly." Such drastic shifts have happened before, the IPCC notes—for instance, at the end of ice ages. And other such changes may be in store.

Indeed, the higher we let temperatures go, the greater our risk of triggering one or more tipping points. A [report](https://global-tipping-points.org/summary-report/key-messages/) published in late 2023 says that these risks "pose some of the gravest threats faced by humanity. Their triggering will severely damage our planet’s life-support systems and threaten the stability of our societies."

A detailed study of each such threshold is beyond the scope of this close, but five of them "are already at risk of being crossed due to warming right now," the report says, and three more will become threats in the next few years as the world inches ever closer to 1.5 C. Huge amounts of cropland could be disrupted, along with some governments and economic systems.

The danger and uncertainty here heavily underscore the importance of halting global warming as quickly as possible.

> [!NOTE] How important is the Antartic Ice Sheet? 
> The **Antarctic Ice Sheet** is one of Earth’s most vital and powerful climate regulators. It holds **nearly 70% of the planet’s freshwater** and covers about **14 million square kilometers**, making it the largest single mass of ice on Earth. Understanding its role — and the consequences of its melting — is essential for assessing climate risks, biodiversity impacts, and long-term planetary stability.
> - **Regulating Sea Level**
> 	- The ice sheet stores the majority of global freshwater.
> 	- If completely melted, it would raise **global sea levels by about 58 meters** — though even small losses already contribute measurably to rising seas.
> 	- Currently, Antarctica contributes roughly **0.4 millimeters per year** to sea-level rise, and this rate is accelerating.
> - **Climate Regulation & Albedo Effect**
> 	- The bright, white surface of the ice sheet reflects **up to 80% of incoming solar radiation** back into space — a process known as the **albedo effect**.
> 	- As the ice melts, darker ocean water and exposed rock absorb more heat, amplifying **global warming** in a **positive feedback loop**.
> - **Ocean Circulation & Deep Water Formation**
> 	- Antarctic waters are central to the **global thermohaline circulation** (“the ocean conveyor belt”).
> 	- Cold, dense water formed around Antarctica sinks to the ocean depths, driving global nutrient and heat distribution.
> 	- Melting ice dilutes this water with freshwater, reducing salinity and potentially **disrupting global ocean currents and deep-water upwelling**, which in turn affects **marine biodiversity and carbon cycling**.
> - **Carbon Storage & Ecosystem Stability**
> - The Southern Ocean around Antarctica absorbs **around 40% of the ocean’s CO₂ uptake**, making it a crucial **carbon sink**.
> - ### **Ecological Importance**
> 	- The **Antarctic ecosystem** — from krill to whales — depends on the **seasonal formation and melting of sea ice**, which provides habitat and influences nutrient cycles.
> 	- Loss of ice cover disrupts **food webs**, particularly for species like **Antarctic krill**, a foundation species for penguins, seals, and whales.
> 	- Changing ocean temperatures and ice retreat also open new zones for **invasive species** and **fishing pressures**, threatening biodiversity resilience

### Carbon cycle feedbacks

#Permafrost 
Our chances of exceeding tipping points are connected to carbon cycle feedbacks. The carbon cycle, as you know, is comprised of the exchanges of carbon between the atmosphere, land, ocean, and the organisms they contain. “Feedbacks” refer to the changes that could occur in these exchanges themselves as a result of warming temperatures and increasing CO2 levels.

![[Screenshot 2024-02-08 at 13.23.49.png]]

Carbon cycle feedbacks can be negative or positive. A negative feedback could be the fertilization effect of increased CO2, where we see more vegetation growth, which absorbs more CO2 from the atmosphere. A positive feedback is of the kind featured in the opening video of this class—warming releases methane from the permafrost which then accelerates further warming.

> Carbon cycle feedbacks and tipping points are intimately connected. Positive feedbacks increase our chances of hitting a tipping point, which in turn accelerates further positive feedback.

> “The exact threshold at which carbon-cycle feedbacks become significant remains uncertain and we really don’t know how close we are to reaching it,” [writes](https://www.theclimatebrink.com/p/a-truly-worst-case-climate-scenario) climate scientist Andrew Dessler.
