## Extreme Events Attribution
[LINK](https://textbook.terra.do/class/m-local-impacts-2?r=https://app.terra.do/app/learning/86/my-schedule#m-local-impacts-2)

> Climate scientist can not say with certainty that an event was "caused" by climate change, but what they can do is model different versions of the events with or without the effect of climate change. 

Dr. Friederike Otto is a pioneer and expert in extreme event attribution (Imperial college London)
- [ ] Reference: https://www.worldweatherattribution.org/about/

> For example, in July 2021, just 11 days after temperatures in Pacific northwest areas of the U.S. and Canada reached high above 40ºC, shattering records by as much as 5ºC, [a WWA study](https://www.worldweatherattribution.org/without-human-caused-climate-change-temperatures-of-40c-in-the-uk-would-have-been-extremely-unlikely/) found that climate change made the heatwave at least 150 times more likely and 2ºC more intense.

- [ ] [Video][ how does climate change impact the weather](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=CNEj45UwWsY&ab_channel=UniversityofOxford)


There are limitations though:
- Models can be very conservative. In a study that found a 2022 heatwave in the UK would've been extremely unlikely in a world 1.2 C cooler, [Otto and others caution](https://www.worldweatherattribution.org/without-human-caused-climate-change-temperatures-of-40c-in-the-uk-would-have-been-extremely-unlikely/#:~:text=all%20models%20systematically%20underestimate%20the%20observed%20trends.%20The%20combined%20results%20are%20thus%20almost%20certainly%20too%20conservative) "all models systematically underestimate the observed trends," making their results "almost certainly too conservative."
- Extreme events are also, by their nature, rare at any given location. This makes it difficult to discern a climate change induced "signal" amid the complex "noise" of natural variability.
- Causes are complex, so extreme events are hard to simulate
- There are also some statistical issues that arise in modelling extreme events. Models, for example, may be too "Gaussian," meaning the range they suggest may be too close to a symmetrical bell curve and fail to show as many extreme events as we see in real life
- Finally, there is an injustice built into extreme event attribution: [Current research is heavily biased toward events that affect the Global North](http://journals.ametsoc.org/view/journals/bams/101/10/bamsD190317.xml). A relative lack of historical observations in the Global South also hinders our capacity to evaluate models against the

## Unpacking Climate Disasters

> It's important to note that disasters are never just a result of weather or climate events alone. 

###### *"Disasters are produced when a vulnerable population is exposed to a particular weather or climate event"*

> [! A disaster consists of:]
> - A weather event
> - A vulnerable population
> - Level of exposure
> 
> ![[Screenshot 2024-02-12 at 17.58.16.png]]


> While we see increase in the number of extreme events, there is a decline in overall deaths

But this sort of trend highlights why it is equally important to understand _underlying vulnerabilities_ and _exposure_ to extreme weather events. Indeed, there is a downside to blaming all disasters on climate change alone. [Researchers have found](https://wires.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/wcc.750) that “describing climate and weather as the cause of disasters can be misleading, since disasters are caused by pre-existing fragilities and inequalities on the ground….Thus, while politicians may want to blame crises on climate change, members of the public may prefer to hold government accountable for inadequate investments in flood or drought prevention and precarious living conditions.”

#### What happened in Pakistan? 
Dr. Otto and her team have also [highlighted the role of underlying vulnerabilities](https://www.worldweatherattribution.org/climate-change-likely-increased-extreme-monsoon-rainfall-flooding-highly-vulnerable-communities-in-pakistan/) in the case of the 2022 extreme flooding event in Pakistan that affected over 33 million people, even while they trace the fingerprint of climate change on the rain event. 

> “The devastating impacts,” they report, “were also driven by the proximity of human settlements, infrastructure (homes, buildings, bridges), and agricultural land to flood plains, inadequate infrastructure, limited ex-ante risk reduction capacity, an outdated river management system, underlying vulnerabilities driven by high poverty rates and socioeconomic factors (e.g. gender, age, income, and education), and ongoing political and economic instability.”

## Implication of the Local Impact of Climate Change
#### More Disasters: The Cumulative Impacts of Many Local Events

In May of 2022, temperatures in Jacobabad, Pakistan hit a brutal 50 C, not long before devastating flooding left parts of the city underwater. ["From furnace to flood," one headline declared.](https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/furnace-flood-worlds-hottest-city-pakistan-now-under-water-2022-08-31/)

This situation is increasingly the reality of climate-fueled disasters worldwide: They're coming thick and fast, together and one on the heels of another. We've already seen disaster response services like fire departments in California and Australia and flood response in India completely overwhelmed in the face of large or multiple disasters.

And it's also not just back-to-back disasters in one place, but a relentless assault of disasters around the world in multiple locations: "We are living through a 'summer of disasters' in which extreme weather is becoming mere wallpaper," [the _Uninhabitable Earth_ author David Wallace-Wells wrote on Twitter](https://twitter.com/dwallacewells/status/1555596804997238785?s=20&t=nE9gIRWBTz5-QnYbzFRv8w).

What does this all mean for our collective future?

#### Food Security: Implications of Worsening Weather (Almost) Everywhere for Food Security

It is beyond the scope of this class to fully unpack the implications of climate change for food security. We will return to this topic in our class on adaptation and resilience to climate change, where you can explore a case study on the topic. 

> It’s worth highlighting some trends and dimensions of climate change and food security, as it’s high on many people’s minds. Many news stories call our attention to the devastating impacts of climate change-fueled weather events—from a ["historic megadrought" in the U.S.](https://www.nbcnews.com/science/environment/us-megadrought-worst-least-1200-years-researchers-say-rcna16202) to a ["worst-ever crop harvest in 2022" in China](https://www.spglobal.com/commodityinsights/en/market-insights/blogs/agriculture/060722-china-climate-change-food-security#:~:text=China%27s%20agricultural%20ministry%20conceded%20in%20March%20that%20the%20country%20is%20staring%20at%20the%20worst%2Dever%20crop%20harvest%20in%202022%20on%20the%20back%20of%20record%20floods%20and%20other%20natural%20catastrophes%20last%20year.) to [French and Italian farmers beset by hailstorms and drought](https://www.reuters.com/markets/commodities/french-italian-farmers-count-cost-storms-drought-2022-06-06/) to [record high food prices in Somalia](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2022/6/6/famine-risk-rises-in-somalia-as-rains-fail-food-prices-soar-un#:~:text=Nearly%20a%20quarter%20of%20a%20million%20people%20are%20on%20the%20brink%20of%20starvation%20in%20drought%2Dstruck%20Somalia%20as%20food%20prices%20soar%2C%20United%20Nations%20agencies%20say.).

But do these individual events, even if large in number, add up to an overall depression in agricultural production? [Research](http://www.nature.com/articles/s41558-021-01000-1) [published in _Nature Climate Change_](http://www.nature.com/articles/s41558-021-01000-1) finds that climate change has already reduced agricultural productivity by about 21 percent since 1961, “a slowdown that is equivalent to losing the last 7 years of productivity growth.” 

We are now at a particularly precarious point. [Food prices](https://yaleclimateconnections.org/2022/09/ags-challenging-future-in-a-changing-climate/#:~:text=Food%20prices%20during%20the%20first%20eight%20months%20of%202022%20were%20at%20an%20all%2Dtime%20high) [during the first eight months of 2022 were at an all-time high](https://yaleclimateconnections.org/2022/09/ags-challenging-future-in-a-changing-climate/#:~:text=Food%20prices%20during%20the%20first%20eight%20months%20of%202022%20were%20at%20an%20all%2Dtime%20high). And per a [recent United Nations report](https://www.wfp.org/stories/hunger-famine-and-starvation-750000-people-are-front-line-un-study-says#:~:text=They%20are%20among%20an%20%C2%A0%E2%80%98all%2Dtime%20high%E2%80%99%20of%2049%20million%20people%20in%2046%20countries%20at%20risk%20of%20succumbing%20to%20famine%20or%20famine%2Dlike%20conditions%2C%20the%20report%20states.), a record 49 million people in 46 countries are at risk of falling into famine or famine-like conditions.

However, it’s important to emphasize that the future of food security is not easily predictable—and is determined by many factors. For example, [research by NASA](https://climate.nasa.gov/news/3124/global-climate-change-impact-on-crops-expected-within-10-years-nasa-study-finds/) finds that over the next ten years, while maize crop yields will likely decline, wheat yields could potentially grow by about 17 percent as climate change expands wheat’s growing range further north. 

> Food security is also deeply connected to issues of justice, distribution and food preferences. There are [vast differences](http://ourworldindata.org/grapher/daily-per-capita-caloric-supply?tab=chart) [in the daily supply of calories available](http://ourworldindata.org/grapher/daily-per-capita-caloric-supply?tab=chart) to people in different parts of the world. Climate change is likely to exacerbate existing inequities in food access, as entire world regions like South Asia experience potential [disruptions to monsoons](https://www.bbc.com/future/article/20220517-the-uncertain-fate-of-asias-monsoons) that sustain food production for over a billion people.  

Land use choices also have a significant impact on current and future food availability. 

###### *For example, [in the U.S. alone, “replacing all animal-based items with plant-based replacement diets](http://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1713820115) can add enough food to feed 350 million additional people” from the same land.* 

This is all to say, food security cannot be understood through the lens of climate change alone, though climate change will play an increasingly important role in determining our shared food future.

## SUMMARY

- The science of attributing local extreme weather events to climate change is fast-evolving, but still in its infancy. Due to the coarse resolution of climate models, connecting the dots between extreme weather in a particular area to global climate change is tricky. Yet we are now at a place where science can tell us the extent to which a particular event is/was more likely to occur as compared to an identical world without climate change.  
- The IPCC AR6 report summarizes recent research on extreme event attribution. Climate change's "fingerprint" on some weather events, like heat waves, is more evident than on other weather events, like tropical cyclones. Impacts are also unevenly distributed geographically.   
- [World Weather Attribution looks to rapidly analyze](https://www.worldweatherattribution.org/analyses/) extreme weather events that may have been more likely—or destructive—due to climate change.

- It's important to remember that extreme weather events alone do not make a disaster—the intensity of a disaster is based on a vulnerable population (and physical assets) being exposed to a given natural hazard. This is important to keep in mind when making decisions around mitigating climate risk.  
- We are increasingly facing multiple, overlapping disasters in some places, which stretches the capacities of disaster response services and governments to the extreme. Research indicates that this is going to continue to get worse over time.  
- Local climate impact stories are powerful communications tools. The best pieces frequently combine moving personal stories interwoven with a clear explanation of the science.

## Risk of Mass Death as heatwaves start to pass Survivability threshold
==Source: *https://www.newscientist.com/article/2391326-risk-of-mass-deaths-as-heatwaves-start-to-pass-survivability-threshold/?utm_campaign=RSS%7CNSNS&utm_source=NSNS&utm_medium=RSS&utm_content=currents)*==

##### *"Between 1.5 and 2°C of global warming will lead to heatwaves so extreme that healthy people can't survive outdoors for long, in areas where people aren't used to extreme heat"*

As the world warms beyond 1.5°C, large parts of the world will start to have heatwaves so extreme that healthy young people could die within several hours if they fail to find respite, a study has warned

> “You could have a very extreme heatwave that departs from historical norms substantially, crosses this threshold and causes much more mortality than you would otherwise expect,” he says. “What we [will] see, particularly in Europe and North America, is an enormous increase in the incidence of these heatwaves [as the world warms] between 1.5 and 2 degrees [C].”

> [!Increased number of fatalities]
> Global warming is already sparking more intense and more frequent heatwaves, so is [causing large numbers of fatalities](https://www.newscientist.com/article/2278919-climate-change-to-blame-for-37-per-cent-of-worlds-heat-related-deaths/). It is estimated that there were [62,000 heat-related deaths across Europe](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-023-02419-z) in [the summer of 2022](https://www.newscientist.com/article/2336829-why-have-we-seen-so-many-heatwaves-around-the-world-in-2022/), for instance. However, the vast majority of these were people aged over 65 who may have had existing health issues.

> [!What is the limit of Survivability? ]
> 
> Based on theory, they decided the limit of survivability is when the temperature measured by a thermometer covered in a wet cloth exceeds 35°C (95°F). This is the so-called wet-bulb temperature. It reflects the fact that humidity affects our ability to stay cool by sweating. At this wet-bulb reading we can no longer keep core body temperature in check naturally and it will rise to deadly levels if we don’t take action to stay cool in other ways.
> 
> At present, the wet bulb temperature very [seldom exceeds 31°C (88](https://www.newscientist.com/article/mg20827864-300-countdown-to-thermogeddon-has-begun/)[°](https://www.newscientist.com/article/mg20827864-300-countdown-to-thermogeddon-has-begun/)[F) anywhere on Earth’s surface](https://www.newscientist.com/article/mg20827864-300-countdown-to-thermogeddon-has-begun/). Huber’s team concluded that large areas would only start to exceed the 35°C wet bulb limit if the world [warmed by more than 7°C](https://www.newscientist.com/article/2194781-earth-could-warm-by-14c-as-growing-emissions-destroy-crucial-clouds/) – which is thought highly unlikely.

Some important caveats here:
- it is an upper limit. A lot of people would not survive before that, way before
- Some countries in the tropics could exceed this lower limit in a short time
- Some sturdies would suggest the limit closer to 31 degrees.

Powis and his colleagues have now used data from weather stations and climate models to see where in the world such conditions may currently occur based on Vecellio’s 31°C wet-bulb findings, and how this will change at the world warms.

> For instance, with 1°C of global warming – a level already passed – only 3 per cent of weather stations in Europe are likely to pass Vecellio’s threshold more than once in 100 years. With 2°C of warming, 25 per cent are likely to. In the US, 20 per cent of stations are likely to pass the threshold more than once in 100 years with 1°C global warming, rising to 28 per cent for 2°C.

## 5 Solutions in Catalunya to deal with water shortages
*==Source: ecologie 360 (#6, 112)==*

Situation in Catalunya has been worsening. It is now taking some of the most agressive measures to deal with water shortages as it knows it can not supply its crucial industries: Agriculture and Tourism. 
###### Water reserved had gone under 16% back in february, thankfully back up to 25% in May after heavy rain

> Back in 2008, authorities had to pay 500M to bring drinkable water by boat to Barcelona. 

> [!Water Restrictions]
> Today., 80% of the Catalan population (out of 6M) who depend on the Ter-Llobregat water bassin, have to limit their daily consumption of water to 230l. Could go donw to 160l if things get worse. 
> The Impact can already be felt
> - Harbour is getting ready to accomodate delivery of drinking water
> - A factory of Slat removal is being built
> - Cleaning your car with drinking water could face 750€ fine
> - Pressure for water gardening has been reduced and it is prioritise to save the 35,000 trees at risk
> - Water invoice is up 11% 
> - Water reduction for the industry are drastic: 25% for industry, 50% for cattle and 80% for agriculture
> 
>  This will of course impact the economy, with a predicted reduction of 30% of wine production for instance. 
> 


> [!NOTE] The 5 Solutions
> 1. Regeneration of its water bassins. The risk is that the flow of water is slo low that it would allow salty water to come up and distroy life. for that, the plan is to bring more regenerated water. Not sure what that means and how
> 2. Water use optimisation per sector: impact is also on tourism, with restrictions on filling pools, spas, golfs in a region highly dependant on tourism
> 3. More de-salting factories to provide general water . Today it is 23% of the water supply
> 4. Reduce leaks: 183M announced to help localities deal with water leaks
> 5. Regeneration of used water: Israel re-use 70% of its used water and is used as a reference. include 25 nuit factories creation
