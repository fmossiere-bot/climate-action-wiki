## June 2026: Swiss Alps Hit Second-Earliest Glacier Loss Day on Record

The June 2026 European heatwave pushed Swiss glaciers into their **second-worst year on record**, behind only 2022. Matthias Huss, head of [GLAMOS](https://www.glamos.ch/) (Glacier Monitoring in Switzerland), warned that **all the snow accumulated during the previous winter would have melted away by 30 June 2026**, marking the so-called **glacier loss day**. Any melting after that date eats into the glacier itself rather than the seasonal snowpack.

The only earlier loss day in 25 years of records was 26 June 2022. This century, the loss day has averaged mid-August. In Huss's words, Swiss glaciers were running "three months too early compared to a healthy state."

### What is happening on the ice

- On the **Rhone Glacier**, Huss recorded **one metre of vertical ice melt in just 10 days** during the heatwave
- This winter saw **25% less snow replenishing the glaciers** than the 2010–2020 average
- A warm May had already melted much of the snowpack early
- **Saharan dust deposits in March** darkened the ice and accelerated melt
- Once the reflective white snow is gone, the darker bare ice absorbs more solar radiation, creating an **accelerating melt feedback loop**

### The longer trend

- Swiss glacier volume shrank by **38% between 2000 and 2024**
- Switzerland has already **lost 1,200 glaciers in 50 years**, leaving roughly 1,300
- Huss: if warming continues, "by 2100 we will only be left with some little remnants of ice"

### Why it matters beyond Switzerland

Much of the water that flows into **the Rhine and the Rhone**, two of Europe's major rivers, comes from Alpine glacier melt. Losing this reservoir threatens summer water supply for tens of millions of people and the agriculture, industry, hydropower and ecosystems that depend on it.

### The compounded problem

Huss is clear that single heatwaves are not the issue: "one heatwave alone is not a big problem for glaciers. The problem is rather that we have very high temperatures that last for a very long time."
