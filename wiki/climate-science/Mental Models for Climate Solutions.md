## Introduction

If you had €100 million to invest in one or more climate solutions, which would you pick and why?

This is not a simple question. Before even looking at specific technologies or sectors, it is worth stepping back to examine how we think about climate solutions in the first place. The frameworks we use to evaluate options are not neutral. They reflect assumptions, priorities and blind spots that shape which solutions get attention and which do not.

For the purpose of this module, climate solutions cover two areas: mitigation, meaning the reduction and elimination of greenhouse gas emissions, and removal, which involves physically extracting excess carbon from the atmosphere and storing it safely. Adaptation, while important, is a separate topic covered elsewhere, as it does not address the root cause of the problem.

---

## Why mental models matter

A mental model is a way of structuring how we think about a problem. When it comes to climate solutions, the model you use determines what you measure, what you prioritise, and ultimately what you recommend.

There is a common tendency, even among experts, to present evaluations of climate solutions as purely objective. In reality, every framework rests on underlying assumptions. Some of those assumptions are explicit, many are not. Understanding which model is being used, and what it leaves out, is essential to making sound decisions. Some of the models explored here are mutually exclusive, others can be used together.

---

## Mental Model 1: Climate Impact and Costs

The most widely used approach to comparing climate solutions focuses on two variables: how much greenhouse gas a solution reduces or removes, and at what cost. This model is the foundation of two well-known frameworks.

**Project Drawdown** analyses a broad range of technologies and practices, estimating their potential to reduce or remove emissions, the cost of implementation, and the financial savings or expenses compared to a business-as-usual scenario. It is a useful starting point for understanding which solutions have the greatest potential impact at scale.

**The Marginal Abatement Cost (MAC) curve**, first popularised by McKinsey in 2007, maps solutions on two axes: emissions reduction potential and cost per tonne of CO2 avoided. It allows for a quick visual comparison across many options and has been widely adopted by governments and businesses.

The strength of this model is also its main limitation. It works well for what can be measured, but it tends to smooth over significant uncertainty in both cost and impact estimates. It excludes context-specific factors, and it says nothing about cross-cutting areas of intervention such as policy, finance, communications or education. Most importantly, it is silent on justice and equity. When entire sectors of the economy are being reshaped, there are always winners and losers. This model does not help identify who they are.

## Mental Model 2: Growth vs Degrowth

Few debates in climate circles generate more heat than this one. The two positions reflect fundamentally different views on what kind of economic future is compatible with a stable climate.

**The green growth model** holds that rapid decarbonisation is achievable without sacrificing economic expansion. It argues that technological innovation, driven by market incentives and policy support, can decouple emissions from growth. This is, in practice, the model underlying virtually all climate policy today, including major legislation such as the US Inflation Reduction Act. Project Drawdown and MAC curves are also built on the assumption of continuous economic growth.

**The degrowth model** argues that this is not sufficient. Its proponents, including economist Jason Hickel and journalist George Monbiot, contend that the growth model is itself a driver of the crisis. More economic activity means more energy demand, which makes the transition harder and slower. Degrowth would reduce overall consumption and energy use, and if accompanied by shorter working weeks and living wages, could improve social outcomes at the same time.

Both positions have strengths and blind spots. Green growth may underestimate the pace and scale of transformation required. Degrowth raises legitimate questions about political feasibility and the distribution of sacrifice. The debate is worth taking seriously rather than dismissing either side.

## Mental Model 3: A Systems Lens

A systems approach recognises that climate change does not exist in isolation. It is connected to public health, social inequality, land use, economics and governance. Solutions that address only one dimension often create problems elsewhere.

One practical expression of this thinking is **multisolving**: designing interventions that tackle several problems at once. Energy efficiency improvements to homes, for instance, reduce emissions, lower energy bills for low-income households, improve indoor air quality and health, and create skilled local employment. The climate benefit is real, but it is one of several outcomes.

**Doughnut Economics**, developed by economist Kate Raworth, offers another systems-based framework. It sets two boundaries: a social foundation below which no one should fall, and an ecological ceiling beyond which we should not push. A good climate solution, in this model, is one that stays within both.

Tools such as **En-ROADS**, a free online climate simulator developed by Climate Interactive, allow users to explore how different combinations of policies and technologies interact across the whole system. It is built on systems thinking and reflects the multisolving approach in practice.

The systems lens is more complex to apply than a simple cost-impact comparison, but it is closer to how the real world works.

## Mental Model 4: Masculine and Feminine Framings

This model draws on the concept of complementary forces, sometimes described as Yin and Yang, to ask a different kind of question: not just what to do, but how to do it well.

The outward, action-oriented dimension focuses on building, scaling and deploying solutions. It is the drive to roll out renewable energy, electrify transport, and restore forests as fast as possible.

The inward, reflective dimension asks whether those actions are being done in the right way. It is the voice that questions whether scaling up electric vehicles is truly clean if the lithium supply chain replicates the extraction harms of fossil fuels. It challenges land grabs for solar farms that displace communities. It insists on listening before acting.

Neither dimension is sufficient on its own. Unchecked action can cause harm, even with good intentions. Unchecked reflection can become paralysis. The most robust climate solutions hold both in balance: ambitious in scale, careful in execution.

## Summary

- Mental models fundamentally shape how we think about, understand and compare climate solutions. There is a strong tendency among "experts" to present analyses of solutions as a purely objective exercise. This module aims to demonstrate that even experts operate with underlying mental models that strongly influence how climate solutions are discussed, elevated or de-prioritized, and compared. Note that while some of the models are mutually exclusive (e.g. growth vs degrowth), others are not.
    
- _Mental Model 1: Climate Impacts and Costs._ The most common type of mental model used for comparing climate solutions is one that is defined by some measure of greenhouse gas reductions and costs. This model has its strengths in that it is practical, quantifiable and allows for easy comparison. The drawback is it allows no space to consider the importance of cross-cutting solutions areas such as politics or communications; it hides or otherwise waves away considerable uncertainty, and it allows no space to consider justice and equity issues.
    
- _Mental Model 2: Growth vs. Degrowth._ The green growth model believes we can achieve rapid decarbonization without hurting economic growth. The degrowth model argues that to truly address our various environmental and socioeconomic problems, western economies have to shrink, not grow. This debate elicits strong feelings for people one way or the other, and both sides make compelling arguments but also have blind spots.
    
- _Mental model 3: A Systems lens._ This includes frameworks like multi-solving, in which we bundle problems together, and solve them with a single approach. It also includes Doughnut Economics, which is both a model and a tool for achieving multiple environmental and socioeconomic goals.
    
- _Mental Model 4: Masculine vs feminine framings._ This approach asks: In what ways are you going out to take action and create a more just and sustainable world, and how are you being sure to listen to feedback and ensure that your actions are the right ones and done in the right way?
