## Intro
> If you had $100 million to invest in one or more climate solutions, which would you pick and why? 

The goal of this module is to introduce some mental models for thinking about and comparing across different climate solutions areas, so you have the tools and resources you need to answer the $100 million question.  

But first let's get one thing straight: When we say "climate solutions" we include:  
- **Mitigation,** namely reducing and eliminating greenhouse gas emissions 
- **Removal,** which involves physically removing excess carbon from the atmosphere and storing it safely  

We do not include adaptation as a climate solution in the context of this class, as it does not address the underlying problem itself. But we do have an entire future class devoted to this topic!

Next, let’s discuss what a mental model is, and how this can help us understand and evaluate climate solutions.
## Why mental models ? 
When it comes to deciding on Climate Solutions, Climate Impact is not enough. This is why we need to use instead Mental Models. For instance, Impact + Cost is one model. 

Mental models fundamentally shape how we think about, understand and compare climate solutions. There is a strong tendency among "experts" to present analyses of solutions as a purely objective exercise. This module aims to demonstrate that even experts operate with underlying mental models that strongly influence how climate solutions are discussed, elevated or de-prioritized, and compared. Note that while some of the models are mutually exclusive (e.g. growth vs degrowth), others are not.

## Isn’t climate impact enough?

The longer answer is that while we can use scientific methods to understand the manifestation and proximate causes (greenhouse gas emissions) of the problem, when it comes to unpacking root causes of climate change, we always run up against _human_ systems (institutions, human behaviour, power dynamics, culture, political systems, etc) that are far harder to reduce to neat measurements.  

To make this more concrete: The most obvious reason we can’t just use a measure of climate impact is costs

## MM1: Climate Impact and Costs
- quantifiable
- practical
- Doesn't take into account Justice and equity

_Mental Model 1: Climate Impacts and Costs._ The most common type of mental model used for comparing climate solutions is one that is defined by some measure of greenhouse gas reductions and costs. This model has its strengths in that it is practical, quantifiable and allows for easy comparison. The drawback is it allows no space to consider the importance of cross-cutting solutions areas such as politics or communications; it hides or otherwise waves away considerable uncertainty, and it allows no space to consider justice and equity issues.

2 widely-use framework use that model:

### Project Drawdown
Seems to be a similar approach to what TeamForThePlanet uses in terms of estimating impact. 
Project Drawdown “[seeks to determine how and when we can reach drawdown](https://drawdown.org/solutions/methods#:~:text=Project%20Drawdown%20seeks%20to%20determine%20how%20and%20when%20we%20can%20reach%20drawdown)—the point in time when levels of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere stop climbing and start to steadily decline—using technologies and practices that reduce sources of greenhouse gases, remove greenhouse gases from the atmosphere, or catalyze societal changes that lead to reduced sources or enhanced sinks.” 

[Drawdown runs simulation models for a few different scenarios that assess a wide range of technologies and solutions based on](http://drawdown.org/solutions/methods):

- “the amount of greenhouse gas that would be reduced, avoided, or stored 
- the cost of implementation
- the operating cost or savings relative to the reference scenario.”

For a deeper dive into Project Drawdown, consider these resources: 

- [The Drawdown Review](https://www.drawdown.org/publications/the-drawdown-review/)
- Drawdown’s [Climate Solutions](https://drawdown.org/solutions) page
- Jamie Alexander, Head of Drawdown Labs, [guest talk](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3Fwlkq47SxU) at Terra.do 

Project Drawdown is a great starting point for understanding the relative importance of different climate solutions areas. It is helpful for narrowing down your interest areas and guiding your choice of solutions to focus on.

### **Marginal Abatment Cost Curve** 
A marginal abatement cost (MAC) curve is another useful tool for assessing how different technologies compare in terms of emissions impact and costs. It was first popularized by McKinsey & Company in 2007.
[<< Interactive Version >>](https://public.tableau.com/app/profile/jamil.farbes/viz/MACC2_0_20210702_EDF/2050MACC2_0wrange) 

![[Screenshot 2023-09-26 at 14.42.57.png]]


> [!PROS and Cons]
> 
> We’ve briefly covered two different and widely used frameworks for evaluating climate solutions based on an integrated approach that looks at both climate or emissions impacts and costs. 
> 
> The advantage of this mental model is that it provides a straightforward, elegant, measurable way to compare and contrast different solutions, with the thing we care about most—climate impact—at the front and center. Both Drawdown and the MAC curve are useful starting points for assessing different solutions areas. 
> 
> This mental model’s strength, however, is also its weakness: These frameworks only cover what you can measure, and they tend to wave away uncertainties and context-specific differences in both estimated emissions impact and costs. This mental model also leaves out crucial cross-cutting areas of intervention such as finance, policy, activism, advocacy, communications, and education.  
> 
> MAC curves, as per [one study](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/14693062.2011.582347), “omit ancillary benefits of greenhouse gas emission abatement, treat uncertainty in a limited manner, exclude intertemporal dynamics and lack the necessary transparency concerning their assumptions.”  
> 
> > Crucially, this mental model is silent on justice and equity—who wins and who loses in any climate solutions area is of great relevance, but this model does not address this issue. This is a significant shortcoming: When we’re talking about remaking entire sectors of the economy, there are always, _always_ winners and losers in such shifts.

## MM2: Growth vs Degrowth
#Degrowth 
Many debates in teh commmunity comes from this argnument, on whether you are a growther or a degrowther

_Mental Model 2:  Growth vs. Degrowth._ The green growth model believes we can achieve rapid decarbonization without hurting economic growth.  The degrowth model argues that to truly address our various environmental and socioeconomic problems, western economies have to shrink, not grow. This debate elicits strong feelings for people one way or the other, and both sides make compelling arguments but also have blind spots. 

### The green growth mm:
- we should not stop growing economy
- we can decouple economic growth and emissions
- our current capitalist model provides the tooles that we need. 

> This is the model more or less underlying any policy everywhere,. 
> Project Drawdown and MAC also assume continuous economic growth


> Note that this is the model underlying virtually all climate policies everywhere. The much-lauded U.S. federal climate legislation titled the [Inflation Reduction Act](https://www.whitehouse.gov/briefing-room/statements-releases/2022/08/15/by-the-numbers-the-inflation-reduction-act/) practically defines this model. Project Drawdown and the MAC curves are also built on this idea, in that one assumption of their models is continuous economic growth.


### The degrowth mm:
- the current growth model is not enough, only impacting the margins, and the growth model led us where we are now. 
- Hickel's is probably the most prominent degrowth proponent. His argument is along the line of:
	- we can't go fast enough wuth economy growth
	- More growth means more energy demand
	- degrowth would actyall reduce demand and energy demand, helping us to faster transitiion
	- Do it while shortetning working week, introducing living wages and you could improve social outcome

Another well-known proponent of degrowth is George Monbiot, an environmental researcher and journalist with _The Guardian_.  He argues that "[less of everything is the only way to avert catastrophe.](http://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2021/sep/29/green-growth-economic-activity-environment)" [Professor Julia Steinberger makes the point](https://twitter.com/JKSteinberger/status/1587562407920123911?s=20&t=SHx6yXuBa-uUMnpTtf7f9g)

## MM3: A systems lens
This is Terra MM

_Mental model 3: A Systems lens._ This includes frameworks like multi-solving, in which we bundle problems together, and solve them with a single approach. It also includes Doughnut Economics, which is both a model and a tool for achieving multiple environmental and socioeconomic goals.

> 1 framework based on System Thinking is Multisolving :  an approach of protecting Climate while improving health, equity and wellbeing. 


![[Screenshot 2023-09-26 at 14.57.34.png]]

For instance, energy efficiency upgrades to homes also improve the health of residents, generate well-paying jobs for contractors, and lower utility costs for low-income residents—and it’s good for the climate as well.

[En-ROADS](https://www.climateinteractive.org/en-roads/) is “a freely-available online simulator that provides policymakers, educators, businesses, the media, and the public with the ability to test and explore cross-sector climate solutions.”
- En-ROADS operates within a systems thinking and multisolving model.

## MM4: Masculine vs Feminine framing
... or Yin and Yang. 

Yin = intrspection: listen to the earth
Yang: outgoing, go and take action

You need both to fight the climate, and this is a good example of its utilization:

> For example, if Yang is doing the work to scale up renewable energy and electric vehicles, Yin is saying to not blindly scale up EVs without safeguarding against duplicating the mining problems of coal and oil with a new natural resource like lithium

_Mental Model 4: Masculine vs feminine framings._ This approach asks: In what ways are you going out to take action and create a more just and sustainable world—and how are you being sure to listen to feedback and ensure that your actions are the right ones and done in the right way?

> For example, if Yang is doing the work to scale up renewable energy and electric vehicles, Yin is saying to not blindly scale up EVs without safeguarding against duplicating the mining problems of coal and oil with a new natural resource like lithium—or that we can’t do all the same land grabs for prime solar real estate that we did for other natural resources.

## TERRA.DO approach
At Terra.do, we equally emphasize the following "lenses" to build our mental model for understanding and analyzing climate solutions:    
 

- Systems thinking
- Global outlook
- Masculine _and_ feminine
- Skills-based

  
Let's unpack what each of these means:

**Systems thinking**

We briefly covered systems thinking in an earlier slide. Climate action touches virtually every aspect of human systems and the natural world, and the more comprehensive your understanding, the less likely you are to create unanticipated new problems, or hit show-stopping blocks in your work. By “systems lens” we mean we look at each solutions area through the integrated perspective of climate impacts, other impacts, technological innovation, costs, markets, actors, policies, politics, equity, and justice.

We especially emphasize justice throughout this course as we see inequity, poverty, suffering, and injustice to be _profoundly unacceptable._ Plus there is evidence that [inequality fuels climate change](http://twitter.com/fergusgreen/status/1529486305414877184?s=21&t=_bZxESn9TBT-mmXUa77PuQ)—and of course, we know about all the ways in which climate change unfairly impacts those who have contributed the least to the problem. So we choose to see climate action and justice in an integrated way.   
   
 This integrated lens reveals connections you may not otherwise anticipate when only considering a narrower view of the solution.

**Global outlook**

We also do our best to keep a _**global lens**_ on climate solutions, an approach that we find is usually missing elsewhere. We believe it is important to counter narrow, “carbon-only” perspectives, while still keeping climate goals front and center. Much of the world is still struggling to address basic human needs, eradicate poverty, and grapple with other critical environmental problems like air and water pollution—and “carbon-only” solutions can be nonstarters. So beware of fake boundaries, because these often lead to non-viable solutions that will struggle to get buy-in from a diverse set of constituents. 

As it is impossible to cover solutions for every place and context, our global coverage is necessarily limited. However, our assignment on climate solutions is designed for you to dive deeper into relevant solutions for your context.

**Masculine and feminine**

Terra.do’s name, and the name of the Learning For Action course, embody the idea of bringing Yin and Yang into balance. We are Terra.do, not Terra.edu, because just learning without taking action would be an imbalance of too much Yin, and taking action without properly learning about the core problem and identifying the right work to do would manifest too much Yang. Another way we attempt to balance these energies in the program is by taking time to honor and acknowledge the truth and emotional weight of understanding climate change—but then, instead of staying there, using that emotional energy to drive forward with actionable solutions.

**Skills-based**

Through a series of assignments and peer, group, and mentor conversations, we will help you find that sweet spot of intersection between _your_ skills, your interests, and the potential for maximal climate impact in your context.  As Project Drawdown puts it, "[every job is a climate job,](https://twitter.com/ProjectDrawdown/status/1435979315666202624?s=20&t=3Gq0Vu_zKo1nsATcncICsQ)" and we firmly believe that the climate crisis needs each and every one of your skills applied to the problem.
