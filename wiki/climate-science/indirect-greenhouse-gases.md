# The Forgotten Pollutants: Indirect Greenhouse Gases

## What is it

Most climate policies focus on the three big greenhouse gases: CO₂, methane, and nitrous oxide. But a significant chunk of the warming we have already caused comes from a different category of pollutants that rarely appear in emissions targets. **Carbon monoxide (CO), volatile organic compounds (VOCs), and black carbon (soot) have together caused approximately 15% of all global warming since pre-industrial times.** That is double the contribution of nitrous oxide, the third most common greenhouse gas. A 2026 study in the journal Science, led by researchers including Ilissa Ocko at Spark Climate Solutions, is calling for these "forgotten" gases to be included in national climate action plans.

## Why it matters

These gases are not greenhouse gases in the traditional sense. They do not directly trap heat. Instead, they cause warming through chemical reactions in the atmosphere. Because they break down within hours to a few years (compared to CO₂ which persists for centuries), cutting them would produce fast results: the warming effect would begin to disappear almost immediately. Alex Archibald at the University of Cambridge describes them as "low-hanging fruit" to prevent catastrophic climate tipping points.

## How indirect greenhouse gases warm the planet

**Carbon monoxide and VOCs** react with other compounds in the lower atmosphere to form ozone. Unlike the protective ozone layer high in the stratosphere, this ground-level ozone traps heat that would otherwise escape to space. At the same time, CO and VOCs consume hydroxyl radicals, a natural atmospheric "detergent" that breaks down methane. When more hydroxyl is diverted to react with CO and VOCs, less is available to destroy methane, which traps about 80 times more heat than CO₂ in the short term.

**Black carbon** (soot) is produced mainly by the incomplete combustion of fossil fuels and agricultural burning. It absorbs solar radiation directly in the atmosphere, and when it settles on snow and ice it darkens the surface, causing it to absorb more heat and melt faster.

Together, these indirect greenhouse gases have caused approximately 0.3°C of warming to date. A portion of this has been offset by cooling aerosols such as sulphur dioxide, and by nitrogen oxides which have a net cooling effect overall (because they generate hydroxyl radicals), though nitrogen oxides can also warm locally by producing ozone.

## Where these gases come from

The main sources are not exotic or difficult to understand:

- **Carbon monoxide:** incomplete combustion in gas boilers, gas stoves, older petrol and diesel vehicles, and agricultural burning of grasslands and forests
- **VOCs:** hydrocarbons that evaporate from fossil fuels, paint, and cleaning solvents
- **Black carbon:** diesel engines, industrial processes, and biomass burning

Air quality regulations in countries such as the UK have already reduced these gases significantly by tightening vehicle emissions standards and limiting the VOC content of paints. But the motivation has been health, not climate, and many countries have looser rules. And critically, almost no country includes indirect greenhouse gases in its Paris Agreement climate pledges.

## The hydrogen risk

The transition away from fossil fuels may inadvertently increase some indirect greenhouse gas emissions if not managed carefully. Hydrogen, being the smallest molecule, leaks and is sometimes vented into the atmosphere. In the atmosphere, it consumes hydroxyl radicals in the same way as CO, prolonging the lifetime of methane. Alastair Lewis at the University of York warns that if the most ambitious hydrogen-burning plans (for steel, fertiliser, and aviation) go ahead without tight leak controls, the additional warming from hydrogen venting could reach 0.1°C by 2100.

The lesson is that choosing batteries over combustion wherever possible avoids this risk entirely. Burning a low-carbon fuel may look carbon-neutral on paper while still contributing to warming through indirect effects.

## Key facts

- Indirect GHGs (mainly CO and VOCs) have caused ~15% of total warming since pre-industrial times — double the contribution of N₂O ([Science, Ocko et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.aee5790))
- CO + VOCs + black carbon together have caused approximately 0.3°C of warming
- These gases break down within hours to a few years — cutting emissions would quickly slow the rate of warming
- Almost no country includes indirect greenhouse gases in Paris Agreement action plans
- Ambitious hydrogen-as-fuel scenarios could add 0.1°C of warming by 2100 from leakage alone
- Key researcher: Ilissa Ocko (Spark Climate Solutions): "a set of forgotten climate pollutants strongly contributing to today's warming"
