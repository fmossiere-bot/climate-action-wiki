### Other factors that affect the Earth's climate include:

- Aerosols from volcanoes and human activities
    - Some of these gases and particles can actually cool the Earth.
- Changes in solar flux over time
    - That’s variation in the amount of solar energy the Earth receives.
- Changes in Earth's orbital cycle
    - The path our planet takes around the sun also varies over time; in fact, these periodic wobbles are the cause of ice ages.
- Changes in land usage
    - Altering how much of the Earth is covered in forests, and seasonal changes in sea ice, for example, both affect the Earth’s reflectivity, or albedo.

### Temperatures are up, too.

This image shows the corresponding temperature variations dating back to the last ice age—you can click on it for a closer look. Since the relatively recent pre-industrial era, we've already warmed up about 1.1 degrees Celsius.  
  
According to [NOAA scientists](https://www.climate.gov/news-features/understanding-climate/climate-change-atmospheric-carbon-dioxide), the last time the atmospheric CO2 amounts were this high (above 400 ppm) was more than **3 million years ago**, when temperature was 2–3 C (3.6–5.4 Fahrenheit) hotter than during the pre-industrial era, and sea level was 5–25 meters (16–80 feet) higher. And this finding is reinforced by [research](https://www.annualreviews.org/doi/abs/10.1146/annurev-earth-082420-063026?journalCode=earth) that has reconstructed temperatures over the past 66 million years.

That said, present-day CO2 levels won't affect the planet's temperatures or sea levels quite as dramatically as they did a few million years ago. As Paleoecologist Jacquelyn Gill explains ([X thread](https://twitter.com/jacquelyngill/status/1532858346797244416); [PDF](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1HyEzB2kq62c14mTp0ci4eElXbmmS0pvt/view?usp=drive_link)), that's thanks to some quirks of geography that make the two time periods very different.![[Screenshot 2024-01-23 at 18.16.49.png]]
Source: [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-021-03984-4)

### Temperatures are rising because we are increasing radiative forcing.

Radiative forcing is also known as "climate forcing." Think of it as the force driving up temperatures.

In our climate modeling module, we found Earth is taking on energy faster than losing it. Radiative forcing is a technical term for that imbalance:   
  
**Radiative forcing = Incoming Energy – Outgoing Energy.**

When incoming energy gets trapped and cannot all escape as outgoing energy, there is an increase in radiative forcing. That's intensifying as levels of CO2 and other greenhouse gases increase, resulting in Earth's average temperature rising.

### Scientists have attempted to quantify how different factors “force” the climate system one way or another.

This graph shows the [radiative forcing](https://www.climate.gov/maps-data/primer/climate-forcing) we get from these warming and cooling factors. Some notes:  
 

- The graph uses the year 1750 as a baseline to represent the system's state since the industrial revolution. 
- The units are in watts per square meter. Watts measure energy transferred over time, and climate scientists are particularly interested in heat energy.
- Bars moving to the left show cooling, while those moving right show warming. The cumulative effect from humans is the red bar at the bottom.
![[Screenshot 2024-01-23 at 18.20.57.png]]
  
The key takeaway is that while individual factors can have warming or cooling effects, and the estimates here are inexact, the net effect is clearly increased warming.
