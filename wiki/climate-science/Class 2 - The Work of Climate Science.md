### Observations in Climate Science

An international collaboration called the **Global Climate Observing System (GCOS)** has identified 55 Essential Climate Variables (ECVs) that scientists are tracking to better understand the Earth’s climate system.  
  
The GCOS [defines](https://gcos.wmo.int/en/essential-climate-variables/about) an ECV as "a physical, chemical or biological variable or a group of linked variables that critically contributes to the characterization of Earth’s climate." Take a minute to go to this [GCOS site](https://gcos.wmo.int/en/essential-climate-variables/) on ECVs and explore some of these variables.

Let’s dig deeper into two that we especially care about:  

- Carbon dioxide, our chief greenhouse gas villain
- Global average temperatures (You may have noticed on the ECVs site that temperatures are measured in several different places. Yet typically, in discussions on climate change, one mainly hears about global average surface temperature.)

### How do we measure carbon dioxide and global average temperatures?

Carbon dioxide concentrations are at an all-time high. We know this thanks to measurements taken at about a hundred sites around the world, along with satellite data. And we can compare today's data against past records going back hundreds of thousands of years, thanks to data from ice cores, where air bubbles containing clues about our past climate have been trapped. 

As for temperature, measurements are taken on land as well as via ships and buoys at sea, and sometimes satellites. These measurements are then combined to build global data sets.  This helps researchers see how temperatures are changing over time by identifying "anomalies," or temperatures that stick out from long-term averages. These data sets vary in several ways, like how they deal with areas or time periods where data is sparse, and how much detail they provide.

#### About Aerosols

As [this chart we mentioned in the hint](https://cbhighcharts.s3.eu-west-1.amazonaws.com/100%25+Human/berkeley_global_all.html) shows, aerosols have reduced global warming by 0.4 C or more since 1850. This is because they reflect incoming solar radiation.

While, on net, we still need to curb our fossil fuel use, the effect of reducing aerosol will push the global average temperature even higher.

## Statistics in Climate Science
[LINK](https://textbook.terra.do/class/m-climate-science2-3?r=https://app.terra.do/app/learning/86/my-schedule#m-climate-science2-3)

Raw observations aren't that useful on their own, so to find meaning in the data we gather about temperature and CO2, we need statistics.

To understand climate change, we need to answer questions like:  
 
- Are temperatures actually increasing? At what rate?
- How—if at all—is this variation in temperatures tied to changing levels of greenhouse gases?
- What is the probability that temperatures will continue to increase in the future and how confident can we be about our claims?
- What is the probability that increasing wildfire activity results from climate change, and can we quantify this effect?

### Correlation ≠ Causation

A very common error that folks tend to make, especially in the media, is conflating correlation with causation. As you may have concluded in the optional statistics [assignment](https://docs.google.com/document/d/13CcdZRMIFoJOBpmBuBhkdcg62IPri2q9cLnFVyrrmBs/edit), the correlation between temperatures and CO2 levels does not necessarily imply a “causal” relationship between the two.   
  
In fact, before human activity became a dominant force affecting the climate, warmer temperatures from natural planetary cycles led to more ocean mixing and CO2 _release_ from oceans, leading to an increase in atmospheric CO2, which then caused more warming as CO2 trapped heat in the atmosphere. So in that long-ago time, an increase in temperatures caused an increase in CO2 levels!

Now, of course, we say the rise in temperatures is "caused by" rising CO2 from anthropogenic emissions. So why can we say this definitively? For this, we have to return to science.

In the previous class, we learned conceptually how greenhouse gases trap heat. This physical science knowledge—combined with a statistical analysis of how temperature and CO2 trends relate—gives us confidence there's a causal connection.

Observations, scientific principles, and statistics are combined in complex ways to build climate models, as we'll cover in the next module!

### Integrated assessment models (IAMs)

The most significant reason models aren't quite as precise at predictions as we would like them to be, however, is [because of people](https://www.csmonitor.com/Environment/2021/0122/Gavin-Schmidt-The-problem-with-climate-models-People), according to Dr. Gavin Schmidt, Director of NASA's Goddard Institute for Space Studies. We are the main drivers of emissions, and our collective behavior is much less predictable than the laws of planetary physics!

Models that incorporate people, policies, and economics are known as Integrated Assessment Models (IAMs) These are quite different from the physical models we discussed in the previous slide, and are increasingly being used to guide policymakers' choices. But while "hugely influential," they also have critics ([X thread](https://twitter.com/AjayGambhir11/status/1600770050486546437); [PDF](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1iVHQxDMcNXpb7G0vvBzVk-0h_u2MfRna/view?usp=drive_link)).

Some simple IAMs can be run in a basic spreadsheet, and even the most complex ones have been criticized for being overly simplistic. It is beyond the scope of this class to go into all the criticisms of IAMs, but because they deal with people, they cannot escape being based on contested—some would say, incorrect—[assumptions](https://twitter.com/martavictoria_p/status/1494372815260340231) about the economy and human behavior.

They have also been criticized for having unjust biases against the Global South—something we discuss more in the next module.

### Climate Sensitivity: Connecting Carbon Dioxide Increase to Temperature Increase

So just how much might the planet warm as the result of a given radiative forcing? (Remember, radiative forcings occur when there’s an imbalance between incoming and outgoing energy, like when that energy is trapped by greenhouse gases.)

> Climate sensitivity is the average change in global mean surface temperature in response to a radiative forcing. There are three chief types of climate sensitivity:   
 
- The "equilibrium climate sensitivity" (ECS) is the long-term increase in global average temperature we expect to reach once the effects of a doubled CO2 concentration have had time to reach a steady state.
- The shorter-term "transient climate response" (TCR) reveals how much warmer the Earth is expected to get at that time when we double the concentration of CO2 compared to pre-industrial levels—around 280ppm. 
- The “Earth system sensitivity” (ESS) includes very long-term Earth system feedbacks, such as changes in ice sheets or in the distribution of vegetative cover.

Climate sensitivity is usually estimated in three ways, using:  

- Direct observations of key climate variables like temperature and levels of greenhouse gases, 
- Indirectly estimated temperature and other measurements from the Earth's more distant past, and 
- Computer models
  
We’re focusing on climate sensitivity here because it has long been "[the key uncertainty](https://esd.copernicus.org/articles/11/737/2020/) in projections of future climate change."

#### Equilibrium Climate Sensitivity

To understand why climate sensitivity matters, let’s dive into equilibrium climate sensitivity. ECS is the long-term increase in global average temperature we expect to reach once the effects of a doubled CO2 concentration have had time to stabilize. (The specific focus on doubling gives scientists a common metric for comparing their work.)

ECS is estimated by climate models, and in turn can be an input into models that help us understand impacts and risks, thereby helping us calculate how quickly we need to reduce emissions.
But until recently, it’s been elusive. According to _[Carbon Brief](https://www.carbonbrief.org/in-depth-qa-the-ipccs-sixth-assessment-report-on-climate-science/)_, "Despite decades of intensive research, ECS had remained stubbornly uncertain." But the last few years have marked a [major leap forward](https://www.carbonbrief.org/guest-post-why-low-end-climate-sensitivity-can-now-be-ruled-out/).

Indeed, theIPCC's AR6 report provides a much more specific estimate than was possible for many years, as illustrated here—note how the red band is narrower than those preceding it. This affords researchers a more precise idea of what to expect:  

- AR6 estimates if atmospheric carbon were to double from pre-industrial levels, it’s _very likely_ (9-in-10 chances) the resulting warming would be between 2 and 5 degrees Celsius.  
- And it’s _likely_ (2-in-3 chances) the range would be narrower—between 2.5 and 4 degrees Celsius. Its central estimate is 3 C.  
- The report is _virtually certain_ that if CO2  levels were to double, the resulting warming would exceed 1.5 C. It also cautions that “currently it is not possible to rule out ECS values above 5 C.”

#### Uncertainties in climate sensitivity

Climate sensitivity is difficult to estimate largely because of uncertainties in complicated feedback mechanisms that may result from warming, which might kick in and cause further warming. Again, we'll discuss climate feedbacks in another module. 

It’s worth noting that [some scientists](https://twitter.com/GeoffVallis/status/1295393517464965120) prefer to focus on another measure of climate sensitivity: TCR, or transient climate response. It might be decades before equilibrium is reached, but the TCR is much closer to the reality of gradually increasing CO2 concentrations in the atmosphere. In the end, it comes down to which impacts you’re trying to study and on what time scales.

### What is CMIP?

With so many institutions developing and running climate models, there is a risk that each group approaches its modelling in a different way, reducing how comparable their results will be.

This is where the [Coupled Model Intercomparison Project](https://www.wcrp-climate.org/wgcm-cmip) (“CMIP”) comes in. CMIP is a framework for climate model experiments, allowing scientists to analyse, validate and improve GCMs in a systematic way

### The IPCC
https://www.ipcc.ch/about/

![[Screenshot 2023-09-11 at 16.49.25.png]]

The [IPCC](https://www.ipcc.ch/about/), or Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, is the United Nations body for assessing the science related to climate change. It involves the work of thousands of scientists from around the world, and provides regular assessments of the scientific basis of climate change, its impacts and future risks, and options for adaptation and mitigation.

The IPCC periodically publishes hefty, collaborative Assessment Reports. The sixth and most recent of these is often just referred to as [AR6](http://www.ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg1/#spm), which we'll detail more on the next slide. These reports are _the_ definitive source on international climate research and policy implications.

### UNFCCC (& its COP)
Another body of the United Nations called the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change, or UNFCCC, hosts the annual "[Conference of the Parties](https://unfccc.int/process/bodies/supreme-bodies/conference-of-the-parties-cop)" or COP. This body, which brings national leaders together to forge agreements on climate policy, does not produce any science, but rather, uses the scientific reports of the IPCC to guide their decision-making.

COPs started in 1995 and are hosted in different cities each year. The most recent one took place in Egypt in late 2022.

A particularly significant COP was COP21 in 2015, when the [Paris Agreement](https://unfccc.int/process-and-meetings/the-paris-agreement/the-paris-agreement) was forged committing world leaders to self-determined emissions reductions aimed at keeping warming below 2 (or 1.5 if possible) degrees Celsius. We will discuss this political process and the Paris Agreement in a later class.

> The main thing to keep in mind is that the IPCC produces _scientific studies_ while the COPs are a forum for _political negotiations_ between country governments.

### About the IPCC AR6
The IPCC latest report is called [AR6](https://www.ipcc.ch/assessment-report/ar6/) , published in August 2021
#### "Every tonne of CO2 we add to the atmosphere impact the climate change"

Check this [video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=b-IPsCWVIRA&ab_channel=ClimateAdam) for a summary of the AR6 report and also [this article from Kamal Kapadia on the report](https://myclimatejourney.substack.com/p/what-the-ipcc-report-means-for-climate)

The IPCC's Sixth Assessment Report, or [AR6](http://www.ipcc.ch/assessment-report/ar6/), came out in several installments, starting in August 2021, and concluding with a [Synthesis Report](https://www.ipcc.ch/report/sixth-assessment-report-cycle/) in March 2023.

That report grabbed headlines in part because it says that, unless we reduce greenhouse gas emissions drastically, global surface temperatures are on track to reach 1.5 C above preindustrial levels in the first half of the 2030s. For years, IPCC reports have circled the 1.5 C mark as one we’d best avoid crossing; we discuss this more in other modules.

Still, the report is not cause for despair. For one thing, it notes some encouraging signs—like how mitigation options like solar, wind, and other infrastructure improvements “are becoming increasingly cost effective and are generally supported by the public. From 2010–2019 there have been sustained decreases in the unit costs of solar energy (85%), wind energy (55%), and lithium ion batteries (85%), and large increases in their deployment.”

#### AR6, equity, and justice

An important criticism of the IPCC's report is that it makes unfair assumptions about how different parts of the world need to respond to the need for emissions cuts, and how quickly. 

The report's integrated assessment models (IAMs, which we discussed in the last module) take into account "both human and earth systems and interactions between the two," as [Rishika Pardikar writes in _CarbonCopy_](https://carboncopy.info/ipccs-climate-reports-reveal-an-unequal-science/), but these models don't consider which countries bear the most historic responsibility for climate change. And because their underlying assumptions rest on developing countries taking a disproportionate amount of action, Pardikar notes ([X](https://twitter.com/rishpardikar/status/1638843053426323456); [PDF](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1CtIzUsOXpttli3n03YSDu-F9G9DV30Lz/view?usp=drive_link)) the models arguably "assume perpetuation of existing inequities for decades to come."

Here, we have to note that the wording of an IPCC report's summary can be the subject of intense international lobbying. In the 2023 report's case, the final draft was shaped largely by the rich countries that remained in the room after delegates from many poorer countries had gone home ([X](https://twitter.com/NiranjanAjit/status/1638490319464419328); [PDF](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1DH85wo2LvOwKocc3soDWN-lyMnYCUJqp/view?usp=drive_link)). We discuss this dynamic more in our Politics class.

The upshot, some argue, is that the report [understates how much urgent action is needed](https://theconversation.com/ipccs-conservative-nature-masks-true-scale-of-action-needed-to-avert-catastrophic-climate-change-202287) to avert catastrophe.
