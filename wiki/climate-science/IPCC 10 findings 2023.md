## Overview

In March 2023, the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change published the final part of its Sixth Assessment Report, the most comprehensive scientific review of climate change ever produced. Drawing on the work of more than 780 scientists across three working groups, the report spans nearly 8,000 pages. Its overall message is clear and serious: the situation is worse than previously assessed, the window for action is narrowing, and the consequences of inaction will be severe and in some cases irreversible.

Here are the ten key findings every informed citizen should know.

---

## 1. We have already warmed the planet by 1.1 degrees, and the effects are visible everywhere

Human activity has raised global average temperatures by 1.1 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels. This may sound modest, but it has already triggered changes to the climate system that are unprecedented over centuries and millennia. Every region of the world is affected, from rising sea levels and disappearing Arctic ice to more frequent and intense extreme weather events.

---

## 2. Impacts on people and ecosystems are worse than scientists expected

The report finds that the damage being caused by climate change is more widespread and more severe than previous assessments anticipated. Ecosystems are being disrupted, livelihoods lost, and communities fragmented. Critically, future risks will escalate rapidly with every additional fraction of a degree of warming. The difference between 1.5 and 2 degrees is not small. It is the difference between manageable and devastating for many parts of the world.

---

## 3. Adaptation works, but it is severely underfunded

At least 170 countries now include adaptation measures in their climate policies. But in most cases, these measures remain small in scale, reactive, and focused on near-term risks rather than long-term resilience. The gap between the adaptation taking place and what is actually needed is largely a funding problem.

> The IPCC estimates that developing countries alone will need 127 billion dollars per year by 2030 and 295 billion dollars per year by 2050 to adapt to climate change. Between 2017 and 2018, adaptation finance reached only 23 to 46 billion dollars, covering just 4 to 8% of what is tracked as climate finance overall.

The good news, according to the report, is that proven and affordable adaptation solutions already exist. With sufficient finance and political will, they can build resilience while also delivering broader development benefits.

---

## 4. For some communities, it is already too late to adapt

Some climate impacts are now so severe that no adaptation strategy can fully prevent losses and damages. Coastal communities in tropical regions have seen coral reef systems that once supported local food security largely destroyed. Low-lying communities have been forced to relocate and abandon cultural sites. These are not distant scenarios. They are happening now, with just 1.1 degrees of warming.

The IPCC distinguishes between "soft" limits to adaptation, where solutions exist but financial or political barriers prevent their use, and "hard" limits, where no available strategy can avoid the damage. Both are already being reached in some parts of the world.

---

## 5. Emissions must peak before 2025 to stay below 1.5 degrees

The IPCC finds there is a greater than 50% chance that global warming will reach or exceed 1.5 degrees Celsius between 2021 and 2040. Under a high-emissions scenario, this threshold could be crossed even sooner. By the end of the century, temperatures in that scenario could rise to between 3.3 and 5.7 degrees.

To keep warming to 1.5 degrees, emissions must peak immediately and decline by 43% by 2030 and 60% by 2035, relative to 2019 levels. The last time global temperatures were more than 2.5 degrees above pre-industrial levels was over three million years ago.

---

## 6. Fossil fuels must be phased out, and quickly

Fossil fuels are the primary cause of the climate crisis. The carbon budget remaining to stay within 1.5 degrees is around 510 billion tonnes of CO2. But emissions from existing and already planned fossil fuel infrastructure alone could reach 850 billion tonnes, exceeding that budget by a wide margin.

The solutions are clear: retire existing fossil fuel infrastructure early, cancel new projects, retrofit where necessary with carbon capture technology, and scale up renewables. Solar and wind are now cheaper than fossil fuels in many parts of the world. The barriers to transition are increasingly political and financial, not technical.

---

## 7. Deep cuts are needed across the entire economy, not just energy

While fossil fuels dominate the picture, emissions cuts are needed across all sectors. Power generation, buildings, industry, and transport together account for close to 80% of global emissions. Agriculture, forestry, and land use account for the rest. There is no pathway to net zero that relies on changes in only one sector.

---

## 8. Carbon removal is now unavoidable

Even with rapid emissions reductions across all sectors, the IPCC finds that all credible pathways to limiting warming to 1.5 degrees require some level of carbon removal. This includes both natural approaches, such as restoring forests and improving soil carbon, and emerging technologies that capture carbon dioxide directly from the atmosphere.

The amount of removal required depends on how quickly emissions fall. Estimates range from 5 to 16 billion tonnes of CO2 per year by mid-century.

---

## 9. Climate finance must increase dramatically this decade

Current financial flows for fossil fuels still far exceed those directed toward climate mitigation and adaptation. Although climate finance has grown by around 60% since the previous IPCC assessment, it remains far below what is needed.

To achieve mitigation goals alone, climate finance must increase between three and six times by 2030. The gap is most extreme in the regions that need it most. Mitigation investment in Southeast Asia and the Pacific needs to increase at least sixfold by 2030. In Africa, fivefold. In the Middle East, fourteenfold. For agriculture and land use, recent financial flows are 10 to 31 times below what the Paris Agreement requires.

---

## 10. Climate change will deepen inequality unless we act differently

The wealthiest 10% of households are responsible for more than 45% of global greenhouse gas emissions. The poorest 50% are responsible for at most 15%. Yet climate impacts fall hardest on the poorest and most vulnerable.

Between 3.3 and 3.6 billion people currently live in countries that are highly vulnerable to climate impacts. The regions most at risk include the Arctic, Central and South America, small island states, South Asia, and much of sub-Saharan Africa. Without deliberate action to ensure a fair and equitable transition, climate change will widen existing inequalities rather than reduce them.

---

## Conclusion

The IPCC's Sixth Assessment Report leaves little room for ambiguity. The science is settled, the risks are escalating, and the solutions are largely known. What remains missing is the scale and speed of action required. Every fraction of a degree of warming avoided matters. Every year of delay makes the challenge harder and more costly.
