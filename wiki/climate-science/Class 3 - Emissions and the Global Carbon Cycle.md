## 1. Comparing Emissions
[LINK](https://textbook.terra.do/class/m-climate-science3-2?r=https://app.terra.do/app/learning/86/my-schedule#m-climate-science3-2)

### Comparing different types of Emissions
#### Carbon Dioxide
When you drive your car and pump out CO2 through the tailpipe, more than half of that CO2 is absorbed by carbon sinks like plants and trees or dissolves into the ocean over a period of 100 years.

> The rest stays in the atmosphere for 300 to 1,000+ years, according to [NASA](https://climate.nasa.gov/news/2915/the-atmosphere-getting-a-handle-on-carbon-dioxide/#:~:text=Once%20it's%20added%20to%20the,timescale%20of%20many%20human%20lives.). This means that once carbon dioxide is in the air, it can continue to affect climate for a _very_ long time

#### Not all GHGs persist the same way
Different GHGs have different lifetimes in the atmosphere. 

> Methane (CH4), for instance, only stays in the atmosphere for an average of 12 years. It is chemically removed fairly quickly.

**Methane **also differs from CO2 in its ability to absorb energy—that is, its "radiative efficiency." It is a much more "potent" GHG than CO2. While it is in the atmosphere, methane traps, on average, 83 times the heat (or infrared radiation) that an equivalent mass of CO2 would.

Every GHG has a different radiative efficiency.

#### About Carbon Dioxide Equivalent

**Establishing a common denomination for different greenhouse gases**

CO2 is the most abundant GHG produced by human activities, and the most important pollutant to address for limiting dangerous climate change. However, human beings create and emit numerous other GHGs, most of which have a far greater heat-trapping effect, pound for pound, than CO2. The most prevalent of these gases are methane (CH4), nitrous oxide (N2O), hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs), perfluorocarbons (PFCs), nitrogen trifluoride (NF3), and sulfur hexafluoride (SF6). 
###### *"Fully addressing climate change will require reducing emissions of all GHGs."*

Scientists and policymakers have established “global warming potentials” (GWPs) to express the heat-trapping effects of all GHGs in terms of CO2-equivalents (annotated as “CO2e”). GWPs are defined for different time horizons, to account for differences in the residence time of different gases in the atmosphere. By convention, all carbon offset credits certified under established standards are denominated using 100-year GWPs. This makes is easier to compare the effects of different GHGs and to denominate carbon offset credits in units of CO2-equivalent emission reductions.

[Learn more about GWPs here.](https://www.offsetguide.org/what-is-a-carbon-offset/gwp/)

Not all gaz are equal.It  GWP (Global Warming Potential) is a mix of lifetime and its relative heat-trapping capacity. For instance, Methae is 83 time more powerful than CO2, but only stays in ttmosphere 12 years. To compare those, we talk about CO2e

- GWPs are expressed in terms of the equivalent mass of CO2 that would cause the same amount of warming. This is denoted as **CO****2****e**, or carbon dioxide equivalent.
- This table uses CO2 as the reference gas. Note how its value is 1 across the top row; we've marked these cells in pink.
- Working across the next two rows, for methane (CH4), the first column shows its average lifetime—around 12 years, as we've marked in green. We then have values for 20-year, 100-year, and 500-year GWPs, which we've marked in blue. 
- GTP stands for global temperature change potential, defined as "the ratio of change in global mean surface temperature at a chosen point in time from the substance of interest relative to that from CO2". 
- There are significant uncertainties related to both GWP and GTP.
- Some gases are removed faster than others, so the GWP of a given gas varies depending on what timescale you're considering. For instance, looking at the orange box we've added, methane's GWP 100 is lower than its GWP 20 because, as Dr. Zeke Hausfather [puts it](https://twitter.com/hausfath/status/1425572803508465664?s=20), "methane is temporary, while CO2 is forever."
- Note the halocarbons—HFC-134a and CFC-11—in the red box. These are chiefly used as refrigerants, and while we don't emit a lot of them, they are POTENT! In fact, Project Drawdown rates improved [refrigerant management](https://www.drawdown.org/solutions/refrigerant-management) among the most impactful climate solutions. The global agreement known as the [Kigali Amendment,](https://www.fluorocarbons.org/environment/climate-change/kigali-amendment/) which to date includes [155](https://treaties.un.org/pages/ViewDetails.aspx?src=TREATY&mtdsg_no=XXVII-2-f&chapter=27&clang=_en#:~:text=%3A-,Parties%20%3A%20155,-Text) countries, represents a landmark global effort to eliminate these pollutants. 
- GWP-100 is the most commonly used metric for policy analysis and decisions.

### So... what should we be doing about methane leakage and emissions?

As we mentioned, methane is a potent—albeit short-lived—GHG. And as shown here, its effect on temperature has been appreciable, though less than that of CO2.

This [Twitter thread](https://twitter.com/hausfath/status/1425572803508465664?s=20) by Dr. Zeke Hausfather provides an excellent short explainer on how to think of CH4 vs. CO2 when it comes to climate action. The main takeaway of Dr. Hausfather's analysis is: "We should work to cut methane emissions quickly, but not at the expense of cutting CO2." That said, [other researchers demonstrate](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2123536119) that "non-CO2 greenhouse gases (GHGs) are responsible for nearly half of all climate forcing from GHG," and say the importance of non-CO2 pollutants, especially short-lived ones like methane, has been underrepresented in climate mitigation.

Fossil fuel operations are among the [most significant sources](https://twitter.com/gcarbonproject/status/1529357157388648448?s=20&t=DKuSdRmGl2NpWYo9aLItCg) of human-caused methane emissions, alongside agriculture, which we discuss more in a later class. The [IEA estimates](https://www.iea.org/reports/global-methane-tracker-2023) that the energy sector accounts for 40% of methane emissions from human activities—and that total methane emissions are [70% higher](https://www.iea.org/news/methane-emissions-from-the-energy-sector-are-70-higher-than-official-figures) than what national governments have reported. And did you know that your gas stove leaks methane, even [when it’s turned off](https://www.npr.org/2022/01/27/1075874473/gas-stoves-climate-change-leak-methane)?

Technologies like infrared cameras help us “see” these leaks. Check out the work of [Kairos Aerospace](https://kairosaerospace.com/methane-detection/) for another example of innovation in detecting methane leaks. The IEA has also published a [guide](https://www.iea.org/reports/curtailing-methane-emissions-from-fossil-fuel-operations) on curtailing methane emissions from fossil fuel operations.
 ![[Screenshot 2024-01-25 at 13.15.46.png]]
 #Wetlands #Methane 

Current methane concentrations in the atmosphere are at [record-high levels](https://phys.org/news/2022-01-methane-atmosphere-all-time-high-climate.html). While it’s critical to address direct emissions from fossil fuel and agricultural systems, it's also worth noting climate feedbacks are contributing to this rise. We discuss feedbacks more in a later class, but for now it's worth noting a 2022 [study](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-022-31345-w.pdf) found that rising temperatures can help produce _more_ methane—for instance, by speeding up microbe activity in wetlands. At the same time, methane _removal_ is slowed down by increasing numbers of wildfires. In a [story](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2022/jul/05/global-heating-causes-methane-growth-four-times-faster-than-thought-study) in the _Guardian_, one study author called the results "shocking."

### _Any_ amount of CO2 emissions adds to global warming.

Some recent research suggests the trend in global annual CO2 emissions has been essentially flat for the last decade, as [this piece in](https://www.carbonbrief.org/global-co2-emissions-have-been-flat-for-a-decade-new-data-reveals) _[Carbon Brief](https://www.carbonbrief.org/global-co2-emissions-have-been-flat-for-a-decade-new-data-reveals)_ discusses—meaning we’re not increasing annual emissions of CO2 more than the year before. While that would be better news than total emissions continuing to grow, _even steady state emissions contribute to warming._ In fact, even a _declining_ rate of emissions will still contribute to warming, just at a slower rate. 

 

Why? Because CO2 has a long life in the atmosphere, every single extra molecule we put up there generates additional warming. So really, what we care about is the trend in _concentration_ of GHGs, and especially CO2, in the atmosphere. And that trajectory is alarming. 
The only way to arrest climate change is to get to _net-zero emissions:_ cutting greenhouse gas emissions to as close to zero as possible, with any remaining emissions re-absorbed from the atmosphere.

## 2. The Global Carbon Cycle 
[LINK](https://textbook.terra.do/class/m-climate-science3-3)

> - 5.2 GT surplus Gt of C emitted every year
> - 55% absorbed, 25% from the ocean, 30% from land
> - Acidification of the ocean is an issue. some Sink are starting to become net emitter instead. Permafrost in polar latitude could thraw and realase vast amount of Methane

The Global Carbon Cycle means that there are stock of carbon in the air, land and water, stocked for a varying lengths of time, and there are flux from one stock to another. 
- There are **stocks** of carbon in the air, land, and water, denoted by circles. This is carbon that is stored for varying lengths of time in these different places. Stocks have units of mass—typically gigatonnes of carbon (GtC). 1 Gt is 1 x 109 metric tons. (If you're used to thinking of tons as weighing 2,000 pounds, a metric ton, or tonne, is 1,000 kilograms, or about 2,205 pounds.)
- If a stock is emitting carbon into the atmosphere, it's called a "source:"
- If it removes, it is called a "sink"
- Today about 55% of Carbon is removed, or 5.5Gt of C per year. which leaves a Net extra Carbon of 5.2Gt, added to the existing stock of Carbon

> Carbon is different from CO2> !!!! This is why we talk about CO2 emissions (50GT a year) vs Carbon stock in the atmosphear (880GT accumulated) for a concentration of 412ppm

### Key Take-Away
Not all of this extra carbon stays in the air. About a quarter of it ends up in the ocean, and roughly 30 percent is absorbed by plants and trees. This leaves some [44 percent](https://essd.copernicus.org/articles/14/4811/2022/#:~:text=trend%2C%20remaining%20at-,around%2044%E2%80%89%25,-%2C%20albeit%20showing%20a) of our CO2 emissions piling up in the atmosphere, netting out to more than 5 billion metric tons of carbon added every year on average over the last decade. The rate of absorption by vegetation and in the oceans has increased over time, as we have continued adding more carbon to the atmosphere.

### Worries about reliability of our Carbon Sinks
BUT... existing sinks and stores may become less reliable.

- Wildfires are one example of this. As wildfires become more frequent and intense, more trees that had been absorbing and storing carbon are burned, releasing that carbon straight back into the atmosphere.
- There is evidence, in fact, that the entire Amazon rainforest may be [turning into a carbon source](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2021/jul/14/amazon-rainforest-now-emitting-more-co2-than-it-absorbs) as opposed to being a sink. We will discuss sinks in much more detail in a later class on carbon removal.
- And in California, emissions from the massive wildfires of 2020 were more than enough to [cancel out](https://twitter.com/globalecoguy/status/1432550767790108675?s=11) the state's years-long efforts to reduce GHGs. A 2022 [analysis](https://carbonplan.org/research/fire-forests-inventories) by CarbonPlan found that in light of the prior fire season, "California forests are likely to be seen as a net source of emissions, not a sink," as they discuss in [this Twitter thread](https://twitter.com/carbonplanorg/status/1509296676145487874).
- **Ocean acidification:** As the ocean absorbs carbon, it becomes more acidic. This is already affecting coral reefs and broader food chains, and it may lead to new feedbacks and complications that are [hard to predict](https://www.pmel.noaa.gov/co2/story/Ocean+Carbon+Uptake).
- **Permafrost:** The frozen soil layer found in polar regions could warm and thaw, releasing vast amounts of methane.

See https://essd.copernicus.org/articles/14/4811/2022/ for details on the Carbon accountings for 2022.
