### Summary

Heating is one of Ireland's most overlooked climate problems. It accounts for nearly a quarter of all national greenhouse gas emissions, it is still dominated by oil and fossil fuels, and emissions from the sector have actually been rising since 2014. The SEAI National Heat Study, commissioned by the Sustainable Energy Authority of Ireland, provides the most comprehensive analysis to date of what it will take to decarbonise heating by 2050. The findings are clear: current policy is not enough, and the pace of change needs to accelerate significantly.

_Source: [SEAI National Heat Study](https://www.seai.ie/data-and-insights/national-heat-study/key-insights)_

---

### Key Numbers

- Heat-related CO2 emissions account for **38% of total energy-related CO2 emissions** in Ireland, or **24% of total national greenhouse gas emissions**
- Emissions from the heating sector have increased by **13% since 2014**
- **Oil** is the dominant heating fuel in Irish homes, meeting approximately **41% of residential energy demand**
- **Electricity** accounts for around **25%** of home energy use, **natural gas** around **19%**, and coal and peat a combined **15%**
- Ireland's renewable energy share in the heat sector is just **6.8%**, compared to an EU average of **22%**
- **Residential heating** makes up around **50% of total heat demand**
- Heat pumps are projected to cover **12 to 20%** of heating demand by 2030 and **33 to 38%** by 2050
- District heating has the potential to meet up to **50% of building heating demand** in Ireland
- Ireland's legally binding target is to cut emissions by **51% from 2018 levels by 2030**, and reach net zero by **2050**

---

### Why Heating Is Such a Problem in Ireland

Ireland heats its homes and buildings differently from most of its European neighbours. Oil boilers remain by far the most common heating system, particularly outside urban areas. Natural gas is concentrated in cities. Peat and coal, both high-carbon fuels, still represent a significant share of the mix.

Reducing greenhouse gas emissions from the energy used for heating and cooling is a difficult challenge, and this study shows that current Climate Action measures are unlikely to deliver enough cuts from heat-related CO2 emissions. Unlike electricity generation, where wind and solar have made rapid progress in Ireland, the heating sector has not seen a comparable shift. Efficiency improvements in buildings have been offset by increased heating demand, and the stock of oil boilers remains very large.

The problem is structural. Most heating systems have lifetimes of 15 to 20 years. Decisions made today about what gets installed in homes, offices, and factories will lock in emissions for decades. This is why the study emphasises the urgency of acting now rather than waiting for better or cheaper technologies.

---

### What Needs to Change

The study identifies several areas where action is most critical.

**Heat pumps are the central technology for buildings.** In every scenario modelled by SEAI, heat pumps play a leading role in decarbonising homes and commercial buildings. They are more efficient than fossil fuel boilers and run on electricity, which is becoming cleaner as Ireland's grid expands renewable capacity. Heat pumps will play a crucial role in rapid decarbonisation, and the study suggests that undertaking energy efficiency measures in homes before or alongside installation improves outcomes significantly. Insulation, windows, and fabric improvements make heat pumps more effective and lower running costs.

**District heating has significant untapped potential.** This is one of the more underexplored findings of the study. District heating, where a network of insulated pipes delivers heat from a central source to multiple buildings, could meet up to 50% of building heating demand in Ireland. It is already widely used in Scandinavia and parts of central Europe. In Ireland, it has barely been developed. The study calls for policy frameworks to address the planning, regulatory, and financing barriers that are holding it back.

**Bioenergy and biomethane will play a supporting role.** Sustainable bioenergy, including biomethane produced from agricultural and food waste, can help decarbonise parts of the heat sector where electrification is harder, particularly in industry and in rural areas not connected to a gas grid. However, the study is clear that the availability of sustainable biomethane is limited and depends on land-use choices. It cannot be treated as a straightforward replacement for natural gas at scale.

**The gas grid's role will shrink.** In all scenarios modelled, the use of the existing gas network declines as buildings switch to heat pumps and other low-carbon technologies. The future of the grid depends on whether it can be repurposed to carry biomethane or hydrogen, but this transition needs to be actively planned. Continuing to connect new buildings to gas infrastructure now risks creating assets that become stranded within the lifetime of the pipes.

**Fossil fuels need a phase-out date.** The pace of fossil fuel phase-out must increase, and fossil fuels must begin to be phased out before 2025. The study calls for a clear timetable for ending fossil fuel use across all heating sectors. Without this signal, investment in low-carbon alternatives will be slower and more uncertain.

---

### The Role of the Electricity Grid

One aspect of the heat transition that often gets overlooked is its dependency on electricity. If Ireland is going to heat its buildings with heat pumps, it needs a grid that can supply clean electricity at the scale required. The study is explicit that decarbonising the electricity grid is not just an energy sector priority; it is a prerequisite for decarbonising heat.

This creates a link between different parts of Ireland's climate strategy that needs to be managed together. Expanding offshore wind, improving grid infrastructure, and deploying battery storage are all relevant to whether the shift to heat pumps actually results in lower emissions.

---

### What This Means for Industry

The challenge in the industrial sector is different from buildings. Industrial processes often require very high temperatures that heat pumps cannot reach. Technology lifetimes are longer, meaning companies are less frequently in a position to switch. And the cost of transition can be significant.

The study identifies carbon capture, utilisation, and storage (CCUS) as likely to be needed in some industrial settings to achieve full decarbonisation by 2050. This is particularly relevant for sectors like cement and food processing, where some emissions cannot be eliminated through fuel switching alone. Policy decisions on how CCUS will be regulated and supported in Ireland remain to be made.

---

### The Investment Picture

The scale of investment required is significant. The study calls for major capital spending across energy efficiency improvements, low-carbon heating technologies, and infrastructure upgrades. This investment needs to flow into homes, businesses, and industrial sites simultaneously over the coming decades.

For households, the main barriers are upfront cost, access to reliable contractors, and uncertainty about the best long-term option. SEAI grant schemes for heat pump installation and home insulation are already in place, but uptake needs to increase substantially. The study stresses the importance of long-term and stable policy signals: if homeowners and businesses are not confident that support schemes and market conditions will hold over time, they will delay decisions.

---

### Where Ireland Stands Against Its Own Targets

Ireland has enacted legally binding climate targets to achieve net zero by 2050 and cut emissions by 51% from 2018 levels by 2030, aligned with its commitments under the Paris Agreement and the EU's objective to cut emissions by 55% from 1990 levels by 2030.

The heating sector is not currently contributing its proportional share of those cuts. An unprecedented ramp-up of effort and additional measures are needed if the heat sector is to deliver its share of the emissions reductions. The study was published in 2022, which means Ireland has already used several of the years available before the 2030 deadline.

The direction is clear. The gap between where the sector is and where it needs to be is large but not unbridgeable. What is required is faster deployment of technologies that already exist, a clear end date for fossil fuel heating systems, and sustained investment backed by credible long-term policy.
