The [Climate Change in the Irish Mind Wave 2 Report](https://dcu.us5.list-manage.com/track/click?u=3c7ea21dfd519e320c706eaa5&id=79a392200f&e=08c8e4a3e4) was released late last month and has revealed broadly encouraging statistics. Across the 1,355 respondents in Ireland, 95% have accepted climate change as reality.  
  
Nevertheless, still only 53% of Irish respondents see climate change as mostly caused by human activity, with 39% seeing the cause partly due to human behavior and partly to natural changes. While absolute climate change denial is now only at 5% of the population, the relative high number of respondents that identify the cause of climate change as only partially due to human factors is worrying.  
  
If we consider one of the most commonly employed and validated models explaining human behavior, the theory of planned behavior, this raises a number of questions. Core to this model is the assumption that behavior is enacted if individuals judge it as a) positive, b) societally normative, and c) within their control. To some extent it makes sense to break down the report from this perspective to understand where advances are being made and where gaps still exist in the psycho-social factors motivating climate change preventive behavior.  

> [!Key Stats]
> - 95% have accepted climate change as reality
> -  5% climate Denial only
> - 53% see climnate change mostly caused by human activities
> - 79% support government priorities, but seem that those should be that one that less Directly impact them. 
> - **Many participants (33%) also discuss climate change often or at least occasionally (45%) with friends and family.** 
> - 28% believe that other citizens need to do more, while only 16% believe THEY should be doing more. 



On a high level, Irish respondents are both worried about climate change and support actions that prevent further climate deterioration. The majority of respondents believe that climate change is harming people in Ireland right now (54%), that it will harm future generations a great deal (83%), and also see climate change as very important or extremely important to them (62%).  
  
Based on this, it is not unexpected that the majority of respondents believe that climate change should be a priority for the government (79%). **Yet, when we look at the specific interventions respondents support, they seem to be ones which have a limited direct impact on themselves, such as greater investment in environmentally friendly transport or funds to support the sale of electric vehicles. In contrast, they are more sceptical about interventions such as banning oil or peat for home heating or higher taxes on diesel.**  
  
News about climate change has become a part of daily life for the majority of respondents with most hearing about it on the news at least once a week (74%). **Many participants (33%) also discuss climate change often or at least occasionally (45%) with friends and family.**  
  
Taken together, Irish respondents see climate change as worth acting on both personally and as a social norm, fulfilling two of the core components underpinning action according to the theory of planned behavior.  

  
> So, what is stopping us from action? While the factors are probably multi-faceted and nuanced, it seems from the report that participants might not perceive themselves as having agency to act. If we look at respondents’ opinions on who should do more, they believe that other citizens should do much more (28%) while substantially fewer participants believe they themselves should be doing much more (16%). Looking at specific acts, only 12% of respondents regularly use their economic power to punish companies damaging the environment by boycotting them or specifically buying from companies that act environmentally friendly (17%).  

###### substantially fewer participants believe they themselves should be doing much more (16%)


  
In summary, we see progress being made in supporting Irish respondents to act on climate change, the attitude and norms are there, but **perceived agency might still be lacking**. Scientists are in a prime position to address this as a wide margin of respondents trust them the most (61%).
