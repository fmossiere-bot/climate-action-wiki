## Summary Electricity Capacity
Ireland’s current **installed electricity capacity** (the maximum power it can produce at any moment) is approximately:

- **~13.3 GW total** capacity (2022 figure) [seai.ie+11en.wikipedia.org+11climatecouncil.ie+11](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electricity_generation?utm_source=chatgpt.com). This matches more recent estimates around 13 GW .
- Of that, about **7.36 GW is fully dispatchable** (i.e., gas, coal, oil, biomass, reliable on-demand plants) based on 2022‑23 CRU/EirGrid data [seai.ie](https://www.seai.ie/sites/default/files/publications/Energy-in-Ireland-2023.pdf?utm_source=chatgpt.com).
- The remainder is **intermittent renewables**, including:
    - **~4.8 GW wind** in Republic of Ireland (2023 figure), with the entire island at ~5 GW onshore capacity; offshore still in development [strategicenergy.eu+15en.wikipedia.org+15kpmg.com+15](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renewable_energy_in_the_Republic_of_Ireland?utm_source=chatgpt.com).
    - **Solar PV** is still modest, with grid-scale peaks ~750 MW and small-scale output of ~560 MW (~0.56 GW) .

As of end-2023, **energy storage** capacity (batteries) stood at around **0.72 GWh**, set to grow to ~1.7 GWh by end-2025 [energy-storage.news](https://www.energy-storage.news/ireland-utility-scale-energy-storage-forecast-to-exceed-1-5gwh-in-2025/?utm_source=chatgpt.com).

### 🌐 Converting to GWh

- **Capacity (GW)** refers to power (instantaneous output), not energy (GWh).
- But you can estimate **potential energy output**:
    - If Ireland ran its full 13.3 GW capacity **continuously for an hour**, that equals **13.3 GWh** energy output in that hour
    - Over a full day: 13.3 GW × 24 h ≈ **319 GWh/day**.
    - Over a year: 13.3 GW × 8,760 h ≈ **116,500 GWh/year** (116 TWh).

### Imports

> [!How much of our energy is imported? ]
> - Ireland imports a little over 70% of the energy we use. This is well over the EU average, which is 58%.  
> - There are just seven other countries out of the EU 27 member states who import more than Ireland. They are Malta, Cyprus, Luxembourg, Greece, Belgium, Lithuania and Italy.

## What are the Opportunities and Challenges
#### 1. Opportunities
- Ireland has a massive opportunity with Wind energy, especially off shore, following the lead of other countries such as Denmark and the UK. Ireland has high wind and a long coast line. 
- Bio energy, is also a more debated opportunity and potential 
- There is definitely scope for solar houses, and we start to see some Solar Farms, and as well as Bybrid Land with solar. There is a grant and a along way to go, and the lack of sun makes it a more residential solution than a mass production one. 
- There is large scope to reduce demand throuh house renovation for instance. So home imprvoement has a large scope, so does transportation to reduce car transport

#### 2. Challenges
- Permit for Wind Farms. Wind engery could represent close to 90% of Ireland energy by 2050, but planning permission seems to be very complex these dauys, and is probably slowing us down
- Controversy on the bio production, and how much real potential there is, and the land it would require vs our effort to reforest.
