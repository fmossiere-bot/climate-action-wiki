> The National Heat Study provides a rigorous and comprehensive analysis of the options to reduce CO₂ emissions associated with heating in Ireland.

This summary report sets out the key insights from the National Heat Study. It details the challenges policymakers face across sectors and technologies and outlines the actions and decisions they can take now to deliver on the Climate Action legislation ambition. The report also identifies some areas that require immediate further investigation now and it highlights areas where there are interdependencies across policy goals.

## Some of the Key Findings

The sources offer a comprehensive analysis of Ireland's transition to a net-zero emissions energy system by 2050, focusing on the challenges and opportunities in decarbonizing the heating sector. Here are some of the key insights:
- Rising heat-related CO2 emissions: Despite progress in energy efficiency, heat-related CO2 emissions have been rising since 2014. These emissions make up a significant portion of Ireland's total emissions.12
- Need for policy changes: Current policy measures are unlikely to achieve the necessary emissions reductions in the heating sector. This suggests a need to evolve energy efficiency and fabric-first principles to focus on more rapidly replacing fossil fuel heating systems.
- ###### **District heating potential:** District heating has significant potential in Ireland, with the capacity to meet about 50% of building heating demand. Policy frameworks are needed to address barriers related to planning, regulation, and financing to realize this potential.
- Importance of bioenergy: Sustainable bioenergy will play an important role in decarbonizing heating, but robust sustainability governance is needed to ensure its environmental integrity. 
	- ###### The availability of sustainable biomethane is linked to land-use choices and requires increased productivity in the agricultural sector. It can be a competitive option if the costs and benefits are shared across all gas grid users or used off-grid to displace oil.
- ###### **Prominence of heat pumps**: Heat pumps are a key technology for decarbonizing buildings and will see substantial deployment in all scenarios.These are a prominent renewable technology, covering approximately 12-20% of heating demand in 2030 and projected to grow to 33-38% by 2050 in various decarbonization scenarios.
- Crucial role of electricity grid decarbonization: Decarbonizing the electricity grid is essential for reducing heat-related emissions, as electricity use for heating is expected to increase significantly.1718
- Declining role of the gas grid: The use of the gas grid is projected to decline in all scenarios as buildings transition to other heating technologies. Policy planning is needed to manage this transition and to determine the future role of the gas grid in supplying biomethane and hydrogen.
- Need for fossil fuel phase-out: A timetable for phasing out fossil fuels in all sectors is needed to achieve net-zero emissions. The timing of this phase-out presents challenges, particularly for the industry sector, where technology lifetimes are longer.
- Role of carbon capture, utilization, and storage (CCUS): CCUS and negative emission technologies are likely to be required to achieve economy-wide carbon neutrality. Policy decisions are needed to determine the role and deployment of CCUS and to address regulatory and financial barriers.
- Capital investment required: Significant capital investment will be needed in energy efficiency improvements, low-carbon heating technologies, and infrastructure to achieve net-zero emissions in the heating sector.31
- Need for long-term policy signals: Given the long lifetimes of heating systems, long-term and stable policy signals are needed to encourage investment in low-carbon options and to support the development of robust supply chains.32
- Economic impacts of decarbonization: Further research is needed to understand the broader economic impacts of deep decarbonization pathways on economic growth, government finances, and energy demand.33

These insights highlight the complexities and opportunities of decarbonizing the heating sector in Ireland. Addressing these challenges through policy changes, technological advancements, and stakeholder collaboration will be essential for achieving a net-zero emissions energy system by 2050.

## Energy Used for Heating Buildings in Ireland

> The sources state that heat-related CO2 emissions come from the combustion of fossil fuels in homes, businesses, and industry, and indirectly from the use of electricity for heating. 


> [!NOTE] Heat Relate CO2
> - Heat-related CO2 emissions make up 38% of total energy-related CO2 emissions, or 24% of total national greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions 
> - Residential Heat represent 50% of the demand
> ![[Screenshot 2024-11-08 at 09.56.25.png]]


The main sources for heating buildings in Ireland today include a mix of fossil fuels and renewable technologies. As of 2022, the energy sources used for heating homes in Ireland are distributed as follows:
Source: [Sustainable Energy Authority of Ireland](https://www.seai.ie/sites/default/files/publications/Energy-in-Ireland-2022.pdf
- **Oil**: Approximately 41% of residential energy demand is met by oil, making it the dominant heating source.
- **Electricity**: Accounts for about 25% of home energy use.
- **Natural Gas**: Contributes around 19% to residential energy consumption.
- **Coal and Peat**: Combined, these solid fuels make up approximately 15% of the energy used in Irish households.
- **Renewables**: The use of renewable energy sources, including biomass and heat pumps, is growing but still represents a small fraction of the total residential energy mix.
