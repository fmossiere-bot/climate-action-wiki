### Challenges of Climate Change on Water Resources
- Climate change will pose significant risks to water management and will **exacerbate existing pressures** in terms of water supply, quality and flooding.
- In combination with projected increases in evapotranspiration, projected decreases in summer and autumn precipitation will result in **reductions in available water resources**.
- Projected increases in temperature will results in **increased algal/plant growth with detrimental impacts** for water quality.
- The frequency and duration of **low river flows are likely to increase** in many areas and will **exacerbate many forms of water pollution and contamination**.
- Increasing climate-change induced demands (e.g. irrigation) on water resources during the summer months will put **already depleted water supply under pressure**.
- Projected increases in levels of winter precipitation will put **areas already prone to flooding at increased risk** while **areas not previously subject to flooding will also become subject to flood risk**.

### Environmental Crisis at Lough Neagh
Source: https://www.breakingnews.ie/ireland/muir-tells-mlas-of-sense-of-shame-over-environmental-crisis-at-lough-neagh-1597316.html

Lough Neagh is facing an unprecedented crisis, with the development of Algae which is affecting the quality of the water, and putting at risk its biodiversity. 
###### Lough Neagh is the biggest freshwater lake, by surface area, in Ireland and Britain, supplies 40 per cent of the North’s drinking water, and sustains a major eel-fishing industry.

#### What is causing this? 
- Noxious blooms covered large parts of the lough last summer, and also affected other waterways and beaches in the region.
- Nitrogen and phosphorus from agricultural fertiliser running off fields were believed to be a major contributory factor.
- The spread of the invasive zebra mussel species is also understood to have played a role, as they have made the water clearer, allowing more sunlight to penetrate, stimulating more algal photosynthesis.
- Climate change is another factor cited, with the highest water temperature at Lough Neagh recorded last June.
