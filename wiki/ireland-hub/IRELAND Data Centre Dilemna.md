## What about ICELAND as the new Eldorado for Data CEnters
Iceland has indeed become an increasingly popular location for data center operations, and there are several compelling reasons why companies are considering Iceland as an attractive option:

#iceland 
### Renewable Energy

Iceland's electricity is generated almost entirely from renewable sources—approximately 75% from hydropower and 25% from geothermal power. This abundance of renewable energy is a significant attraction for data centers, which are intensive energy users and are increasingly seeking to reduce their carbon footprint in response to global climate change concerns.

### Natural Cooling

Iceland's cool climate offers natural cooling for data center operations, significantly reducing the energy required for cooling systems. This can lead to substantial savings in operating costs and further decrease the environmental impact of data centers.

### Stability

Iceland is known for its political, economic, and geological stability, making it a safe and reliable location for data centers. The country is situated in a region with low risk of natural disasters, such as earthquakes and hurricanes, that could disrupt operations.

### Connectivity

Recent years have seen improvements in Iceland's connectivity to North America and Europe through high-capacity submarine fiber optic cables. Good connectivity is crucial for data centers to ensure fast and reliable data transmission to and from the facility.

### Competitive Costs

While the cost of building data centers in Iceland may be higher due to its remote location, the lower operating costs, primarily from savings on cooling and competitive electricity pricing, can make it an economically viable option in the long term.

### Government Support

The Icelandic government supports the development of data centers, recognizing the economic benefits and the opportunity to position Iceland as a leader in green data storage and processing. This support can translate into incentives and assistance for companies looking to establish operations in Iceland.

### Market Response

Several international companies have already established data centers in Iceland, drawn by these advantages. The success and positive experiences of these early adopters are encouraging more companies to explore Iceland as a potential location for data center expansion.

### Conclusion

While Iceland presents a compelling case for data center operations, especially for companies prioritizing sustainability and cost-effectiveness, it's important for each company to evaluate its specific needs, including connectivity requirements, service level agreements, and access to markets. Iceland is indeed becoming a new hub for data centers, but whether it's the best choice depends on the particular criteria and priorities of each data center operator.

## Article on WDHT

[AI, Data Centre and the Water debate.](https://app.wedonthavetime.org/posts/9baa9eb6-dab2-4a6a-9a19-9a5a6460e402)


These days, it is hard not read something around AI, it's impact on energy demand and water. I was surprise during the COP30 coverage to hear from a panel speaker (can't remember the name) that Water and AI is a non-issue. I did find it very dismissive. The HArdfork had a podcast recently with a guest who has been digging more into the topic and I would highly recommend it. Like everything in "Accounting" (whether carbon or water), nothing is black or white. What we need is more honesty and transparency, and may be this will help with the debate. But before we talk more about water, I guess the hotter topic is energy consumption associated with the increase in Data Centres driven (a lot ) by projected AI usage.

1. The Global Picture: Efficiency That Drives More Consumption

I remember, 2 years ago, when people talked about AI building efficiency so we shouldn't worry too much about energy consumption? Data centres worldwide are expected to increase their power demand by **165% by 2030**, according to Goldman Sachs. This isn't despite efficiency improvements – it's because of them.

This is the Jevons Paradox in action: when technology becomes more efficient, it gets cheaper to use, so we use it far more. NVIDIA's new chips might be 20 times more efficient than older processors, but they also consume **300% more power** individually. Instead of doing the same work with less energy, we're building "AI factories" and training ever-larger models.

AI workloads, which currently make up 14% of data centre power use, are projected to jump to **27% by 2027**. The infrastructure is growing so fast that it's outpacing our ability to build clean energy to power it.

In summary, we are using more, because it is cheap. Remember in the US why we built car so inefficients in fuel consumption because fuel was so cheap? we didn't care right?

2. Ireland: A Case Study in Unintended Consequences

Here in Ireland, the situation shows what happens when this growth collides with reality. Data centres now consume **18% of our national electricity** – a share that jumped 31% in 2022 alone. By 2026, the IEA projects this will reach **32%**.

Here's the crucial point: **data centres are eating our renewable energy progress**. Since 2015, nearly all growth in Ireland's electricity demand has come from data centres. This means the wind farms we're building aren't replacing fossil fuels – they're just keeping up with new AI demand. Between 2020 and 2023, only 16% of increased data centre demand was matched by new renewable purchases. The rest came from the standard grid mix, keeping us dependent on gas and coal.

![Image of post in post detailed view](https://storage.googleapis.com/wdht-media/8027aa39f9d742de9524162f7a6423ca_original.jpeg)

The scale is staggering: proposed new data centres could need up to **5,800 MW of power** – almost equal to Ireland's entire peak demand of 6,000 MW.

The government's response? New data centres in Dublin now must build their own power generation. Because renewables are intermittent and nuclear is years away, this often means gas-powered generators. We're essentially forcing facilities to create private power systems because our grid can't handle them.

But at least, I can understand the strategic location of ireland to build some of those Data Centres, from it's European location to its cool climate. But globally, the picture is different.

3. Built in All the Wrong Places

Globally, the location problem is straightforward: **nearly 7,000 of the world's 8,808 data centres** are built in climates that are too hot for efficient cooling. Countries like Singapore, Nigeria, and the UAE have 100% of their centres in "wrong climate" zones.

Why? Because data centres are built where laws require data to be stored (data sovereignty), not where it makes environmental sense. This forces massive energy use for cooling in places like Arizona deserts or humid tropical regions, when these facilities should ideally be in naturally cold climates like Scandinavia.

Power availability has become the main factor in location decisions. It now takes **four years or more** to extend transmission lines to new sites in major hubs. Developers are choosing locations based purely on whether power lines exist there, not whether it's a sensible place to build.

So we build them in the wrong location, and because of that, we need water to cool things down.

4. Water: A Local Crisis Hidden in Global Statistics

The water debate around AI is being dismissed, and I understand why at a global level. A single AI query uses about 2 millilitres of water – you'd need to send 8,000 prompts a day to increase your water footprint by just 1%. AI water use is tiny compared to agriculture.

**But water isn't like electricity – you can't just transmit it across countries.**

The real issue is local. Data centres are being built in water-stressed areas like Arizona and the Thames Valley, where they compete directly with drinking water and farming. In these specific locations, a single large facility can strain an entire watershed.

There are two types of water use:

- **Direct use** for cooling (which evaporates and doesn't return to local sources)

- **Indirect use** at power plants generating the electricity (which accounts for 40% of all US freshwater withdrawals)

As AI drives energy demand up, it drives water withdrawals up at power plants. And with the shift to more powerful chips requiring liquid cooling, direct water use is also increasing.

We are building those data centres in water strain regions, and little transparency is required. In the UK, where you would think that water is widely available, there is growing concern about summer access to water. Without data centre having to report on their water usage (like other industries), it means that we are flying blind ... and concerns are that there could be serious water shortage as soon as 2030

The same as for energy, there should be a pre-requisite to examine water supply in the area, and put some limitation on its consumption (as for other industries). Farming should of course be prioritised. `But more importantly, I don't think we can say that Water and AI is a non-issue, because it is not.
