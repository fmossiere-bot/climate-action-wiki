> 6.6% of the household Carbon Footprint is linked to the furniture, painting and so on that we use inside our houses

### What is it

When we think about our carbon footprint at home, we usually focus on heating, electricity, or the car. But a less obvious part of it comes from what is actually inside our homes: the furniture, the paint on the walls, the floor under our feet, the cushions and curtains around us. All of these products need to be made, transported, and eventually thrown away, and each of those steps has an environmental cost.

Around 6.6% of a household's total carbon footprint comes from furniture, decoration, and home fittings. That might sound small, but it adds up across millions of homes. And unlike energy use, which you can reduce by switching tariffs or improving insulation, this part of your footprint is often completely invisible.

What makes it harder to see is that many of these products contain plastic, and most people do not realise how much. Around 90% of all plastics are derived from fossil fuels, and plastic is not just in packaging. It is in sofas, paint, flooring, shampoo bottles, and synthetic textiles. Choosing more natural or second-hand alternatives is one of the simplest ways to reduce that hidden impact. 

---

### Why it matters

Most people do not think twice about buying a new sofa or repainting a room. But the materials used in everyday home products, such as synthetic paints, particle board, foam cushions, and plastic flooring, often come with hidden costs. Some release chemicals into the air inside your home. Others are made from materials that travel thousands of kilometres before arriving in your house.

The good news is that alternatives are growing. The European bio-composite materials market stood at around $1.86 billion in 2023 and is expected to grow steadily through 2029, driven in part by EU regulations pushing toward a circular economy. Eco-friendly paints, natural fibre flooring, and plant-based cleaning products are no longer niche. They are becoming easier to find and increasingly affordable. 

In France, for example, there has been a big rise in second-hand furniture markets, and more manufacturers are now using natural or recycled materials instead of plastics or synthetic foams. For someone who wants to reduce their environmental impact without overhauling their whole lifestyle, this is a useful entry point. You do not have to renovate your home all at once. Small changes, like buying second-hand, choosing a natural paint, or replacing a synthetic rug with a wool one, can make a real difference over time.

---

### Key facts

- Around 6.6% of a household's carbon footprint is linked to furniture, decoration, and home fittings.
- Around 90% of all plastics are made from fossil fuel derivatives, and plastic is present in many home products beyond packaging, including furniture, flooring, and personal care items. 
- In Europe, wood fibre is now the dominant bio-based composite material, accounting for 58.7% of the European biocomposites market in 2024. 
- The European bio-composite materials market is growing at around 11% per year, driven partly by EU circular economy policy. 
- Eco-friendly paints, such as clay or lime-based products, can significantly reduce volatile organic compounds (VOCs) inside the home, a common source of indoor air pollution.
- In France, the second-hand furniture market has grown sharply, with platforms like Leboncoin, Vinted, and Selency reporting strong annual growth.
