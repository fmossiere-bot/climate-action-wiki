## IMPORTANT FACTS 

> - **1 million plastic bottles** are purchased every minute
> - up to **five trillion plastic bags** are used worldwide every year. 
> - In total, **half of all plastic produced is designed for single-use** purposes, the UNEP confirms.
> - About 400 million tonnes of plastic waste are generated every year.
> - Only **9%** of plastic produced is Recycled
> - About half of all of the plastic waste that ends up in the oceans comes from just five countries: China, Indonesia, the Philippines, Thailand and Vietnam. These countries are experiencing rapid economic growth, but as these economies grow, consumption booms — and so does their use of plastic goods.
> - 500 billion single-use plastic bags are used worldwide every year

![[Screenshot 2023-08-21 at 16.38.33.png]]

> [!NOTE] Some more FACTS about Plastics
> [Plastic production soared from 2 million tonnes in 1950 to 348 million tonnes in 2017, becoming a global industry valued at US$522.6 billion, and it is expected to double in capacity by 2040](https://www.pewtrusts.org/en/research-and-analysis/articles/2020/07/23/breaking-the-plastic-wave-top-findings). The impacts of plastic production and pollution on the triple planetary crisis of climate change, nature loss and pollution are a catastrophe in the making:
> - Exposure to plastics can harm human health, potentially affecting [fertility, hormonal, metabolic and neurological activity](https://www.endocrine.org/-/media/endocrine/files/topics/edc_guide_2020_v1_6chqennew-version.pdf), and open burning of plastics contributes to [air pollution](https://www.unep.org/resources/report/actions-air-quality-global-summary-policies-and-programmes-reduce-air-pollution).
> - [By 2050 greenhouse gas emissions associated with plastic production, use and disposal](https://www.unep.org/resources/pollution-solution-global-assessment-marine-litter-and-plastic-pollution) would account for 15 per cent of allowed emissions, under the goal of limiting global warming to 1.5**°**C (34.7**°**F).
> - [More than 800 marine and coastal species](https://www.cbd.int/doc/publications/cbd-ts-83-en.pdf) are affected by this pollution through ingestion, entanglement, and other dangers.
> - [Some 11 million tonnes of plastic waste flow annually into oceans](https://www.pewtrusts.org/en/research-and-analysis/articles/2020/07/23/breaking-the-plastic-wave-top-findings). This may triple by 2040.
> - A shift to a circular economy [can reduce](https://www.resourcepanel.org/reports/policy-options-eliminate-additional-marine-plastic-litter) the volume of plastics entering oceans by over 80 per cent by 2040; reduce virgin plastic production by 55 per cent; save governments US$70 billion by 2040; reduce greenhouse gas emissions by 25 per cent; and create 700,000 additional jobs – mainly in the global south.
>

### Lobbyist are all over the negotiation room

While the petrostate delegations are power players in the meeting rooms and corridors of the treaty negotiations, one group outnumbers every nation: plastic industry lobbyists. At the December talks in Busan there were a [record 220 corporate lobbyists in attendance](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2024/nov/27/plastic-lobbyists-biggest-group-un-treaty-talks-busan-korea).

That was far more than even the host nation’s delegation of 140 and was three times the number of independent scientists. Dow and ExxonMobil sent nine lobbyists between them, according to an [analysis by the environmental law group Ciel](https://www.ciel.org/news/inc-5-lobbyist-analysis/). Some lobbyists were included in country delegations, rather than with observer organisations, giving them access to sensitive member-state-only sessions, Ciel said.

> More than 30 plastic and chemical industry lobby organisations have joined the negotiations since the start of 2023, almost doubling the total number.

These include the US Plastics Industry Association, Plastics Europe and national plastic industry groups from the UK, Australia, New Zealand, India, Brazil, Colombia, Malaysia and Korea. The [group is co-chaired](https://www.unep.org/civil-society-engagement/major-groups-modalities/major-groups-facilitating-committee-mgfc#Business) by a Saudi official.

> Another lever of influence being pulled by corporate interests is via the system through which Unep gives full access to the negotiations to [civil society sectors](https://www.unep.org/civil-society-engagement/civil-society-engagement) including women, farmers, Indigenous peoples, children and scientists


### Misinformation and misunderstandings

Scientific experts, also keen to share their wealth of knowledge, say they have struggled to keep up with correcting wrong or misleading statements made by industry groups during the talks.

There is no official scientific advisory panel for the treaty. Instead, the self-organised [Scientists’ Coalition for an Effective Plastics Treaty](https://ikhapp.org/scientistscoalition/) (Scept) has attempted to fill the gap. It has 450 members, none funded by industry, and advises the smaller nations plagued by plastic who cannot afford to send scores of delegates to the negotiations.

However, in February 2025, [Scept wrote to the bureau](https://ikhapp.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/Open-letter-to-Bureau-from-Scientists-Coalition_-cc_-chair-secretariat-_-re-observer-access-at-INC5.2.pdf) running the talks and Unep’s Andersen to complain about a lack of access to meetings during the December negotiating round.

“Consequently, our ability to follow the negotiations was severely limited,” the scientists said. “We were unable to identify the knowledge gaps, misunderstandings or misinformation that require clarification, often spread by actors with conflicts of interest.”

Scientists also said their [criticisms of a significant 2023 Unep report](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2023/may/18/scientists-say-they-have-been-locked-out-of-the-room-at-unep-talks-on-plastic-waste) on “how the world can end plastic pollution” were ignored. The scientists said the report failed to reflect the whole range of health and environmental impacts of plastic pollution and was over-optimistic about technical solutions to deal with waste plastic.

Scept experts had been invited to participate before the report’s publication and provided more than 300 comments. Unep said a “technical issue” meant an email containing Scept’s comments was not received in time for publication. It said it had taken feedback from other experts and denied [the report](https://www.unep.org/resources/turning-off-tap-end-plastic-pollution-create-circular-economy) underplayed the impacts of plastic.

### THE GUARDIAN - The Mirage of Recycled Plastic: Greenwashing the Petrochemical Crisis
Source: [The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2026/jan/27/recycled-plastic-packaging-claims-misleading-say-experts)

This investigative report exposes how the petrochemical industry employs **deceptive accounting and intense lobbying** to market plastic packaging as sustainable while it remains predominantly fossil-based. Major oil corporations use a controversial method called **mass-balance bookkeeping** to label products as recycled even when they contain negligible amounts of recovered material, effectively hiding the continued expansion of virgin plastic production. The text highlights a **greenwashing crisis** where energy-intensive chemical recycling and manipulated carbon footprint assessments allow brands to claim environmental benefits that do not exist in reality. Ultimately, the author warns that **impending European regulations** may soon codify these misleading practices, allowing the oil industry to protect its profits under the guise of ecological responsibility.

## Biodegradable Plastic made from Bamboo
*==Source: [NS](https://www.newscientist.com/article/2499052-biodegradable-plastic-made-from-bamboo-is-strong-and-easy-to-recycle/?utm_campaign=RSS%7CNSNS&utm_source=NSNS&utm_medium=RSS&utm_content=currents)==*

###### This Plastic would be STRONG and EASY TO RECYCLE. 

> Bamboo is a highly renewable resource, and its cellulose fibres can be turned into a hard, mouldable plastic for use in cars and appliances

Hard plastic made from bamboo is as strong and durable as conventional plastics for uses such as household appliances and car interiors, but is also recyclable and biodegrades easily [in soil](https://www.newscientist.com/article/2440821-biodegradable-microplastics-may-actually-be-worse-for-soil-and-plants/).
###### *Plastics derived from biological matter, [or bioplastics](https://www.newscientist.com/article/2486653-bioplastic-habitats-on-mars-could-be-built-from-algae/), are increasingly popular, but they still only make up around half a per cent of the more than 400 million tonnes of plastics produced each year.*

A team at a CHina University have developed a way to produce plastic from cellulose derived from bamboo. It is strong and suitable for car parts. While not cheap, it can be completely recylced and keep 90% of its strength, which could make it economically attractive.

### Executive Summary
This comprehensive report **investigates the strategies employed by the plastics industry** to impede meaningful solutions to the escalating plastic pollution crisis. It details how companies often utilize **voluntary commitments and industry-backed alliances as a facade**, while simultaneously **lobbying against proven legislative measures** like deposit return systems and single-use plastic bans. Through global case studies, the document highlights **tactics of delay, distraction (such as blaming consumers or promoting ineffective "end-of-pipe" solutions), and actively undermining legislation**, revealing a pattern of prioritizing business interests over environmental responsibility. The report argues that these **false solutions and deceptive practices** have hindered progress and exacerbated the global issue of plastic waste.
