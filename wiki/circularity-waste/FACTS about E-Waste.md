> [!NOTE] E-Waste - EEE
> The report "Global Transboundary E-waste Flows Monitor - 2022.pdf" defines E-waste as all electrical and electronic equipment (EEE) and its parts that have been discarded by its owner as waste without the intent of reuse. This includes a wide range of products with circuitry or electrical components that have a power or battery supply.


Specifically, the report categorizes e-waste based on the European Union WEEE Directive, and one of these categories is "Large equipment"1.... Under the UNU-KEY system used in the report, "Washing Machines (incl. combined dryers)" are listed under this "Large equipment" category3....

While digital electronics like computers and screens are significant contributors to the E-waste stream5.., the definition is not limited to them. Other household items with electrical components, such as vacuum cleaners, microwaves, electric kettles, and even electric toys, are also considered E-waste1. The increasing consumption of electronic devices and their shorter lifecycles are contributing to E-waste being the fastest-growing waste stream5....

## What is driving that increase
Based on the sources, the largest contributors to e-waste generation include:
- Increased consumption of electronic devices is a significant factor contributing to the growing amount of e-waste1 .... The more electronics are purchased and used, the more will eventually be discarded3 .
- Short lifecycles of electronic devices also play a crucial role3 .... Rapid technological advancements and consumer preferences for newer models lead to shorter periods of use for electronic equipment4 ...
- Limited repair options exacerbate the problem by making it less feasible or desirable to fix broken devices, leading to their disposal and replacement with new ones3 . The new EU legislation on "right to repair" is mentioned as potentially consequential in addressing this9 .
- Multiple device ownership contributes to higher rates of e-waste generation4 .... Individuals and households often own several electronic devices, such as smartphones, tablets, and computers, increasing the overall volume of discarded electronics4 ....
- The growth of cloud computing services can also indirectly contribute by driving demand for more and newer devices to access these services4 ....
- Short replacement cycles driven by technological advancements and market trends lead consumers to replace their devices more frequently, even if they are still functional4 ....
- Issues related to software, such as programmes being supported by only specific hardware or not being usable with newer equipment, can also lead to shorter replacement cycles and increased e-waste10 ....
- The continuous demand for new electronics necessitates the extraction of minerals and metals, and these materials, once part of electronic devices, eventually end up as e-waste1 ....

The combination of these factors makes e-waste one of the fastest-growing waste streams globally3 .... In 2019 alone, the world generated 53.6 Mt of e-waste, and this is projected to increase significantly in the coming decades unless practices are modified

> To meet this growing demand, the extraction of minerals and metals like graphite, lithium, and cobalt could increase by 500% by 2050 severely impacting the land, water, air, and biodiversity.  

When poorly governed, mining can threaten our environment: destroy natural landscapes, pollute groundwater, and displace indigenous people. Ultimately these minerals and metals - fused together as an electronic device - end up in large e-waste dumping sites

## An EU e-waste target?
#Ireland #E-Waste 
Source: [Independant](https://m.independent.ie/irish-news/eu-targets-dont-properly-reflect-e-waste-recycling-efforts/a627907177.html)

An organisation that facilitates the collection of electronic waste has expressed concern that EU recycling targets ignore the impact of a growing circular economy in Ireland.

> Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment (WEEE) Ireland, which is marking 20 years in operation this year, has highlighted that more than 250 million old and broken electrical items have been prevented from entering landfill since 2005.

WEEE said it has facilitated the collection and recycling of more than 609,000 tonnes of electrical waste i the last 20 years.

That haul included 7.6 million large household appliances, 1.8 million fridges, 5.1 million televisions and monitors, 47.5 million lamps, and the equivalent of 725 million used AA batteries.

However, the not-for-profit organisation, which operates Ireland's largest e-waste compliance scheme, said the country will struggle to meet EU targets on electronic collections going forward due to the way the goals are set.

> [!NOTE] What is the requirement? 
> EU member states are required to collect a minimum of 65pc of the average weight of electrical and electronic equipment placed on the market over the preceding three years.
> 

WEEE Ireland chief executive Leo Donovan said Ireland's efforts are not being properly recognised within the targets as they do not account for the improved lifespan of technology, as well as efforts to repair and reuse products.

###### "The EU's current linear weight-based targets do not consider the extended lifespans of modern electronics or the growing emphasis on repair and reuse, meaning Ireland's true environmental progress goes unrecognised. 

> So if a mobile phone is repaired or refurbished, it does not count toward Ireland's official environmental performance, despite keeping raw materials in circulation.

He said those link-ups have led to more than 30,000 tonnes of steel, plastic and glass being extracted from e-waste every year.

Other valuable metals such as aluminium, copper and iron are also extracted.
