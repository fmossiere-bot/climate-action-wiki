Archeological evidence suggests that composting has been used since Neolithic times, That’s about 12,000 years ago

Composting is nature's ultimate recycling program. It also packs quite a punch as it both reduces emissions AND improves the health of the soil. It's the process of transforming kitchen scraps, yard waste, and other organic materials into a rich, soil-like substance called compost. Sometimes called "black gold" by gardeners, compost is key to healthy plants.

A compost pile is a tiny ecosystem with microorganisms like bacteria and fungi being the stars of the show. They work hard breaking down organic matter, fueled by a balance of:

- Browns (Carbon source): Dry materials like leaves, shredded paper, and wood chips.
- Greens (Nitrogen source): Food scraps, grass clippings, coffee grounds.
- Water: To keep things moist but not soggy.
- Air: Turning your pile helps those hardworking microbes breathe.


Composting is awesome because it…


### Diverts harmful methane emissions from landfills

When organic waste decomposes in a landfill, the lack of oxygen forces a process called anaerobic decomposition, **which releases methane, a greenhouse gas significantly more harmful than carbon dioxide over short timeframes**. Methane is responsible for more than 25% of global warming and is 80 times as harmful over a 20 year period.

Composting, on the other hand, creates an oxygen-rich environment, promoting aerobic decomposition – a fundamentally different process that primarily releases carbon dioxide instead. Yes CO2 is something we’re trying to curb, but way better than releasing methane. By composting, we shift the decomposition process and significantly reduce harmful methane emissions.

### Stores carbon in soil

Finished compost - when used as soil to grow plants and veggies - a**cts as a carbon sink, storing carbon in the soil long-term**. This helps keep it out of the atmosphere, slowing the rate of climate change

  
### Reduces the Need for Fertilisers

Manufacturing chemical fertilisers is an energy-intensive process that releases nitrous oxide (over 300x as bad as carbon dioxide!). If we use compost  instead as a natural alternative, we reduce the need for these fertilisers. …plus the plants will thank you, they much prefer natural fertilizers!  

  

### Improves Soil Health and Resilience

Healthy compost-amended soil is better at holding moisture, crucial during droughts (which are increasing due to climate change). This reduces the need for excessive irrigation, conserving water resources.

# A real Climate Solution
Composting can range from backyard bins to industrial-scale operations. Regardless, it converts organic waste into soil carbon, averting landfill methane emissions in the process and it is a real solution with multiple co-benefits. 

> Backyard bins and industrial-scale composting turn organic waste into valuable fertilisers, while preventing it from producing methane as it decomposes in a landfill, reducing emissions by 50% 

 **Impact**
> In 2015, an estimated 38 percent of food waste was composted in the US; 57 percent was composted in the EU. If all low-income countries reached the US rate and all middle-income countries achieved the EU rate, composting could avoid methane emissions from landfills equivalent to 1.13–1.40 gigatons of carbon dioxide by 2050. That total excludes additional gains from applying compost to soil. Compost facilities cost less to construct than to operate, which is reflected in the financial results. For this reason, composting is a real solution.


**Co-benefits**
> - Composting reduces waste sent to landfills which is often incinerated and generates carbon emissions.
> - Reducing landfill waste can reduce air pollution and associated respiratory illnesses such as chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, asthma, and pneumonia.
> - When used for gardening or farming, compost reduce the need of fertilizers and pesticides in soils and helps improve soil health and thereby increase agricultural yields.
> - Compost retains water, prevents erosion, reduces runoff, establishes vegetation, and improves downstream water quality by retaining pollutants like heavy metals, nitrogen, phosphorus, oil, grease, fuels, herbicides, and pesticides.

# Getting started with composting at home

Even those with a tiny patch of outdoor space can join the composting revolution. Here's a breakdown of how to get started and tackle those key questions.

## Finding your Spot and Sizing it Up

You don't need acreage to compost. Hunt for a small corner in your yard, on a patio, or even a balcony. A 1x1m area usually suffices. To estimate capacity, consider your household's food waste output. Are you a solo dweller with minimal scraps, or a family with lots of vegetable peels? Choose a compost method matching your volume.

### Compost Bin Options

- DIY Wooden Frame: This budget-friendly option allows you to customise size and materials. However, it requires building skills and might not be as durable as a purchased bin.
- Ready-made Compost Bin: These come in various sizes, materials (plastic, recycled materials), and features (ventilation, lids, tumbling mechanisms). They are convenient, often easier to manage, and some models can be aesthetically pleasing. Consider factors like durability, capacity, and budget when choosing

### Compost Supplies and Setup 

The budget-friendly approach is building a pile directly on the ground or in a simple DIY wooden frame. A pitchfork or aerating tool will make turning the pile easier.  Setup time can be as quick as an afternoon!

## Watch the Magic Happen

### Filling Your Compost

The golden rule is a good mix of "browns" and "greens". Browns include leaves, shredded paper, cardboard. Greens are your veggie scraps, fruit cores, coffee grounds, etc. In urban environments, it’s best to avoid meat, dairy, bones, or oily foods - they can slow down the decomposition rate and create unpleasant odors. Plus, it’s prohibited by some local authorities due to its potential to spread pathogens or attract pests. 


### Time to Compost

It takes several months to a year for a home compost pile to turn into usable "black gold." Turning the pile regularly speeds things up.

## Composting Top Tips & Tricks

- Chop it up: Smaller pieces break down faster.
- Moisture matters: Compost should feel like a wrung-out sponge.
- Turn, turn, turn: Giving your pile a regular mix with your pitchfork helps it work its magic. Once a week in warm weather, and less often in colder months.
- Location matters: Place the bin in a partially shaded area with good air circulation. Avoid direct sunlight, which can dry out the compost, and keep it away from your house to prevent unwanted pests.
- Layer it up: Alternate between browns and greens when adding materials.
- Monitor temperature: Ideally, your compost should reach temperatures between 54-71°C for optimal decomposition. If the temperature is too low, add more browns or turn the pile more frequently.
- Balance is key: A good ratio of browns to greens is 3:1. If your compost has a foul odor, it likely needs more browns to absorb moisture and balance the nitrogen content.
- Embrace the worms: While not essential, adding worms can be beneficial for faster decomposition, improved aeration, and creating nutrient-rich castings.
- Be resourceful: Look for creative ways to collect browns. Shredded paper, cardboard egg cartons, and leaves from your yard are all great options.

# Share the Love: What to do with your compost

## Enhance your own garden

Once your compost has matured and reached a dark, crumbly texture, you can directly incorporate it into your outdoor garden beds. Aim for a 5-10 cm layer and mix it gently into the top layer of soil. Or you can use it as a potting mix for houseplants or outdoor containers by adding 20-30% compost to provide vital nutrients and improve drainage.

---
## Share it with others

Many community gardens welcome contributions of finished compost to enrich their shared growing space. Contact your local community garden to inquire about their policies.

If your local municipality offers curbside composting or green waste collection, it’s likely you can leave your compost out for collection, too. Check with your local waste management department to check the options available. If you’re unsure who that might be, Google “Food recycling in

And finally, you can share your "black gold" with gardening friends or neighbors! This fosters community spirit and promotes sustainable practices within your local network.
