#fashion #sustainability 


Go fully circular with your fashion decisions (shop only pre-loved), minimizing the negative impacts of your self-expression.

### “Fast fashion isn’t free. Someone, somewhere is paying.” – Lucy Siegle.

Our clothes are an essential part of comfort and self-expression. However, we rarely consider the full life cycle of our garments and their large scale environmental and social impacts. The fashion industry is currently one of the largest polluters on the planet, but there are many ways that you can participate in the transformation of this system. This action guide will equip you with knowledge and resources to engage with fashion in a way that is sustainable, circular, and stylish.

The Impact of the Fashion Industry Today

Consider the life-cycle of a cotton-polyester blend T-shirt (the most common material used in clothing). Cotton is cultivated, usually using chemical fertilizers and a large amount of water. Polyester is derived from petroleum in an energy-intensive process called polymerization.These materials both need to be spun into fibers before being woven together to make a textile. This textile is then processed in various ways, including dyeing, which uses highly toxic chemicals. Once the textile has been sewn into a T-shirt, it is then shipped to a retail store, where it is purchased, worn, and washed a limited number of times,with each wash releasing microplastic fibers. Eventually, the garment is donated, upcycled, thrown away, or incinerated. Between each step in this process, the material is being shipped long distances, excess material is being thrown away, and toxic chemicals are being leached into the air, water, and soil.

> [!Here are some statistics about the environmental impacts of fashion:]
> - The fashion industry currently accounts for about 10% of global emissions and the industry is the second-largest consumer of water on the planet.[1](https://earth.org/fast-fashions-detrimental-effect-on-the-environment/)
> - Between 80 and 100 billion new clothing garments are produced globally every year.[2](https://theroundup.org/textile-waste-statistics/)
> - 87% of the materials and fibers used to make clothing will end up in either incinerators or landfills.[2](https://theroundup.org/textile-waste-statistics/)
> - Only 20% of discarded textiles are collected.[2](https://theroundup.org/textile-waste-statistics/)
> - Only 1% of clothes will get recycled into new garments.[2](https://theroundup.org/textile-waste-statistics/)
> - About 35% of the microplastics that enter the ocean come from washing  synthetic textiles.[1](https://earth.org/fast-fashions-detrimental-effect-on-the-environment/)]


Beyond the environmental costs, the fashion industry perpetuates a massive amount of social injustice. Unsafe working conditions are the norm for garment workers. Forced labor is not uncommon, neither is child labor. Wages are often unfair and insufficient. Large fast fashion brands defer responsibility for these human rights abuses to the complex network of subcontractors that they work with. While most brands claim to support ethical labor practices, there is little regulation or oversight.[3](https://sites.uab.edu/humanrights/2018/04/26/fast-fashion-unethical-and-unsustainable/)

## A Primer on Circular Fashion

There are significant initiatives underway to shift the fashion industry to be less harmful, and you can be a part of the change. Currently, the economy is based on a linear model, which moves from material extraction to use to disposal. The idea of a circular economy is based on the cycles of the natural world, which form a closed loop with zero waste. All byproducts of natural processes are materials for another process. For example, fallen leaves are consumed by worms that provide nutrients to the soil so that plants can thrive. In the context of fashion, this means minimizing waste in, utilizing healthy materials, extending the life of garments, and finding new life for textiles when they can no longer be worn.

Through changes in supply chains and consumption habits, we can close the loop on fashion. There are many ways that brands are taking action to close the loop, such as upcycling, recycling, and experimentation with regenerative, bio-based materials. Governments are also shifting laws to incentivize these changes. The EU in particular is making great strides toward forcing fast fashion brands to take accountability for the full life cycle of their products.[4](https://climatemoney.substack.com/p/the-eu-vs-fast-fashion-and-the-end?utm_medium=email) Additionally, you can make choices as a consumer that extend the life of your garments, divest your resources from harmful supply chains, and connect you to a growing circular economy.

  

## Actions to Make Your Fashion More Circular

For the next four weeks, consider your fashion consumption choices and look for opportunities to be more circular. If you don’t buy clothes often, do a deep clean of your wardrobe and see if you can repair, upcycle, recycle, or donate any of your garments.

> [!TIPS]
> - Assess and organize your wardrobe, it is crucial to know what you already have in your closet to avoid over-buying.
> - Buy vintage clothes whenever possible, prioritizing local brick-and-mortar establishments to minimize shipping.
> - Buy less, buy better: prioritize quality over quantity. While you will pay more per garment, the lifespan of each item is much longer than a cheap, fast-fashion garment.
> - Care and repair: extend the life of your clothes through careful use, not overwashing, and repairing when possible.
> - Upcycling and customization: look for opportunities to upcycle or otherwise give clothing a new life, even if just cutting it up to use as rags.
> - Utilize alternatives to buying clothes for specific occasions: renting, swapping, and consignment are all great options.
> - Supporting circular and sustainable brands when buying new.
> - Prioritize organic, single-material textiles when buying new.
