We produce more than the planet can regenerate. Every year, Earth Overshoot Day marks the point at which humanity has consumed more natural resources than the Earth can renew in twelve months. In 2024, that date fell on 1 August. For the rest of the year, we are running on borrowed resources.

> The circular economy is one of the most serious structural responses to this problem. It is not a niche concept. It is increasingly central to EU climate and industrial policy, and it is starting to reshape regulation, business models, and consumer rights across Europe.

---

### What Is the Circular Economy?

The current economic model is largely linear: take raw materials, make a product, use it, throw it away. The circular economy is built on a different logic. As the European Commission defines it, a circular economy preserves the value of products, materials and resources for as long as possible, in order to minimise waste.

In practice, this means designing products to last longer, be repaired, be reused, or have their components recovered and fed back into production. It means asking, at the design stage, what happens to this product at the end of its life, rather than treating that question as someone else's problem.

The three core principles are often summarised as:

- **Eliminate waste and pollution** at the design stage, before it is created
- **Keep products and materials in use** for as long as possible
- **Regenerate natural systems** rather than depleting them

### What Does It Look Like in Practice?

The circular economy takes different forms depending on the sector.

**Repair and reuse** is perhaps the most visible entry point for consumers. Rather than replacing a broken appliance, a circular model supports getting it fixed. Sharing platforms and tool libraries, sometimes called Libraries of Things, are growing fast across Europe. A 2025 European market study found that the number of sharing stations had increased by 70% in a single year, with over 1,300 stations now serving more than 350,000 users.

**Extended producer responsibility** shifts the cost of waste from public budgets to the companies that create the products. Under this model, manufacturers are financially responsible for what happens to their products at end of life, which creates a direct incentive to design them better from the start.

**Industrial symbiosis** is a less visible but equally important dimension. In some industrial zones, the waste or by-products of one company become the raw material for another. The Kalundborg Symbiosis in Denmark is one of the oldest and best-documented examples, where a network of companies exchange steam, water, ash, and other materials that would otherwise go to waste.

**Textile and fashion** is one of the most urgent areas of application. T**he EU generates 12.6 million tonnes of wasted textiles per year, with clothing and footwear accounting for 41% of that total.** The shift to fast fashion has made this worse. Circular approaches here include rental and resale platforms, garment take-back schemes, and design standards that make clothes easier to repair or recycle.

### Is It Part of the EU Climate Plan?

Yes, and significantly so. The **European Green Deal,** launched in 2019, positions the circular economy as a central pillar of the EU's path to climate neutrality by 2050. The logic is straightforward: approximately half of global greenhouse gas emissions come from the extraction and processing of raw materials. Reducing that extraction, by keeping materials in circulation longer, directly reduces emissions.

The EU has published two Circular Economy Action Plans. The first, in 2015, outlined 54 specific actions. The second, in 2020, set more ambitious targets, most notably doubling the EU's circularity rate by 2030. The current target, confirmed and slightly raised under the Clean Industrial Deal, **is to reach 24% by 2030, up from 12.2% today.**

The gap between ambition and reality is significant. 

> Although the EU's circular material use rate has increased since 2010, rising from 10.7% to 12.2% in 2024, progress is considered slow and the EU is currently not on track to meet its 2030 target. To put that in perspective: achieving the target would require the circularity rate to grow by more than 1.7 percentage points annually, which is more than the total increase recorded over the entire period from 2010 to 2024.

The picture varies considerably across member states. In 2024, the Netherlands led with a circularity rate of 32.7%, followed by Belgium at 22.7% and Italy at 21.6%, already meeting or approaching the 2030 target. At the other end, Romania recorded just 1.3%, while Finland and Ireland each stood at 2%. This gap shows that the target is achievable, but only for countries that have made it a genuine priority.

Financing remains a structural challenge. Recent estimates put the annual financing needs for a circular economy transition in the EU at 55 billion euros. The European Investment Bank's lending to circularity projects reached 3.8 billion euros over the period 2019 to 2023, a positive trend but still far short of what is needed.

Looking ahead, the European Commission launched a public consultation in August 2025 on a forthcoming Circular Economy Act, expected to be adopted in 2026. The Act aims to establish a single market for secondary raw materials, increase the supply of high-quality recycled materials, and stimulate demand for these materials across EU industries.

### New Policies and Laws

Despite slow overall progress, the legislative landscape is shifting. Several significant measures have been introduced or are in development.

**Right to Repair Directive (2024)** The European Parliament passed new rules requiring sellers to repair products rather than replace them, unless repair is more expensive. The law also creates clearer rights for consumers to access repairs after a product's guarantee has expired. The timing matters: electronics are the fastest growing source of waste in the EU. More than 3.5 million tonnes of electronic waste were collected in 2017, and only 40% was recycled.

**Textile and Food Waste Rules (provisional, 2025)** EU lawmakers have reached a provisional agreement on new rules requiring meaningful reductions in both textile and food waste. Around 59 million tonnes of food waste are generated in the EU each year, representing an estimated direct economic loss of 132 billion euros. Under the new framework, food manufacturers will need to reduce waste by 10% by 2030, while retailers, restaurants and households will be required to cut food waste by 30% per capita. Fashion brands will face fees tied to their circularity performance, with companies that design more durable and recyclable products paying less. The provisional agreement still needs to be formally endorsed by the European Council and Parliament before becoming law.

**Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation** This broader regulation, agreed in 2024, extends ecodesign requirements beyond energy-related products to cover most goods sold in the EU. It sets requirements on durability, repairability, recyclability, and recycled content, with the goal of making unsustainable products the exception rather than the norm.

### Companies and Initiatives Worth Watching

**Renewlondon / Repair Cafes** Community repair initiatives have grown substantially across Europe. Repair Cafes, which originated in the Netherlands, now operate in over 2,500 locations across the continent, offering free repairs for household items with the help of volunteers.

**Backmarket** The French platform for refurbished electronics has become one of Europe's most prominent circular economy success stories, now operating in over 16 countries and valued at several billion euros. It demonstrates that circular models can scale commercially.

**Patagonia** Though US-based, Patagonia operates a well-regarded worn wear programme in Europe that buys back, repairs, and resells used clothing. It has influenced a wider shift in outdoor and sportswear brands towards take-back schemes.

**Libraries of Things** Growing networks of shared tool and equipment libraries across the UK, Ireland, Belgium, and the Netherlands allow people to borrow items they need occasionally rather than buying them. In Dublin, the Share Library operates from several locations and serves as a practical local example of the sharing economy in action.

**WEEE Ireland** Ireland's largest e-waste collection non-profit has prevented more than 250 million electrical items from entering landfill since 2005, collecting over 609,000 tonnes of electrical waste in twenty years. It has also raised a legitimate concern: EU recycling targets, based on weight, do not adequately recognise repair and reuse efforts, or account for the longer lifespans of modern appliances. This is a gap in the current regulatory framework that needs addressing.

---

### Where Things Stand

In 2024, 78% of Europeans acknowledged that environmental problems directly affect their daily lives, and around 60% expressed concern about growing quantities of waste, a share that has nearly doubled over the past 15 years. Public awareness is not the problem. The gap is in translating that concern into systemic change at the speed and scale the targets require. The companies and initiatives that are making circularity work tend to share one thing: they have found a way to make it financially viable, not just environmentally desirable. Closing that gap, across all 27 member states, is the defining challenge of the next decade.
