We buy them without much thought, wear them until they fall apart, and throw them away. But shoes are one of the most environmentally complex products in our daily lives, built from multiple materials, assembled across multiple countries, and almost impossible to recycle at the end of their life.

> The footwear industry produced around **23.9 billion pairs of shoes in 2024.** The industry accounts for approximately 1.4% of global carbon emissions, representing around one fifth of the fashion industry's total emissions. Manufacturing processes alone generate approximately 313 million metric tonnes of CO2 annually, equivalent to the emissions of 66 million cars. To put that in context, global aviation accounts for around 2.5% of emissions. Shoes, as a single product category, are not far behind.

**Around 95% of used footwear ends up in landfill or incineration**. There is no mature large-scale circular system for shoes. Most pairs are made from a combination of leather, synthetic rubber, polyester, foam, glue and metal eyelets, all bonded together in ways that make separation and recycling technically very difficult.

---

### Why Shoes Are So Hard to Make Sustainable

A typical pair of trainers can involve 65 or more distinct components, often sourced from different countries and assembled in factories where labour standards vary enormously. The environmental problems span the entire supply chain.

**Leather** is one of the most common materials. Its production involves livestock farming, which generates significant greenhouse gas emissions and uses large amounts of land and water. The tanning process uses chromium and other heavy chemicals that are difficult to manage and can seriously pollute water sources.

**Synthetic materials** like polyester and nylon are petroleum-based, energy-intensive to produce, and shed microplastics during use and washing. They are not biodegradable.

**Rubber** for soles, particularly synthetic rubber, is also fossil-fuel derived. Natural rubber from tropical forests is a better option, but supply chain transparency is often limited.

A life cycle assessment by MIT on a single pair of trainers found it generated 13.6 kg of greenhouse gas emissions, equivalent to keeping a 100-watt bulb on for a week. Multiply that by 24 billion pairs and the scale becomes clear.

### Brands Trying to Do It Differently

A small but growing number of brands are rethinking how shoes are designed, sourced and made. Here are some worth knowing about.

**Veja (France)**
URL: https://project.veja-store.com/en
Veja is probably the best-known name in ethical footwear. Founded by two French graduates in 2004, the company built its model around direct relationships with small family cooperatives in South America. Its materials include organic cotton from Peru for canvas and laces, natural Amazonian rubber for soles, and leather sourced from Uruguay. Shoes are produced in factories in Brazil and Portugal with high labour standards. Recycled plastic bottles and recycled polyester are also used for certain ranges. Distribution in Europe is handled by a social inclusion company. Veja was one of the first brands to make supply chain transparency a central part of its identity, not just a marketing claim.

**Ubac (France and Portugal)**
URL: https://ubac-store.com/en/ 
Ubac makes sneakers from recycled wool, produced locally in France or Portugal, using natural and recycled materials. The sole contains 35% recycled material. The brand offers an end-of-life recycling scheme, giving customers a way to return old pairs rather than sending them to landfill. It is also committed to the LPO, the French league for bird protection, linking the brand to broader biodiversity concerns.

**Circle Sportswear (France)**
URL: https://en.circlesportswear.com/collections/chaussures-de-running
Circle Sportswear has built its running shoe around circular economy principles from the ground up. Every component has been chosen with end of life in mind. Materials include wood fibre, biosourced compounds and biodegradable elements. The goal is that when the shoe is finished, it can re-enter a material cycle rather than going to waste.

**MOEA (Portugal)**
URL: https://moea.io/en-int 
MOEA is a newer brand producing vegan shoes entirely in Portugal, keeping the supply chain short and local. The brand is digital-first, relying heavily on social media rather than physical retail, which also reduces its distribution footprint. Supply chain transparency is a core part of its positioning.

### The Flip Flop Problem

Flip flops might seem trivial, but the numbers are striking. Around 3 billion pairs are produced every year. One in two people on the planet wears them regularly. They are the best-selling footwear item in China, India and across Africa. And 10% of all plastic collected on beaches is made up of flip flops.

The vast majority are made from cheap EVA foam or PVC, neither of which biodegrades. They are typically made in a single low-cost factory run, worn for a season, and discarded.

Some alternatives are beginning to emerge. Bamboo, natural rubber and recycled materials are being used by smaller brands to make more durable and less damaging versions.

> One example worth highlighting is [**Hodoki**](https://hodokisandals.com/en/hodoki-soul/), a Spanish brand that has rethought the flip flop from scratch. Hodoki sandals are made in one piece, which both avoids the common failure point where straps separate from the sole and makes recycling easier. The material includes a compound derived from sugar cane, considered more sustainable than conventional fossil-fuel-based plastics. The brand produces entirely in Spain, avoiding intercontinental transport and ensuring labour standards in line with European regulations. The design itself is inspired by a 13th century Indian sandal model, updated with modern materials.

### What to Look For When Buying

The sustainable footwear market is still small and sometimes confusing. A few principles help cut through:

- **Fewer materials, better end of life.** Shoes made from fewer bonded components are generally easier to repair or recycle.
- **Local or regional production** reduces transport emissions and usually implies higher labour standards.
- **Natural and bio-based materials** tend to outperform synthetics, provided they are sourced responsibly.
- **Durability matters more than anything.** The most sustainable shoe is the one you wear for ten years rather than two.
- **Brand transparency** is a reasonable proxy for seriousness. Brands that publish detailed supply chain information are generally more accountable than those that do not.

The industry is moving, slowly. But consumer pressure, tightening EU regulations on product sustainability, and a new generation of brands treating ethics as a design constraint rather than an afterthought are all pushing in the right direction.
